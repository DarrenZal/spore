# Boundary-Making Translation Memo

## 1. Scope And Rule

This memo is a translation memo only. It narrows the `boundary-making-and-entangled-systems` family after the scoping assessment (`boundary-making-scoping-assessment.md`) determined that the family is "possibly viable but too translation-sensitive."

The goal is to identify a portable governance/design core — vocabulary and design principles that can function in the grammar without depending on contested formal apparatus. This is not a pursuit of formal completeness.

No promotion-status change, synthesis note, bridge note, claim sheet, lexicon entry, graph projection, or canon edit is produced by this memo.

## 2. Starting Point

The scoping assessment found:

- **The design insight is real.** Governance design should focus on shaping mediation structures (the quality of coupling between domains) rather than drawing better boundaries (the completeness of separation between domains). Three independent traditions confirm this: Beer's variety engineering, Star & Griesemer's boundary objects, and Ostrom's boundary rules.

- **The formal apparatus is unstable.** The specific vocabulary the bridge note proposes — Markov blankets, conditional-independence gradients, screening relations — faces serious translation problems. The Bruineberg et al. critique (2022) shows that "Markov blanket" equivocates between a trivially true statistical concept and an empirically undemonstrated physical/ontological claim. No published work applies the full Markov-blanket formalism to governance design. The Active Inference Institute's organizational work remains programmatic, not productive.

- **The family remains interesting** because the grammar already uses membrane, protocol, and projection vocabulary to describe its coordination architecture but describes them functionally (what they do) rather than formally (what design principle governs their interface properties). No promoted or synthesized family occupies this territory.

This memo must decide:
- What to keep as the future family core
- What to keep only as qualified background language
- What to cut from future active use
- How to resolve the tension between Ostrom's clear-boundary requirement and the family's anti-container posture

## 5. Ostrom Tension

### What Ostrom means by clear boundaries

Ostrom's first design principle for long-enduring commons institutions is "clearly defined boundaries." This means:

- **Who has rights:** Individuals or households with rights to withdraw resource units from the commons are clearly defined.
- **What the resource boundary is:** The boundaries of the commons resource itself are clearly defined.
- **What the rules are:** Entry, exit, and participation conditions are specified, known, and enforceable.

Ostrom's evidence (from hundreds of long-enduring commons worldwide) shows that when boundaries are ambiguous — when it is unclear who has access, what the resource includes, or what the participation rules are — governance fails. Free-riding increases, collective action collapses, and the commons degrades. Clarity is not rigidity: Ostrom's boundaries can be graduated, conditional, seasonal, and revocable. But they must be clear.

### What Johar is criticizing in naive boundary language

Johar's critique targets a different level. The bridge note argues that standard systems-thinking vocabulary — "boundary," "porous boundary," "semi-permeable boundary" — carries container metaphysics: the image of a line that separates inside from outside, with couplings as secondary modifications of that separation. The critique is that this framing:

- Treats the boundary as primary and the coupling as secondary, when the coupling is the functionally important structure
- Encourages design attention on the boundary's spatial properties (how thick, how porous) rather than on what the interface does (what it transmits, filters, regulates)
- Makes dissolution look like edge-breach rather than coupling-regime change

### Whether these are actually in tension

They are not in tension. They address different levels:

**Ostrom addresses operational governance rules.** Who can use the commons, under what conditions, with what obligations. These must be clear because governance requires accountability, and accountability requires knowing who is responsible and what they agreed to. This is about the rules at the interface, not about the nature of the interface itself.

**Johar addresses systems ontology.** How we conceptualize what a system IS and how it relates to other systems. Here, container metaphysics misleads because systems are not cleanly separable — they are regimes of mediated coupling whose identity is constituted by how they process what passes through them.

The apparent tension dissolves into a productive synthesis: **clear rules, non-container ontology.**

- Governance interfaces must have clear, enforceable, known rules (Ostrom)
- Those interfaces are mediation structures, not walls (Beer, Star/Griesemer, Johar)
- The rules should specify what the interface does — what it transmits, filters, translates, and regulates — not merely who is inside and who is outside (synthesis)

Clear mediation rules are *more* precise than clear boundary rules. They specify not just who is in/out but what passes through, with what fidelity, at what rate, under what conditions, and with what regulatory function. Beer's variety engineering models this exactly: the interface is a variety attenuator/amplifier with specified regulatory functions. It is clear (you can specify exactly what it does) but it is not a container (it is a regulatory channel in a coupled system).

### What Spore should preserve from each

**From Ostrom:**
- Governance interfaces must be clear, enforceable, and known
- Ambiguous interfaces are a governance failure mode
- Graduated and conditional rules are acceptable — ambiguous rules are not
- Nested governance requires explicit interface rules at each level

**From the mediation-over-demarcation principle:**
- Design attention should focus on what the interface does, not on its spatial properties
- Interfaces are mediation infrastructure: protocols, shared artifacts, regulatory functions
- System identity is constituted by mediation regime, not by boundary location
- Failure is coupling-regime change, not edge-breach

**The synthesis for Spore's grammar:**
- Membranes are mediation infrastructure with clear, specified regulatory functions
- "Authorized boundary crossing" (already in the grammar's constitutional commitments) is an interface rule: it specifies what must be true for something to pass through, not what the wall looks like
- Holon membranes should be designed by specifying their transmission, filtering, translation, and regulatory properties — not by describing their spatial containment
- The grammar's existing vocabulary (membranes, protocols, projections, authorized boundary crossing) already instantiates mediation-over-demarcation; the family's contribution is naming the design principle explicitly

## 6. Narrow Portable Core

If the family survives, its future core is:

**Minimum claim:** Governance boundary design should focus on the quality and selectivity of mediation between domains — the interfaces, protocols, and shared artifacts through which they interact — rather than on the completeness of their separation. Interfaces should be specified by what they transmit, filter, translate, and regulate, not by their spatial properties. Operational interface rules must be clear and enforceable (Ostrom) even when the underlying systems ontology is non-container (Beer, Star/Griesemer).

**Stable vocabulary:**
- *Mediation structure* — the interfaces, protocols, and shared artifacts through which governance domains interact. The primary design target for governance boundary architecture.
- *Differential transmission* — the observable property that governance interfaces transmit different kinds of information, authority, and resource flows at different rates and fidelity levels. A membrane design parameter, not a formal primitive.
- *Membrane-as-mediation-infrastructure* — the reframing of the grammar's existing membrane vocabulary under the mediation-over-demarcation principle.

**Level of operation:** Design principle for the grammar's membrane and protocol architecture. Informs how holonic membranes, authorized boundary crossing, learning membrane, and federation interfaces are designed. Does not require its own standalone lexicon primitive — it is more likely a design principle section within the grammar's architectural documentation, or a qualifying principle within an existing membrane-related entry.

**Why this is not already covered by relational-topology or holding-complexity:**

- *Relational-topology* covers the patterning of connection, distance, and encounter within the relational field — how contexts generate identity, distribute power, and produce coherence. It answers: "what does the pattern of relations look like?"
- *Holding-complexity* covers the civilisational condition in which complexity exceeds control — why adaptive coordination is needed. It answers: "why do we need this grammar?"
- *Mediation-over-demarcation* covers the formal description of interfaces between systems — how governance boundaries should be designed. It answers: "what design principle governs the interface properties of the grammar's membranes and protocols?"

Relational topology describes the field. Holding complexity describes the condition. Mediation-over-demarcation describes the interface design principle. These are structurally different claims about different aspects of the architecture.

## 7. What Becomes Background Or Gets Cut

### Qualified background (useful as intellectual context and occasional analytical lens, not part of the active family core)

- **Differential pass-through (reframed as differential transmission):** The observation that interfaces transmit with varying rates and fidelity is useful as a membrane design parameter. But it does not need its own formal status — Beer's variety attenuation provides the same analytical power with better grounding.
- **Breakdown as regime reconfiguration:** The diagnostic that governance failure is coupling-regime change (conditions for coordination cease to hold) is a useful complement to holding-complexity's two-attractor analysis. But it is a supplementary diagnostic, not a core design principle. It should be available as an analytical frame when examining governance failures but should not be maintained as a family-level claim.

### Cut from active use

- **Markov blankets:** Do not survive even as an optional technical analogy in active family vocabulary. The term imports contested mathematical authority, equivocates between trivially true and empirically undemonstrated senses, and adds no design content that Beer's variety engineering does not already provide. Markov blankets remain relevant only as intellectual genealogy — the bridge note's origin story — not as operational vocabulary. If a future Spore document references Markov blankets, it should be in a historical context ("the bridge note originally proposed...") not as a design recommendation.
- **Conditional-independence gradients:** Do not survive in any form. The statistical language suggests precision the social domain cannot support, and Ostrom's evidence shows that gradient boundaries are a governance failure mode. Carlile's three knowledge-boundary types (syntactic, semantic, pragmatic) provide the analytical equivalent — different functions need different mediation — without the statistical apparatus.
- **The claim that Spore's holons literally ARE Markov blankets:** Cut entirely. This ontological claim requires the Friston-sense Markov blanket to be empirically valid for social systems, which the philosophical literature does not support and which adds nothing useful to the grammar's design.

## 8. Translation Decision

**Family survives on a narrowed core.**

The narrowed core is: *mediation-over-demarcation as a design principle for the grammar's membrane architecture, grounded in Beer (variety engineering), Star/Griesemer (boundary objects), Carlile (knowledge boundaries), and Ostrom (boundary rules), with Ostrom's clear-operational-boundary requirement fully integrated.*

Why not "family survives only as background design language": The narrowed core is specific enough to guide membrane design in ways that the grammar's existing vocabulary does not. "Authorized boundary crossing" is a constitutional commitment but does not specify a design principle for how boundary-crossing interfaces should be designed. Mediation-over-demarcation fills that gap with multi-tradition support.

Why not "family should return to dormancy": The design insight is real, independently confirmed, and addresses a genuine gap in the grammar. The narrowed core is distinct from all adjacent families and is grounded in non-contested traditions. Dormancy would lose a contribution that no other family provides.

Why the narrowed core is defensible: It does not depend on any single contested formalism. It is supported by four independent traditions (cybernetics, organizational theory, knowledge management, institutional economics). It is reconciled with Ostrom's clear-boundary requirement. It is thin enough to be testable and thick enough to guide design.

## 11. Guardrails

- **Mediation is not the same as vagueness.** The mediation-over-demarcation principle requires specifying what interfaces do — what they transmit, filter, translate, and regulate. An unspecified interface is not a mediation structure; it is an ambiguous boundary. Beer's variety engineering and Ostrom's boundary rules both insist on specificity.

- **Clear governance boundaries and non-container systems ontology can coexist.** The Ostrom tension is resolved, not suppressed. Operational governance rules must be clear, enforceable, and known. The systems ontology that informs how those rules are designed does not need to treat systems as containers. Clear rules at non-container interfaces is the synthesis.

- **Technical analogy must not be mistaken for direct governance proof.** If Markov blankets or any formal apparatus from computational neuroscience or physics re-enters the family's vocabulary in future work, it must be explicitly qualified as analogy, not as governance evidence. The Bruineberg critique remains unresolved and the translation gap remains real.

- **If Markov blankets remain, they must remain heavily qualified.** Per this memo's judgment, they do not remain in the active family core. They survive only as intellectual genealogy — the bridge note's origin story. Any future invocation must engage the Bruineberg critique and must not borrow mathematical authority the governance domain cannot cash.

- **If the portable core is too thin, the family should not stay active.** The narrowed core (mediation-over-demarcation + anti-container corrective + clear-operational-boundary integration) is thin. If the corroboration pass finds that the design principle is already implicit in the grammar's existing membrane vocabulary and adds nothing operationally new, the family should be dissolved into background design language rather than maintained as an active family. Thickness must come from operational application, not from formal elaboration.
