**This is a mapping memo, not a corroboration memo.** It identifies where to search, what to search for, and what would count as success — but does not perform the search or evaluate results. A subsequent run will execute the actual corroboration research for the first target.

**Johar cross-theme recurrence is not independent evidence.** All 21 existing synthesis notes draw entirely from one author's corpus. Source counts measure conceptual breadth within Johar's work. Corroboration must come from outside the Johar corpus — from independent authors, traditions, empirical research, or institutional practice that arrive at the same structural claims through different routes.

**The goal is not to find agreement.** Many traditions will loosely resonate with these concepts. The goal is to find sources that independently name the same *structural mechanism* the family identifies — not merely adjacent concern, thematic similarity, or overlapping vocabulary.

## 2. What Counts as Corroboration

### Evidence Type 1: Conceptual parallel from another author or tradition

**What it can validate:** That the structural mechanism the family names has been independently identified by a different intellectual lineage — confirming that the insight is not idiosyncratic to Johar but discoverable from multiple starting points.

**What it cannot validate by itself:** That the mechanism operates as described in real institutions. A second theorist naming the same pattern does not make the pattern empirically real. It strengthens the conceptual claim but leaves the operational claim untested.

**When it is strong enough to matter:** When the external source arrives at the same structural account through a genuinely different route — not merely when both authors cite a common upstream tradition (which is convergence of reading, not convergence of analysis). The external source must name the *mechanism*, not just the *topic*.

### Evidence Type 2: Empirical or historical case

**What it can validate:** That the structural mechanism the family names has observable effects in real systems — that the failure mode, the design response, or the institutional condition can be seen operating in actual institutions, organisations, or governance settings.

**What it cannot validate by itself:** That the mechanism is general rather than local. A single historical case shows the pattern can exist, not that it typically does. Multiple cases from different contexts strengthen generality.

**When it is strong enough to matter:** When the case describes the *same structural mechanism*, not merely a similar outcome. An institution that failed is not evidence for a specific failure mechanism unless the case analysis identifies the mechanism the family names.

### Evidence Type 3: Operational design precedent

**What it can validate:** That the design response the family proposes has been independently developed or tested — that someone built something that embodies the same structural logic, whether or not they used the same vocabulary.

**What it cannot validate by itself:** That the design response works or is optimal. An operational precedent shows the design is buildable, not that it succeeds. Evaluation data strengthens the evidence.

**When it is strong enough to matter:** When the precedent's design logic matches the family's structural claim, not just its surface features. A cooperative is not automatically evidence for commitment-based coordination unless its governance structure specifically addresses the same problems (wage-specifiability failure, standing, commitment types).

### Evidence Type 4: Production or institutional practice evidence

**What it can validate:** That the structural mechanism operates in living institutional practice — not in theory, not in history, but in currently observable governance, coordination, or organisational behaviour. This is the strongest evidence type because it demonstrates the mechanism under current conditions.

**What it cannot validate by itself:** That the mechanism operates *because of* the structural conditions the family names. Practice evidence shows "this works" but may not isolate why. The causal mechanism still needs conceptual analysis.

**When it is strong enough to matter:** When the practice exhibits the family's structural logic and the practitioners can articulate why they designed it that way (not just what they built). Self-aware institutional design that independently arrived at the same conclusions is the gold standard.

## 4. Per-Target Search Lanes

### 4.1 linguistic-closure

**Definition checkpoint:**
Linguistic closure is a structural property of language — not a cognitive error or bias — in which naming compresses reality into operable categories, producing a sense of sufficiency that terminates inquiry. The closure mechanism operates at every scale: individual perception, discursive practice, institutional governance, civilisational coordination. The corrective is not better individual thinking but structural institutional mechanisms (claim lifecycle, discourse graph, error-finding incentive structures, staged promotion) that keep inquiry open. The family's distinctive claim is that closure is a property of *language and naming itself*, not of human cognition, and that the corrective must therefore be *institutional architecture*, not cognitive training.

**Likely corroboration lanes:**

1. **STS: black boxing and inscription (Latour, Callon, Law).** Bruno Latour's concept of "black boxing" in *Science in Action* (1987) — once a scientific fact or technical artifact is stabilized, its internal construction becomes invisible and inquiry stops. Michel Callon on "irreversibility" in techno-economic networks. John Law on "heterogeneous engineering." These describe exactly the mechanism: stabilisation produces the sense of sufficiency that terminates further questioning. The match is structural, not metaphorical.

2. **American pragmatism: inquiry and the fixation of belief (Peirce, Dewey).** C.S. Peirce's "The Fixation of Belief" (1877) — four methods of fixing belief, of which only the scientific method preserves self-correction. Dewey's *The Quest for Certainty* (1929) — certainty as an impediment to inquiry, and the argument that institutions must be designed to sustain inquiry rather than settle it. The match is precise: both name certainty/fixation as a structural attractor that institutional design must resist.

3. **Paradigm theory (Kuhn, Lakatos).** Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) — paradigms determine what can be perceived as a problem, what methods are legitimate, what solutions count. "Normal science" is linguistic closure at the disciplinary level. Imre Lakatos on "degenerating research programmes." The match is to the amplification conditions: when a paradigm hardens, it determines what can be noticed before the question of truth arises.

4. **Organisational sensemaking (Weick).** Karl Weick's *Sensemaking in Organizations* (1995) — organisations "enact" their environments by imposing categories on ambiguous stimuli, then treat the resulting categorisation as given reality. "Enacted sensemaking" describes the institutional version of linguistic closure: the organisation's categories create the world it then navigates. The match is to the institutional mechanism, not just the cognitive one.

5. **Reification in social theory (Lukács, Berger & Luckmann).** Berger and Luckmann's *The Social Construction of Reality* (1966) — how socially constructed categories become experienced as objective, natural, and unquestionable. Lukács on reification as the process by which human-created structures come to dominate human perception. These describe the social mechanism by which categories harden.

**Likely false friends:**

- **Cognitive bias / behavioural economics (Kahneman, Tversky).** Confirmation bias, anchoring, and framing effects describe *individual* cognitive mechanisms. The Johar claim is specifically that closure is a property of *language in institutional contexts*, not of individual cognition. Evidence that humans have cognitive biases does not corroborate that naming itself produces institutional closure.
- **Post-structuralism (Derrida, Barthes).** Deconstruction demonstrates that meaning is never fully stable — but Derrida's project addresses the *impossibility* of final closure at the philosophical level, not the *institutional design* required to resist practical closure. The corrective Johar proposes is structural (claim lifecycle, discourse graph), not deconstructive.
- **Conceptual metaphor theory (Lakoff, Johnson).** *Metaphors We Live By* demonstrates how metaphors structure thought and constrain perception. The mechanism is related but the level is wrong: Lakoff addresses cognition, not institutional governance. Metaphor constraint is a mechanism *within* linguistic closure but does not name the institutional condition.

**Best search queries:**
1. Latour "black boxing" stabilization institution inquiry closure
2. Peirce "fixation of belief" institutional design self-correction
3. Dewey "quest for certainty" institutional inquiry design
4. Kuhn paradigm "normal science" "category" governance institutional
5. Weick sensemaking "enacted" category organizational "terminates inquiry"
6. Berger Luckmann reification "social construction" institutional category
7. "epistemic closure" institution governance NOT cognitive-bias NOT political
8. "conceptual lock-in" institutional organization governance
9. "category ossification" institution revision mechanism
10. "black box" institutional knowledge "structural mechanism" reopening

**Best evidence type first:** Conceptual parallel (Latour/STS and Peirce/pragmatism are the strongest lanes — both independently name the structural mechanism, not just the topic).

**Why this target is a good but not best first research pass:** Linguistic closure has the deepest tradition resonance and the broadest corpus reach. But the exact claim (closure as structural property of language in institutional contexts, not as cognitive error) is specific enough that false-friend filtering will require careful reading. The STS and pragmatist lanes are rich but diffuse — many sources will be *adjacent* without naming the exact mechanism. For a first corroboration run, legitimacy-and-shared-consequence offers a higher probability of finding direct matches quickly.

### 4.2 legitimacy-and-shared-consequence

**Definition checkpoint:**
Institutions become viable when legitimacy and consequence are held together — when actors are bound into the outcomes they help shape and when legitimacy is a co-held architecture across plural dimensions (democracy, stewardship, experimentation, radical markets), not merely procedural approval. When authority and consequence separate — when some actors hold decision power without bearing outcomes, or bear outcomes without holding decision power — institutions fracture into control spirals, relational asymmetry, and epistemic collapse. The family's distinctive claim is that legitimacy is *polycentric and co-held* (no single axis suffices), that shared consequence is an *infrastructural condition* (not a moral preference), and that *orchestration replaces dominance* as the coordination mode.

**Likely corroboration lanes:**

1. **Polycentric governance (Elinor Ostrom, Vincent Ostrom).** Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (1990) and her 8 design principles for long-enduring commons institutions. Vincent Ostrom's *The Meaning of Democracy and the Vulnerability of Democracies* (1997). The Bloomington School's core insight — that effective governance requires multiple, overlapping centres of authority rather than a single sovereign — is a direct match to the "plural and co-held" claim. Ostrom's design principles (especially #2: congruence between rules and local conditions; #4: monitoring by accountable parties; #7: minimal recognition of rights to organize) directly address authority-consequence binding.

2. **Voice, exit, and institutional design (Hirschman).** Albert Hirschman's *Exit, Voice, and Loyalty* (1970) — how the relationship between voice (the power to influence through participation) and exit (the power to leave) shapes institutional behaviour. When consequence-bearing actors lack voice, they exit; when authority-holding actors face no exit consequences, institutions degrade. This is the structural mechanism by which authority-consequence separation produces institutional failure.

3. **State legibility and institutional failure (Scott).** James C. Scott's *Seeing Like a State* (1998) — how state-imposed legibility schemes that separate authority from local consequence systematically fail. "High modernist" planning produces control spirals: the state simplifies, the simplification fails, the state simplifies further. This matches the control-surveillance spiral described in the Johar synthesis.

4. **Legitimacy theory (Beetham, Scharpf).** David Beetham's *The Legitimation of Power* (1991) — legitimacy as a multi-dimensional condition (legal validity, normative justifiability, expressed consent), not merely procedural correctness. Fritz Scharpf's input/output legitimacy distinction — the recognition that institutional legitimacy requires both the right to decide (input) and the quality of outcomes (output). These provide the theoretical framework for polycentric legitimacy.

5. **Commons governance and digital commons (Bollier, Hess, Benkler).** David Bollier's *Think Like a Commoner* (2014). Charlotte Hess and Elinor Ostrom's *Understanding Knowledge as a Commons* (2006). Yochai Benkler's *The Wealth of Networks* (2006). Digital commons (Wikipedia governance, open-source projects) provide production evidence for shared-consequence governance — participants who both shape and bear the outcomes of governance decisions.

6. **Indigenous governance systems.** Indigenous governance traditions (e.g., Haudenosaunee Confederacy's Great Law of Peace, Maori tikanga, Aboriginal consensus decision-making) that structurally bind authority to consequence through kinship, reciprocity, and intergenerational obligation. These provide historical case evidence for polycentric legitimacy outside the Western institutional tradition.

**Likely false friends:**

- **Deliberative democratic theory (Habermas).** Habermas's theory of communicative action addresses how legitimate decisions are produced through rational discourse. But it focuses on the *procedure* for reaching agreement, not on the *structural condition* of authority-consequence binding. Habermasian deliberation can produce legitimate-seeming decisions where consequence is still structurally separated from authority.
- **Principal-agent theory.** Agency theory addresses delegated authority through incentive alignment (contracts, monitoring, penalties). But it treats consequence-binding as a *contractual mechanism* for controlling agents, not as an *institutional condition* for co-held legitimacy. The Johar claim is about polycentric co-holding, not about principals controlling agents.
- **Corporate governance / accountability frameworks.** Corporate accountability (board oversight, auditing, ESG reporting) addresses consequence-binding within the firm-as-entity frame. But it typically assumes a single legitimacy axis (shareholder value or stakeholder balance) and treats accountability as compliance, not as shared consequence.

**Best search queries:**
1. Ostrom "polycentric governance" legitimacy "consequence" binding
2. Ostrom "design principles" commons "authority" "accountability" binding
3. Hirschman "exit voice" institutional failure consequence authority
4. Scott "Seeing Like a State" control spiral legibility failure
5. Beetham legitimacy "multi-dimensional" NOT democratic-theory-only
6. Scharpf "input legitimacy" "output legitimacy" institutional design
7. "shared consequence" institutional design commons governance
8. "authority-consequence separation" institutional failure organizational
9. "control surveillance spiral" centralization governance failure
10. Wikipedia governance "shared consequence" commons decision-making

**Best evidence type first:** Empirical case (Ostrom's commons cases and Scott's state-failure cases are the most direct evidence, and both are well-documented and widely available).

**Why this target is the best first research pass:** Legitimacy-and-shared-consequence has the highest probability of yielding direct matches quickly. The Ostrom lane is dense, well-indexed, and directly relevant. Scott's *Seeing Like a State* matches the control-spiral claim almost exactly. Hirschman provides the mechanism-level analysis. Multiple case studies (commons governance, indigenous governance, digital commons) are available. Success here validates the corroboration process and provides a template for the harder targets. The dual benefit to relational-topology (shared institutional context) and governance-under-volatility (legitimacy as resilience) makes it the highest-leverage first move.

### 4.3 holding-complexity-beyond-control

**Definition checkpoint:**
Complexity cannot be adequately handled by control, prediction, or optimisation alone. Distributed control's representational capacity is exceeded by the complexity now becoming manifest. The system faces a double mismatch: the complexity exceeds the coordination model AND the energetic surplus that subsidised control is tightening. Two default attractors — authoritarian compression (narrowing degrees of freedom for short-term stability) and chaotic decomposition (loss of higher-order coordination) — both reduce complexity rather than holding it. The third path requires deliberate construction of five structural capacities: adaptive coordination, complexity-handling capacity, scaffolded relationality, interdependent sovereignty, and anti-fragility through learning. The family's distinctive claim is that the third path does not emerge automatically — it must be built under conditions that actively constrain its construction.

**Likely corroboration lanes:**

1. **Cybernetics: requisite variety (Ashby, Beer).** W. Ross Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety — a regulator must have at least as much variety as the system it regulates. Stafford Beer's *Brain of the Firm* (1972) and the Viable System Model — how organisations must match external complexity with internal variety. This provides the formal basis for the "representational inadequacy" claim: control fails when the controller's model has less variety than the system.

2. **Cynefin framework (Snowden).** Dave Snowden's Cynefin framework explicitly distinguishes complex from complicated domains and argues that probe-sense-respond (not predict-plan-control) is the appropriate strategy in the complex domain. His concept of "safe-to-fail probes" matches the five-capacities framing. Snowden's work with government and military organisations provides operational precedent.

3. **Societal collapse and simplification (Tainter).** Joseph Tainter's *The Collapse of Complex Societies* (1988) — societies collapse when the marginal returns on increasing complexity become negative and the system simplifies. This is empirical evidence for the "chaotic decomposition" attractor: when complexity-holding costs exceed benefits, systems shed higher-order coordination. Tainter's cases (Roman Empire, Maya, Chaco Canyon) demonstrate the mechanism historically.

4. **Adaptive governance and resilience (Folke, Gunderson, Holling).** Carl Folke et al.'s "Adaptive Governance of Social-Ecological Systems" (2005). C.S. Holling's "panarchy" — adaptive cycles of growth, conservation, release, and reorganisation. Lance Gunderson and C.S. Holling's *Panarchy* (2002). These describe the institutional response to complexity in social-ecological systems — adaptive governance as the alternative to control-based management.

5. **Complexity science and institutional design (Kauffman, Page, Miller & Page).** Stuart Kauffman's *At Home in the Universe* (1995) — self-organisation and the "edge of chaos." Scott Page's *The Difference* (2007) — how diversity of perspectives produces better problem-solving in complex systems. John Miller and Scott Page's *Complex Adaptive Systems* (2007). These provide the theoretical base but may not name the institutional design implications.

6. **The energetics of coordination (Georgescu-Roegen, Odum).** Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen's *The Entropy Law and the Economic Process* (1971). Howard Odum's energy-systems analysis. These address the thermodynamic claim: coordination has material energy costs, and tightening energy surplus constrains coordination capacity. This is the most distinctive and hardest-to-corroborate element of the family.

**Likely false friends:**

- **Agile methodology / SAFe / management frameworks.** Agile, SAFe, and related frameworks address complexity through iterative development and feedback loops. But they are specific *management techniques* within organisations, not descriptions of the *civilisational structural condition* the family names. Using agile as evidence for holding-complexity would be a level error.
- **Popular "complexity" / VUCA.** The military's VUCA framework (Volatile, Uncertain, Complex, Ambiguous) acknowledges that conditions are complex but typically responds with enhanced command-and-control adaptations, not with the fundamental rejection of control logic. VUCA-aware management is often control-with-more-sensors, not holding-complexity-beyond-control.
- **Resilience engineering (Hollnagel, Woods).** Resilience engineering addresses how bounded operational systems (aviation, healthcare, nuclear) maintain function under stress. The scope is narrower than the Johar claim: operational resilience within a system vs civilisational capacity to hold complexity across systems.

**Best search queries:**
1. Ashby "requisite variety" governance institutional complexity representational
2. Beer "viable system model" organizational complexity control failure
3. Snowden Cynefin "complex" domain "control" failure institutional
4. Tainter "collapse" simplification institutional complexity
5. Holling panarchy "adaptive cycle" institutional governance
6. Folke "adaptive governance" complexity "institutional capacity" building
7. "authoritarian compression" OR "authoritarian simplification" complexity response
8. "institutional capacity" complexity "deliberate construction" governance
9. Georgescu-Roegen entropy coordination "energy" institutional cost
10. "complexity-holding" OR "holding complexity" institutional governance design

**Best evidence type first:** Mixed — conceptual parallel (Ashby/Beer for the diagnostic; Snowden for the institutional response) plus empirical case (Tainter for the default attractors). The claim is layered enough that no single evidence type suffices.

**Why this target should not go first despite being most corpus-central:** The core diagnosis (control fails under complexity) is widely shared, but the *specific framing* (the fork, two attractors as structural defaults, five capacities, deliberate construction under constraining conditions) is distinctively Johar. Corroboration will require assembling evidence from multiple traditions (cybernetics + collapse theory + adaptive governance), not finding a single direct match. This makes it a harder first-run target. It should come after legitimacy (which validates the institutional-design corroboration approach) and linguistic-closure (which validates the conceptual-parallel approach).

### 4.4 human-economy-and-commitment

**Definition checkpoint:**
Economic participation under discovery and complexity cannot be governed by wage or transaction grammar designed for predictable throughput. The wage contract fails because its four functions (security floor, legible obligation, risk placement, fairness story) depend on work being specifiable in advance. The replacement unit is the commitment signature: bounded responsibility under uncertainty with declared verification modes. Four types: delivery (produce capability), discovery (reduce uncertainty), stewardship (maintain coherence), risk (preserve resilience). Standing provides the security floor — institutional membership that enables participation without requiring specifiable work. The family's distinctive claim is that the unit of economic coordination must shift from time-for-money or payment-for-receipt to obligation-under-uncertainty, and that standing (not UBI or basic income) provides the participation infrastructure.

**Likely corroboration lanes:**

1. **Incomplete contracts theory (Hart, Moore, Grossman).** Oliver Hart and Bengt Holmström (2016 Nobel Prize). The central insight: when contracts are incomplete (which is the norm for complex, discovery-oriented work), the allocation of residual control rights — not the contract terms — determines outcomes. This is the formal economics framework closest to "commitment under uncertainty": when work cannot be specified in advance, the governance structure (who can decide what under which conditions) matters more than the contract. Connects to commitment signatures as structured residual-rights allocation.

2. **Steward ownership (Purpose Foundation, Waschkau, Thomsen).** The Purpose Foundation (Berlin) has developed legal structures where companies are held in "steward ownership" — voting rights stay with active stewards (not investors), and profits are reinvested or shared (not extracted). Armin Steuernagel and colleagues. This is the closest production case to commitment-based economic governance: participation structured through stewardship commitment rather than ownership or employment.

3. **Cooperative governance and ICA principles.** The International Cooperative Alliance's 7 principles (voluntary membership, democratic member control, member economic participation, autonomy, education, cooperation among cooperatives, concern for community). Mondragon Corporation (65,000 worker-owners). Platform cooperatives (Scholz, Schneider). These are production cases where economic participation is structured through membership commitment rather than wage contracts.

4. **Social and solidarity economy (SSE).** The UN Inter-Agency Task Force on Social and Solidarity Economy. SSE organisations (cooperatives, mutual benefit societies, self-help groups, community enterprises) operate on principles of solidarity, participation, and shared benefit. RIPESS (Intercontinental Network for the Promotion of Social Solidarity Economy). This provides the broadest institutional evidence base for non-wage economic coordination.

5. **Enspiral and the "livelihood pod" model.** Enspiral (New Zealand) is a network of social enterprises that has explicitly experimented with commitment-based participation, shared economic infrastructure, and adaptive contribution models. Their "Loomio" decision-making platform and "cobudget" resource allocation tool are operational precedents for commitment-based economic coordination.

**Likely false friends:**

- **Gig economy / freelance platforms (Uber, Upwork).** The gig economy breaks the wage contract but replaces it with task-based micro-contracts — moving toward *thinner*, not thicker, coordination. It addresses the same diagnosis (wage-specifiability failure) but proposes the opposite solution (radical decomposition rather than commitment-based recomposition).
- **Stakeholder capitalism / ESG.** Stakeholder theory (Freeman) argues firms should serve multiple stakeholders. But it operates within the firm-as-entity frame and typically addresses incentive alignment within existing corporate governance, not structural replacement of the wage/transaction grammar.
- **Universal Basic Income (UBI).** UBI provides a security floor (analogous to standing) but without the commitment structure. UBI addresses material sufficiency but does not require participation or contribution — it is unconditional. The Johar claim is specifically about standing *within* a commitment economy: institutional membership that enables participation, not income that replaces it.
- **Token economics / DAOs.** Decentralised autonomous organisations use token-based coordination, which superficially resembles commitment-based participation. But most DAOs operate on token-weighted voting and financial incentive alignment — closer to shareholder governance than to commitment-based coordination. The governance structure of most DAOs does not distinguish commitment types (delivery, discovery, stewardship, risk).

**Best search queries:**
1. Hart Moore "incomplete contracts" "residual control rights" governance
2. "steward ownership" Purpose Foundation "commitment" governance participation
3. Mondragon cooperative "commitment-based" participation economic governance
4. "platform cooperativism" Scholz ownership participation governance
5. Enspiral "livelihood" commitment contribution economic model
6. "social solidarity economy" commitment participation governance structure
7. "commitment economy" OR "obligation under uncertainty" economic coordination
8. "standing" institutional membership participation security NOT UBI
9. Williamson "transaction cost" "governance structure" incomplete uncertainty
10. cooperative governance "bounded responsibility" discovery stewardship

**Best evidence type first:** Operational precedent (Purpose Foundation steward ownership and Enspiral's commitment-based economic model are the most direct production cases).

**Why this target should not go first:** The production cases exist but may not use the same vocabulary — translating between Johar's commitment-signature framework and actual cooperative/steward-ownership practice requires interpretive work. The incomplete-contracts literature is established but points to different solutions (hierarchy, not distributed commitment). This is a medium-difficulty target best attempted after the first two corroboration runs have established the process.

### 4.5 relational-topology-and-context-design

**Definition checkpoint:**
Contexts, interfaces, and organisational forms are not neutral containers but active shapers of relation, identity, perception, and what kinds of action become possible. Relational topology — the patterning of connection, distance, and encounter produced by co-location, protocol design, time structures, communication systems, and data architectures — is the real infrastructure of institutions. It generates "imaginary identity" (the emergent sense of "we" that is always partial and positionally mediated). When relational topologies are misaligned (relational, informational, epistemic, temporal dimensions pulling in different directions), fault lines appear from infrastructure, not personality. Trust and alignment have a half-life of roughly 6-9 months in distributed adaptive teams. The family's distinctive claim is that relational topology is a *design variable* that institutions generate (whether deliberately or not), and that context design (shaping the conditions under which coordination happens) is paradigmatically different from process design (managing the steps within those conditions).

**Likely corroboration lanes:**

1. **Sociomateriality (Orlikowski, Leonardi, Barad).** Wanda Orlikowski's work on how organisational practices are constituted through the entanglement of social and material agencies. Paul Leonardi's "materiality and organizing." Karen Barad's "agential realism" (further upstream). These describe how infrastructure shapes what agents can perceive and do — the theoretical base for context-as-active-shaper.

2. **Situated action and plans (Suchman).** Lucy Suchman's *Plans and Situated Actions* (1987, 2007 revised) — how the context of action shapes what actions are possible and intelligible. Suchman demonstrates that plans are not controlling representations but resources for situated action — the context determines what the plan can mean. This directly supports the context-over-process claim.

3. **Social network analysis: structural holes and weak ties (Burt, Granovetter).** Ronald Burt's *Structural Holes* (1992) — how network position shapes access to information and brokerage opportunity. Mark Granovetter's "The Strength of Weak Ties" (1973) — how the topology of social connections shapes information flow. These provide empirical evidence for how relational topology distributes power and access, though they don't use the "imaginary identity" vocabulary.

4. **Distributed team research (Google Project Aristotle, remote-work studies).** Google's Project Aristotle finding that psychological safety (not individual talent) was the key predictor of team performance — a context variable, not a composition variable. The 2020-2025 explosion of remote-work research on how distributed arrangements shape trust, coordination, and identity. GitLab's public handbook as an attempt to design context for distributed coordination.

5. **Organisational identity and spatial theory (Hatch, Schultz, Dale, Burrell).** Mary Jo Hatch and Majken Schultz's work on organisational identity as emergent from relationships between culture, image, and identity. Karen Dale and Gibson Burrell's *The Spaces of Organisation* (2008) — how physical and virtual spaces shape organisational power and identity. These address the "imaginary identity" claim: how the sense of "we" is produced by relational and spatial infrastructure.

6. **Dunbar's numbers and social brain hypothesis.** Robin Dunbar's research on the relationship between neocortex size and social group size — the ~150 person limit for maintaining stable social relationships. This provides empirical grounding for alignment decay: relational maintenance has cognitive costs that increase with group size and decrease with proximity.

**Likely false friends:**

- **Organisational design / org charts (Mintzberg classical).** Traditional organisational design (Mintzberg's five configurations) focuses on formal reporting structures and coordination mechanisms. The Johar claim is specifically about the *informal* relational topology generated by infrastructure, not about formal hierarchy. An org chart is not a relational topology — it's a legitimacy map that may or may not correspond to actual relational patterns.
- **Knowledge management (Nonaka, Takeuchi).** Knowledge management addresses how knowledge is created, shared, and stored in organisations. But it typically uses a creation/transfer framework rather than a topology-of-relations framework. The Johar claim is about what the topology *generates* (imaginary identity, perceptual capacity, power distribution), not about knowledge flows per se.
- **Social capital theory (Putnam, Bourdieu).** Social capital theory addresses the value of social connections as an individual or community resource. But it treats connections as an *asset* rather than as an *institutional design variable*. The Johar claim is about topology as something institutions *generate* through their infrastructure, whether they intend to or not.
- **Environmental psychology / office design research.** Research on how open-plan offices, hot-desking, etc. affect productivity and satisfaction. This addresses the spatial dimension but typically at the workspace level, not at the institutional topology level. The Johar claim is about relational topology at the institutional scale, not about office layout.

2. Suchman "situated action" context institutional design plans
3. Burt "structural holes" organizational topology power information
4. Granovetter "weak ties" topology organizational information flow
5. "psychological safety" Google "Project Aristotle" team design context
6. "alignment decay" OR "trust decay" distributed teams temporal half-life
7. "context design" OR "context engineering" organizational institutional
8. "imaginary identity" OR "organizational identity" relational density

10. Dale Burrell "spaces of organisation" power topology identity

**Best evidence type first:** Empirical case (distributed team research on alignment decay and Google's Project Aristotle provide the most directly checkable evidence).

**Why this target should come last in the initial sequence:** The individual claims (alignment decay, context as design variable) are testable and the traditions are rich. But the *integrative* claim — that relational topology is the *real* infrastructure and context design is *paradigmatically* different from process design — is harder to corroborate because it's a framing claim, not a mechanism claim. Corroborating the individual components (alignment decay, topology as power distribution, context as perception shaper) is tractable; corroborating their integration into a unified design discipline is harder. This target benefits from the prior runs having established which evidence types are most effective.

## 5. Cross-Target Dependencies

### linguistic-closure vs perceptual-fidelity vs civic-truth

**Shared terrain:** All three belong to Superfamily A (Epistemic Commons). STS sources (Latour's black boxing) serve linguistic-closure directly but could also serve perceptual-fidelity (how instruments shape what can be perceived — Latour on "inscription devices") and civic-truth (how scientific communities maintain revisability — Peirce's self-correcting inquiry). Pragmatist sources (Dewey, Peirce) serve both linguistic-closure (inquiry as self-correcting) and civic-truth (fallibilism as institutional commitment) simultaneously.

**Evidence that might transfer:** If the first corroboration run on legitimacy yields Ostrom cases, those cases may also contain evidence for civic-truth (commons governance as designed epistemic commons). If the linguistic-closure run yields Latour/STS evidence, the same sources may address perceptual-fidelity (how inscription devices shape what scientists can perceive) and civic-truth (how peer review resists closure).

**Evidence that must stay family-specific:** Linguistic closure is specifically about *language as stopping rule*. Evidence that institutions are epistemically limited (which is broad) does not corroborate that *naming specifically* produces *sufficiency that terminates inquiry*. Perceptual fidelity is about *sensing quality* — evidence that must be about contact with pre-abstraction reality, not about language. Civic truth is about *designed commons conditions* — evidence must address institutional design for plural truth, not just the failure of singular truth. Evidence that blurs these boundaries collapses the families rather than corroborating them.

### legitimacy-and-shared-consequence vs relational-topology-and-context-design

**Shared terrain:** Both are in Superfamily F (Institutional Topology and Legitimacy). The spatial-politics source in the legitimacy synthesis explicitly names relational topology as the mechanism through which political asymmetry is produced. Ostrom's commons governance research addresses both legitimacy (polycentric governance) and topology (how commons governance structures create relational fields for monitoring and enforcement).

**Evidence that might transfer:** Ostrom evidence for legitimacy (polycentric governance, consequence-binding design principles) may simultaneously provide evidence for relational topology (how commons governance generates specific relational patterns). Distributed-team research for relational topology (alignment decay) may provide evidence for legitimacy (how relational asymmetry produces legitimacy gaps in distributed organisations).

**Evidence that must stay family-specific:** Legitimacy is about *authority-consequence binding* — a political question about who has standing and who bears outcomes. Relational topology is about *the patterning of connection* — an infrastructure question about how agents relate. Evidence that organisations fail (which is common) does not corroborate either unless it identifies either (a) authority-consequence separation as the mechanism or (b) topological misalignment as the cause. The political and the infrastructural are complementary design dimensions, not the same claim.

### holding-complexity-beyond-control vs governance-under-volatility vs post-permanence-and-adaptive-infrastructure

**Shared terrain:** All three are in Superfamily B (Complexity Conditions). The governing-volatility bridge note is a seed source for both holding-complexity and post-permanence. Ashby's requisite variety serves both holding-complexity (why control fails) and governance-under-volatility (why deterministic governance breaks). Tainter serves both holding-complexity (chaotic decomposition attractor) and post-permanence (monumental infrastructure as failed permanence assumption).

**Evidence that might transfer:** Tainter's collapse cases provide evidence for holding-complexity (the decomposition attractor) and for post-permanence (monumental infrastructure that failed). Snowden/Cynefin provides evidence for holding-complexity (the complex domain) and for governance-under-volatility (why predict-and-plan governance fails). Adaptive governance literature (Folke, Holling) serves all three simultaneously.

**Evidence that must stay family-specific:** Holding-complexity's *distinctive* claim is the *fork structure*: two default attractors and a third path that requires deliberate construction. Evidence that "complexity is hard" or that "governance should be adaptive" does not corroborate this specific claim. Governance-under-volatility's distinctive claim is that *coherence* (not control or recovery) is the success measure. Post-permanence's distinctive claim is that *optionality* replaces *durability* as the infrastructure measure. These are three different claims about three different design domains (civilisation, governance, infrastructure) even though they share a diagnosis.

### human-economy-and-commitment vs agility-contribution-and-organizing

**Shared terrain:** Both are in Superfamily D (Commitment Architecture). Both argue for contribution-based participation over role-based fixity. Both connect to Spore's commitment protocols. Cooperative governance evidence serves human-economy (commitment-based economic participation) and could serve agility (contribution-based organising in cooperative settings).

**Evidence that might transfer:** Purpose Foundation / Enspiral evidence for human-economy (commitment-based economic governance) may also demonstrate agility principles (adaptive contribution, alignment maintenance in non-hierarchical structures). Platform cooperative evidence (Scholz) serves both — digital cooperatives face both the economic coordination and the organising infrastructure challenge.

**Evidence that must stay family-specific:** Human-economy's distinctive claim is about *the unit of economic coordination* (commitment signature, not wage or transaction) and *standing* (security floor for participation). Agility's distinctive claim is about *organising infrastructure* (alignment decay, context metabolism, the paradox of lightweight). Evidence that cooperatives exist does not corroborate either unless it identifies (a) commitment as the coordination unit (not just shared ownership) or (b) deliberate alignment-maintenance infrastructure (not just informal culture). The economic grammar and the organising infrastructure are complementary but different design problems.

2. **Best-documented case evidence.** Ostrom's empirical commons cases (irrigation systems in Spain, fishing communities in Japan, groundwater basins in California) are extensively documented and analysed. 4. **Highest dual benefit.** Corroborating legitimacy simultaneously generates evidence relevant to relational-topology (Ostrom's commons governance creates specific relational patterns), governance-under-volatility (legitimacy as resilience infrastructure), and holding-complexity (orchestration as complexity-holding mode). No other target has this much spillover value.

- **linguistic-closure:** Rich traditions but the exact claim (structural, not cognitive) requires careful false-friend filtering. Better second, after the process is proven.
- **holding-complexity:** Most corpus-central but most distinctively Johar. Requires assembling multi-tradition evidence. Better third.

### Best first evidence lane

**Ostrom / polycentric governance.** Start with Ostrom's 8 design principles and test each against the legitimacy synthesis's three convergence layers: (1) do the principles address plural, co-held legitimacy? (2) do they treat shared consequence as an infrastructural condition? (3) do they describe orchestration over dominance?

### Best backup lane

**Scott / state legibility failure.** If the Ostrom lane yields too-broad results (general commons governance rather than specific authority-consequence binding), Scott's detailed case studies of high-modernist failure provide the negative evidence: what happens when authority and consequence separate.

### Exact success condition

Find 2 to 4 non-Johar primary sources that independently support the core claim that institutions become viable when legitimacy and consequence are held together and fail when they separate — specifically addressing:
- polycentric legitimacy (no single axis suffices) — not just "legitimacy matters"
- shared consequence as an infrastructural condition — not just "accountability is important"
- the control-spiral mechanism (how centralising authority amplifies the problems it tries to solve) — not just "centralisation is bad"

PLUS 1 empirical or operational case where institutional failure demonstrably followed from authority-consequence separation (or success followed from authority-consequence binding) in a way that matches the synthesis's structural analysis.

The corroboration succeeds if the external evidence supports the *specific structural mechanism* the family names, not merely the general topic of legitimacy or the general importance of accountability.

## 8. Guardrails

1. **Resonance is not corroboration.** Finding an author who writes about similar topics or shares Johar's general concerns is not evidence that the structural mechanism the family names has been independently identified. The mechanism must match, not just the topic.

2. **Broad relevance is not enough if the claim loses specificity.** "Complexity is challenging for governance" is broadly relevant to holding-complexity but does not corroborate the specific fork structure, the two default attractors, or the five required capacities. Corroboration must match the *specificity* of the family's claim, not just its general direction.

3. **Operational precedent without conceptual alignment is not enough.** A cooperative that works well is not automatically evidence for commitment-based economic coordination unless its governance structure specifically addresses the same problems (wage-specifiability failure, standing, commitment types) the family names. Existence is not corroboration; structural match is.

4. **Conceptually elegant but thin deferred families should not jump the queue.** The 8 deferred families (especially the 2 concept-only families left dormant) should not be revived just because the corroboration work generates enthusiasm. The triage decisions from the corpus-level work plan stand unless new evidence specifically warrants revision.

5. **Corroboration work should preserve family boundaries rather than flatten them.** When a single external source is relevant to multiple families (e.g., Ostrom serves both legitimacy and relational-topology), the evidence must be parsed: which part corroborates which family. Using a single source to "corroborate" three families at once collapses the distinctions the synthesis notes worked to establish.

6. **The goal is external validation, not external authority.** Finding a prestigious author who agrees does not automatically strengthen the claim. The quality of the match matters more than the prestige of the source. A detailed commons governance case study from a regional journal may be stronger corroboration than a passing mention in a famous book.

7. **Negative results are informative.** If a corroboration search fails — if the external traditions do not support the family's specific claim — that is important information, not a failure of the search. It may indicate that the family's framing is more distinctively Johar than expected, which affects promotion decisions.
