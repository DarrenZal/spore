# Relational Topology and Context Design Corroboration Pass 1

## 1. Scope and Claim Check

**Family under test:** relational-topology-and-context-design

**The exact claim being tested (from the synthesis note):**

Contexts, interfaces, and organisational forms are not neutral containers but active shapers of relation, identity, perception, and what kinds of action become possible. Relational topology — the patterning of connection, distance, and encounter — is the real infrastructure of institutions. It generates the imaginary identity of the organisation, determines who can perceive what, distributes power through what it makes easy or difficult, and shapes whether agents can coordinate coherently or fracture into asymmetric enclaves. Context design is therefore infrastructural: the design of conditions through which relational topology produces coherence, not merely rules or roles. Misaligned relational topologies produce fracture; alignment decays structurally over time without deliberate maintenance.

**Three layers tested:**

1. **Generative mechanism:** context, material arrangement, protocols, and network topology actively shape what relations, identities, and perceptions can form — they generate rather than merely contain
2. **Design shift:** context design is a distinct institutional design variable, paradigmatically different from process design or formal structure
3. **Maintenance / failure mode:** misalignment, decay, or asymmetry in topology produces coordination fracture — and this is structural, not cultural or personality-driven

**Adjacent families this must stay distinct from:**

- **legitimacy-and-shared-consequence:** Legitimacy is about *authority-consequence binding* — a political question about who has standing and who bears outcomes. Relational topology is about *the patterning of connection* — an infrastructure question about how agents relate. Evidence about polycentric governance or consequence separation is legitimacy, not topology. Evidence about how spatial or network arrangements distribute access and generate asymmetry is topology, not legitimacy.
- **agility-contribution-and-organizing:** Agility is about *coordination capacity* — how infrastructure provisions optionality and how alignment decays over time. Relational topology is about *the generative quality* of arrangement — how contexts produce relation, identity, and power. The alignment-decay claim (6-9 month half-life) sits at their boundary: it is a maintenance property of relational topology that has direct consequences for agility. Evidence about decay rates stays in the topology family; evidence about how to maintain adaptive coordination capacity stays in agility.
- **perceptual-fidelity:** Perceptual fidelity is about *the quality of sensing contact* with reality. Relational topology can shape what agents perceive, but evidence about sensing quality, data infrastructure, or encounter depth is perceptual-fidelity. Evidence about how arrangement patterns determine who can see what and who relates to whom is topology.

**Success condition for this run:**

Find 2-4 non-Johar primary or primary-adjacent sources independently supporting:
- that organisational contexts, material arrangements, protocols, or network topologies actively generate relation, perception, identity, or power rather than merely containing them
- and that designing those conditions is a real institutional design problem, not just culture talk or office-layout trivia

Plus 1 strong operational, empirical, or institutional case where context/topology design or misdesign materially shaped coordination, trust, identity, or power distribution.

## 2. Search Strategy

### Generative-mechanism lanes

**Lane 1: Situated action (Suchman)**

Why relevant: Suchman's central thesis is that action is not the execution of a pre-formed plan but emerges from moment-by-moment interaction with the concrete situation. The material/social context generates what actions are possible and intelligible — it is the primary determinant, not a backdrop.

Expected evidence type: Conceptual parallel + empirical case (photocopier interface study).

What would count as a false friend: Generic claims that "context matters" or "technology influences work" without the specific generative mechanism (context determines, not merely influences).

**Lane 2: Sociomateriality (Orlikowski)**

Why relevant: Orlikowski argues that the social and the material are constitutively entangled — organisational practices are always already sociomaterial. Material arrangements do not merely enable or constrain but actively shape what practices are possible.

Expected evidence type: Conceptual parallel at the organisational level — the theoretical framework for why material infrastructure IS relational infrastructure.

What would count as a false friend: Technology adoption studies that treat technology as a tool users choose, rather than as infrastructure that shapes what users can do.

**Lane 3: Structural holes and weak ties (Burt, Granovetter)**

Why relevant: Burt demonstrates that network position generates power, information access, and opportunity — the same individual in different positions produces different outcomes. Granovetter demonstrates that the patterning (not volume) of connections determines information flow.

Expected evidence type: Empirical evidence for topology generating outcomes, controlling for individual attributes.

What would count as a false friend: Social capital theory (Putnam, Coleman) that treats ties as an asset rather than as an institutional design variable.

### Design-shift lanes

**Lane 4: Spaces of organisation (Dale, Burrell)**

Why relevant: Dale and Burrell argue that spatial arrangements actively produce power, identity, and hierarchy. "The organisation of space IS the organisation of power." Space design is power design.

Expected evidence type: Conceptual parallel + historical case studies (panopticon, open-plan offices, factory floors).

What would count as a false friend: Environmental psychology / office design research that is too local or surface-level.

### Maintenance / failure-mode lanes

**Lane 5: Psychological safety and team dynamics (Edmondson, Project Aristotle)**

Why relevant: Google's Project Aristotle found that context variables (psychological safety, norms, interaction patterns), not composition variables (talent, personality), predict team effectiveness. Edmondson's foundational research establishes psychological safety as a group-level emergent property that can be designed through protocols and structures.

Expected evidence type: Empirical evidence (180+ teams, 250+ attributes) for context > composition.

What would count as a false friend: Team composition research that attributes performance to selecting the right people, without the context mechanism.

**Lane 6: Distributed team research, Allen Curve, Dunbar**

Why relevant: Allen Curve shows communication drops exponentially with distance. Distributed-team research shows how arrangement changes produce information asymmetry, siloing, and faultline formation. Dunbar shows cognitive limits on relational maintenance that impose structural constraints on topology.

Expected evidence type: Empirical evidence for topology-generated asymmetry, decay, and fracture.

What would count as a false friend: Generic "remote work is hard" studies without the topology mechanism.

### False-friend / contrast lanes

**Classical org design / Mintzberg:** Formal structure, not generated topology. An org chart is a legitimacy map that may not correspond to actual relational patterns.

**Knowledge management (Nonaka, Takeuchi):** Knowledge flow, but often not topology as design variable. The family claim is about what topology generates, not about information transfer.

**Social capital (Putnam, Bourdieu):** Treats ties as an asset rather than as an institutional design variable. The family claim is about topology as something institutions generate through their infrastructure.

**Generic network science:** Structure description without institutional consequence or design implication.

## 4. Best-Fit Corroborating Sources

### Source 1: Lucy Suchman — *Plans and Situated Actions* (1987/2007)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** Action is not the execution of a pre-formed plan but emerges from moment-by-moment interaction with the concrete situation. The material and social situation — interface design, available resources, legibility of the environment — actively generates what actions are possible and intelligible, not merely constrains them. Designing situational resources is therefore a fundamentally different problem from designing plans/processes. "The problem is less one of specifying increasingly detailed plans, than of providing the resources for situated action."

**What it supports:**
- Context/topology as active shaper: very strong — "the coherence of situated action is an emergent property of moment-by-moment interactions between actors, and between actors and the environments of their action"
- Relation/power/identity generation: strong — the photocopier study demonstrates how interface design generates specific asymmetries of access and intelligibility ("not incidental but constitutive of the interaction")
- Design-of-conditions rather than formal structure: very strong — the explicit argument that designing situational resources ≠ designing sequences/plans; the paradigmatic design shift
- Asymmetry from topology: strong — the interactional asymmetry between user and machine is built into the material arrangement, not a user error
- Operational design implications: moderate — Suchman frames primarily as critique of AI planning models; the positive design programme is implicit

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Suchman provides the clearest articulation of the *design shift* — that designing context is paradigmatically different from designing process. No other source states this as directly. The photocopier study is the most precise empirical demonstration of how material arrangement generates specific relational patterns. The intellectual route (ethnomethodology, anthropology, Xerox PARC) is completely independent of organisational theory, network science, and Johar.

### Source 2: Ronald Burt — "Structural Holes and Good Ideas" (2004) + *Brokerage and Closure* (2005)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** Network position generates power, information access, and idea quality — the same individual in different positions produces different outcomes. Managers who bridge structural holes submit ideas rated higher in blind evaluation, controlling for education, rank, and experience. "This is not creativity born of genius; it is creativity as an import-export business." Topology distributes advantage: formally equal agents get unequal outcomes from position.

**What it supports:**
- Context/topology as active shaper: very strong — the strongest available evidence that topology generates rather than merely correlates with outcomes; controls for individual attributes
- Relation/power/identity generation: very strong — structural holes generate information asymmetry, brokerage power, and entrepreneurial opportunity
- Design-of-conditions rather than formal structure: moderate (1992) to strong (2005) — the 2005 book discusses how organisations can design network topology through team composition, rotation, cross-functional structures
- Asymmetry from topology: very strong — the central finding; formally equal agents have unequal outcomes from position alone
- Operational design implications: moderate — Burt frames topology as individually exploitable strategy (1992) and moves toward organisational design (2005)

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Burt provides the tightest empirical evidence that topology *generates* outcomes. The blind evaluation methodology (2004 AJS) rules out alternative explanations (visibility, politics, self-promotion). Granovetter provides the foundational topological insight but not the controlled evidence. The intellectual route (structural sociology, competition theory) is completely independent of STS, situated action, and Johar.

### Source 3: Wanda Orlikowski — Sociomateriality (2007, 2010)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** The social and the material are constitutively entangled — organisational practices are always already sociomaterial. Material arrangements (technologies, spaces, tools) do not merely enable or constrain but actively participate in the generation of social practice. "This is not a claim that technology matters — a claim few would disagree with. Rather, it is a claim about the constitutive role of materiality in everyday organizing." The BlackBerry generates particular patterns of connection, availability, and temporal structure; Lotus Notes generates particular regimes of visibility and knowledge sharing.

**What it supports:**
- Context/topology as active shaper: very strong — the constitutive entanglement thesis is the strongest available ontological foundation for the claim that contexts are not neutral containers
- Relation/power/identity generation: strong — technologies generate "particular configurations of connectivity, visibility, accountability, and temporality" (2008 Annals paper)
- Design-of-conditions rather than formal structure: moderate — the design implication follows logically from constitutive entanglement but is not Orlikowski's primary focus
- Asymmetry from topology: moderate — visibility and temporality asymmetries are demonstrated in the technology cases but not foregrounded as asymmetry
- Operational design implications: moderate — primarily analytical/descriptive rather than prescriptive

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Orlikowski provides the organisational-level complement to Suchman's micro-level demonstration. Where Suchman shows that interface design generates interaction patterns, Orlikowski shows that technology infrastructure generates organisational practice patterns. The intellectual route (management science, STS, Barad's agential realism) is independent of network theory and situated action.

### Source 4: Amy Edmondson — Psychological Safety (1999) + Google Project Aristotle (2015)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** Team coordination quality is predicted by context variables (psychological safety, norms, interaction patterns), not composition variables (talent, personality, demographics). Google's Project Aristotle studied 180+ teams across 250+ attributes and found that "who is on a team matters less than how the team members interact, structure their work, and view their contributions." Psychological safety is a group-level emergent property — "a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking" — not an individual personality trait. It can be designed through protocol intervention: teams that adopted new group norms improved measurably.

**What it supports:**
- Context/topology as active shaper: very strong — the most direct empirical evidence that context (not composition) determines coordination outcomes
- Relation/power/identity generation: strong — psychological safety generates learning behaviour, risk-taking, and voice; its absence generates silence, risk-aversion, and blame
- Design-of-conditions rather than formal structure: very strong — Edmondson demonstrates that context can be designed through leadership behaviour, team structures, and interaction norms; Google shows measurable improvement from norm intervention
- Asymmetry from topology: not directly addressed — Aristotle is primarily about what makes teams work, not about how topology generates asymmetry
- Operational design implications: very strong — both Edmondson and Google provide actionable design frameworks

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Edmondson/Aristotle provides the broadest empirical base and the most direct design demonstration. The finding that the same individuals perform differently in different contexts is the clearest available falsification of the composition thesis. The intellectual route (organisational behaviour, empirical team research at Google) is independent of STS, network theory, and Johar.

## 5. Operational or Institutional Case

**Case: Google Project Aristotle (2012-2015)**

**Dates:** Research conducted 2012-2015 at Google by People Operations (Julia Rozovsky and team). Published November 2015 via re:Work. Popularised by Charles Duhigg in the New York Times Magazine, February 2016.

**What happened:** Google's People Operations team set out to identify what makes teams effective. They analysed 250+ attributes across 180+ active Google teams over two years. The hypothesis was that the best teams would have the best people — the right combination of talent, personality, education, and demographics.

The finding contradicted this hypothesis entirely. Team composition variables (seniority, education, personality traits, demographics, individual skills) were not significant predictors of team effectiveness. Instead, five context variables predicted team dynamics, all of which are properties of the team's relational arrangement:

1. **Psychological safety** (by far the most important) — a shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking
2. **Dependability** — team members reliably complete quality work on time
3. **Structure and clarity** — clear goals, roles, and execution plans
4. **Meaning** — work is personally important to team members
5. **Impact** — team members feel their work matters

All five are relational-context properties — they describe the conditions under which people interact, not the attributes of the people interacting. Psychological safety in particular is a group-level emergent phenomenon: it arises from the pattern of interaction (conversational turn-taking, social sensitivity, norm-setting by leaders), not from individual personality.

**Whether positive design precedent, topology-misalignment failure, or mixed:** Primarily a positive design precedent. Google demonstrated that context can be deliberately designed: teams that adopted specific new norms (e.g., sharing a personal risk or vulnerability at the start of each meeting) showed measurable improvements — 6% improvement on psychological safety, 10% on structure and clarity. The re:Work tools developed from the research have been used by thousands of teams inside and outside Google.

**How context, protocol, interaction structure actually mattered:**

The critical mechanism is *conversational turn-taking*. Research (drawn from Woolley et al. 2010, "Evidence for a Collective Intelligence Factor") found that the single strongest predictor of group intelligence was equality in conversational turn-taking — all members speaking in roughly equal proportion. This is a structural property of the team's communication topology, not an individual trait. When one person dominates conversation, the group's collective intelligence declines regardless of how smart that person is.

Edmondson's foundational research (1999) identified the mechanism: psychological safety enables learning behaviour (speaking up about errors, asking questions, experimenting), which enables performance. The absence of psychological safety produces silence, error-hiding, and conformity — coordination failure that originates in the relational context, not in individual inadequacy.

The design lever is explicit: leaders who model vulnerability, invite participation, and respond productively to voice create psychological safety. Leaders who punish mistakes, dismiss input, and monopolise conversation destroy it. The "same people, different leadership behaviour" produces different team dynamics — demonstrating that context generates coordination quality.

**Why this case is evidentially stronger than alternatives:**

1. **Scale and rigour.** 180+ teams, 250+ attributes, two-year study at a major technology company. This is not a case study or a small experiment — it is a large-sample empirical investigation with controls.
2. **The composition hypothesis was explicitly tested and rejected.** The research team specifically looked for composition effects (talent, personality, demographics) and found none. This rules out the rival explanation that good teams are made of good people.
3. **The design implication was demonstrated, not just inferred.** Google showed that adopting new norms (a context design intervention) measurably changed team dynamics. Context is not only the predictor — it is the design lever.
4. **Independent replication.** Edmondson's psychological safety construct has been studied in hundreds of subsequent studies across industries, with consistent findings.

**Supporting evidence — Polzer et al. (2006) as failure-mode complement:**

For the failure-mode dimension, the strongest complement is Polzer, Crisp, Jarvenpaa & Kim (2006). In a study of 45 six-person teams distributed across universities, teams split 3-3 between two sites had the *highest* conflict and *lowest* trust. Teams split 1-1-1-1-1-1 (one member per site) had the *lowest* conflict and *highest* trust. The counterintuitive finding: the most "balanced" geographic split produced the worst outcomes because it created two co-located subgroups with aligned geographic and social identities, generating competing faultlines. This directly demonstrates topology-generated fracture — the arrangement, not the individuals, determined where coordination breakdown occurred.

**Whether it supports the family directly or only partially:** Partially direct, with an honest gap.

*What it demonstrates directly:* Context variables (not composition) predict coordination quality. Context can be designed through protocol intervention. The same individuals perform differently in different relational contexts.

*Honest gap:* Project Aristotle is about *teams*, not about *institutions*. The family claim asserts that relational topology is "the real infrastructure of institutions" — that the patterning of connection across an entire organisation or governance system generates its social reality. Project Aristotle demonstrates this at the team level but does not directly address the institutional scale. The scaling argument (what works for teams also works for institutions) is plausible but not demonstrated by this case.

## 6. False Friends and Rejections

### Rejected: Classical organisational design / Mintzberg

Mintzberg's five configurations (simple structure, machine bureaucracy, professional bureaucracy, divisionalized form, adhocracy) describe *formal* reporting structures and coordination mechanisms. The family claim is specifically about the *informal* relational topology generated by infrastructure — the actual patterns of connection, encounter, and asymmetry that may diverge sharply from the org chart. An org chart is a legitimacy map, not a relational topology. Evidence about formal structure design is adjacent but does not address the generative mechanism the family names. Mintzberg would tell you how to design the hierarchy; the family claim asks what relational patterns the hierarchy *generates* whether you intend them or not.

### Rejected: Knowledge management (Nonaka, Takeuchi)

Knowledge management literature addresses how knowledge is created, shared, and stored. But it typically uses a creation/transfer framework rather than a topology-of-relations framework. The family claim is about what topology *generates* — imaginary identity, perceptual capacity, power distribution — not about knowledge flows per se. Knowledge management asks "how does information move?"; the family claim asks "what social reality does the arrangement produce?" The mechanism is different.

### Rejected: Social capital theory (Putnam, Coleman, Bourdieu)

Social capital theory treats social connections as an *asset* that individuals or communities possess. Putnam's "bowling alone" thesis measures declining social capital as a community problem. But the family claim is about topology as an *institutional design variable* — something institutions generate through their infrastructure, whether they intend to or not. Social capital theory asks "how much connection do you have?"; the family claim asks "what does the pattern of connection produce, and can you design it?" The treatment of ties as individual/community assets rather than as institutional design outputs is the distinguishing difference.

### Rejected: Environmental psychology / office design research

Research on how open-plan offices, hot-desking, and biophilic design affect productivity and satisfaction. This addresses the spatial dimension at the workspace level but typically lacks the institutional-topology-as-infrastructure framing. The family claim operates at the institutional scale — how the patterning of connection across an entire organisation generates its social reality — not at the desk-arrangement scale. Some environmental psychology research (particularly Bernstein & Turban 2018 on open-plan offices reducing face-to-face interaction by 70%) touches the mechanism, but most of the literature is too local.

### Rejected: Generic remote-work productivity studies

The post-2020 explosion of remote-work studies frequently finds that remote work "affects" collaboration, trust, or productivity. But most of these studies are descriptive (measuring what happened) rather than mechanistic (explaining why arrangement shapes outcomes). The family claim requires the generative mechanism — not just "remote work reduced collaboration" but "the change in topology generated specific patterns of siloing, weak-tie erosion, and asymmetry." Yang et al. (2021/Microsoft) is the exception that clears this bar; most remote-work studies do not.

### Rejected: Generic network science

Mathematical network science describes topological properties (degree distribution, clustering coefficient, path length, centrality) without addressing institutional consequences or design implications. The family claim is about what topology *generates* in institutions — power, identity, coordination capacity — not about mathematical properties of graph structures. Network science provides the descriptive vocabulary; the family claim makes the institutional-design argument.

### Noted: Valve Corporation as illustrative negative case

Valve's formally flat structure (no managers, self-selection into projects) produced emergent hidden hierarchies — "popular kids who have acquired power." Former employees describe clique formation, information asymmetry, and opaque decision-making. This illustrates the family claim that topology is always generated, whether deliberately or not — the absence of designed topology produces worse topology, not no topology. However, the evidence is primarily journalistic and from former employees, limiting its evidentiary weight. Included in the candidate table's background but not featured as the primary case.

### Noted: Counter-evidence on trust decay

Stavrova (2023) found that remote work did not erode *organisational trust* over 4 months. This must be acknowledged. The best response: (a) 4 months is shorter than the claimed half-life period; (b) organisational trust is not the same as relational grounding, shared identity, or coordination quality; (c) the study was during an exceptional shared-crisis period that may have elevated organisational solidarity. The counter-evidence is real and the family claim should note that decay operates through specific pathways (loss of spontaneous communication, shared context, shared identity) that vary by design, not universally or automatically.

## 7. Preliminary Corroboration Assessment

**Outcome: success condition met**

### What is corroborated

**The generative mechanism — strong corroboration from four independent traditions:**

Four primary sources from completely independent intellectual traditions converge on the same structural insight: contexts, arrangements, and topologies *generate* rather than merely contain social reality.

- **Suchman (1987):** from ethnomethodology — the material/social situation generates action; plans are post-hoc resources, not controlling representations. Interface design produces specific asymmetries of access and intelligibility.
- **Orlikowski (2007):** from management science/STS — the social and material are constitutively entangled; technologies generate particular configurations of connectivity, visibility, accountability, and temporality.
- **Burt (2004):** from structural sociology — network position generates power, information access, and idea quality, controlling for individual attributes. The topology delivers the advantage; the person is the vehicle.
- **Edmondson (1999) / Aristotle (2015):** from organisational behaviour — context variables (psychological safety, norms, interaction patterns) predict team effectiveness; composition variables do not. The same individuals perform differently in different contexts.

The convergence is across ethnomethodology, STS, structural sociology, and organisational behaviour — four traditions that share no common intellectual lineage. None cites Johar. All arrive at the same structural account through different routes.

**Supporting evidence strengthens the picture:** Granovetter (patterning determines information access), Dale & Burrell (space design IS power design), Allen (communication drops exponentially with distance, technology doesn't override), Yang/Microsoft (changing the arrangement changes coordination patterns at scale), Polzer et al. (spatial arrangement generates faultlines independent of individual attributes), Dunbar (cognitive limits impose structural constraints on relational maintenance), Roberts & Dunbar (relationship decay is the structural default without active maintenance).

**The design-shift claim — moderate-to-strong corroboration:**

Three sources support the claim that context design is a distinct institutional design problem:

- **Suchman:** the most explicit — "the problem is less one of specifying increasingly detailed plans, than of providing the resources for situated action"
- **Edmondson/Aristotle:** demonstrated — adopting new group norms (a context intervention) measurably improved team dynamics
- **Dale & Burrell:** argued — "the organisation of space is simultaneously the organisation of power, knowledge and identity"

The design-shift claim is corroborated more as a demonstrated consequence (changing context changes outcomes) than as a named design discipline. No external source articulates "context design" as a standalone institutional design practice the way the family claim does. The sources provide the evidence base; the family claim provides the integration.

**The maintenance/failure-mode claim — strong corroboration:**

- **Polzer et al. (2006):** spatial arrangement generates faultlines — the 3-3 split creates competing subgroup identities that produce coordination breakdown
- **Yang/Microsoft (2021):** remote work caused networks to silo, cross-group collaboration to drop 25%, and weak ties to erode — all holding the people constant
- **Allen Curve (1977/2007):** communication drops exponentially with distance; technology does not override
- **Hinds & Mortensen (2005):** distributed teams report more conflict; moderated by shared identity, shared context, and spontaneous communication — all relational-context properties
- **Roberts & Dunbar (2015):** relationships decay without active maintenance; context change (loss of proximity, shared routines) produces measurable decline

### What remains uncorroborated

1. **"Imaginary identity" as a named concept.** The phenomenon is demonstrated — Polzer et al. shows that spatial arrangement generates competing subgroup identities; Hatch & Schultz show that organisational identity emerges from relational processes. But the specific term "imaginary identity" and the claim that it is "always partial, positional, and structurally mediated" have no external parallel. This is Johar's distinctive vocabulary for a phenomenon the literature recognises but does not name this way.

2. **"Context metabolism."** No external source uses this concept or a functional equivalent. The idea that contexts have a "metabolism" — incentive fields, recognition loops, biases, and learning architectures that determine what inhabitants can perceive and how they evolve — is Johar-specific. The closest external analog is Edmondson's work on how team norms produce or prevent learning behaviour, but "metabolism" implies a more dynamic, systemic concept than any external source articulates.

3. **The integrative claim that relational topology is "the real infrastructure."** Each external source addresses one dimension: Suchman (situation generates action), Burt (topology generates advantage), Orlikowski (materiality constitutes practice), Edmondson (context determines coordination). The family claim integrates these into a single thesis — that relational topology is the overarching infrastructure of institutions, encompassing all these dimensions as facets of one generative mechanism. This integration is the family's distinctive contribution. The sources provide the components; the integration is Johar's.

4. **The 6-9 month alignment half-life.** Structural decay is well-supported (Roberts & Dunbar 2015, Jarvenpaa & Leidner 1999 on "fragile and temporal" swift trust, the distributed-team literature broadly). But no source provides a quantitative half-life estimate. The 6-9 month parameter appears to be Johar's specific claim and is not externally validated. Roberts & Dunbar show significant decay over 18 months but do not provide a half-life. This specific parameter should be treated as a design hypothesis awaiting empirical testing, not as an externally corroborated finding.

### Whether the family stayed distinct from adjacent concepts

**Yes.**

- **Legitimacy:** No evidence gathered here addresses authority-consequence binding or polycentric governance. The topology sources address how arrangements generate patterns of connection, asymmetry, and identity — not who has standing or who bears consequences.
- **Agility:** The alignment-decay finding sits at the boundary. Roberts & Dunbar's decay evidence and the distributed-team literature address topology-generated decay (this family) that has consequences for adaptive coordination capacity (agility family). The evidence is attributed to topology because the mechanism is topological (context change produces relational erosion), not about coordination infrastructure per se.
- **Perceptual fidelity:** Orlikowski's sociomateriality touches perceptual fidelity (material arrangements shape what agents can perceive). But the evidence gathered focuses on how arrangements shape *relational patterns* (who connects to whom, what interactions occur), not on the *quality of sensing contact* with pre-abstraction reality.

### Whether the evidence is conceptual, operational, empirical, or mixed

Mixed — conceptual parallel (Suchman, Orlikowski, Dale & Burrell), empirical with controlled designs (Burt 2004 blind evaluation, Polzer et al. 2006 experimental teams, Edmondson 1999 field study, Allen Curve), large-scale natural experiment (Yang/Microsoft 2021), longitudinal study (Roberts & Dunbar 2015), operational precedent (Google re:Work implementation, GitLab handbook-first), and cognitive-evolutionary evidence (Dunbar). This is the most empirically grounded corroboration pass of the four completed runs. The evidence base is stronger on the empirical/operational side than the prior runs (legitimacy, linguistic-closure, human-economy) which were predominantly conceptual-parallel.
