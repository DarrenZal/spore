# Legitimacy and Shared Consequence Corroboration Pass 1

## 1. Scope and Claim Check

**Family under test:** legitimacy-and-shared-consequence

**The exact claim being tested (from the synthesis note):**

Institutions become viable when legitimacy and consequence are held together — when actors are actually bound into the outcomes they help shape, and when legitimacy is a co-held architecture across plural dimensions, not merely procedural approval. When authority and consequence separate — when some actors hold decision power without bearing outcomes, or bear outcomes without holding decision power — institutions fracture into control spirals, relational asymmetry, and epistemic collapse. Orchestration over dominance is the coordination mode.

**Three sub-claims to test:**
1. Polycentric legitimacy — no single governance axis suffices; multiple overlapping legitimacy sources are structurally necessary
2. Shared consequence as an infrastructural condition — authority-consequence coupling is a structural design requirement, not a moral aspiration
3. The control spiral or functional equivalent — when authority separates from consequence, a self-reinforcing escalation produces institutional failure

**Adjacent families this must stay distinct from:**
- **relational-topology-and-context-design:** Topology is about the patterning of connection and distance; legitimacy is about authority-consequence binding. Evidence about how organisations generate relational fields is topology, not legitimacy.
- **governance-under-volatility:** Volatility is the governance condition; legitimacy is a structural requirement for governance viability. Evidence about coherence under turbulence is governance-under-volatility, not legitimacy.
- **civic-truth:** Civic truth is the epistemic commons condition; legitimacy is the political-institutional condition. Evidence about holding plural truth is civic-truth, not legitimacy.

**Success condition:** Find 2-4 non-Johar primary sources that independently support the core claim, specifically addressing polycentric legitimacy, shared consequence as infrastructure, and the control-spiral mechanism — plus 1 empirical or historical case where institutional failure demonstrably followed from authority-consequence separation.

## 2. Search Strategy

### Positive corroboration lanes

**Lane 1: Ostrom / polycentric governance / commons governance**

Why relevant: Elinor Ostrom's design principles for long-enduring commons institutions directly address authority-consequence coupling (Principle 3: those affected participate in rule-making; Principle 4: monitors accountable to appropriators). Vincent Ostrom's polycentric governance directly addresses plural legitimacy. Commons failure cases provide empirical evidence for authority-consequence separation.

Expected evidence type: Conceptual parallel (polycentricity as structural requirement) + empirical case (commons success/failure under different authority-consequence configurations) + production evidence (actually operating commons governance).

False friends: Generic commons literature that discusses "participation" or "community governance" without identifying the specific authority-consequence coupling mechanism.

**Lane 2: Beetham / Scharpf / legitimacy theory**

Why relevant: Beetham's three-dimensional legitimacy (legal validity + normative justifiability + expressed consent) directly addresses the "no single axis suffices" claim. Scharpf's input/output legitimacy distinction directly addresses the separation-produces-failure mechanism through the EU democratic deficit case.

Expected evidence type: Conceptual parallel (multi-dimensional legitimacy as structural requirement) + empirical case (EU as authority-consequence separation case).

False friends: Weber's three types of legitimacy (traditional, charismatic, rational-legal) — a typology, not a coupling argument. Generic democratic theory that says "participation matters" without identifying the structural consequence-binding mechanism.

### Failure-mechanism / contrast lanes

**Lane 3: Scott / legibility / high modernism**

Why relevant: Scott's four conditions for catastrophic state failure include the structural insulation of planners from outcomes — authority separated from consequence. The documented cases (Soviet collectivisation, Tanzanian ujamaa, Brasilia, German scientific forestry) show the escalation pattern that maps to the control spiral.

Expected evidence type: Empirical/historical case (detailed failure analysis) + conceptual parallel (the formal-informal parasitism claim).

False friends: General critiques of state planning that attribute failure to ideology or incompetence rather than to the structural mechanism of authority-consequence separation.

**Lane 4: Hirschman / exit-voice-loyalty**

Why relevant: Hirschman identifies the precise pathological dynamic by which consequence-bearing actors lose voice: quality-conscious members exit, removing the principal agents of accountability, producing a death spiral where authority faces no corrective feedback.

Expected evidence type: Conceptual parallel (mechanism-level analysis of how authority-consequence separation produces failure).

False friends: Applications of exit-voice to consumer choice or labour markets that address individual switching behaviour rather than institutional governance failure.

## 4. Best-Fit Corroborating Sources

### Source 1: Elinor Ostrom — Governing the Commons (1990) + configural validation (Baggio et al. 2016)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** Institutions that structurally couple authority to consequence-bearing (those affected participate in rule-making; monitors are accountable to those affected; costs and benefits are proportional) are substantially more viable than institutions where authority and consequence are separated. The coupling is a system property — removing any principle greatly increases failure probability (Baggio: successful commons averaged 8.7/10 principles; failed commons 4.3/10).

**What it supports:**
- Polycentric legitimacy: yes (Principle 8: nested enterprises; multiple overlapping governance scales)
- Consequence-bearing as infrastructure: yes (Principles 2/3/4/5 treat coupling as structural design, not moral aspiration; Baggio's configural analysis shows it is measurable and predictive)
- Failure from separation: yes (Nepal irrigation: farmer-managed 70% high performance vs government-managed 40%; Sri Lanka: central authorities fail to obtain cooperation; Russia: Stolypin/Stalin destroy functioning Mir)
- Control spiral: partial (Scott's cases of external authority overriding local governance show escalation, but Ostrom documents the failure more than the spiral)

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Ostrom provides the most complete match: conceptual framework (the design principles), empirical validation (69-case configural analysis), experimental confirmation (DeCaro et al. 2024), and specific failure cases. No other source offers all four evidence types for the same claim.

### Source 2: Fritz Scharpf — Governing in Europe (1999)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** When the level of governance that has authority to decide is separated from the constituency that bears consequences, legitimacy fails structurally. Input legitimacy (right to decide) and output legitimacy (effectiveness for those affected) must both be present; neither alone suffices. The EU democratic deficit is a production case of authority-consequence separation: national governments have input legitimacy but eroded capacity; EU institutions have capacity but no democratic mandate from affected constituencies.

**What it supports:**
- Polycentric legitimacy: yes (input and output are two irreducible dimensions; neither alone suffices)
- Consequence-bearing as infrastructure: yes (output legitimacy requires that decisions actually serve the constituency affected — a structural condition, not a moral preference)
- Failure from separation: yes (the EU case is precisely a separation pathology: authority at one level, consequence-bearing at another)
- Control spiral: no (Scharpf does not describe an escalation mechanism)

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Scharpf provides the most precise mapping between the theoretical claim (both input and output legitimacy required) and a contemporary institutional case (the EU democratic deficit). Beetham provides the richer theoretical framework (three dimensions) but without the institutional case evidence that Scharpf's EU analysis offers.

### Source 3: Albert Hirschman — Exit, Voice, and Loyalty (1970)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** When consequence-bearing actors (quality-conscious members) lose voice by exiting the institution, authority-holders become structurally insulated from the outcomes they produce. This produces a death spiral: exit removes the principal agents of accountability, weakening voice, which accelerates decline, which drives further exit. The "lazy monopoly" is an institution that has achieved structural consequence-avoidance.

**What it supports:**
- Polycentric legitimacy: indirectly (voice, exit, and loyalty must be properly coupled — a multi-mechanism claim)
- Consequence-bearing as infrastructure: yes (Hirschman's entire framework turns on who bears consequences vs who has voice; the pathology is their separation)
- Failure from separation: very strong (the death spiral is the most precise mechanism-level description of how authority-consequence separation produces institutional failure of any source in this search)
- Control spiral: strong analogue (the death spiral is a functional equivalent: exit → loss of voice → decline → more exit → further insulation of authority from consequence)

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Hirschman provides the sharpest mechanism: the exact causal chain by which authority-consequence separation becomes self-reinforcing. Ostrom shows *that* coupling matters; Hirschman shows *how* decoupling escalates. Scott shows *what happens* when it fails; Hirschman shows *why* failure compounds.

### Source 4: James C. Scott — Seeing Like a State (1998)

**The specific claim it independently supports:** When planners are structurally insulated from the consequences of their plans — operating through administrative abstractions rather than local knowledge, backed by authoritarian power rather than local consent — the formal scheme becomes parasitic on informal processes it systematically destroys. The resulting failure is attributed to insufficient standardisation (not to the approach itself), producing an escalation pattern where the state simplifies further in response to the failure of prior simplification.

**What it supports:**
- Polycentric legitimacy: indirectly (Scott's argument implies that authority must be distributed close to consequence-bearing, but he frames this as mētis vs techne, not as polycentric legitimacy)
- Consequence-bearing as infrastructure: yes (the structural insulation of planners from outcomes is the enabling condition for all four of Scott's catastrophic failures)
- Failure from separation: very strong (detailed historical cases: German forestry, Soviet collectivisation, Tanzanian ujamaa, Brasilia)
- Control spiral: strong (the escalation pattern — simplify → fail → simplify further — is documented in all four major cases, though not named as a "control spiral")

**Why it is better than nearby alternatives:** Scott provides the richest historical case evidence. No other source in this search offers four detailed, well-documented cases of authority-consequence separation producing institutional failure across such different contexts (ecological, agricultural, urban, political).

## 5. Empirical or Historical Case

**Case: Nepal Irrigation Systems (Ostrom 1994; Lam 1996)**

**Dates:** Empirical comparison conducted in the early 1990s, drawing on data from 47 irrigation systems in Nepal.

**What happened:** Ostrom and colleagues compared farmer-managed irrigation systems (FMIS) with agency-managed irrigation systems (AMIS — designed, built, and operated by the Nepal Department of Irrigation with international donor funding). Farmer-managed systems used locally designed, low-tech infrastructure (temporary brush-and-mud headworks). Agency-managed systems used modern engineered infrastructure (permanent concrete headworks).

**Key findings:** Farmer-managed systems achieved over 70% high performance on water delivery and agricultural output. Government-managed systems achieved only 40% high performance — despite superior physical infrastructure. The puzzle Ostrom identified: "low-tech systems organized by farmers achieving higher levels of performance than the high-tech systems built largely with donor funding."

**How authority and consequence were coupled (FMIS):** Farmers who built and maintained the systems also made the rules governing water allocation, contributed labour proportional to their benefit, monitored each other, and sanctioned violators. Rule-makers bore the consequences of their rules. The incentive to maintain the system was direct: crop failure meant family hunger. All of Ostrom's coupling principles (2, 3, 4, 5) were present.

**How authority and consequence were separated (AMIS):** Government engineers designed the systems from offices using standard templates. Water allocation rules were set by officials who did not farm the land. Monitoring was performed by government staff accountable to superiors, not to farmers. When the government built modern headworks on previously farmer-managed systems, it simultaneously destroyed the existing institutional arrangements — the rules, monitoring practices, and collective-choice mechanisms that had made the old systems work. Ostrom's finding: "ignoring the impact of changes in physical capital on a form of social capital — the rules used in farmer-organized irrigation systems — can lead to the unintended consequence that the physical capital is not as productive as intended."

**Why this case is evidentially stronger than alternatives:**
1. **Controlled comparison:** Same resource (irrigation water), same country, same time period, different governance structures. This controls for environmental, cultural, and economic variables better than cross-country comparisons.
2. **Counter-intuitive finding:** The physically superior systems performed worse — ruling out the explanation that success is just about resources.
3. **The mechanism is clear:** The specific principles that distinguish FMIS from AMIS map directly to authority-consequence coupling (Principles 2, 3, 4, 5). The failure of AMIS is not attributed to incompetence or corruption but to the structural separation of decision-making from consequence-bearing.
4. **Replication:** The Nepal finding has been confirmed in subsequent studies across multiple countries and resource types.

**Does it support the family directly or only partially?** Directly. The Nepal irrigation comparison demonstrates all three sub-claims: (a) polycentric governance (multiple overlapping farmer groups, each managing their section while coordinating at the system level), (b) shared consequence as infrastructure (farmer-managed systems structurally bind rule-making to consequence-bearing), and (c) failure from separation (government-managed systems with superior physical infrastructure underperform because authority and consequence are separated).

**Sources:**
- Ostrom, E. (1994). "The Performance of Self-Governing Irrigation Systems in Nepal." *Human Systems Management* 13(3).
- Lam, W.F. (1996). "Improving the performance of small-scale irrigation systems." *World Development* 24(8).

## 6. False Friends and Rejections

### Rejected: Habermasian deliberative democracy

Habermas's theory of communicative action addresses how legitimate decisions are produced through rational discourse among participants. The theory does invoke participation, but its core concern is **procedural correctness** — how to structure the ideal speech situation — not the **structural condition** of authority-consequence binding. A Habermasian deliberation can produce legitimate-seeming decisions where consequence-bearing is still structurally separated from authority (e.g., a perfectly deliberative body that governs people who had no voice in the deliberation). The legitimacy synthesis's claim is about institutional infrastructure, not about discursive procedure.

### Rejected: Principal-agent theory

Agency theory addresses delegated authority through incentive alignment — contracts, monitoring, penalties designed to make agents act in principals' interests. But it treats consequence-binding as a **contractual control mechanism** for managing agent behaviour, not as a **polycentric co-holding** of legitimacy. The Johar synthesis argues for orchestration across plural legitimacy axes; principal-agent theory argues for better alignment of a single axis. They address different structural problems.

### Rejected: Weber's three types of legitimacy

Weber's typology (traditional, charismatic, rational-legal) classifies the *basis* of legitimate authority. It does not argue that multiple bases must be co-present, does not address consequence-bearing, and does not describe failure from separation. It is a taxonomy, not a coupling argument. Using Weber as corroboration would collapse the family claim into generic legitimacy theory.

### Rejected: Generic accountability frameworks

Accountability literature (transparency, reporting, auditing, oversight) addresses the *mechanisms* by which authority-holders answer for their decisions. But accountability-as-compliance (did you follow the rules?) is different from shared consequence-as-infrastructure (are you structurally bound into the outcomes you shape?). The legitimacy synthesis's claim is that consequence-bearing must be a property of the institutional architecture, not an add-on oversight mechanism. Accountability frameworks that treat accountability as a monitoring layer rather than a design condition are adjacent but not the same claim.

### Rejected: Social contract theory (Rawls, Hobbes, Locke)

Social contract theory addresses the foundational question of why political authority is legitimate at all — through hypothetical or historical consent. But the Johar synthesis is not about foundational consent; it is about the ongoing structural condition of institutions after they exist. An institution can be founded on a legitimate social contract and still fail if authority and consequence separate during its operation. Social contract theory addresses origin; the synthesis addresses architecture.

### Noted: Beetham's limitation on polycentric design

Beetham provides a strong three-dimensional account of legitimacy but does **not** address polycentric institutional architecture — how multiple overlapping governance centres should be arranged. His framework tells you what legitimacy requires (three coupled dimensions) but not how to institutionally achieve it across multiple scales. This means Beetham corroborates "no single axis suffices" but does not corroborate "polycentric co-holding is the institutional response." For the latter, Ostrom is necessary.

## 7. Preliminary Corroboration Assessment

**Outcome: success condition met**

### What is corroborated

**Sub-claim 1: Polycentric legitimacy — strong corroboration**

Four independent sources arrive at the same structural insight from different disciplinary routes:
- **Ostrom (E. + V.):** Polycentric governance with multiple overlapping centres of authority, from political science and institutional economics
- **Beetham:** Three irreducible dimensions of legitimacy (legal, normative, consent), all required simultaneously, from political theory
- **Scharpf:** Input and output legitimacy both necessary and each contingent on the other, from EU governance studies
- **Hirschman:** Voice, exit, and loyalty must remain coupled for institutional viability, from political economy

None of these authors cites Johar. All arrive at multi-dimensional, coupling-dependent accounts of institutional legitimacy through independent intellectual routes. The convergence is across political theory (Beetham), political economy (Hirschman), institutional economics (Ostrom), and EU governance studies (Scharpf).

**Sub-claim 2: Shared consequence as infrastructural condition — strong corroboration**

Three sources treat authority-consequence coupling as a design variable, not a moral aspiration:
- **Ostrom:** Design principles 2/3/4/5 are structural conditions measurably associated with institutional success (Baggio 2016: 8.7/10 principles in successful commons vs 4.3/10 in failed ones). DeCaro et al. (2024) experimentally confirm that enforcement without participation produces legitimacy collapse.
- **Hirschman:** The exit-voice coupling is an institutional design condition — when consequence-bearers lose voice, the institution enters a death spiral regardless of its participants' intentions.
- **Scharpf:** The EU democratic deficit is a structural separation, not a moral failure — the institutional architecture places authority at one level and consequence-bearing at another.

All three treat the coupling as infrastructure: something that must be built into institutional design, not something that can be achieved through good intentions or added as an oversight layer.

**Sub-claim 3: The control spiral — moderate corroboration**

Two sources describe the escalation mechanism:
- **Scott:** The simplification-failure-further-simplification pattern is documented in all four major cases (German forestry, Soviet collectivisation, Tanzanian ujamaa, Brasilia). The state's response to the failure of simplification is more simplification, not correction.
- **Hirschman:** The exit-voice death spiral is a functional equivalent: exit → loss of voice → decline → more exit → further insulation of authority.

The control spiral is corroborated as a mechanism but not named as such by any external source. The Johar synthesis uses "control-surveillance-energy spiral" (from the coming-spiral claim sheet); the external sources describe the same pattern but from different angles (legibility failure + coercion escalation in Scott; exit-voice decoupling in Hirschman). The mechanism is the same; the vocabulary is different.

### What remains uncorroborated

1. **The four-dimensional legitimacy architecture (democracy, stewardship, experimentation, radical markets).** Ostrom's polycentric governance, Beetham's three dimensions, and Scharpf's input/output are all multi-dimensional but do not use Johar's specific four-axis taxonomy. The claim that legitimacy must be co-held across *these particular* four dimensions remains Johar-specific.

2. **Orchestration over dominance as a named coordination mode.** Ostrom's polycentricity implies orchestration (mutual adjustment among overlapping centres), but "orchestration" as a named institutional design principle — contrasted specifically with "dominance" — is not independently named by any source in this search.

3. **The civic economy as a third pole.** The Johar synthesis (via the coming-spiral claim sheet) argues that interrupting the control spiral requires a civic economy autonomous from both market and state. This specific claim is not corroborated by any source in this search. Ostrom's commons are relevant but she frames them as a "third way" beyond market and state, not as a "civic economy" pole.

### Whether the family stayed distinct from adjacent concepts

**Yes.** The evidence gathered here is specifically about authority-consequence binding and polycentric legitimacy — not about relational topology (how agents are connected), governance under volatility (how to govern under turbulence), or civic truth (how to hold plural truth claims). The Ostrom, Scharpf, Hirschman, and Scott sources address the political question (who has standing, who bears outcomes) rather than the infrastructural question (how connections are patterned) or the epistemic question (how truth is held).

Potential bleed: Ostrom's Principle 7 (minimal recognition of rights to organise) touches relational topology (it addresses the institutional conditions under which self-governance can form). And Scott's mētis argument touches perceptual fidelity (local knowledge as a form of perceptual contact with reality). These boundary touches are noted but do not blur the family: the core evidence addresses legitimacy-consequence coupling, not topology or perception.

### Evidence type

Mixed — conceptual parallel (all four primary sources), empirical case (Nepal irrigation, EU democratic deficit, Scott's historical cases), and experimental evidence (DeCaro et al. 2024). This is a strong mix: the conceptual parallel establishes that the structural insight is independently discoverable; the empirical evidence shows it operates in real institutions; the experimental evidence confirms the mechanism under controlled conditions.

## 8. Recommended Next Move

**Recommended: Move to linguistic-closure**

The legitimacy-and-shared-consequence corroboration has met its success condition. The evidence is strong, multi-type, and multi-disciplinary. The family's three sub-claims are all corroborated, with the four-dimensional taxonomy and "orchestration" vocabulary remaining Johar-specific (which is expected — these are Johar's distinctive contribution, not the structural insight that needs external validation).

Deepening this family further would yield diminishing returns. The next highest-value move is to corroborate linguistic-closure, which has:
- The broadest corpus reach (8 governance clusters, 2 projects)
- Strong tradition resonance (STS, pragmatism, paradigm theory)
- The highest likelihood of spillover to civic-truth and perceptual-fidelity

A short cross-family comparison with relational-topology (the Superfamily F partner) is tempting but premature — relational-topology has its own distinct corroboration lanes (Orlikowski, Suchman, Burt) that should be searched independently rather than piggybacked onto this run.
