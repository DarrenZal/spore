# Boundary-Making Scoping Assessment

## 1. Scope And Question

This memo assesses whether the deferred Johar family `boundary-making-and-entangled-systems` merits active follow-up in the Spore grammar program.

This is a scoping assessment only. No synthesis note, bridge note, claim sheet, lexicon entry, promotion-status change, or canon edit is produced.

The exact question: **Can Markov-blanket / mediation vocabulary translate from formal systems ontology into governance / coordination design language strongly enough to justify future synthesis work?**

The question matters because boundary-making is the sole active survivor from deferred-family triage checkpoint 2. The grammar has 5 enacted canon entries describing structural mechanisms for coordination under complexity, but still lacks formal vocabulary for describing how systems maintain coherence through mediated coupling. Boundary-making proposes that vocabulary. Whether the proposal is translatable determines whether the family enters the active pipeline or joins the dormant set.

## 2. Johar Claim Shape

The bridge note (`johar-boundaries-in-systems`, 1337 words) makes the following proposal:

**What it claims:** Boundary language in systems thinking carries container metaphysics even when softened. "Porous," "semi-permeable," and "fuzzy" boundaries still treat the line as primary and couplings as secondary. The actual observable phenomenon is differential propagation, not disconnection. A replacement vocabulary is needed.

**What new primitive it proposes:** Five interconnected concepts:

- **Differential pass-through** — the observable phenomenon. Impacts propagate with varying speed, gain, and attenuation depending on coupling topology. What appears as a coherent "unit" is a regime in which pass-through properties are structured enough that dynamics become tractable.
- **Markov blankets** — the formal primitive. A statistical condition (screening-off via conditional independence) rather than a geometric image (enclosure). A neighbourhood in a dependency graph, not a spatial interface.
- **Mediation structure** — the design target. The set of variables that concentrate dependency and structure transmission. Governance design becomes "shaping mediation structures" rather than "drawing better boundaries."
- **Conditional-independence gradients** — the topology. Screening relations are approximate, vary by dimension and timescale, producing gradient topology rather than clean partitions.
- **Breakdown as regime reconfiguration** — the failure mode. Dissolution is not edge-breach but the loss of conditions under which screening relations hold — timescale separations collapse, mediating variables saturate, coupling gains shift.

**What is attractive about it:** The vocabulary fills a genuine gap in the grammar. Spore already uses "holons," "membranes," "protocols," and "projections" to describe coordination architecture but describes them functionally, not formally. The bridge note offers a precise replacement for container metaphysics that aligns with how the grammar actually works (overlapping membership, semilattice structure, mediated coupling).

**What remains unproven:** Whether the formal apparatus (Markov blankets, conditional-independence gradients) can actually function as governance/coordination design vocabulary rather than remaining a modeling formalism borrowed from computational neuroscience and theoretical physics. The bridge note asserts the translation; it does not demonstrate it.

## 5. Boundary Checks

### Holding-complexity-beyond-control

**What overlaps:** Both address what happens when complexity exceeds control. Holding-complexity names the civilisational condition; boundary-making proposes vocabulary for describing the interfaces between systems that must hold complexity together.

**What remains distinct:** Holding-complexity is a diagnostic pattern (complexity exceeds control, two attractors, five capacities needed). Boundary-making proposes formal vocabulary for one specific aspect of that response — how systems describe and design their interfaces with coupled systems. The diagnostic tells you *why* you need better coordination; boundary-making would tell you *how to formally describe the interfaces*.

**What would create false strength:** Treating holding-complexity's promotion as validation of boundary-making's formal vocabulary. The diagnostic does not depend on Markov blankets; the formal vocabulary does not depend on the diagnostic. They are structurally independent claims that address the same territory from different angles.

### Relational-topology-and-context-design

**What overlaps:** Both address how institutional architecture shapes what is possible. Relational topology covers the patterning of connection, distance, and encounter; boundary-making covers how systems individuate within coupled dependency graphs.

**What remains distinct:** Relational topology is about the relational field itself — how contexts generate identity, distribute power, and produce coherence or fracture. Boundary-making is about the formal description of system interfaces — what passes through, what is filtered, what is conditionally transparent. Relational topology answers "what does the pattern of relations look like?" Boundary-making answers "what formal vocabulary describes the interfaces between coupled systems?"

**What would create false strength:** Treating boundary-making vocabulary as required infrastructure for relational topology. Relational topology already operates with its own vocabulary (imaginary identity, context metabolism, alignment decay). Boundary-making would add a formal layer underneath, but relational topology does not need it to function as a design concept.

### Entangled-freedom-and-being

**What overlaps:** Both reject the autonomous-individual-as-container model. Entangled freedom refounds agency from autonomy-as-separation to participation-within-relation; boundary-making refounds system identity from container-with-boundary to regime-of-mediated-coupling.

**What remains distinct:** Entangled freedom is about the human developmental condition — freedom as relational agency, being as prior to participation. Boundary-making is about systems ontology — how any system (not just human agents) maintains coherence through mediated coupling. The bridge note itself draws this distinction explicitly: "This essay is about systems ontology. It is distinct from entangled-freedom-and-being, which is about the developmental condition of human freedom under entanglement."

**What would create false strength:** Treating the shared rejection of container metaphysics as evidence that both concepts require the same formal vocabulary. Entangled freedom does not need Markov blankets. Boundary-making does not need developmental ontology. They share an anti-container posture but propose different replacements for different domains.

### Governance-under-volatility

**What overlaps:** Both address regime change and coherence under shifting conditions. Governance-under-volatility names coherence as the governance success measure; boundary-making's "regime reconfiguration" describes what happens to system boundaries when conditions shift.

**What remains distinct:** Governance-under-volatility is about the governance condition (compound volatility) and the governance response (coherence over control). Boundary-making proposes formal vocabulary for describing what happens to coupling structures when regimes change. Governance-under-volatility answers "what is governance success under volatility?" Boundary-making would answer "what formal vocabulary describes how system interfaces reorganize under regime change?"

**What would create false strength:** Treating boundary-making's "regime reconfiguration" as the mechanism behind governance-under-volatility's coherence framework. The coherence framework already has its own explanatory machinery (from Beer's cybernetics, from holding-complexity's diagnostic). Adding Markov blanket vocabulary to volatility governance does not strengthen it — it adds a contested formalism to a framework that already works.

## 6. Scoping Judgment

**Possibly viable but too translation-sensitive.**

The boundary-making family contains a genuine design insight that the grammar needs: **governance design should focus on shaping mediation structures (the quality of coupling between domains) rather than drawing better boundaries (the completeness of separation between domains)**. This insight is independently confirmed by three non-Johar, non-active-inference traditions:

1. **Beer's variety engineering** (cybernetics) — boundaries are variety attenuators/amplifiers with regulatory functions, not walls. This is the strongest independent convergence.
2. **Star & Griesemer's boundary objects** + **Carlile's pragmatic boundaries** (organizational theory) — boundaries between communities are sites of productive mediation, not barriers. Empirical foundation.
3. **Ostrom's boundary rules** (institutional economics) — boundaries are conditional rules governing access and participation, not spatial enclosures. Prescriptive governance vocabulary.

However, the *specific formal apparatus* that the bridge note proposes — Markov blankets, conditional-independence gradients, screening relations — faces serious translation problems:

1. **The Bruineberg et al. critique** (2022) shows that "Markov blanket" slides between a trivially true statistical concept and an empirically undemonstrated physical/ontological claim. This equivocation is amplified, not resolved, by extension to social systems.
2. **No published work** rigorously applies Markov blanket formalism to governance design. The Active Inference Institute's organizational work is programmatic (stating aspirations) rather than productive (delivering frameworks). Kaufmann's work on firms is unpublished.
3. **The design insight is independently available.** Beer's variety attenuation, Star/Griesemer's boundary objects, and Ostrom's boundary rules all provide the "mediation over demarcation" insight with independently grounded formal machinery. The Markov blanket apparatus adds contested mathematical notation but not additional design content.
4. **Ostrom actively argues against gradient boundaries.** Her first design principle is "clearly defined boundaries." The conditional-independence-gradients concept would need to be reconciled with Ostrom's evidence that ambiguous boundaries cause governance failure.

The family is not "not viable beyond metaphor" — the design insight is real and independently confirmed. But the formal apparatus is too translation-sensitive to carry the weight of a lexicon entry in its current form.

## 7. If Viable, What The Reviewable Core Would Be

Since the judgment is "possibly viable but too translation-sensitive," the reviewable core must be separated from the translation-sensitive apparatus.

**What the future reviewable core would be:**

The design principle that governance architecture should be designed as mediation infrastructure — shaping the quality and selectivity of coupling between domains rather than the completeness of their separation. Specifically:

- **Differential transmission** as a design parameter: governance boundaries transmit different kinds of information, authority, and resource flows at different rates and fidelity levels, and this differential character is the functionally important property.
- **Mediation structure** as a design target: protocols, interfaces, and shared artifacts through which domains interact should be the primary focus of governance boundary design.
- **Regime sensitivity** as a failure diagnostic: governance failure is coupling-regime change (conditions that made coordination tractable cease to hold), not boundary breach.

This core is supportable by Beer (variety engineering), Star/Griesemer (boundary objects), Ostrom (boundary rules), and Luhmann (structural coupling) — without depending on the contested Markov blanket formalism.

**What would still remain Johar-specific or too speculative:**

- Markov blankets as the formal primitive for governance boundary description. The formal concept does not transfer intact from statistical/biological contexts to institutional design.
- Conditional-independence gradients as governance topology. The statistical language suggests precision the social domain cannot support, and Ostrom's evidence argues against it.
- The claim that the grammar's existing mechanisms (holons, projections, commitment protocols) literally *are* Markov blankets in the Friston sense. This is an ontological claim that the philosophical literature does not support.

**Whether the likely target is `spore.lexicon` or only background design language:**

The reviewable core is more likely background design language informing how Spore's membrane and protocol architecture is designed than a standalone lexicon entry. The design principle (mediation over demarcation) should inform the grammar's architectural vocabulary, but the specific concepts (Markov blankets, conditional-independence gradients) are too translation-sensitive for lexicon-level formality.

However, **one narrow pathway to lexicon remains open**: if a translation memo can ground "mediation structure" and "differential transmission" in Beer's variety engineering and Ostrom's boundary rules — using those traditions as the formal backbone rather than Markov blankets — then a lexicon entry describing mediation-over-demarcation as a design principle for the grammar's membrane architecture could be viable. This would be a different entry than what the bridge note currently proposes.

## 9. Recommendation

**Do one narrower translation memo first.**

The boundary-making family should not proceed directly to targeted corroboration. The design insight is real, but the formal apparatus is contested. A translation memo should:

1. **Reground the design principle** in Beer (variety engineering), Star/Griesemer (boundary objects), and Ostrom (boundary rules) — traditions that independently support "mediation over demarcation" with non-contested formal machinery.
2. **Determine whether Markov blanket language adds anything** that Beer + Star/Griesemer + Ostrom do not already provide. If yes, identify exactly what. If no, the family should be redesigned around the multi-tradition convergence rather than the single-author Markov blanket proposal.
3. **Resolve the Ostrom tension**: Ostrom's evidence shows that governance requires *clear* boundaries even when they are conditional and graduated. The translation memo must reconcile mediation-over-demarcation with Ostrom's empirical finding that ambiguous boundaries cause governance failure.
4. **Determine the right lexicon target**: is this a new named primitive, a design principle section in the project vision, or background vocabulary informing membrane architecture?

This memo is a smaller, faster artifact than a full corroboration pass. It uses the sources identified in this assessment and produces a clearer answer to the question "what exactly would the grammar gain?"

**Why not "proceed to targeted corroboration pass":** The corroboration pass would need to know what it is corroborating. Currently, the bridge note proposes Markov-blanket vocabulary. The external research shows this vocabulary is too translation-sensitive. Before corroborating, the family needs a clearer target — one that draws on the multi-tradition convergence rather than depending on the contested formalism.

**Why not "return this family to dormancy":** The design insight is real, independently confirmed, and fills a genuine gap in the grammar. Dormancy would lose the contribution. But the contribution needs to be reframed before it can be productively pursued.

## 11. Guardrails

- **Formal elegance is not enough.** The Markov blanket concept is technically elegant. That does not make it translatable to governance design. The translation must be demonstrated, not assumed.

- **Metaphorical systems language is not enough.** "Everything is connected" and "boundaries are really interfaces" are common systems-theory sentiments. They do not, by themselves, constitute a design contribution. The boundary-making family must provide formal content that goes beyond generic systems wisdom.

- **Promoted canon entries do not validate this family by adjacency.** Holding-complexity's promotion confirms the overarching diagnosis but does not validate boundary-making's formal vocabulary. Relational-topology's promotion covers relational patterning but does not require Markov blanket formalism. Each family must stand on its own evidence.

- **Translation into governance/design language must be explicit to count.** A source that uses Markov blankets to model biological boundaries does not count as governance corroboration. A source that uses "boundary" metaphorically in governance does not count as formal support. The translation must be demonstrated with specific governance design implications — not asserted by analogy.

- **If the concept only works as background framing, it should not become a family.** The design insight (mediation over demarcation) is real. If it can only serve as background vocabulary informing how the grammar's membranes are designed — rather than as a reviewable lexicon entry or vision-level concept — then the family should be dissolved into design guidance, not maintained as an active family awaiting promotion.

- **Ostrom's corrective must be taken seriously.** Governance requires clarity about who is in and who is out, what the rules are, and how they are enforced. "Gradient boundaries" can be a governance failure mode, not just a design insight. Any boundary-making vocabulary that enters the grammar must be reconciled with the operational need for clear, enforceable, known governance rules.

- **The Bruineberg et al. critique must be engaged, not avoided.** Any future work in this family that uses Markov blanket language must address the Pearl/Friston equivocation directly. Borrowing mathematical authority from a contested formalism without engaging the criticism is not acceptable.
