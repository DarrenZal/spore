## 3. Drafting Rules

1. **Mediation-over-demarcation is a design principle, not a new systems formalism.** The section states how interfaces should be specified. It does not propose a new theory of what systems are.

2. **Clear governance boundaries must remain explicit.** The Ostrom integration is load-bearing: rules at interfaces must be clear, enforceable, and known. Graduated and conditional rules are acceptable; ambiguous rules are not. The section must carry this with full weight.

3. **Mediation structure is the design target.** "Mediation structure" means: the protocols, shared artifacts, and regulatory functions through which domains interact. This term should appear bolded on first use.

4. **Membrane/interface language must stay concrete.** Every use of "membrane" or "interface" should be grounded in a specific grammar mechanism (authorized boundary crossing, federation exchange rules, holon membranes, learning membrane). No floating abstractions.

5. **Markov blankets and conditional-independence language stay out entirely.** Not as analogy, not as footnote, not as genealogy. The research trail preserves provenance; the foundation section uses only its own vocabulary.

6. **Differential transmission and regime reconfiguration stay out of the section.** These are qualified background. They may appear in other contexts but have no role in a compact foundation section. If they were included, they would expand the section beyond what the architecture doc requires.

7. **The philosophical argument against container metaphysics stays out.** The section says "specify regulatory function, not spatial properties" — an operational corrective. It does not argue that containers are metaphysically wrong.

8. **The research-family label `boundary-making-and-entangled-systems` does not appear.** Canon-facing prose uses only the principle name and design-target term.

9. **Voice must match the host doc.** Declarative, confident, uses em dashes for parenthetical clarification, bold for key terms on first use. No hedging, no footnotes, no academic sourcing within the section prose.

## 4. Section Shape

**Paragraphs:** 3.

- **Paragraph 1 — Principle statement.** Opens by connecting to the preceding section ("where containment meets overlap, interfaces arise"), names the principle, defines the design target. This paragraph does the main conceptual work.
- **Paragraph 2 — Grammar grounding.** Shows that the grammar already practices mediation-over-demarcation through four concrete mechanisms: authorized boundary crossing, federation exchange rules, holon membrane functions, and (implicitly) the learning membrane. Names the principle as making explicit what these mechanisms share.
- **Paragraph 3 — Clarity requirement.** States the Ostrom integration: clear operational rules at every interface. Closes with the anti-vagueness line.

**Bullets:** None. The surrounding sections use prose paragraphs and occasional bullet lists. This section is short enough to work as pure prose. Bullets would fragment the design principle into a checklist, which would lose the compositional quality — the three elements (mediation target, grammar grounding, clarity requirement) build on each other.

**"What this is not" line:** Not as a separate line. The draft avoids the metaphysical argument and the vagueness risk implicitly through its operational tone. A separate disclaimer would break the host doc's voice, which is declarative throughout. The corrective is in the prose: "rather than its spatial properties" (operational, not ontological), "Mediation is not vagueness" (anti-vagueness signal).

**Connection backward to "How the Axes Compose":** The preceding section ends with practice examples showing how holonic and network axes compose in specific artifacts (spec DAG, entity graph, project holon). The new section opens at the same concrete level: "Where containment meets overlap, interfaces arise — a holon's membrane, a federation edge, the learning membrane." This picks up the compositional logic and asks: how should those interfaces be designed?

**Connection forward to "Fractal Scale Levels":** The scale table shows the dual-axis pattern at every level (personal, P2P, organizational, network-of-networks, global/bioregional). The mediation principle applies at every level — personal node membranes, organizational interfaces, federation edges. The connection is implicit and natural: the same interface design principle recurs at every scale alongside the same structural pattern.

## 5. Exact Draft

```markdown
## Interface Design: Mediation Over Demarcation

Where containment meets overlap, interfaces arise — a holon's membrane, a federation edge, the learning membrane that metabolizes external work. The design principle governing these interfaces is **mediation over demarcation**: specify what the interface transmits, filters, translates, and regulates rather than its spatial properties. The design target is the **mediation structure** — the protocols, shared artifacts, and regulatory functions through which domains interact. Design the quality of coupling, not the completeness of separation.

The grammar already practices this. "Authorized boundary crossing" specifies what authorization is required for passage, not what the wall looks like. Federation exchange rules specify event types, provenance, and consent conditions — interface behavior, not containment geometry. Holon membranes have regulatory functions (what they expose, what stays sovereign) rather than spatial properties. The principle names what these mechanisms share: interfaces are mediation infrastructure with specified regulatory properties.

Clear operational rules are required at every interface. Rules can be graduated, conditional, and context-sensitive — but they must be specified, enforceable, and known. Ambiguous interfaces are a governance failure mode, not a design feature. Mediation is not vagueness; it is precise specification of regulatory function.
```

**Word count:** ~180 words. Three paragraphs. No bullets. No footnotes.

## 6. Boundary Check

### vs. `project-vision.md`

**What the draft borrows.** The project vision's "Containment and Connection" section and "Authorized boundary crossing" constitutional commitment. The draft references "authorized boundary crossing" as an existing mechanism that already follows the mediation principle. It also borrows the membrane/protocol vocabulary the vision uses functionally.

**What it does not claim.** The draft does not restate the project vision's containment-and-connection framing, its constitutional commitments list, or its coordination ecology description. It does not position itself as a vision-level concept. It positions itself as an architectural design principle within the structural model.

**Redundancy risk avoided.** The project vision uses membrane language but does not name the design principle governing membrane specification. The draft names that principle. If the draft restated the vision's "Invariants give containment... Protocols shape connection" language, it would be redundant. Instead it says "the grammar already practices this" and gives concrete mechanism-level examples, which is additive.

### vs. `constitutional-artifacts-and-graph-projections.md`

**What the draft borrows.** Nothing directly. The constitutional-artifacts doc describes normative artifacts and their graph projections. The draft does not reference artifacts, projections, dual representation, or sensors.

**What it does not claim.** The draft does not claim to govern how artifacts relate to each other or how projections are structured. It claims to govern how interfaces between governance domains should be specified — a different concern.

**Redundancy risk avoided.** The constitutional-artifacts doc has a "Structure and Flow in the Graph Model" section discussing how DAGs and hypergraphs relate. The draft does not address graph structure — it addresses interface specification. No overlap.

### vs. `relational-topology-and-context-design`

**What the draft borrows.** Nothing. The draft does not use relational topology vocabulary (connection, distance, encounter, imaginary identity, context metabolism, alignment decay).

**What it does not claim.** The draft does not claim that interfaces shape relational patterns or generate identity. It claims that interfaces should be specified by regulatory function. Relational topology describes *the field*. The draft describes *the design principle for interfaces between fields*. These are structurally different and the draft does not cross into relational-topology territory.

**Redundancy risk avoided.** If the draft said "interfaces shape how agents relate to each other" or "the patterning of interface design generates institutional identity," it would collapse into relational-topology. The draft does not say this. It stays on specification: what the interface transmits, filters, translates, regulates. The corrective is operational, not relational.

### vs. `holding-complexity-beyond-control`

**What the draft borrows.** Nothing. The draft does not reference complexity, control failure, two attractors, five structural capacities, or adaptive coordination.

**What it does not claim.** The draft does not claim to explain why the grammar needs interfaces or why adaptive coordination is necessary. It claims to specify how interfaces should be designed. Holding-complexity provides the diagnostic (why). The draft provides a design discipline (how, at the interface level).

**Redundancy risk avoided.** If the draft opened with "under manifest complexity, interfaces become load-bearing" or justified itself through the control-failure diagnostic, it would borrow from holding-complexity without adding design content. The draft opens with the structural fact ("where containment meets overlap, interfaces arise") and proceeds directly to the design principle. No motivational framing from holding-complexity is imported.
