# Human Economy and Commitment: Convergence Synthesis

A synthesis note on the human-economy-and-commitment concept, drawn from the Johar economy intake (Batches 1–2). This is the first cluster to reach synthesis from the economy-and-value theme. It names the structural account of why economic participation under discovery requires commitment-based primitives rather than wage-based or transaction-based primitives — and why Spore's commitment grammar is closer to an economic coordination pattern than it has yet acknowledged.

## Contributing Sources

### Bridge notes (Tier A — full comparative intake)

**spore.connection.johar-free-markets-freedom** — The "free market" has never existed because markets require free agents, and freedom is not absence of interference but systemic capability. Proposes eight interlinked capabilities (cognitive security, temporal wealth, system awareness, material sufficiency, relational agency, option space, sovereignty, care) as structural preconditions for agents to function as distributed intelligence. Markets without capable agents compute distortion, not knowledge.

**spore.connection.johar-commitment-economy** — The wage contract fails under discovery because its four functions (security floor, legible obligation, risk placement, fairness story) depend on work being specifiable in advance. The replacement unit is the commitment signature: bounded responsibility under uncertainty with declared verification modes. Four types: delivery, discovery, stewardship, risk. Standing provides the security floor without requiring specifiable work. Governed renegotiation makes scope change auditable.

### Claim sheets (Tier B — source claims only)

**beyond-the-labor-society-building** — The labor society's scaffolding (performance reviews, role adherence, process compliance) is a relic of a worldview where human value was legible only through predefined outputs. What matters increasingly — care, tenderness, risk-bearing, network activation — defies this framework. Proposes cascades of equity: value and risk travel together, shared across contributors in multi-nodal webs tracked by enabling conditions rather than deliverables.

**theres-no-such-thing-as-purchase** — The self-contained purchase is a fiction; every transaction rests on a dense field of dependencies that extend far beyond the moment of sale. Exchange must thicken from single-layer transaction to multi-layer contract governing ecological maintenance, labour standards, and institutional resilience. Transaction-as-relational-commitment is the beginning of a different economic grammar.

## What Converges

Four independent sources point at the same structural claim:

> **Economic participation under discovery and complexity cannot be governed by wage or transaction grammar designed for predictable throughput. The replacement is commitment-based: bounded responsibilities under uncertainty, with standing for participation security, governed renegotiation for adaptive scope, and relational contribution tracking instead of role-output measurement. Participation itself requires capability conditions — material sufficiency, cognitive security, temporal wealth — without which formal rights or market access are structurally empty.**

The convergence has three layers:

### 1. Wage and transaction grammar breaks under discovery

All four sources describe different mechanisms by which economic forms designed for predictable work fail under conditions of discovery and complexity:

- **Wage-specifiability failure:** The wage contract assumes work can be specified in advance, supervised in execution, and verified by output. Discovery breaks all three assumptions. The result is planning theatre, spurious metrics, and moral injury (commitment-economy).
- **Market-freedom failure:** Markets require agents with systemic capabilities. Without cognitive security, temporal wealth, and material sufficiency, markets compute distortion — prices encode asymmetry and dependency rather than intelligence (free-markets-freedom).
- **Labor-legibility failure:** The labor society's compensation scaffolding maps human value to predefined outputs. What increasingly matters — care, risk-bearing, network activation, atmosphere-shifting — is unbounded by space and time, and defies industrial measurement (beyond-labor-society).
- **Transaction-containment failure:** The self-contained purchase is a fiction produced by exporting disorder to ecological, social, and institutional substrates. When those substrates become fragile, thin transaction grammar no longer secures what it claims to secure (no-such-thing-as-purchase).

The pattern is consistent: economic forms designed for predictable, containable, measurable activity fail when the work is epistemic, the contributions are relational, or the consequences extend beyond the transaction boundary. The problem is structural mismatch between economic grammar and the conditions of the work.

*Supported by:* all 4 sources.

### 2. Commitment-based primitives are the structural replacement

The corrective is not reforming wages or making markets more efficient but changing the primitive unit of economic coordination:

- **Commitment signatures** replace job descriptions: explicit obligations with declared verification modes, four types differentiated by epistemic character — delivery, discovery, stewardship, risk (commitment-economy).
- **Cascades of equity** replace static compensation: value and risk travel together through multi-nodal webs, tracked by enabling conditions rather than deliverables (beyond-labor-society).
- **Multi-layer contracts** replace thin transactions: exchange thickens to govern ecological maintenance, labour standards, and institutional resilience explicitly (no-such-thing-as-purchase).
- **Systemic freedom as capability** replaces formal market access: eight interlinked capabilities scaffold distributed intelligence, not just participation rights (free-markets-freedom).

What these sources share is a reframing: the unit of economic coordination must shift from time-for-money (wage) or payment-for-receipt (transaction) to obligation-under-uncertainty (commitment). This is the same structural move that Spore's grammar makes for governance: commitments are explicit, scope-bound, revisable obligations with provenance and evidence — not permanent contracts or one-time exchanges.

*Supported by:* commitment-economy (commitment signatures), beyond-labor-society (cascades of equity), no-such-thing-as-purchase (multi-layer contracts), free-markets-freedom (capability as infrastructure).

### 3. Participation requires standing and capability conditions

All four sources converge on the recognition that commitment-based participation cannot function without structural prerequisites:

- **Standing** provides the security floor: institutional membership that enables participation without requiring work to be specifiable. It turns "who can afford to take the risk" into "who can legitimately participate" (commitment-economy).
- **Material sufficiency and temporal wealth** prevent discovery from becoming entrepreneurial precarity. Without slack, agents collapse into survival and cannot learn, deliberate, or adapt (free-markets-freedom).
- **Provenance-based participation** tracks relational contribution rather than predefined output, making visible the care, risk-bearing, and network activation that industrial metrics erase (beyond-labor-society).
- **Substrate accountability** makes the hidden conditions of exchange explicit: ecological, social, and institutional dependencies that the thin transaction model offloads (no-such-thing-as-purchase).

The implication for Spore is that the grammar's commitment protocols are participation infrastructure only if the capability conditions for participation are also addressed. Commitment without standing is precarity with better vocabulary. Standing without commitments is membership without obligation.

*Supported by:* commitment-economy (standing), free-markets-freedom (8 capabilities), beyond-labor-society (provenance-based participation), no-such-thing-as-purchase (substrate accountability).

## What Does Not Converge

No source opposes the concept, but three open questions remain unresolved:

1. **Is Spore's commitment grammar already implicitly a commitment-economy grammar?** The structural overlap is substantial: commitment signatures ≈ commitment protocols, standing ≈ holon membership, governed renegotiation ≈ commitment lifecycle revision, contribution mixes ≈ layered participation rights. Whether Spore should explicitly claim the commitment-economy framing — or whether the parallel is close enough that the grammar already provides this and simply needs to name it — is unresolved. The risk of claiming it is over-extension; the risk of not claiming it is missing a structural insight about what the grammar already does.

2. **Does the delivery/discovery/stewardship/risk taxonomy belong in Spore's grammar?** The commitment-economy essay proposes four commitment types differentiated by epistemic character: delivery (produce capability), discovery (reduce uncertainty), stewardship (maintain coherence), and risk (preserve resilience). Spore currently treats commitments as generic. Adding a commitment-type taxonomy could enrich expressiveness but also adds complexity. Whether these types are universal or specific to economic coordination is unresolved.

3. **Should standing be modeled as a governance/economic participation primitive or treated as contextual infrastructure?** Standing provides the security floor that makes commitment-based participation broadly accessible. It could be a named primitive in Spore's grammar (alongside commitment, intent, evidence) or it could be treated as an external precondition that Spore's grammar assumes but does not model. The free-markets-freedom essay argues for explicit capability modeling; the commitment-economy essay treats standing as a mechanism within commitment economy specifically.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate pattern** — Human-economy-and-commitment names an economic coordination pattern that Spore's grammar already embodies structurally but has not articulated as a named design context. Four sources converge on the same account: commitment-based primitives replace wage and transaction grammar under discovery, and participation requires standing and capability conditions. The structural overlap with Spore's existing commitment grammar is the strongest of any economy-and-value concept.

**Promotion status: defer** — The convergence is real (4 sources, 12 supports, 0 opposes) but carries two limitations:

1. **All sources are Johar-derived.** All 4 contributing sources are from the same author (Indy Johar). While they address different facets of the human economy from different angles (systemic freedom, commitment economy, labor society, transaction critique), they share a common discursive tradition. The source count overstates independence.

2. **The commitment-economy parallel needs testing.** The structural overlap between Johar's commitment economy and Spore's commitment grammar is asserted by convergent design, not demonstrated by production evidence. Whether Spore's governance-oriented commitment protocols actually function as economic coordination mechanisms — and whether the commitment-type taxonomy (delivery/discovery/stewardship/risk) is universal or domain-specific — has not been tested.

Promotion should wait for either: (a) a non-Johar source that independently arrives at commitment-based economic primitives, or (b) production evidence from an organisation operating on commitment-signature governance that validates the structural claims.
