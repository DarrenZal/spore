# Hyperstition Governance: Convergence and Tensions

A synthesis note on the hyperstition field family, drawn from the learning field's first convergence cycle. This is the pilot synthesis — it tests the workflow as much as it resolves the content.

## Cluster

- **Field family:** hyperstition
- **Governance clusters:** `spore.project-vision:hyperstition`, `spore.commitment-pooling:hyperstition`
- **Source bridge notes:** `spore.connection.hyperstition`, `spore.connection.constructive-hyperstition`
- **Cross-project:** no (Spore only)
- **Synthesis readiness:** READY (has tension) — 6 supports, 6 opposes, 2 distinct source notes

## Source Bridge Notes

**spore.connection.hyperstition** — Establishes the structural parallel between hyperstition theory and Spore's coordination grammar. Core argument: Spore adds accountability, contestability, and evidence requirements that the hyperstition mechanism alone lacks. Maps intent pressure to the belief-encoding force in hyperstition dynamics. Stance toward canon: **clarify** existing terms in project-vision and commitment-pooling. All 3 claims support the review claims.

**spore.connection.constructive-hyperstition** — Tests whether Spore's constitutional commitments resolve the constructive/extractive distinction that the Gitcoin article identifies but does not structurally ground. Finds that the commitments offer *candidate criteria* but not complete answers — they carry Spore's own design assumptions and leave three dimensions unresolved. Stance toward canon: **no change** — the grammar already contains the safeguards; what the comparison adds is a communication frame. All 3 claims oppose the review claims (they push back on completeness, not on direction).

## Convergences

Both bridge notes agree on the following:

1. **Spore's grammar maps onto hyperstition dynamics.** The coordination ecology (vision → intent → commitment → evidence → learning) structurally parallels the hyperstition mechanism (belief → collective action → reality construction). This is not contested between the notes.

2. **Constitutional commitments are the relevant safeguard layer.** Both notes identify provenance, forkability, contestability, meaningful local autonomy, authorized boundary crossing, and reviewable authority as the structural criteria. The disagreement is about their completeness, not their relevance.

3. **Intent pressure is the coordination-grammar form of hyperstition force.** The gap between normative frontier and epistemic frontier operates like belief encoding into action. The constructive-hyperstition note does not contest this mapping.

4. **The Gitcoin article identifies the constructive/extractive distinction but does not resolve it.** Both notes treat this as the open structural problem — they diverge on how far Spore's grammar goes toward resolving it.

## Active Tensions / Objections

The following objections come from `spore.connection.constructive-hyperstition` and **oppose** the review claims that Spore's grammar can govern hyperstition dynamics:

### Tension 1: Temporal dynamics are unresolved

> "The criteria carry Spore's own assumptions and do not resolve: temporal dynamics (constructive → extractive over time)..."

A hyperstition might pass all constitutional criteria at inception and still become extractive as it scales. Static structural criteria may not capture phase transitions. The question for canon: should Spore's governance model include temporal monitoring — periodic re-evaluation of whether coordination that was once constructive has crossed into extraction?

### Tension 2: Perspective-dependence

> "...perspective-dependence (what looks constructive inside may look extractive outside)..."

Spore's constitutional commitments assume a view from within the coordination grammar. What looks like authorized boundary crossing from inside a commitment pool may look like illegitimate expansion from outside. The criteria are scope-relative, not universal. This challenges any promotion that would frame Spore's safeguards as sufficient rather than provisional.

### Tension 3: Material power asymmetries

> "...material power asymmetries that make formal exit rights practically inaccessible."

Forkability, contestability, and exit rights can be formally available while practically inaccessible due to economic, social, or informational power differences. The structural criteria describe what *should* be true of constructive coordination, not what *is* true when power is unevenly distributed. This is the deepest objection — it questions whether formal governance design can substitute for material conditions.

### Tension 4: Criteria are Spore-relative

> "Spore's constitutional commitments — provenance, forkability, contestability, meaningful local autonomy, authorized boundary crossing, reviewable authority — offer candidate structural criteria for the constructive/extractive distinction in hyperstition theory."

This claim from the constructive-hyperstition note *sounds* supportive but was projected as an opposes edge because the note's overall disposition is "no change" — the criteria are offered as candidates, explicitly framed as carrying Spore's design assumptions. A different coordination grammar might draw the line differently. The opposition is epistemological: the criteria are theory-laden, not neutral.

## Production Evidence

What would resolve these tensions:

1. **Case study test:** Apply the six constitutional criteria to a concrete hyperstition that turned extractive (e.g., a DAO that locked participants in). Would the criteria have flagged the transition? At what point?

2. **Cross-scope evaluation:** Have an external evaluator (outside Spore's grammar) assess whether Spore's own coordination artifacts pass the constructive threshold. If the criteria only work from inside, that's a finding.

3. **Power audit:** Identify a commitment pool where formal exit rights exist but material conditions make exit costly. Document the gap between formal and practical accessibility.

4. **Temporal monitoring pilot:** Run a lightweight check on an active commitment pool over time — do the criteria degrade? Does constructive coordination show early warning signs before becoming extractive?

## Disposition

Clarify existing terms. The hyperstition comparison illuminates what Spore's coordination grammar is doing without proposing structural changes. The tensions are real but they are about completeness, not about error — the grammar's safeguards are relevant; the question is whether they are sufficient.
