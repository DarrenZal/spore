# Market Legibility and Moral Provenance: Convergence Synthesis

A synthesis note on the market-legibility-and-moral-provenance concept, drawn from the Johar economy intake (Batches 1–2). This is the second cluster to reach synthesis from the economy-and-value theme. It names the structural account of why markets perceive selectively — hearing signals that fit their existing interpretive machinery while remaining deaf to substrate-level conditions — and what translation infrastructure is required to restore what markets cannot hear on their own.

## Contributing Sources

### Bridge note (Tier A — originating source)

**spore.connection.johar-moral-provenance** — Modern economic systems sever moral context from transactions by design: products are abstracted from their social and environmental origins. The essay proposes moral provenance as infrastructure: mandatory, non-destructible records that make the full chain of custody visible, restoring moral agency by making consequences non-deniable. Connects to Spore's provenance constitutional commitment — what Spore does for knowledge claims, moral provenance does for economic goods.

### Claim sheets (Tier B — source claims only)

**the-superpower-of-design-re-coding** — Design's power is expanding value grammars, not optimising within them. Design precedes valuation: it crystallises desires not yet codified, making new forms of value legible to coordination systems. The gap between desire and price is where design operates. Re-coding value grammars is institutional power that precedes and shapes market coordination.

**the-signals-markets-can-hear-and** — Markets do not price importance; they price translatability. A structural distinction exists between shocks within the system (repricing of labour, rate adjustments) and stress beneath the system (ecosystem degradation, institutional decay). Markets metabolise the first kind but are deaf to the second until damage materialises. The gap is not a failure of information but a failure of translation infrastructure.

**theres-no-such-thing-as-purchase** — The self-contained purchase is a fiction produced by exporting disorder to ecological, social, and institutional substrates. Exchange must thicken from single-layer transaction to multi-layer contract governing continuity conditions. Every exchange is already a position in relation to the material and institutional order — the question is whether that position is made explicit or left unaccountable.

## What Converges

Four independent sources point at the same structural claim:

> **Markets perceive selectively: they hear signals that fit their existing interpretive machinery and are structurally deaf to substrate-level conditions (ecological, social, institutional) that make economic stability possible. The corrective is not better information but translation infrastructure — moral provenance, value grammar recoding, and contract thickening that makes the hidden conditions of exchange explicit and contestable. Market legibility is a design problem, not an awareness problem.**

The convergence has three layers:

### 1. Markets are selectively perceptive

All four sources describe the same structural property of market perception from different angles:

- **Moral severance:** Transaction mechanisms are architecturally designed to abstract products from their social and environmental origins. People hold ethical standards but purchase goods in a sphere devoid of moral context. The problem is structural — ignorance is encouraged, not accidental (moral-provenance).
- **Translatability filter:** Markets do not price importance; they price what can be converted into expected changes in cash flows, discount rates, or volatility. Substrate-level threats (ecological degradation, institutional decay) remain structurally inaudible because they lack a clean pricing function (signals-markets-can-hear).
- **Desire-price gap:** Between what the world needs (systemic, ecological, relational desires) and what markets can hear (codified, priceable value) lies a structural gap. Markets cannot perceive value forms that have not yet been codified into their grammar (superpower-of-design).
- **Substrate invisibility:** The simplicity of purchase was produced by hiding the conditions of possibility — soil depletion, labour precarity, institutional fragility — that sustain it. When substrates destabilise, the hidden becomes active (no-such-thing-as-purchase).

The pattern is consistent: markets are not broken — they are selectively perceptive by design. They metabolise what fits their existing interpretive machinery (price-adjacent information) and structurally ignore what does not (substrate-level conditions, moral context, ecological consequence). The perception gap is architectural, not informational.

*Supported by:* all 4 sources.

### 2. The corrective is translation infrastructure, not more information

No source proposes that markets would perceive correctly if given more data. All four converge on infrastructure that makes invisible conditions legible to coordination systems:

- **Moral provenance** makes the chain of custody visible and non-deniable, restoring moral agency by re-embedding context in transactions. This is governance through transparency, not prohibition (moral-provenance).
- **Value grammar recoding** expands what markets can hear by producing new grammars that reshape what can be perceived, valued, and coordinated. Design creates legibility for value forms that did not previously exist in economic language (superpower-of-design).
- **Institutional translation** converts ecological, social, and systemic risk into formats that financial systems can recognise as actionable. The gap is a design problem — the translation infrastructure does not yet exist at scale (signals-markets-can-hear).
- **Contract thickening** makes exchange govern its own conditions of possibility explicitly — ecological maintenance, labour standards, institutional resilience — rather than offloading them to invisible substrates (no-such-thing-as-purchase).

What these sources share is a reframing: the corrective to selective market perception is not education or disclosure but designed infrastructure that translates what markets cannot hear into forms they can act on. Spore's provenance commitment, discourse graph, and multi-projection are structural precedents — they make governance knowledge legible. The synthesis suggests extending this principle to economic coordination: make market-relevant conditions legible to the coordination field.

*Supported by:* moral-provenance (provenance infrastructure), superpower-of-design (value grammar recoding), signals-markets-can-hear (translation infrastructure), no-such-thing-as-purchase (contract thickening).

### 3. Transaction must thicken from isolated event to relational commitment

Three sources explicitly describe the thickening of transaction:

- **Multi-layer contracts** govern continuity conditions (ecological, labour, institutional) that thin transactions offload. When holding conditions become fragile, a thin transaction no longer secures what it claims (no-such-thing-as-purchase).
- **Mandatory provenance** attaches the full chain of custody to every exchange, making moral context non-deniable and creating a "natural moral economy" where exploitative goods lose competitive advantage (moral-provenance).
- **New value grammars** re-code what institutions can recognise and what capital can follow, thickening the interpretive machinery available to markets (superpower-of-design).

The signals-markets-can-hear essay completes the picture by diagnosing why thickening is necessary: the distinction between shocks within the system (which markets can metabolise) and stress beneath the system (which markets cannot perceive until materialised). Thickening is the structural response to a perception architecture that systematically ignores what sustains it.

This connects to the human-economy synthesis: transaction thickening into relational commitment is the economic form that the commitment-economy essay describes from the labour side. Both are arguing that economic coordination must shift from contained events to ongoing governed relations.

*Supported by:* no-such-thing-as-purchase (multi-layer contracts), moral-provenance (mandatory provenance), superpower-of-design (grammar recoding), signals-markets-can-hear (within vs beneath).

## What Does Not Converge

No source opposes the concept, but three open questions remain unresolved:

1. **Is moral provenance the durable core concept, or one mechanism inside a broader market-legibility frame?** The bridge note names moral provenance as the anchor concept. The claim sheets describe market perception, value grammar recoding, and contract thickening — broader mechanisms of which provenance is one instance. Whether the family should be anchored on "moral provenance" (a specific infrastructure) or "market legibility" (a systemic property) affects what Spore names and where it targets canon work. The synthesis treats them as facets of the same structural claim but the naming tension is real.

2. **How can markets be made more reality-responsive without collapsing everything into pricing?** The priceability bridge note argues that refusing to price produces invisibility. The signals-markets essay shows that markets can only hear price-adjacent signals. This creates a dilemma: making substrate-level conditions priceable is the translation infrastructure's goal, but the priceability synthesis (not yet written) may argue that pricing itself must be reconstructed as multi-vector coordination rather than scalar equivalence. Whether market-legibility requires better pricing or an alternative to pricing is unresolved.

3. **Where does the boundary lie between market-legibility and priceability as separate families?** Both families address what economic coordination systems can perceive. Market-legibility focuses on what markets actually hear and how to restore what they miss. Priceability focuses on what can become visible to coordination systems and how to reconstruct the grammar of valuation. They overlap substantially — the superpower-of-design and signals-markets claim sheets serve both clusters. Whether they should remain separate families or eventually merge into a single economic-perception concept is unresolved.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate pattern** — Market-legibility-and-moral-provenance names an economic perception pattern: markets are selectively perceptive by architecture, and the corrective is designed translation infrastructure. Four sources converge on the same structural account from different angles (provenance, design, market signals, transaction critique). The pattern extends Spore's existing provenance and transparency principles to economic coordination.

**Promotion status: defer** — The convergence is real (4 sources, 12 supports, 0 opposes) but carries two limitations:

1. **All sources are Johar-derived.** All 4 contributing sources are from the same author (Indy Johar). While they address different facets of market perception from different angles, they share a common discursive tradition. The source count overstates independence.

2. **The naming tension is unresolved.** Whether the durable concept is "moral provenance" (a specific infrastructure mechanism) or "market legibility" (a systemic property) affects how Spore names this pattern. The synthesis treats them as facets of the same claim, but the choice matters for canon placement.

Promotion should wait for either: (a) a non-Johar source that independently names the same structural perception gap, or (b) production evidence that moral provenance or value grammar recoding actually shifts market behaviour at scale.
