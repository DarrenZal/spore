# Human-Machine Complementarity: Convergence Synthesis

A synthesis note on the human-machine-complementarity field family, drawn from the Johar corpus intake (Batches 1–2) and the two pre-existing Johar bridge notes. This is the first cluster to reach synthesis-readiness from the intelligence-and-learning theme.

## Cluster

- **Field family:** human-machine-complementarity
- **Governance clusters:** 7 (spanning `spore.relational-agency-and-holons`, `spore.project-vision`, `spore.governance-artifacts`, `spore.mycelial-holarchy-architecture`, `ic.intelligence-primitives`, `ic.memory-layers`, `ic.project-vision`)
- **Source documents:** 9 distinct (4 bridge notes + 5 claim sheets)
- **Cross-project:** yes (Spore + IC)

- **Synthesis readiness:** READY (convergent)

## Contributing Sources

### Bridge notes (Tier A — full comparative intake)

**spore.connection.johar-word-not-thing** — Humans contribute non-symbolic knowing (embodied, relational, ecological) that machines cannot replicate. The membrane protects sovereignty, but the question of what kind of translation loss different agent types introduce remains open. Stance: clarify.

**spore.connection.johar-neuroplastic-field** — Machines cannot bear consequences, therefore cannot be adaptive agents. Adaptation requires consequence-bearing, and only humans and planetary life bear consequences in the full sense. Candidate resolution: structural agent symmetry at primitive level, asymmetry at membrane/governance pattern level. Stance: clarify.

**spore.connection.johar-brain-self-rewriting-field** — The most detailed account of what the human side actually is: a self-rewriting field with eight coupled architectures (memory/processing inseparability, event-driven cognition, continuous becoming, embodiment/horizon-management, doubt/curiosity/stance-shifting, social recursion, intuition as whole-field inference, recursive context design). The asymmetry is not capability but developmental architecture. Stance: clarify.

**spore.connection.johar-entangled-intelligence** — The structural distinction between separated and coupled architectures: in a coupled architecture, complementarity is not a design preference but a condition for the field's capacity to learn. Separated architectures lose it. Stance: clarify.

### Claim sheets (Tier B — source claims only)

**from-singlepoint-optimisation-to** — LLMs are the first tools capable of scaling conversation rather than broadcast. Machine intelligence requires a civic protocol stack. Tenderness is an operating capacity, not just an ethic.

**three-primitive-tests-of-intelligence** — Intelligence reads as competence across three irreducible objective functions. Partial knowing is the shared substrate. Tenderness and care are operational necessities under partial knowledge.

**the-hidden-costsfutures-of-thinking** — Machine co-creation compresses ambiguity, creating epistemic monoculture. Confident outputs transfer authority away from halting, doubt, and revision. The deepest risk is normalisation of hybrid cognition.

## What Converges

Nine independent sources across two projects point at the same structural claim:

> **Spore's structural agent symmetry at the primitive level is correct and should be preserved. The asymmetry between human and machine agents should be placed at the membrane and governance pattern level.**

The convergence has three layers, each supported by distinct sources:

### 1. The asymmetry is structural, not residual

The human contribution is not a shrinking list of tasks machines cannot yet do. It is a developmental architecture: embodied, socially recursive, self-scaffolding, horizon-managing. This architecture produces doubt, curiosity, stance-shifting, and consequence-bearing as structural features, not psychological preferences.

*Supported by:* word-not-thing (non-symbolic knowing), neuroplastic-field (consequence-bearing), brain-self-rewriting-field (8 architectures), three-primitive-tests (partial knowing as shared substrate).

### 2. Complementarity requires coupling, not separation

The choice is between separated architecture (query → output → consume) and coupled/entangled architecture (human and machine embedded in shared learning systems that are instrumented and governed). Only coupled architectures preserve learning-loop residency. Separation outsources the feedback loops that make the field adaptive.

*Supported by:* entangled-intelligence (separated vs coupled), learning-as-infrastructure (learning as relational infrastructure), hidden-costs (normalisation risk of separation), singlepoint-optimisation (conversation vs broadcast).

### 3. The governance layer carries the asymmetry

Machine agents contribute inference speed, pattern completion, and scale. Human agents contribute horizon-management, consequence-bearing, and the capacity to revise objectives. The membrane and governance patterns — not the primitive holon model — are where this division of labour should be made explicit and inspectable.

*Supported by:* neuroplastic-field R3 (membrane/governance placement), brain-self-rewriting-field R1 (developmental vocabulary for why), entangled-intelligence R1 (coupling as condition for field learning), singlepoint-optimisation C4 (civic protocol stack).

## What Does Not Converge

No source opposes the candidate resolution, but three open questions remain unresolved:

1. **Can a collective holon bear consequences?** The neuroplastic-field note asked whether consequence-bearing is irreducible to individual humans or whether a governance body or commons can bear consequences in a morally relevant sense. The brain-self-rewriting-field essay implies that consequence-bearing is coupled to embodiment, which would make it irreducible. No source directly addresses collective consequence-bearing.

2. **Where does developmental capacity sit in the agent model?** If human agents have developmental architecture (self-rewriting field) and machine agents do not, should the holon model carry a `developmental_capacity` property, or is this better handled as a governance-level capability requirement? The brain essay suggests the former; the entangled-intelligence essay suggests the latter (own context, not own model).

3. **Is cognitive ecology a distinct governance object?** The brain essay introduces "cognitive ecology" as the extended environment that shapes what kinds of minds can emerge. If scaffold ownership determines developmental capacity, then governance must attend to cognitive ecology — not just to individual agents or individual commitments. This may require a pattern-level construct that Spore does not currently have.

## Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §What Converges — layer 1]
Human-machine complementarity is a structural asymmetry in developmental architecture, not a residual capability gap. Nine independent sources across two projects converge on this claim with zero opposition. The asymmetry is between agents capable of horizon-management, stance-shifting, consequence-bearing, and recursive context design (human) and agents optimised for inference speed, pattern completion, and scale (machine). This distinction should be explicit in Spore's governance patterns and IC's intelligence primitives.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §What Converges — layer 3]
The correct architectural placement for complementarity is the membrane/governance layer, not the primitive/holon layer. Spore's structural agent symmetry at the primitive level should be preserved. The asymmetry should be expressed as governance-level requirements: what kinds of agents must be present for specific governance functions (consequence-bearing review, horizon-setting, context design) and what kinds of agents may serve as inference infrastructure.

**C3** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §What Converges — layer 2]
Complementarity requires coupling. Separated architectures (query → output → consume) gradually outsource learning-loop residency, producing institutional dependence on representational infrastructure the institution does not shape. This supports the existing membrane design (LLM proposes, governance decides) but adds a diagnostic criterion: any proposed system change that reduces coupling between human judgement and machine inference should be treated as a learning-loop residency risk.

**R1** [review claim] [target: spore.relational-agency-and-holons] [concept: human-machine-complementarity]
The holon model should be extended with a governance-level distinction between developmental agents (capable of horizon-management, stance-shifting, and consequence-bearing) and inference agents (capable of pattern completion and scale). This distinction should not alter primitive-level structural symmetry. It should be expressed as a governance pattern requirement: specific governance functions (evidence review, commitment acceptance, scope setting) require developmental agent participation.
*R1 is supported by C1, C2.*

## Disposition

**Primary: clarify existing term** — Human-machine complementarity is already present in Spore's grammar as an unresolved tension. Nine sources now converge on the same resolution: structural symmetry at primitive level, developmental asymmetry at membrane/governance level. The synthesis provides the vocabulary and evidence for making this explicit.

**Promotion status: defer** — The convergence is strong (9 sources, 0 opposes, cross-project) but the three open questions (collective consequence-bearing, developmental capacity placement, cognitive ecology as governance object) indicate that while the direction is clear, the implementation design is not yet settled. Promotion should wait for either: (a) production evidence from running governance that tests the developmental/inference distinction, or (b) a design proposal that addresses the open questions.
