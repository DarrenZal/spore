## What is Spore?

Spore is an infrastructure for collective agency — a common grammar for plural, sovereign coordination across scales and scopes. The formal name for the protocol family is Agent Commons. Spore is the project that develops, tests, and publishes it.

Why "Spore"? A spore is portable, generative, and context-sensitive: it moves through larger living networks, lands in a place, and unfolds locally. The same logic applies here — a shared coordination grammar that can be adopted across projects, grow in different forms, and remain interoperable without requiring centralization.

Agent Commons defines patterns and protocols for how agents — humans, AIs, teams, organizations, federations — coordinate without surrendering sovereignty. The "commons" is the shared coordination surface: memory, claims, commitments, discourse, and protocols stewarded in common rather than owned by a central platform.

Coordination at every scale and scope requires the same primitives — sovereign identity, shared memory, commitment protocols, governance patterns, and federation rules. These compose fractally: the same patterns that let a person manage their workflow let a bioregional network coordinate ecological restoration. The goal is collective agency: the situated capacity of agents — individual or collective — to sense, decide, and act meaningfully within the systems that sustain them.

# SECTION 2: THE COORDINATION GRAMMAR (full document)

## The Grammar Thesis

Spore is a common grammar for plural, sovereign coordination across scales. It helps people, agents, and collectives coordinate coherently without flattening difference.

The grammar is not a neutral data model. It encodes commitments across the full worldview stack — ontological (what exists), epistemological (what counts as knowing), axiological (what matters), praxical (how we act), and ethical (how we inhabit participation). This makes it a worldview grammar for plural coordination, not merely a coordination ontology.

The three-layer architecture:

- **Grammar** = primitives, relations, membranes, transitions (the alphabet and syntax)
- **Pattern language** = named recurring arrangements of primitives that metabolize tension across scales (the idioms)
- **Protocols** = explicit operational contracts for parts of patterns that must interoperate between nodes (the scripts)

The grammar is the deep structure. The pattern language makes it legible. The protocols make it reliable.

## The Coordination Loop

```
Sense --> Interpret --> Claim --> Attest --> Intend --> Commit --> Coordinate --> Act --> Revise
```

Every transition in this loop can happen within a single holon (internal coordination) or across a membrane between holons (federated coordination). The membrane is what makes each transition a coordination event, not just a cognitive one.

The loop is not a pipeline. It is a cycle — revision feeds sensing, and multiple iterations run concurrently at different scales and speeds.

## Ten Coordination Primitives

| Primitive | Definition | Worldview Layer |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Holon** | Bounded center of coherence that can participate. Simultaneously a whole and a part. Can be a person, AI agent, team, organization, federation, or mixed collective. | Ontological |
| **Membrane** | Boundary conditions: visibility, consent, jurisdiction, access. Not just a wall — a transformation interface. | Ontological / Praxical |
| **Signal** | Directional cue: needs, offers, alerts, sensor outputs. The raw material of sensing. | Epistemological |
| **Claim** | Proposition about world, system, or relationship. The unit of epistemic coordination. | Epistemological |
| **Evidence** | What bears on a claim. Observations, measurements, deliverables, fulfillment records. | Epistemological |
| **Attestation** | Situated witnessing, endorsement, or dispute. Attestation is itself a claim at a higher level — this recursion gives trust without centralization. | Epistemological / Ethical |
| **Intent** | A declared directional signal rooted in care, need, offer, concern, or possibility — the pre-commitment primitive. Where plurality enters. | Axiological |
| **Commitment** | A promise accepted into a coordination scope with scope-bound accountability. | Axiological |
| **Artifact** | Durable memory surface: vision, spec, agreement, note, pool configuration, proposal, token. | Ontological / Praxical |
| **Event** | Something that happened in time. State changes, domain events, settlement events. The temporal primitive. | Ontological |

**Self-similarity:** A holon at one scale is a coherent point; at another, it is a graph of these same primitives. A team is a single node in a federation, and a graph of roles, commitments, intents, and evidence internally. This recursion is why the same grammar works from personal workflow to planetary federation.

## Seven Membrane Operations

| Operation | What it does |
|-----------|-------------|
| **expose** | Make something visible across a membrane |
| **translate** | Re-encode for a different context |
| **authorize** | Grant crossing rights |
| **attest** | Witness or endorse across boundary |
| **contest** | Challenge across boundary |
| **revoke** | Withdraw authorization |
| **fork** | Create a new autonomous branch |

Every membrane operation is governed. Authorization has scope. Revocation is always possible. Consent is structural, not decorative.

## Eight Graph Projections

Multiple views over one coordination ecology — not separate databases but projections of the same living system:

1. **Constitutional graph** — Visions, values, principles, constraints, domains
2. **Roadmap DAG** — Initiatives, milestones, dependencies, sequencing
3. **Epistemic graph** — Entities, claims, evidence, attestations, provenance
4. **Intent hypergraph** — Needs, offers, intents, conditions, thresholds
5. **Commitment graph** — Promises, pools, agreements, roles, obligations, fulfillment
6. **Event graph** — State changes through time, provenance chains
7. **Flow graph** — Capital, information, attention moving through system
8. **Discourse graph** — Questions, proposals, arguments, objections, decisions

## Seven Constitutional Commitments

Not eternal truths, but chosen design commitments. Together they optimize for expanding viable continuation sets — keeping more good futures reachable under novelty:

- **Provenance** — shared memory is unreliable without it
- **Forkability** — sovereignty is fake without it
- **Pluralism with interoperability** — without it, this becomes one more centralizing platform
- **Meaningful local autonomy** — authority and boundary must be explicit
- **Authorized boundary crossing** — boundary crossing requires legitimate authorization
- **Explicit and reviewable authority** — authority should have scope, legibility, and revocation conditions
- **Contestability** — claims can be questioned, decisions reviewed, outputs challenged

## Containment and Connection

The holonic axis organizes containment, authority, and nested integrity. The network axis organizes overlap, cross-cutting participation, and lateral reach. The result is a semilattice, not a tree: holons participate in multiple overlapping wholes simultaneously, because living systems cannot be captured by a single clean hierarchy (Alexander, "A City is Not a Tree").

- **Invariants** give containment (constitutional commitments, vision, governance memory)
- **Protocols** shape connection (how intents travel across membranes, how commitments settle)
- **Patterns** mediate between them (recurring forms that let connection happen without collapse of containment)

Containment without overlap is dead — legible on paper, lifeless in use. Overlap without containment is noise.

## How Adoption Works

You don't restructure your project. You add coordination surfaces:

- **Frontmatter** on your docs — they become legible to the governance-memory pattern
- **A sensor node** on your knowledge garden — your existing content becomes queryable by agents
- **Edge permissions** — you declare what you share and with whom
- **Profile declarations** — you state which patterns and protocols you implement

Adoption is incremental and reversible. A project can use one pattern without adopting the full stack. Spore is designed for coexistence with legacy systems, not total rupture.

# SECTION 3: COMMITMENT POOLING — THE ECONOMIC PATTERN (full document)

## Context

Multiple agents have activated intents — offers and needs that have been matched and governance-approved. These are no longer hypothetical: someone has said "I will do X" and governance has confirmed the alignment. Now these commitments need to be coordinated, tracked, and fulfilled collectively. But there is no central coordinator, no single project manager, no hierarchy to impose sequencing.

## Problem

How do you coordinate many commitments from many agents without centralizing control?

## Pattern: Pool as Field

A pool is a shared field where many vectors can be composed. Needs (pull vectors) and offers (push vectors) from multiple agents enter the pool. Routing and scoring compute field-level alignment rather than pairwise matching — the question is not "does A match B?" but "how do all the forces in this pool compose into coherent flow?"

### Lifecycle

```
PROPOSED --> VERIFIED --> ACTIVE --> EVIDENCE_LINKED --> REDEEMED
                                          |
                                     DISPUTED --> RESOLVED
```

### Pool Federation

Pools can federate — a local pool (e.g., Victoria Landscape Hub) can route overflow to a broader pool (e.g., Cascadia Bioregion Stewardship). Each pool retains sovereignty. Routing is advisory — the receiving pool's governance decides whether to accept.

### Settlement

Settlement can take multiple forms: in-kind fulfillment, token settlement (commitment vouchers), or hybrid. Demurrage (configurable decay) encourages circulation — vouchers lose value over time, discouraging hoarding and encouraging flow.

### Current Implementations

- **BKC Victoria + Cascadia pools**: 2 pools activated, 23+ commitments tracked, routing visualization live
- **VCV on Celo**: Victoria Commitment Vouchers on Celo mainnet (33,400 VCV minted)
- **Grassroots Economics / CLC protocol**: Community Inclusion Currencies and the commitment voucher framework developed by Will Ruddick

# SECTION 4: MYCORRHIZAL FEDERATION PROTOCOL (full document)

How sovereign nodes connect through mutualistic exchange — the rules governing what flows between agents, holons, and collectives, how trust is established, and how federation maintains coherence without central authority.

## What Counts as a Node

Any sufficiently coherent sovereign party participating in federation: a person with local tools and memory, a team or working group, an organization, a federation or network-of-networks, a software-hosted agent acting on behalf of one of the above.

## Sovereignty Invariants

These hold at every scale and are non-negotiable:

1. **Each node maintains sovereign authority over its own knowledge representation.** No external agent can write to a node's authoritative data without explicit consent. Data arrives as proposals, not commands.
2. **Sharing is opt-in.** A node chooses what to share, with whom, and under what conditions. There is no global read access.
3. **Identity is self-sovereign.** Each node generates and controls its own cryptographic identity.
4. **Local-first operation.** Every node must function fully when disconnected. Federation enhances; it does not enable.

## What Flows Between Nodes

Federation exchanges domain events — structured notifications that a node has created, updated, or retracted a piece of knowledge. Events are idempotent, carry provenance, are scoped, and may be encrypted.

## Trust Model

Trust is established progressively between sovereign parties. Currently bilateral: out-of-band introduction, edge proposal with deferred approval, SAS verification over trusted channel, mutual approval, ongoing event flow with either peer able to revoke at any time. Future: transitive trust, organizational endorsement, reputation from event history.

## Relationship to the Holonic Network

Federation is primarily network — it creates lateral connections between sovereign nodes. But it respects holonic structure: federation edges do not bypass governance boundaries, shared entities flow through federation but governance relationships do not, cross-project references are informational not authoritative.

# SECTION 5: HYPERSTITION AS COORDINATION (full document)

What if the stories we tell about the future are not decorations on top of coordination infrastructure — but the infrastructure itself? That is the thesis behind hyperstition: fictions that make themselves real through collective belief and action.

Kevin Owocki's Gitcoin research article makes the case that onchain mechanisms — DAOs, bonding curves, conviction voting — are precisely this: shared narratives encoded into executable coordination. But it leaves a structural question: what makes a hyperstition constructive rather than extractive?

## Commitment Pooling as Non-Extractive Hyperstition Market

Both bonding curves and commitment pooling coordinate collective resource-locking toward shared objectives. Both encode escalating conviction. But they use different signals:

- **Bonding curves** encode conviction via price dynamics — creating speculative incentives
- **Commitment pooling** encodes conviction via scope-bound accountability — each stage more visible, more accountable, more operative

The substitution matters. Speculative price dynamics create incentives for exit-driven participation. Scope-bound accountability creates incentives for follow-through participation.

## Intent Pressure as Coincidence Intensification

Intent pressure is the gap between the normative frontier (what visions say should be true) and the epistemic frontier (what sensors and evidence report is true). When these diverge, the system feels pressure. That pressure is not a failure state — it is the engine of coordination.

The structural parallel with hyperstition's self-fulfilling dynamic: in both cases, a declared desired future creates force on the present. Spore makes this force legible and governable.

## The Constructive Distinction

Spore's constitutional commitments are candidate criteria for what makes a shared narrative safe to encode into infrastructure:

- **Provenance**: Can you trace where the claims come from?
- **Forkability**: Can you leave and take your work with you?
- **Contestability**: Can you object from within?
- **Meaningful local autonomy**: Can you participate partially?
- **Authorized boundary crossing**: Does the narrative cross boundaries with consent?
- **Reviewable authority**: Who decides what it means, and can that be challenged?

A constructive hyperstition passes these tests. An extractive one fails them.

# SECTION 6: DISCOURSE AS GOVERNANCE (full document)

Structured reasoning as the self-reflective layer by which a coordination ecology governs its own evolution.

## Problem

How does a coordination ecology govern its own evolution through structured reasoning rather than ad-hoc conversation?

## Pattern

The discourse graph is the 8th graph projection — the self-reflective layer. It is composed entirely of existing coordination primitives:

| Discourse element | Grammar primitive |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Proposal** | Claim (about what should change) |
| **Argument** | Attestation (endorsement or dispute) |
| **Evidence** | Evidence (what bears on the proposal) |
| **Decision** | Artifact (durable record of resolution) |
| **Question** | Signal (something is unresolved) |

No new primitives are needed. The discourse graph fills the Revise phase of the coordination loop — where the ecology asks "what worked?", "what should change?", and "on what grounds?"

# SECTION 7: THE BIOREGIONAL KNOWLEDGE COMMONS (BKC) — PRODUCTION PROVING GROUND

## What BKC Is

The Bioregional Knowledge Commons is federated infrastructure for bioregional knowledge sovereignty. It enables communities to maintain their own knowledge gardens — structured collections of entities, relationships, evidence, and commitments — and selectively share across bioregional boundaries without centralizing control.

## Three-Plane Architecture

| Plane | Function | Examples |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **Knowledge** | Structured memory, evidence, provenance | BKC (KOI protocol, knowledge graphs, entity resolution) |
| **Capital** | Resource flows, commitment pooling, redistribution | GE integration, TBFF, commitment vouchers |
| **Coordination** | Real-time agent collaboration, task routing | A2A protocol, agent swarms |

## Why Federation, Not Aggregation

Bioregions are nested. The Greater Victoria landscape group sits within the Salish Sea bioregion, which sits within Cascadia. Knowledge commons should mirror this structure — each node sovereign, each connection chosen. The alternative — a central knowledge graph — violates the principle that communities should control what they share, with whom, and under what terms.

This is the cosmolocal principle applied to knowledge infrastructure: light protocols and ontologies shared globally, heavy relationships and governance held locally.

## Design Principles

1. **Protocol, not platform.** Three invariants — what is shared, who attests, who can use it and how — without prescribing any particular transport.
2. **Sovereignty-preserving federation.** Each node runs its own deployment. Edge-approval governance means nodes explicitly authorize which peers can connect.
3. **Consent-first data flow.** Visibility scoping (public, authorized, node_private) enforced at the query layer.
4. **Bounded authority.** The same pattern at every layer: steward approval for knowledge, multisig review for capital, evidence-based verification for commitments.
5. **Living system economics.** Four metabolic functions: provisioning (pooling), redistribution (TBFF), activation (community practices), repair (dispute resolution, forkability, sufficiency floor).
6. **Holonic structure.** Nodes nest: leaf nodes within coordinators within meta-coordinators. Each level has appropriate scope.

## What BKC Is Not

- Not a blockchain (on-chain anchoring provides proof of existence; the knowledge graph lives in PostgreSQL)
- Not a social network
- Not a centralized platform
- Not a universal ontology (25 types, 39 predicates — one reference profile among potentially many)

## Current State

- 4 federated nodes (Salish Sea, Greater Victoria, Cowichan Valley, Front Range)
- 1,000+ entities across nodes
- 2 commitment pools activated (Victoria Landscape Hub, Cascadia Bioregion Stewardship)
- 23+ commitments, 33,400 VCV minted on Celo mainnet
- Hybrid retrieval (BM25 + RRF + FlashRank cross-encoder reranking)
- Full bidirectional KOI-net federation

# SECTION 9: THE LEARNING MEMBRANE — HOW SPORE LEARNS FROM THE ECOSYSTEM

Spore needs to learn from the wider ecosystem. Through a learning membrane, it ingests outside works, translates them into bridge notes and comparative claims, and selectively promotes what proves useful into canon — without flattening difference.

The workflow is comparative intake: external frameworks, papers, repos, and protocols enter as bridge notes with bilateral claims linking them to Spore concepts. Each synthesis records a disposition:

- **no change** — Spore already has this
- **clarify existing term** — external work names something Spore does but hasn't articulated
- **candidate pattern** — external work suggests a new pattern
- **candidate protocol** — external work suggests a new protocol
- **candidate primitive** — external work suggests a new primitive (rare, high bar)
- **unresolved tension** — productive disagreement to preserve

LLMs and embeddings propose mappings; governance accepts, contests, revises, or rejects them. The durable records are the bridge notes, claims, and dispositions — not the inference that produced them.

# SECTION 11: BRIDGE NOTE — JOHAR "THE WORD IS NOT THE THING"

Indy Johar argues that language introduces a structural risk: the word becomes a "stopping rule" that terminates inquiry, substitutes recognition for perception, and severs contact with relational reality. Under conditions of accelerating change, this risk becomes critical.

**What this offers Spore:**

1. **Linguistic closure** — when a name terminates inquiry. Spore's claim lifecycle prevents this structurally, but the concept makes the design rationale explicit. "Holon" is a compression. "Membrane" is a compression. The grammar must guard against its own terms becoming stopping rules.

2. **Sufficiency error** — when compression is mistaken for comprehension. Intent pressure measures the gap, but what if the epistemic frontier itself feels sufficient?

3. **Human-machine complementarity** — machines extend the symbolic layer (graph composition, routing, pattern matching); humans anchor the system in what exceeds representation (embodied perception, relational awareness, felt wrongness). The coordination ecology works best when designed for this complementarity.

4. **Context as relation** — context is not data to be preserved but a relational field to be participated in. The knowledge graph is not a database of facts but a relational field.

# SECTION 12: DEEP RESEARCH SYNTHESIS — KEY FINDINGS FROM TWO INDEPENDENT REPORTS

Two independent deep research reports (one strategy-focused via ChatGPT Deep Research, one academic-focused via Claude/Compass) were run against the full Spore corpus plus sibling projects. Their convergence is striking:

## Core Thesis — Both Reports Agree

Spore is already structurally sound as a governance-memory and trust layer. The grammar holds up against external scrutiny. The urgent problem is not more theory — it is adoption, executable artifacts, and filling three specific gaps: actor governance, federation with independent teams, and temporal dynamics.

## Seven High-Confidence Convergences

1. **Tier-0 Adoption Kit is top priority** — docs-only, no Postgres required, external team can run it in their repo today
2. **Discourse-as-governance needs formalization** — proposal/decision doc kinds + lifecycle checker
3. **Claims/Evidence/Attestation should align with W3C Verifiable Credentials** — don't invent a credential envelope, map onto VC standard
4. **Federation requires a bilateral pilot with an independent team** — OpenCivics as best-aligned partner
5. **MCP as default integration layer** — Spore provides governance semantics MCP lacks
6. **Cambria-style lenses for translation** — reversible, testable translation manifests
7. **Temporal dynamics are the biggest theoretical gap** — trust decay, phase transitions, renewal protocols

## Three Design Tensions

1. **Formal foundations — how much, how soon?** The Poly/lens formalization is "the single highest-value formal contribution" but shouldn't delay adoption.
2. **Geographic concentration vs. multi-node federation** — 4 nodes is fragile; concentrate activation energy on one for density.
3. **Viability signals — feature or risk?** Viability dashboards can become "post-hoc story generators" if not tied to specific governance actions.

## Key Formal Findings

- **Membranes are lenses in Poly** (Spivak & Niu, 2024). The inside/outside face maps exactly to get/put. Fractal nesting formalizes as composition product. This is the mathematical backbone.
- **Sheaf-theoretic consistency radius** quantifies federation health. H1 cohomology detects structural obstacles invisible to pointwise checks.
- **Session types guarantee deadlock-free membrane crossings** (Wadler's theorem). Fork naturally modeled by pi-calculus name-passing.
- **CTL (not LTL) is required for forkability** — "it is always possible to fork" requires branching-time reasoning.
- **Three-tier renewal protocol**: operational (6mo), governance (18mo), constitutional (36mo) — no perpetual commitments.
- **Sen's capability approach closes Gap 8** — formal rights vs. practical accessibility.

## Key Strategic Findings

- **The activation problem is P0** — 560+ entities across 4 nodes is NOT past critical mass; network effects require interaction density, not entity count
- **"Come for the tool, stay for the network"** — provide standalone value before network effects
- **Terms like "membrane" and "commitment pooling" are confusing to external audiences** — need adoption-facing surface language
- **Three unresolvable tensions**: grammar normativity paradox, fork-pluralism trade-off, reflexivity limit

## Unique Academic Findings

- **Mycorrhizal metaphor needs recalibration** — Karst et al. 2023 meta-analysis challenges "Mother Tree" claims; keep the metaphor but revise framing toward infrastructure provision and mutualism-parasitism continuum
- **Young's critique is fundamental** — the grammar IS a set of communication norms that privileges certain expression modes; cannot be "solved," only continuously addressed
- **Epistemic justice analysis (Fricker)** — if the grammar defines what can be expressed, it defines who can participate fully; Grammar Extension Protocol needed
- **Graduated AI agency model**: Instrument -> Delegate -> Associate (AI should NOT be full sovereign holons in current design)
- **Bilateral conviction matching** — extending conviction voting to two-sided commitment markets; novel mechanism with no published precedent
- **Hypercerts bridge** — fulfilled + attested commitments create proto-impact-certificates interoperable with public goods funding ecosystem
- **Stigmergy as explicit layer** — Event Graph IS a stigmergic medium but lacks trace decay; without salience decay, noise accumulates indefinitely
- **Two Row Wampum** — parallel coexistence without convergence as sovereignty model stronger than current federation

# SECTION 16: THE COORDINATION STACK — FROM LOCAL WORKSHOP TO BIOREGIONAL INTELLIGENCE (full BKC document)

This is the narrative overview of how the pieces compose — from a single mapping workshop to federated commitment pools, threshold-based capital flows, and AI-navigable bioregional knowledge.

## The Thread

Start with one person in a room in Victoria.

It's a spring morning, maybe May 2026. Fifteen people are sitting in a circle in a community hall in the Greater Victoria landscape — watershed stewards, food systems organizers, Indigenous land defenders, restoration practitioners, community gardeners. They've been invited to a bioregional mapping workshop. The facilitator asks:

*"What can you offer to this community's regeneration work? And what do you need that you can't currently get?"*

One person says: "I can offer twenty hours of Garry Oak habitat restoration — I have the tools and I know the sites."

Another says: "I need someone who understands riparian restoration in the Saanich Peninsula. I have the land but not the knowledge."

These two people may or may not find each other in the room. But they've just made the most important move in a coordination system that extends far beyond this workshop.

## Layer 1: The Workshop as Sensing

The mapping workshop is where the landscape looks at itself. Five parts unfold across three or four hours:

- **Who's here** — every person, organization, project, and place gets named
- **What's happening** — the group maps the landscape: what exists, what's connected, where the gaps are
- **What matters most** — the gap between where we are and where we want to be becomes visible
- **Offers and needs** — each person articulates what they can contribute and what they require
- **Commitments** — concrete pledges, as specific as possible

This is a sensing event. The landscape becomes aware of itself through the people who inhabit and care for it.

## Layer 2: The Knowledge Graph as Memory

After the workshop, the recording goes through a structured pipeline:

1. Local transcription (no cloud upload)
2. Human review: stewards structure the outputs — entities, relationships, offers, needs, commitments. Nothing enters the knowledge base without review.
3. Approved items enter the knowledge graph

The graph holds: people, organizations, locations, offers, needs, and commitments with lifecycle states (PROPOSED → VERIFIED → ACTIVE → FULFILLED → EVIDENCE). This graph lives in the Greater Victoria node — one leaf node in the Bioregional Knowledge Commons network. It is sovereign to Victoria.

## Layer 3: The Commitment Pool as Circulation

A commitment pool is a shared field where individual offers and needs compose into collective capacity. Think of it like a cooperative: individually, a promise of 20 hours of Garry Oak restoration is just a note. Pooled with fifteen other promises — watershed monitoring, food systems knowledge, equipment loans, facilitation skills, seed saving, grant writing — it becomes something the community can draw on, route, and redeem.

Each offer becomes a voucher with lifecycle states. Vouchers are verified by the community. When redeemed, they generate evidence of fulfillment. The evidence closes the loop.

Will Ruddick of Grassroots Economics: "You can think of the long tail of pooling being that we all express our agreements and the network of overlapping pools is the fully intangible un-ownable commons."

When enough communities do this, the overlapping network of pools becomes the commons itself.

## Layer 4: Flow Funding as Redistribution

Commitment pools handle in-kind exchange. But some needs require capital.

### Hub Cultivator — Trust-Based Stewardship

Regenerate Cascadia's model. A trusted steward receives a capital allocation. Their decisions are informed by the mapping workshop: which needs are unmet? Which offers could be activated with small capital injection? Every decision is documented and feeds back into the knowledge graph as evidence. The loop closes: need → commitment → capital → work → outcome → evidence → learning.

Kinship Earth's four reflective questions govern reporting: What inspired you? What surprised you? What challenged you? What moved you? Narrative, not metrics. Trust, not compliance.

### TBFF — Threshold-Based Algorithmic Redistribution

The Mycopunks / MycoFi model. Each participant sets a needs threshold and a comfort level. When capital flows above comfort, excess auto-routes to those below threshold. The algorithm iterates until equilibrium. Capital flows like water through a watershed.

These are not competing mechanisms. Hub Cultivator operates through human steward judgment at landscape scale on seasonal cycles. TBFF operates algorithmically at network scale continuously.

## Layer 5: AI Agents as the Nervous System

The Octo AI agent (Salish Sea coordinator) can answer: "What needs does Cowichan Valley have that match what Victoria offered?" These are graph traversals across federated nodes. The agents also assist with transcript processing, entity extraction, and relationship identification.

Critical separation of concerns: the agents don't own the knowledge. The communities do. Every entity was put there by a human who reviewed it. Every commitment was verified by a community.

## Layer 6: Federation as Bioregional Structure

```
Greater Victoria (leaf node)
  └── Salish Sea (coordinator node)
        └── Cascadia (meta-coordinator)
              └── Turtle Island (network-of-networks)
```

Each node runs its own knowledge graph, controls its own consent and visibility, decides which peers it federates with, and signs events cryptographically. What travels between nodes is signals, not data dumps.

Cosmolocal principle in practice: light protocols shared globally, heavy relationships held locally.

## Layer 7: Bioregional Finance as the Capital Layer

Bioregional Financing Facilities (BFFs) are the connective tissue between the global capital system and ground-level regenerators. Four types:

| Facility | Function | Instruments |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Bioregional Trust** | Catalytic grants and eco-credits | Grants, eco-credits, bioregional tithing |
| **Venture Studio** | Incubation of bioregional enterprises | Equity, patient capital, technical assistance |
| **Investment Company** | Systemic capital at landscape scale | Regeneration bonds, systemic funds |
| **Bioregional Bank** | Lending, mutual credit, currency issuance | Lending, complementary currency, obligation clearing |

All measured against 4 Returns: Inspiration (cultural vitality), Ecological (ecosystem health), Social (community wellbeing), and Economic/Financial.

The most radical version: nature-based currency — value tracks ecosystem health indicators. Salmon counts, river flow rates, forest carbon density, species richness. A healthy watershed literally increases the money supply.

## The Convergence Point: TIBC

The Turtle Island Bioregional Congress (September 14–19, 2026, Vernonia OR) — the first in 17 years — is where the distributed work becomes a shared visible landscape. Every LHC landscape group runs a mapping workshop by July 2026. At TIBC, it becomes possible to see Cascadia's landscape groups in aggregate.

## What This Is

This is a coordination stack — composable, sovereignty-preserving layers:

| Layer | What it does | Governed by |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Mapping workshop | Sensing — landscape becomes aware of itself | Community, facilitated by stewards |
| Knowledge graph | Memory — structured, consent-gated, sovereign | Local node stewards |

And the Bioregional Bank that one day issues a nature-based currency pegged to salmon abundance in the Salish Sea begins with one person in a room in Victoria saying: "I can offer twenty hours of Garry Oak habitat restoration."

# SECTION 17: CIVIC INFRASTRUCTURE AND THE COORDINATION GRAMMAR (full Spore positioning document)

There is a question that keeps surfacing across very different communities — bioregional organizers, protocol designers, knowledge commoners, civic technologists: how do you build shared infrastructure that coordinates without centralizing, and that cannot be captured?

## What Civic Infrastructure Builders Keep Asking For

The Open Civics thesis articulates requirements echoed across civic communities:

**Self-correcting feedback.** Systems that can sense when they're failing and adjust. Not dashboards watched by administrators, but structural feedback loops embedded in the coordination itself.

**Aligned incentives.** Participants benefit from cooperation rather than defection — not because a centralized authority enforces it, but because the coordination structure makes follow-through more rewarding than exit.

**Non-enclosability.** Protocols, patterns, and infrastructure that cannot be captured by private interests. If someone builds a wall around it, participants can fork and rebuild without losing the pattern.

**Boundary crossing with consent.** Different organizations, communities, and knowledge traditions need to exchange without either surrendering sovereignty or building walled gardens.

**Pluralism.** Multiple worldviews, ontologies, governance structures, and ways of knowing must coexist in the same system without being flattened into a single schema.

These are hard requirements. Most coordination infrastructure satisfies one or two while violating others. Platforms solve boundary crossing but capture the participants. Blockchains solve non-enclosability but struggle with pluralism. Federated systems preserve sovereignty but often sacrifice coherence.

## What We've Found Works

### Self-correcting feedback → the coordination loop

The grammar describes a coordination ecology that cycles through phases: sense → interpret → claim → attest → intend → commit → coordinate → act → revise. This is not a pipeline — it is a loop. The self-correcting capacity is structural, not bolted on.

### Aligned incentives → commitment pooling

Commitment pooling coordinates many commitments from many agents without centralizing control. The BKC operates two commitment pools on Celo mainnet: 23+ commitments, 33,400 VCV minted.

### Non-enclosability → forkability + contestability

Two constitutional commitments compose to produce non-enclosability. Forkability means participants can exit and take their contribution. Contestability means claims can be questioned, decisions reviewed.

### Boundary crossing → membrane operations

Seven operations govern what moves across boundaries: expose, translate, authorize, attest, contest, revoke, fork. Every membrane operation is governed. Consent is structural, not decorative.

Open Civics names "extitutions" — self-organizing frameworks with permeable boundaries. In the coordination grammar, an extitution is a holon with explicit membrane operations. The grammar makes extitutions buildable, not just describable.

### Federation without centralization → sovereignty invariants

Four sovereignty invariants: sovereign authority over knowledge, opt-in sharing, self-sovereign identity, local-first operation. Federation enhances; it does not enable.

### Pluralism → worldview grammar

Five explicit layers: ontological (what exists), epistemological (what counts as knowing), axiological (what matters), praxical (how we act), ethical (how we inhabit participation). Different communities answer these differently, and the grammar supports translation between positions rather than flattening them.

## Health and Viability

Open Civics' three health indicators map naturally onto viability signals:

- **Resilience** — repair debt, ecological thresholds
- **Choice** — forkability exercised, authorization diversity
- **Vitality** — intent pressure, unused capacity

The civic framing provides a legible organizing layer over these more granular signals.

## What We Don't Have Yet

- **Activation** — how new participants join and find their way in
- **Multi-level holonic nesting** beyond two levels
- **External adoption** by an independent team
- **Continuous conviction signals** for commitment pooling

These are not fatal gaps. They are the work ahead.

## What This Means

For civic infrastructure builders, the primitives exist. The coordination problems that civic communities face — federation without centralization, non-enclosable shared infrastructure, boundary crossing with consent, pluralism without fragmentation — have structural responses, not just principles.

The door is open for convergence — between OCIF and Agent Commons, between civic and bioregional and personal coordination, between any communities willing to do the translation work across membranes.
