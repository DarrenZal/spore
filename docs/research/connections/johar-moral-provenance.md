# Johar — Relinking Value: How Rebuilding Moral Provenance Can Transform Our Markets

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Relinking Value: How Rebuilding Moral Provenance Can Transform Our Markets," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/relinking-value-how-rebuilding-moral

## What It Is

An essay (2731w) arguing that modern economic systems are structurally designed to sever the moral context of transactions. Products are divorced from their social and environmental backstory — what Polanyi called "disembedding" economic transactions from their moral contexts. The essay proposes moral provenance as infrastructure: mandatory, non-destructible records (blockchain, digital identifiers, real-time tracking) that make the full chain of custody visible. By relinking transactions to their origins, provenance restores moral agency to economic actors and creates a "natural moral economy" where exploitative goods lose their market advantage. Inspired by J.C. Kumarappa's *Economy of Permanence*.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Provenance as constitutional commitment:** Spore's first constitutional commitment is provenance — shared memory is unreliable without it. Claims carry provenance, evidence chains, and attribution. The essay extends this principle to economic transactions: market memory is unreliable without provenance, and what is invisible in the chain of custody is effectively licensed.

2. **Transparency through discourse graph:** Spore's discourse graph makes the state of knowledge legible — who claimed what, with what evidence, subject to what review. The essay proposes the same transparency architecture for supply chains: who produced what, under what conditions, with what environmental and social consequences.

3. **Contestability as governance mechanism:** Spore's commitment to contestability (claims can be questioned, decisions reviewed, outputs challenged) parallels the essay's argument that provenance enables contestation. When the chain of custody is visible, consumers, regulators, and civil society can contest exploitative practices that were previously hidden.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Markets as Amoral by Architecture, Not by Nature

The essay's diagnostic: economic systems are amoral enablers not because humans don't care about harm but because transaction mechanisms encourage ignorance. Products are abstracted from their origins — "commodity fetishism" makes it easy to ignore exploitative or destructive practices. People hold ethical standards in one sphere of life but purchase goods in another sphere devoid of that context. The problem is structural: the absence of moral information in the transaction architecture.

For Spore, this connects to the civic-truth synthesis: just as civic truth requires infrastructure for making knowledge legible, markets require infrastructure for making moral context legible. The invisible is uncontested; the uncontested is structurally licensed.

### 2. Moral Provenance as Re-embedding Infrastructure

The essay proposes provenance — the documented history of ownership, handling, and transformation of a product from raw material to final sale — as the mechanism for re-embedding moral context in economic transactions. Key properties: non-destructible records (tamper-resistant ledgers), unique identifiers (QR codes, RFID, digital tokens), verifiable credentials (third-party attestation), and layered additive records that accumulate evidence across the chain.

For Spore, this is structurally the same architecture as the claim lifecycle: claims carry provenance, evidence accumulates through attestation, and the governance layer can query the full history. The essay applies this pattern to economic goods rather than knowledge claims — but the infrastructure is the same.

### 3. Mandatory Provenance as Market Transformation

The essay's policy proposal: if provenance is mandatory rather than voluntary, exploitative goods can no longer compete on ignorance. The market transforms not through regulation of outcomes but through regulation of information architecture — making the backstory visible so that existing moral preferences can actually influence purchasing decisions. This is governance through transparency, not through prohibition.

For Spore, this echoes the informational-duty concept from governance: the duty not to pollute the epistemic commons. The essay proposes an economic equivalent — the duty not to obscure the moral provenance of goods. Both argue that information architecture is governance infrastructure, not just a reporting convenience.

## Open Questions

1. **Is moral provenance a Spore concept or an application of Spore principles?** The essay proposes provenance infrastructure for supply chains. Spore already has provenance as a constitutional commitment for knowledge claims. Whether "moral provenance" is a new concept that extends Spore's vocabulary or an external application that validates Spore's existing provenance principle is unresolved.

2. **How does mandatory provenance avoid the informational-duty boundary problem?** The civic-truth synthesis's Open Question 3 asked who governs the boundaries of the epistemic commons without recreating monopoly. The same question applies to moral provenance: who determines what counts as adequate provenance, what level of detail is required, and who audits the auditors? Mandatory provenance requires governance of the provenance infrastructure itself — a meta-governance problem.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate pattern** — Moral provenance names a market-transformation pattern that applies Spore's provenance principles to economic transactions. The structural parallel is direct: provenance is already Spore's first constitutional commitment; the essay extends it to supply chains and economic goods. The pattern is larger than a lexicon term — it describes infrastructure for market transformation through transparency.

**Promotion status: defer** — Single-author source. The essay draws heavily on Karl Polanyi and J.C. Kumarappa, which provides theoretical grounding beyond Johar. But the specific infrastructure proposal (blockchain-based mandatory provenance) is speculative and untested at scale. Promotion should wait for convergence from another source or production evidence from a working provenance system.
