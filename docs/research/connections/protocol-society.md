# Protocol Society

This bridge note grounds the seed observation from `spore.connection.p2p-wiki-field-scan` — that "Protocol Society" is the cleanest external cousin for framing Spore as a protocol substrate for collective agency — against the P2P Foundation wiki's primary source on the protocol-society concept (Nathan Schneider), the Bauwens/Borders debate on P2P governance and digital contracts, and Jessop's typology of governance modalities. It is one of three Tier 1b anchor bridge notes (alongside `boundary-commoning` and the cross-project Intelligence Commons and Poietic Match anchors) exercising the wiki-RID-in-anchor metadata contract.

## 1. What "Protocol Society" names

Nathan Schneider introduces "protocol society" as an invitation to see social structures through the lens of the protocol — "a set of voluntary rules governing communication and conduct." His canonical examples span monastic rules, cross-border financial systems, diplomatic rituals, Internet standards, and blockchain contracts. The concept carries three claims that matter for Spore:

1. **Protocols coexist with states and markets, and can be an enabling substrate for them, but are not reducible to them.** Protocol-based social ordering is a third mode of coordination, not a subset of hierarchy or exchange.
2. **Recognizing protocol-based social ordering reveals underappreciated aspects of historical and present societies.** The frame is diagnostic as well as generative.
3. **Protocols can be the basis for imagining social futures less dependent on enforcement through state violence.** This is the normative edge of the concept — and the edge most at risk of naive reading.

## 2. What Spore's grammar already does

Spore's mycelial-holarchy architecture, mycorrhizal federation protocol, and field/membrane vocabulary already perform the structural work Schneider's "protocol society" names: voluntary rule systems coordinating bounded actors, rule-making visible to participants, protocols as the substrate composing hierarchies and markets rather than replacing them wholesale. The wiki prior art provides external language with published lineage for this substrate framing — language audiences in the commons, cryptoeconomic, and governance traditions already recognize.

Adopting "protocol society" as an external framing does not replace Spore's internal vocabulary. It gives Spore a commons-and-protocol-audience-legible translation of what the grammar already does, while importing three disciplines from the source tradition: the coexistence-not-replacement stance, the typology-of-governance-modalities stance (protocols as one mode among several), and the power-capture vigilance the source tradition explicitly carries.

## 3. The typology discipline Spore should import

Bob Jessop's typology of governance modalities (via the Ronfeldt commentary in the wiki) names three distinct coordination modes — exchange (markets), command (hierarchies), and dialogue (networks) — each with its own spatial-temporal horizon and characteristic system-failure modes. Protocol-based coordination sits closest to the dialogue/network column in Jessop's scheme, but the typology's discipline matters more than any single column placement: treating the modalities as distinct, composable, and each failure-prone in characteristic ways rebuts the flattened framing in which "decentralized" or "protocol-based" simply means "better than centralized." Each modality fails; Spore should name what protocol-mode failure looks like and how composition with the other modalities is meant to work.

This is a stronger discipline than Schneider's framing alone provides. Schneider names that protocols coexist with states and markets and can be "an enabling substrate for those structures" but does not specify the failure modes of the protocol modality itself. Jessop's typology supplies the missing failure analysis, which Spore should adopt as part of the protocol-society framing rather than carrying the concept in its Schneider-only form.

## 4. Two stances from the Bauwens/Borders debate Spore must keep distinct

The Bauwens/Borders podcast (hosted by Niklas Anziger) captures a genuine split inside the protocol-society adjacent discourse. Bauwens's commons-governance stance treats community governance (not market-only or state-only mechanisms) as the distinctive feature of commons; he is "critical of both the market and state-led suppressions of local governance solutions to commons problems," while treating proprietarian/crypto solutions as potentially enabling for commons governance. Borders's stance is more proprietarian/libertarian: "proprietarian / libertarian solutions are meant to enable pluralism" — a feature, not a bug. Both positions use protocol-society language; they arrive at different governance commitments.

Spore's grammar should explicitly state which stance it is adopting — or clarify that it composes with elements of both without collapsing the distinction. The note's working thesis: Spore sits closer to Bauwens's cosmo-local / open-value-network framing than to Borders's proprietarian framing, because Spore's coordination grammar centers community and field agency rather than individual property rights as the primitive unit. Leaving the stance ambiguous under "protocol society" language risks reading-in of either position by downstream audiences, which is a governance failure mode in its own right.

## 5. The corroborating arc: Galloway, Song, Cottica

Three corroborating pages in the wiki deepen the protocol-society framing in different directions:

- **Galloway's "Protocol" (2004)** treats protocol as "a technique for achieving voluntary regulation within a contingent environment" and as "a distributed management system that allows control to exist within a heterogeneous material milieu." This is the critical-theory edge: protocol is not politically neutral, and the framing invites (per the Mendoza commentary) a differentiated examination of "what each one does, who owns them, through what processes they are managed." Spore should carry that differentiated-examination stance explicitly; protocol-society language without it is insufficient.
- **Max Song's "Protocol Sovereignty"** frames blockchain networks as self-governing systems by analogy to Westphalian sovereignty (territorial integrity, self-governance, non-interference, recognition and legitimacy, cultural identity). This is a concrete recent instantiation of protocol-society logic applied to blockchain-native systems — useful as a sibling concept but distinct from Spore's positioning (which is not Westphalian-sovereignty-modeled and does not require full non-interference).
- **Cottica's reading of the Benedictine Rule as a protocol** is the strongest historical instantiation of the Schneider claim: a protocol (the Rule) spreading via voluntary adoption, surviving 1500+ years, enabling decentralized abbeys to federate loosely without a central order, and avoiding sterile conflict by not competing with Vatican politics. The Rule is the historical proof of concept Schneider's framing needs: protocol-society is not a crypto-era novelty.

These corroborating anchors strengthen the claim that protocol-society language sits in a real tradition, not just a Schneider-era coinage.

## 6. Tensions (cautions for any Spore canon edit)

1. **Naive reading risk**: Schneider's framing ("less dependent on enforcement through state violence") can be read as anti-governance. The source tradition (especially Galloway, Jessop, and the Bauwens side of the Bauwens/Borders debate) does not support that reading; protocol is a mode of governance, not its absence. Spore canon adopting protocol-society language should state this explicitly.
2. **Stance ambiguity risk**: The Bauwens/Borders split inside protocol-society discourse is real and unresolved. Spore should name its stance (commons-governance-anchored) rather than inheriting the framing as neutral.
3. **Power-capture risk**: Galloway's central claim is that protocol is how control exists after decentralization. Framing Spore as a "protocol society" without naming the power-capture dynamics protocols themselves enable (protocol lock-in, standards gatekeeping, governance-capture by protocol authors) leaves the governance surface too soft. This aligns with the C7 caution from the Tier 1a field scan on IC's value-capture/power-capture risks — same class of failure, different surface.
4. **Typology under-specification risk**: Schneider's framing treats protocol as a third mode alongside states and markets but does not specify the failure modes of the protocol modality itself. Spore should adopt Jessop's typology as a companion discipline so protocol-mode-specific failures (rigidity, exclusion by default parameters, slow adaptation under crisis) are named, not elided.

## 7. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The wiki evidence warrants adopting "protocol society" as an external cousin/aliased framing for Spore's grammar-as-coordination-substrate stance, importing Jessop's typology-of-modalities discipline and the Galloway differentiated-examination stance, explicitly aligning with the Bauwens side of the Bauwens/Borders split, and naming protocol-mode power-capture as a first-order canon concern. None of these clarifications require new canon primitives, which is consistent with the Move 0 moratorium (R-claims below do not become canon PRs during the clock).
