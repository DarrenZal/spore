# Johar — The Infrastructure of Relational Topology: How Organisations Generate Imaginary Identities

**Source:** Indy Johar, "The Infrastructure of Relational Topology: How Organisations Generate Imaginary Identities," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/the-infrastructure-of-relational

## What It Is

A short, dense essay (796w) arguing that the real infrastructure of an organisation is its topology of relationships — the subterranean patterns of relational density that determine who relates to whom, at what intensity, and in which modality. These infrastructures are not managing work but managing intimacy, exposure, and identification. They generate "imaginary identity" — the lived pattern of relational density that produces an emergent sense of "we" which is always partial, positional, and structurally mediated. When relational density is uneven, imaginary identity fractures: individuals and clusters operate through differentiation, defensive autonomy, or grievance. The essay reframes organisational design from charts and mandates to relational geometries — and identifies the silent infrastructures (co-location, protocolisation, role architecture, communication systems, incentive landscapes, calendar design) that produce or undermine organisational coherence.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Holonic architecture as relational topology:** Spore's semilattice holon model is itself a relational topology — agents participate in multiple overlapping wholes, and identity is constituted by that participation. The essay names the deeper dynamic: these topologies generate imaginary identities that shape belonging, coherence, and political power.

2. **Governance through artifacts, not authority:** Spore distributes authority to artifacts rather than concentrating it in command hierarchies. The essay identifies artifacts (protocols, calendars, communication channels) as generators of relational topology — they are not neutral tools but shapers of who sees whom and what is sensed together.

3. **Multi-projection as topology management:** Spore's eight projections provide distinct relational lenses. The essay argues that every organisation is an ecology of topologies (spatial, relational, informational, epistemic, temporal) that must align for coherence. Multi-projection is one mechanism for surfacing these misalignments.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Relational Topology as Real Infrastructure

The essay's foundational claim: the infrastructure that defines an organisation is not its formal structure but its topology of relationships. Co-location, protocolisation, role architecture, communication systems, incentive landscapes, and calendar design form "a kind of subterranean topology of relational density." These determine who "sees" whom, what is sensed together, what is interpreted in common, and where relational heat accumulates. Power is not only what people choose — it is what infrastructures make easy or difficult.

For Spore, this names the design dimension that the grammar addresses structurally but has not articulated explicitly: the grammar's protocols, projections, and holon architecture are relational infrastructure that generates the topology within which coordination happens. Designing these is not administrative but architectural.

### 2. Imaginary Identity as Emergent Property

Organisations are held together not by strategy documents but by the imaginary identity that emerges from relational density patterns. Dense clusters imagine themselves as the centre; distributed actors imagine themselves as peripheral; hybrid groups oscillate. When relational topologies contradict formal authority — when the people with relational influence are not the people with formal power — the organisation becomes politically volatile. Fault lines appear not from personality but from infrastructural misalignment.

For Spore, this is a diagnostic for the grammar's holon model: if holons generate uneven relational density (some agents have high relational access, others are structurally peripheral), the grammar will produce its own fault lines. The imaginary identity concept provides a lens for evaluating whether the grammar's topologies produce coherence or fracture.

### 3. Organisational Physics: Aligned vs Misaligned Topologies

Every institution is an ecology of topologies: spatial, relational, informational, epistemic, and temporal. When these align, the organisation feels coherent and generative. When they misalign — relational topology favours one group, informational another, formal power yet another — fault lines appear. The question is how to design topologies that maintain relational balance while distributing power fairly.

For Spore, this is a direct design challenge: the grammar's commitment protocols (relational), discourse graph (informational), holon structure (formal), and sensing patterns (epistemic) are distinct topologies. Whether they align or misalign determines whether the coordination ecology produces coherence or fracture.

## Open Questions

1. **Does Spore's holon model produce uneven relational density?** The grammar allows overlapping membership, but some holons may become "relational hotspots" that concentrate influence while others remain peripheral. Whether the grammar has mechanisms to detect or correct this (analogous to the essay's "relational geometry" approach) is unresolved.

2. **How does relational topology relate to identity-and-inter-becoming?** The human-development synthesis named inter-becoming as identity formed through relational participation. Relational topology names the infrastructure that shapes which relations form. Whether these converge (relational topology is the infrastructure for inter-becoming) or remain distinct (one is about identity formation, the other about institutional design) affects how the families should relate.
