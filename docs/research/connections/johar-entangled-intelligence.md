# Johar — Entangled Intelligence: On Not Outsourcing the Capacity to Learn

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Entangled Intelligence: On Not Outsourcing the Capacity to Learn," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/entangled-intelligence-on-not-outsourcing

## What It Is

A structural argument that machine-mediated cognition risks repeating the pattern of industrial outsourcing: not merely relocating tasks, but relocating learning. The essay draws a sustained analogy between late-twentieth-century manufacturing outsourcing and the current adoption of large language models. When firms separated design from fabrication, what migrated was not only production but feedback loops, tacit knowledge, and process innovation capacity. The same dynamic applies to cognition: when modelling, debugging, and iterative refinement migrate to external systems whose internal mechanics are not locally governed, the institution retains fluency but loses grounding.

The essay makes five interlocking claims:

1. **Learning-loop residency is the strategic variable.** The question is not whether to use computational systems but where learning resides when we do. Institutions that externalize failure signals, representation defaults, and iteration processes lose granularity even as they gain speed.

2. **Outsourcing is safe when learning remains internal; it becomes hollowing when learning migrates without replacement.** The failure mode is not delegation but the separation of execution from learning. Firms that maintained strong oversight and co-evolved systems with suppliers preserved innovation capacity. The cognitive analogue: use models, but own the surrounding infrastructure of context, evaluation, and update.

3. **Ontology is infrastructure.** Large models embody compressions of language, norms, and associations. When institutions operate through similar model architectures trained on overlapping data, epistemic monoculture becomes a risk. The concern is not that models are flawed but that they become invisible infrastructure shaping questions before they are consciously formed.

4. **The real choice is between separation and coupling.** In a separated architecture, cognition follows a linear path: query, output, consume. In a coupled (entangled) architecture, human and machine are embedded within shared learning systems that are instrumented and governed. Entangled architectures embed models within locally owned context structures, domain schemas, provenance chains, and evaluation harnesses.

5. **Own your context, rent your models.** What remains strategically significant is not ownership of the most advanced model but ownership of context: how information is structured, how decisions are linked to consequences, how commitments are recorded, and how risk is categorised.

The essay complements the previous Johar bridge note (neuroplastic-field). That essay identified the field as the real unit of intelligence and named the gap between inference and adaptation. This essay supplies the architectural principle: entangled architecture is the design response, and context ownership is the strategic criterion.

## What Spore Already Does

### Spore's architecture is a coupled/entangled architecture

Johar's central distinction — separated vs. coupled — maps directly onto Spore's design. In a separated architecture, an agent queries a model, receives output, and consumes it. In Spore, every LLM-generated artifact enters a governed field:

- **Context structures are locally owned.** The entity graph, commitment lifecycle, claims-evidence-attestation protocol, and governance memory are all local infrastructure. They are not hosted inside an LLM or delegated to a vendor. They persist, accumulate, and evolve independently of any model.

- **LLMs are inference components, not foundations.** The learning membrane uses LLMs to draft bridge notes, generate synthesis proposals, and identify convergence clusters. But governance decides what becomes durable. The model proposes; the field disposes.

- **Provenance chains are explicit.** Every claim carries source attribution. Every commitment carries evidence requirements. Every attestation is situated and traceable. This is precisely Johar's "evaluation harness" — the institutional capacity to contest representations and measure performance against outcomes.

The entangled architecture is not aspirational in Spore. It is the operating design.

### "Own context, rent models" is the membrane's division of labor

Johar's pragmatic principle — "own your context, rent your models" — is the implicit operating rule of the learning membrane. The membrane's four surfaces (personal intake, project semantic memory, governance memory, production proving ground) are all context infrastructure. LLMs touch every surface as accelerants, but the context structures they operate within are locally maintained.

This maps to federated knowledge exchange as well: sovereign nodes share selectively while retaining full control over their local ontologies, naming conventions, and governance. The federation protocol is context-ownership at the inter-institutional scale.

### Learning-loop residency lives in the coordination loop

Johar identifies "learning-loop residency" as the strategic variable: where do the feedback loops that generate institutional intelligence actually reside? In Spore, the coordination loop (Vision, Roadmap, Intent, Commitment, Evidence, Learning) is an explicit answer. Each stage is a governed feedback point:

- **Evidence attachment** registers consequences as constraints on future behaviour.
- **Dispute resolution** activates the discourse layer when fulfillment is contested.
- **Supersession** (claim versioning via `supersedes_rid`) records when learning revises prior understanding.

The feedback loops are not inside the LLM. They are in the commitment lifecycle, the claim register, and the governance memory. Learning-loop residency is local by design.

### Claims-evidence-attestation is the evaluation harness

Johar calls for institutions to "retain update rights" and "embed continuous evaluation into practice, detecting drift, brittleness, and bias." The claims-evidence-attestation protocol is this evaluation infrastructure:

- **Claims carry lifecycle state** (proposed, supported, challenged, superseded, reinstated) — the field can contest and revise any assertion.
- **Evidence bears directionally on claims** through `supports` and `challenges` relations — the field can weigh competing signals.
- **Attestation provides situated witnessing** — trust is built through distributed observation, not centralized authority.

This is not an add-on evaluation layer. It is the epistemic coordination grammar through which the field learns.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. The outsourcing analogy as a communicable frame

Johar's manufacturing outsourcing analogy is more immediately legible than the neuroplasticity framing. "Own your context, rent your models" is a one-line principle that communicates the membrane's design rationale to audiences who may not engage with field theory or coordination grammar. It is a translation device, not new content.

The analogy also carries a useful warning: the loss of learning-loop residency is gradual and often invisible. "At first it appears as convenience and speed." This maps to a real risk in agent infrastructure: if LLM-generated outputs are accepted without membrane processing, context migrates outward one convenient shortcut at a time.

### 2. Separated vs. coupled as a diagnostic criterion

The separated/coupled distinction gives Spore and IC a binary diagnostic for evaluating proposed integrations or features:

- **Does this design keep context structures locally owned?** If yes, coupled. If the context lives inside a vendor system or model, separated.
- **Does this design preserve feedback loops within the governed field?** If evaluation, revision, and update happen locally, coupled. If failure signals are absorbed by an external system, separated.

This is a fast pre-flight check for architectural decisions. It does not replace deeper analysis, but it catches the most common failure mode early.

### 3. Epistemic monoculture as a named risk

Johar identifies a risk that Spore's federation model is designed to address but has not named explicitly: when most actors operate through similar model architectures trained on overlapping data, "blind spots may correlate, interpretive variance may narrow, innovation may continue but within increasingly similar ontological boundaries."

Federated knowledge exchange mitigates this by design — sovereign nodes maintain independent ontologies and share selectively — but the mitigation is implicit. The named risk (epistemic monoculture) sharpens the rationale for federation: it is not only about sovereignty and consent, it is about preserving interpretive variance across the coordination ecology.

### 4. Ontology-as-infrastructure sharpens the grammar claim

Johar writes: "The issue is not simply who owns data. It is who shapes the grammar through which risk, value, and possibility are interpreted."

This is remarkably close to Spore's foundational claim that coordination grammar — not algorithms, platforms, or models — is the generative layer. Johar arrives at this from institutional design; Spore arrives from agent infrastructure. The convergence is worth noting because it validates the grammar claim from an independent direction: if ontology is infrastructure, then the grammar that structures coordination is the highest-leverage design surface, not the model that generates within it.

### 5. Reflexive infrastructure as a design checklist

The essay's final section provides a compressed checklist for what "reflexive infrastructure" requires:

- Context must be explicit and governable.
- Model plurality should be intentional.
- Continuous evaluation must be embedded.
- Human intervention should feed back into system improvement.
- Institutions must retain update rights.

Each item has a Spore/IC analogue (governance memory, agent-agnostic inference, claims-evidence-attestation, discourse-as-governance, membrane control). The checklist is useful not because it introduces new requirements but because it provides a compact external validation of the membrane's design surface. If a proposed system change would compromise any of these five properties, the separated/coupled diagnostic flags it.

## Open Questions

1. **Is "own your context, rent your models" sufficient as a constitutional commitment, or does it need qualification?** The principle is clean and communicable, but context ownership is not binary. Some context structures (embeddings, retrieval indices) may depend on model-specific representations. At what point does a context structure become model-entangled enough that "ownership" is nominal? This matters for practical membrane governance.

2. **How should epistemic monoculture risk be monitored in practice?** The federation model mitigates it structurally, but the risk also operates within a single node when all inference passes through a single model family. Should the membrane track model diversity as a governance variable, or is structural federation sufficient?

3. **Does the outsourcing analogy carry a false implication of reversibility?** Manufacturing outsourcing was difficult to reverse because capability had migrated. Johar implies that cognitive outsourcing follows the same pattern. But if context structures remain locally owned, is the dependency on any particular model actually reversible? The answer depends on whether "context" includes the tacit adaptations an institution makes to a specific model's behaviour over time.

## Disposition

**Primary: clarify existing term** — The essay provides a structural analogy (manufacturing outsourcing) and a pragmatic principle ("own context, rent models") that make Spore's existing membrane design legible to a wider audience. It names what the architecture already does, not what it should become.

**Secondary: diagnostic criterion** — The separated/coupled distinction is a fast pre-flight check for architectural decisions. It does not introduce new mechanisms but provides a binary diagnostic that catches the most common failure mode (context migration) early.

**Tertiary: named risk** — Epistemic monoculture is a risk that federation mitigates by design but has not named explicitly. Adding the name sharpens the rationale for federated knowledge exchange.

**Promotion status:** defer — the essay validates existing architecture rather than introducing new mechanisms. The two R-claims support already-open clusters (human-machine-complementarity and inference-infrastructure) and do not require immediate promotion.
