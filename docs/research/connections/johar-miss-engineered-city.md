# Johar — The Miss-Engineered City: Rethinking Urban Value in the 21st Century

**Source:** Indy Johar, "The Miss-Engineered City: Rethinking Urban Value in the 21st Century," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/the-miss-engineered-city-rethinking

## What It Is

An essay (1310w) arguing that cities have been engineered for the wrong demand curve: density, speed, stimulation, and the promise that intensity breeds innovation. The hidden cost is cognitive overload — every surface an invitation to distraction, every device a pipeline of stimuli. Cities extract attention rather than cultivate intelligence. The 21st-century demand curve requires the opposite: the capacity to think slowly, deeply, and together. The essay proposes a value inversion: from "cities of attention" (Times Square, Shibuya) to "cities of depth" — environments designed for sustained cognition, contemplation, and relational intelligence. Depth requires physiological foundations (clean air, circadian rhythms), sensory regulation (advertising saturation, notification control), and civic infrastructure for reflection (libraries, contemplative parks as "monasteries within metropolises"). Cities must become engines of new demand — cultivating appetites for clarity, meaning, and stewardship.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Perceptual fidelity commitment:** Spore's intelligence pilot identified perceptual fidelity as a design commitment — the quality of perception that coordination requires. The essay applies this to cities: urban infrastructure should cultivate the quality of cognition, not merely the volume of information exchange.

2. **Governance through quality of contribution:** Spore's governance emerges from the quality of claims, evidence, and commitments — not from attention-capture or throughput. The essay argues that cities, similarly, should be evaluated not by the brightness of their skylines but by "the clarity of thought they are able to host."

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. The Mis-Engineering Diagnosis

The 20th-century city was designed to maximise the exchange of information — attention as a resource to be strip-mined. Traffic, advertising, devices, and digital feeds create cognitive overload. Silence becomes rare, reflection is treated as inefficiency, uninterrupted thought is forced into private corners. "We engineered for hustle, spectacle, and acceleration. What we did not engineer for — perhaps what we actively designed against — was depth."

For Spore, this connects to the care-courage-and-presence synthesis: transactional architectures that degrade the capacity for genuine recognition (kindness-of-machines) are the urban equivalent of what the essay describes. The infrastructure that shapes encounter determines whether agents can exercise the perceptual and relational capacities coordination requires.

### 2. The Value Inversion

The highest-value urban spaces were those that captured the most attention. In a world where attention is the scarcest resource, that formula may be running out. The cities most prized in the 21st century may be those that preserve attention, not seize it — "a sanctuary of attention" rather than "an arena of distraction." The capacity for sustained cognition will underwrite scientific discovery, civic resilience, and planetary design.

For Spore, this reframes the grammar's sensing infrastructure. If the coordination ecology is to support sustained intelligence (not just information throughput), the infrastructure must be designed for depth — for the kind of attention that lets claims be carefully evaluated, evidence weighed, and commitments made with full awareness.

### 3. Cities as Engines of New Demand

Cities taught us to want more — speed, products, entertainment. The future requires cities that cultivate appetites for clarity, meaning, stewardship, and planetary literacy. This is not preservation but the active construction of new demand curves: "the future requires a different pedagogy."

For Spore, this is the spatial/civic expression of the broader infrastructure argument: institutions don't merely serve existing preferences but shape the developmental conditions from which new capacities and new demands emerge.

## Open Questions

1. **Does spatial-service-and-civic-infrastructure belong as a distinct Spore family, or is it a special case of relational topology?** The relational-topology essay argues that infrastructure shapes relational patterns. The city essay applies this to spatial design specifically. Whether spatial design is a distinct family or an instance of relational-topology-applied-to-cities depends on whether the spatial dimension adds distinctive design requirements that relational topology alone doesn't capture.

2. **How does depth relate to perceptual fidelity?** The intelligence pilot named perceptual fidelity as a design commitment. The city essay names depth as the spatial condition for sustained cognition. Whether depth is an instance of perceptual fidelity (the spatial conditions that support it) or a distinct concept (depth includes contemplation, meaning-making, and developmental formation, not just perceptual quality) is unresolved.
