# Johar — The Unalienable Right of Being

**Source:** Indy Johar, "The Unalienable Right of Being," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/the-unalienable-right-of-being

## What It Is

A manifesto (348w) declaring that being — voice, face, gait, expressive existence — is unalienable. It cannot be extracted, modified, copied, sold, or automated because it is not detachable from the person. Consent is structurally insufficient at planetary scale (coerced, misunderstood, impossible in architectures of surveillance and simulation). Inviolability is the only rightful boundary. The essay calls for technologies built to stand beside us, not simulate us — "systems that listen without recording, tools that amplify without extracting, machines that know the boundary between partnership and plunder."

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Sovereignty as constitutional commitment:** Spore commits to meaningful local autonomy — agents control their own data, participation is voluntary, and forkability ensures no one is locked in. The essay extends this to embodied being: sovereignty applies not only to claims and commitments but to the expressive substance of personhood.

2. **Provenance and attribution:** Spore's provenance commitment ensures that claims carry attribution. The essay argues for a more foundational provenance: the provenance of being itself, which should never be separated from the person who expresses it.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Being as Unalienable

The essay names a bright line: "there are no terms of service for being." Voice, face, gait, behaviour are not assets, not data, not code. They are the person. This is not a property claim (ownership of data) but an ontological claim (being is not detachable). The distinction matters: property can be contracted away; being cannot.

For Spore, this extends the sovereignty commitment from governance participation to embodied existence. If agents are relational beings (per the entangled-freedom essay), their relational expression — the patterns that make them recognisable — is constitutive of who they are, not an output that can be separated.

### 2. Consent Is Insufficient

The essay argues that consent frameworks fail at scale: "coerced, misunderstood, or structurally impossible" in architectures of automated replication. Inviolability replaces consent as the boundary. This connects to Spore's contestability commitment — the right to challenge — but goes further: some things should not require contestation because they should never be taken.

### 3. Technologies of Respect

The essay calls for "systems that listen without recording, tools that amplify without extracting." This is a design ethic for Spore's sensor and inference infrastructure: sensing should preserve relational quality (perceptual fidelity) without converting presence into extractable data.

## Open Questions

1. **Where does inviolability sit in Spore's design commitments?** The essay names an absolute boundary (being cannot be extracted) that is stronger than any of Spore's existing constitutional commitments (which are revisable by design). Whether inviolability should be a constitutional commitment or whether it is upstream of the grammar entirely — a precondition for any grammar — is unresolved.

2. **How does this relate to the human-machine-complementarity synthesis?** That synthesis placed the asymmetry between human and machine agents at the governance/membrane level. This essay adds a harder claim: some properties of human agents (expressive being) are not just asymmetric but inviolable — they cannot be extracted, modelled, or transferred. Whether this strengthens or complicates the complementarity framework is unresolved.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate primitive** — Embodied inviolability names a property of being that Spore's sovereignty commitment approaches but has not made absolute. The essay is a manifesto (348w) — declarative rather than analytical — but the concept is sharp: being is not detachable, and consent cannot substitute for inviolability at scale.

**Promotion status: defer** — Single-author source. The essay is very short and manifesto-form. The concept (inviolability of being) is powerful but needs analytical development and convergence from other sources before it can pressure canon. This note is source-led: it supports the entangled-freedom-and-being review cluster rather than minting its own.
