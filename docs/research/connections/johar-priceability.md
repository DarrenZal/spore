# Johar — Price, Priceability, and Living in the System World

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Price, Priceability, and Living in the System World," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/price-priceability-and-living-in

## What It Is

A long essay (5254w) arguing that the refusal to price non-market values does not protect them — it renders them invisible to the coordination systems that determine their survival. The essay proposes priceability as a systemic capacity: the ability of systems to perceive and express interdependent value across temporal, spatial, and relational dimensions. Price is reconstructed from a scalar extraction signal into a living, multi-vector coordination grammar. The argument moves from epistemic critique (single-system valuation produces blindness) through structural proposal (plural exchange modalities, systemic sensing infrastructure) to a redefinition of economy: from optimisation toward composition, from prediction toward learning, from transaction toward relational maintenance.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Multi-projection as value pluralism:** Spore's eight projections offer distinct lenses on the same coordination reality. This is structurally parallel to the essay's argument that value is multi-vector: no single projection captures it.

2. **Claim lifecycle as revisable valuation:** The discourse graph keeps claims revisable and attestation decays. This mirrors the essay's argument that price must be dynamic — a living negotiation, not a settled declaration. Spore treats knowledge as provisional; the essay argues value must be too.

3. **Governance as coordination rather than control:** Spore's coordination ecology (Vision → Intent → Commitment → Evidence → Learning) replaces hierarchical command with adaptive coordination. The essay proposes the same move for economy: composition and attunement replacing optimisation.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Priceability as Systemic Perception

The essay's central concept: priceability is the capacity of a system to perceive and express its interdependent forms of value. This is not the ability to put a number on something — it is the systemic infrastructure for making value legible to the coordination field that governs its survival. What is unpriced in a system governed by financial allocation becomes invisible, and what is invisible is eroded. The refusal to price is structural abdication, not moral purity.

For Spore, this reframes the grammar's legibility mechanisms. If coordination requires that value be visible to the governing field, then the discourse graph, claim register, and multi-projection are priceability infrastructure — making coordination-relevant value legible to the agents that must act on it.

### 2. Price as Grammar, Not Signal

The essay reconstructs price from a unidimensional equivalence signal into a semiotic operator — a grammar of translation between incommensurable value forms. Price becomes "not the endpoint of agreement but the grammar of an ongoing conversation through which life learns to sustain itself." This connects to Spore's self-description as a "common grammar": both propose that coordination across difference requires a shared grammar, not a shared metric.

The essay identifies three foundational capacities: (a) systemic sensing — relational ledgers that register non-financial contributions, (b) plural valuation protocols that preserve ontological difference while enabling coordination, and (c) negotiated allocation that treats contribution as process rather than event.

### 3. Compositional Economy

The essay's deepest move: the shift from optimisation (improving a single function) to composition (sustaining relational coherence among heterogeneous systems). The economy becomes "not a machine of allocation but a metabolism of attention." For Spore, composition is already the grammar's organising principle. The essay argues the same compositional logic should apply to economic coordination: economy as learning system, pricing as perception, perception as care.

## Open Questions

1. **Is priceability a Spore concept or a general economic concept?** The essay proposes priceability as a universal systemic capacity. Spore could name it without claiming to solve the full infrastructure problem. But the essay's three capacities (systemic sensing, plural valuation, negotiated allocation) map closely onto Spore's existing primitives (sensors, multi-projection, commitment protocols). Whether Spore should claim priceability as part of its grammar or treat it as an external concept that Spore's grammar supports is unresolved.

2. **Does compositional economy require new Spore primitives?** The essay proposes relational ledgers, conversion protocols, and participatory allocation. Spore has discourse graphs, commitment protocols, and governance patterns. The structural parallel is strong but may be superficial — economic coordination involves money, contracts, and capital allocation that governance coordination does not.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate primitive** — Priceability names a systemic capacity that Spore's grammar embodies structurally (multi-projection, claim lifecycle, compositional coordination) but has not named as an explicit design concept. The essay provides vocabulary for articulating what Spore's legibility mechanisms do in economic terms.

**Promotion status: defer** — Single-author source. The connection between Spore's governance grammar and economic pricing infrastructure is asserted by analogy, not demonstrated. Promotion should wait for convergence from another source or production evidence.
