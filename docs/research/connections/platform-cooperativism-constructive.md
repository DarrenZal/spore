# Platform Cooperativism — Constructive

This bridge note is Pass 2 Phase B Wave 2 Supporting Note (Seed 11c, paired-constructive). It grounds Spore's governance-layer vocabulary — specifically the `mycorrhizal-federation-protocol` and the holonic-network-architecture as a federation-of-instances substrate — in the platform-cooperativism (Scholz 2016) and open-cooperativism (Bauwens / Conaty-Bollier 2015) lineages, as surfaced through six P2P Foundation wiki pages: `Platform_Cooperativism`, `Open_Cooperatives`, `Toward_an_Open_Cooperativism`, `Commons-Oriented_Open_Cooperative_Governance_Model` (DisCO v3.0), `Loomio`, and `Enspiral`. It is **paired with the Wave 2 opposition note** `cooperative-degeneration-opposition` (Kasmir / Mondragon ethnography): that note specifies three acceptance tests (membership-tier, external-labor, scale-drift) the constructive import MUST pass or the opposition edges remain active. §7 of this note shows how the three ACs are addressed, and §9's R-claims explicitly name which AC each R-claim satisfies.

> **Decentralization-myth-bundle header.** This note's claims inherit the decentralization-myth-bundle critique (Pass 2 opposition notes: `decentralization-theater-opposition`, `digital-labor-peer-production-opposition`, `peer-governance-wikipedia-opposition`). Cooperative ownership does NOT automatically discharge decentralization-theater: a multi-stakeholder co-op can still hide admin-key control, free-labor dynamics, and administrator-capture. This note imports the constructive framing while the opposition notes constrain how "platform-cooperativism" is allowed to appear in Spore/PM canon — the constructive import is read alongside the opposition, never as substitute for it.

## 1. What platform cooperativism and open cooperativism name

**Platform cooperativism**, framed by Trebor Scholz (*Ours to Hack and to Own*, OR Books 2016), is the reconstructive counterpart to the "on-demand" / "sharing economy" platforms that extract value from users and workers without co-ownership: the thesis is that the governance and ownership structure of digital platforms can be democratized — multi-stakeholder ownership (users, workers, communities) replaces the shareholder model, governance votes are distributed to the stakeholders, and the platform's matching / routing / ledger logic operates on behalf of the participants rather than a silent external owner. The `Platform_Cooperativism` wiki entry opens with the question-form: "Can we bring co-operative ownership and governance to the online platforms that we increasingly use and depend upon?" — establishing the problematic as a governance-of-coordination-substrate problem, not a technology problem.

**Open cooperativism**, framed by Michel Bauwens and the Conaty-Bollier (2015) convergence thesis, adds a further constraint: cooperative ownership alone reproduces the old cooperative-sector's extractive tendencies toward the commons outside the co-op; what is needed is a convergence between *commons-based peer production* (Wikipedia, FOSS, the digital-commons tradition) and *cooperative ownership*, producing what Bauwens calls "cooperative accumulation" — value accumulates for a multi-stakeholder membership *while* the commons the co-op produces (software, design, documentation) remains open-licensed and commons-available to other co-ops and commoners. Bauwens's four criteria for an "open" cooperative, preserved on the `Open_Cooperatives` wiki page, are: (1) statutorily oriented toward the common good (internally), (2) multi-stakeholder governance (users / workers / communities, not worker-only), (3) commons co-production (the productive output is licensed as commons, not enclosed as co-op property), and (4) glocal organizational structure (local autonomy + global coordination, i.e., subsidiarity). These four criteria together are the open-cooperativism discipline that distinguishes an open co-op from a conventional worker co-op.

The `Toward_an_Open_Cooperativism` wiki page (Pat Conaty + David Bollier 2015, Heinrich Böll Foundation report) synthesizes the convergence thesis: peer production provides abundance logic (non-rival digital commons, generative value); cooperative ownership provides accountability and resource stewardship; together they produce an economic substrate whose accumulation dynamic is *cooperative* rather than *capitalist* — value compounds into the commons and the multi-stakeholder membership rather than into external shareholders. This is the lineage the constructive import brings to Spore's governance-layer: a named discipline for how a federated coordination substrate can accumulate value without reproducing extractive platform dynamics.

## 2. What Spore's governance layer already does (and doesn't specify)

Spore's governance-layer vocabulary — `mycorrhizal-federation-protocol`, holonic-network-architecture, instance-model — carries "federation of instances" and "multi-party coordination" language, with federation-agreements governing how instances interoperate. What Spore's current canon does *not* carry explicitly is (a) the ownership-structure layer that determines which parties hold governance rights inside each Spore instance and across the federation; (b) named discipline for how federation-participating organizations (co-ops, non-profits, single-entity hosts) relate to the federation-as-commons substrate they draw on; (c) the scale-drift mitigation that the paired opposition note (Kasmir / Mondragon) requires any cooperative-at-scale framing to carry; and (d) a named alternative to the shareholder-owned-platform default that most digital-coordination infrastructure today runs on.

The constructive proposal is not that Spore *becomes* a platform co-op — Spore is a coordination grammar, not a platform operator. The proposal is that Spore's governance-layer canon can import the platform-cooperativism / open-cooperativism vocabulary as the named external lineage for how Spore-hosting organizations SHOULD be structured when they operate instances for multi-party participation, and as the named discipline for how federation-agreements must carry ownership-structure clauses (not just interoperability clauses). This is a clarify-existing-term disposition: add external lineage, import four-criteria discipline, import scale-drift mitigation from the paired opposition.

## 3. The four open-cooperativism criteria as federation-agreement clauses

Bauwens's four criteria (on `Open_Cooperatives`) are the operating discipline that distinguishes an open co-op from a Mondragon-pattern degeneration-vulnerable co-op. They map directly onto clauses Spore's federation-agreement template should carry for any Spore-hosting organization operating a public instance:

- **Criterion 1 (common-good orientation, internal statutes).** The hosting organization's founding documents must state common-good orientation as primary purpose — not as marketing framing but as legally binding statutory purpose. For Spore federation-agreements this means: instance hosts operating as for-profit single-owner entities must either (a) carry a separable governance-board with stated common-good fiduciary duty, (b) operate the Spore instance under a commons-governance contract distinct from the host's for-profit activities, or (c) be excluded from the federation's full-participant tier. This is the first open-cooperativism filter.
- **Criterion 2 (multi-stakeholder governance, not worker-only).** Governance includes affected parties beyond employees — users, participant communities, downstream-commons stewards. The Mondragon degeneration pattern is specifically a failure of this criterion: Mondragon is worker-owned but not multi-stakeholder, so external-labor subcontractors and affected communities have no governance voice. Spore federation-agreements should require instance-host governance to carry multi-stakeholder representation for non-trivial instances (federation-participant tier), and should carry explicit specification for **how membership tiers relate to governance voice** — see §6 for the paired-opposition AC1 treatment.
- **Criterion 3 (commons co-production).** Productive output (Spore's canon, schemas, grammar; the instance's operational practice; the federation's coordination protocols) is licensed as commons and remains available to other instances and the broader commons. A co-op that builds Spore infrastructure cannot enclose that infrastructure as co-op property — it must flow back to the commons under open licensing (this note itself is CC BY-SA 4.0 per the wiki-derived license chain). This criterion is what makes "cooperative accumulation" cooperative-*accumulative* rather than cooperative-*enclosing*.
- **Criterion 4 (glocal organizational structure).** Local autonomy (per subsidiarity, see `cosmo-local-production`) plus global coordination (federation-protocol, shared canon). This criterion is the governance-structure-level statement of the cosmo-local subsidiarity principle — decisions defer to the lowest competent scale, the federation coordinates across scales, and no federation-participant holds authority higher than necessary for the specific coordination function it serves.

These four criteria, taken together, are the open-cooperativism discipline. Spore federation-agreements should import them as first-class clauses — not as aspirational culture, but as operating conditions for participating in the federation's coordination substrate.

## 4. DisCO v3.0, Loomio, and Enspiral as operational exemplars (with scale caveats)

The `Commons-Oriented_Open_Cooperative_Governance_Model` wiki page preserves the DisCO (Distributed Cooperative Organizations) governance model, developed by the Guerrilla Media Collective as a commons-oriented open-coop governance pattern. DisCO v3.0's operating primitives: **equipotential opt-in** (members choose which decisions to engage without losing membership), **contribution-tracked shared decisions** (decision weight reflects actual contribution via tracked work, not capital ownership), and **multi-stream value accounting** (productive work, reproductive/care work, and "love work" are tracked as separable contribution streams rather than collapsed into wage-labor). DisCO is a concrete governance pattern that instantiates Bauwens's four criteria — it is multi-stakeholder, commons-co-productive (outputs are CC-licensed), glocal, and explicitly statute-oriented toward the common good via its "DisCO DNA" framing.

The `Loomio` wiki page records Loomio as an open-source consensus-decision tool, developed by a worker-cooperative emerging from Enspiral (Alanna Krause et al.), used by ~80,000 groups in 93 countries (as of the wiki capture). Loomio is itself structured as a worker-coop whose product is commons-licensed (open-source), instantiating criteria 2 and 3 simultaneously: the tool and the organization share the open-cooperativism form.

The `Enspiral` wiki page records the Enspiral network as a "network of networks" — a loose federation of independent ventures coordinating through a shared Foundation, shared Services company, and tools like Cobudget (collaborative funding by contribution). Enspiral demonstrates the glocal criterion (criterion 4): ~300 participants, ~15 ventures, coordinated by protocol (Cobudget budgeting, Loomio decisions, periodic in-person summits) rather than by corporate hierarchy. The 2015 "Refactor" process — where Enspiral consciously redesigned its own governance via a multi-month distributed deliberation — is documented evidence that a network of this shape *can* maintain non-hierarchical governance, though only at scale ~100–300 participants.

**Scale caveat (from paired opposition AC3).** Loomio's consensus-tool literature and Enspiral's Refactor record both suggest multi-stakeholder consensus-based governance scales to ~50–200 person networks; above that, consensus overhead drives either (a) representative substructures (which re-introduce managerial-class drift), (b) tool-mediated algorithmic summarization (which re-introduces admin-control asymmetry), or (c) federation into smaller subsystems (which is the glocal / subsidiarity response, preferred per criterion 4). The `Toward_an_Open_Cooperativism` page reinforces this: Conaty-Bollier explicitly treat glocal federation as the scale-response, not monolithic at-scale consensus. This is load-bearing for §6's AC3 treatment.

## 5. Ownership, not just governance — the structural-contradiction test

The `From_Platform_Cooperatives_To_Open_Cooperatives` wiki corroborating page records the specific structural-contradiction argument that distinguishes platform cooperativism from open cooperativism: if a platform is governed cooperatively but *owned* by an external entity (e.g., a VC-backed holding company that grants governance rights but retains asset title), the governance can be revoked at any moment by ownership transfer, IPO, or covenant default. The `Toward_an_Open_Cooperativism` page preserves the Conaty-Bollier explicit requirement that multi-stakeholder *ownership* is load-bearing, not just multi-stakeholder *governance*. This closes DH-PM-3 from Phase A: PM (and any Spore-hosted coordination substrate) *cannot* be architected as "VC-backed platform with cooperative governance overlay" — the ownership structure must be cooperative at the asset-title layer or the governance-democratization is revocable.

For Spore's governance-layer canon this is a first-class constraint: Spore's federation-protocol should specify ownership-structure conditions for participating instances at the highest federation tier — concretely, instance hosts participating in the federation-canonical tier must carry ownership structures consistent with criteria 1–4 (common-good statute, multi-stakeholder title, commons-licensed output, glocal organizational form). Lower-tier participation (observer, syndication-only, external-adapter) can relax some conditions, but the full-participant federation tier must carry the open-coop ownership discipline — this is the specific clause that makes "platform cooperativism" different from "platform with cooperative UX."

## 6. Decentralization-myth-bundle header

> This note's constructive import inherits (and does not discharge) the decentralization-myth-bundle critique bundled in the Pass 2 opposition notes: cooperative ownership form does not automatically prevent admin-key retention, free-labor dynamics, administrator-capture, or the Mondragon-pattern managerial-class drift that the paired opposition note names. Open-cooperativism's four criteria are the proposed discipline, but they are only-as-strong-as-their-implementation — R-claims below specify the operational surface that must be preserved for the import to be carriable, not merely nameable.

## 7. Tensions and paired-opposition acceptance-test treatment

This constructive note is **paired with** `cooperative-degeneration-opposition` (Wave 2 Seed 8, Kasmir / Mondragon). The opposition note specifies **three acceptance tests** the constructive import MUST satisfy or its opposition edges remain active against PM / Spore canon invoking platform-cooperativism language. This §7 treats each AC explicitly; §9's R-claims then name which R-claim satisfies which AC.

**AC1 — Membership-tier specification (from opposition §4 AC1, §9 R3).**
The opposition note requires the constructive import to specify "permitted/prohibited structure of membership tiers and the governance rights of associate/non-member participants" — "silence defaults to tolerating Mondragon-pattern hierarchy." This constructive note's treatment:
- **Permitted tiers:** Spore-federated instance hosts MAY carry multiple membership tiers (founding members / contributing members / observers / syndication-only) IFF every tier with access to non-public operational data or governance-affecting contribution (e.g., claim ingest, federation-edge authorship) has a named governance-voice proportionate to the scope of access. Tier differentiation by access-to-resources-and-decisions is permitted only where each access-bearing tier has proportionate governance voice.
- **Prohibited tiers:** No tier may combine operationally-load-bearing contribution (sustained work maintaining the Spore instance's coordination function) with zero governance voice. This directly rules out the Mondragon "associate member" pattern where members contribute labor proportionate to full-member labor but hold reduced / zero voting rights. This is the `inclusive-governance` concept's structural-inclusion content rendered as a federation-agreement clause.
- **Associate / non-member governance rights:** Any non-full-member participant whose data, work, or commitments are metabolized by the federation substrate must have (a) standing to contest federation decisions that affect their contribution, (b) exit rights (withdrawal of contributions, per the portable-memory principle from `hess-ostrom-knowledge-commons-framework`), and (c) transparency rights (federation-decision audit trail accessible to all affected parties, not just full members). Satisfaction: R1 and R2 specify these as federation-agreement clauses.

**AC2 — External-labor specification (from opposition §4 AC2, §9 R3).**
The opposition note requires the constructive import to specify "how non-member external labor is handled at PM scale — permitted with what conditions, prohibited, or structurally absent." Silence defaults to "tolerating external-labor subcontracting under cooperative branding." This constructive note's treatment:
- **External labor in the open-cooperativism frame:** Bauwens's criterion 2 (multi-stakeholder governance) and criterion 3 (commons co-production) jointly constrain external-labor handling. External contributors to the Spore substrate (volunteer contributors, open-source maintainers, downstream-commons stewards) are stakeholders under criterion 2 — they have a governance-voice claim proportionate to their contribution — and the productive output returns to the commons under criterion 3, so external labor is not extracted into co-op property.
- **Prohibited pattern:** A Spore-participating organization MAY NOT operate a two-class workforce where full co-op members bear co-op governance rights while a structurally equivalent "external contractor" class performs load-bearing work outside the governance frame. This is the Mondragon external-labor subcontracting pattern explicitly. Federation-agreements MUST require hosting organizations to disclose workforce structure sufficient for this determination.
- **Permitted pattern:** Short-term, scope-bounded external contributions (one-off contract work, periodic advisory engagements, occasional volunteer contributions) are permitted without full-member governance rights IF the contribution is scope-bounded (not load-bearing for sustained operations) AND the contributor retains the work-product under licensing consistent with criterion 3 (commons-licensed or mutually-agreed open-licensed). Load-bearing sustained external labor must be absorbed into the multi-stakeholder governance frame per criterion 2. Satisfaction: R3 specifies this as a federation-agreement clause.

**AC3 — Scale-drift mitigation (from opposition §4 AC3, §9 R3).**
The opposition note requires the constructive import to specify "the at-scale governance-capacity surface (not just ownership-structure) that PM participants must preserve as cooperatives federate." Silence defaults to "accepting Mondragon-pattern managerial drift as the implicit trajectory." This constructive note's treatment:
- **Preserved governance-capacity surface (from C4 of opposition note — drift is resistible where political-organizing surface is preserved):** Spore-federated instance hosts must preserve (a) internal political-organizing surface (members and affected parties can coordinate positions without requiring management approval), (b) direct-governance votes on material decisions (not only on-paper board authority), (c) shop-floor-level or participant-level governance-capacity audits distinct from ownership-share audits, and (d) explicit caps or checks on coordinator/manager authority spans and income ratios per federation-tier — the specific Kasmir-opposition mitigation.
- **Glocal scale-response:** Criterion 4 (glocal organizational structure) is the constructive scale-response: when federation-participating instances exceed the ~50–200 consensus-sustainable scale, the response is *federation into smaller subsystems with subsidiarity* — not centralized representation nor tool-mediated algorithmic summarization. The Loomio/Enspiral evidence (§4) supports the ~50–200 ceiling; the DisCO v3.0 equipotential opt-in and multi-stream value accounting support the glocal response pattern.
- **Periodic governance-capacity audit:** Federation-agreements should specify periodic audit cycles where governance-capacity is measured separately from ownership-share (e.g., are there active member-organizing channels; are direct-governance votes happening on material decisions; is the coordinator/manager income ratio within the specified cap; have participants exercised political-organizing to resist management proposals as Mondragon workers did per opposition C4). Failure of governance-capacity audit triggers federation-tier demotion, not silent drift. Satisfaction: R4 and R5 specify these as federation-agreement and periodic-audit clauses.

**Additional tensions beyond the three ACs.**
1. **DH-PM-2 (consensus-scaling).** Platform-cooperativism's multi-stakeholder governance tools (DisCO, Loomio, Enspiral) scale to ~50–200 participants. Spore federation may exceed this at the network level. Mitigation: tiered governance — local consensus within instances, federation-level coordination via protocol-heuristics + subsidiarity (criterion 4) rather than network-wide consensus. This is the glocal response pattern. Carried as R4 scope-constraint.
2. **DH-PM-3 (ownership-not-just-governance, closed by §5).** Resolved as structural constraint: federation-canonical tier requires open-coop ownership discipline per criteria 1–4, not just cooperative governance overlay. Carried as R2.
3. **Algorithmic-governance asymmetry (from Dubal/Rosenblat critique in Agent B dossier).** Cooperative ownership does not solve algorithmic-governance asymmetry: multi-stakeholder governance must include algorithm-choice consent, not just outcome consent. For Spore: federation-protocol algorithm choices (claim-ingest heuristics, matching/routing logic, de-duplication policies) MUST be multi-stakeholder-governed, not set by a silent protocol-steward class. Carried as a §9 R-claim scope-note.
4. **Infrastructure-dependency on commons hosts (from `cosmo-local-production` §6).** The platform-cooperativism tradition's infrastructure substrate is, in practice, hosted on centralized commons-hosts (GitHub, Loomio's own servers, Enspiral's Cobudget instance). Federation-canonical instance hosts cannot fully decentralize this without federation-level commons-governance of the infrastructure layer. Named as out-of-scope-constraint carried by the cross-referenced `cosmo-local-production` tensions register.

## 8. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The platform-cooperativism / open-cooperativism wiki evidence, read through the paired-opposition acceptance tests, warrants (a) naming platform-cooperativism (Scholz 2016) and open-cooperativism (Bauwens / Conaty-Bollier 2015) as the external named lineage for Spore governance-layer canon discussing federation-participating organization ownership structure; (b) importing Bauwens's four criteria as first-class federation-agreement clauses for the federation-canonical participation tier; (c) importing the ownership-not-just-governance structural constraint (Conaty-Bollier) as a tier-gating condition; (d) carrying the three paired-opposition acceptance tests (membership-tier, external-labor, scale-drift) as operational-surface requirements in the federation-protocol; (e) naming DisCO v3.0, Loomio, and Enspiral as operational exemplars — with explicit scale caveat (~50–200 consensus ceiling, glocal response above). None of these introduce new canon primitives; they ground existing governance-layer language in a published lineage and import operating clauses and acceptance-tests the current canon lacks.

**Pairing with opposition note.** This disposition is read *with* the `cooperative-degeneration-opposition` disposition (`no change`). The pairing operates as: the opposition note's disposition (`no change`) prevents silent adoption of cooperative-at-scale vocabulary as self-legitimating coordination architecture; this constructive note's disposition (`clarify existing term`) permits adoption *only* when the opposition's three ACs are carried as operational-surface specifications. Neither disposition can be read in isolation — both govern the import jointly.

## 9. Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Platform_Cooperativism]

The Conaty-Bollier framework explicitly treats glocal federation (criterion 4) as the scale-response to open-cooperativism's coordination ceiling — rather than monolithic at-scale consensus or centralized representation, which both reproduce managerial-class drift — positioning glocal federation as the constructive scale-mitigation consistent with the paired opposition note's scale-drift acceptance test.

**C9** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Characteristics·Criteria as Proposed by Michel Bauwens · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Open_Cooperatives]
Bauwens's criterion 2 (multi-stakeholder governance) explicitly distinguishes open cooperativism from worker-only cooperativism by requiring governance inclusion of users, producers, and other stakeholders beyond employees — an explicit guard against the Mondragon degeneration pattern in which worker ownership coexists with non-member external-labor subcontracting and with associate-member governance-reduction, since both degeneration patterns violate multi-stakeholder inclusion at the governance layer.

**C10** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Loomio + §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Enspiral]
The Loomio and Enspiral cases jointly provide empirical evidence that multi-stakeholder consensus-based governance scales to roughly 50–200 person networks via direct-consensus tooling, and that coordination above this scale in the open-cooperativism tradition is achieved by federation into smaller consensus-sustainable subsystems with protocol-mediated glocal coordination (criterion 4) — not by representative substructures (which reintroduce managerial drift) nor by tool-mediated algorithmic summarization (which reintroduces admin-control asymmetry).

**C11** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Characteristics·Criteria as Proposed by Michel Bauwens · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Open_Cooperatives]
Criterion 3 (commons co-production) requires that the productive output of an open cooperative be licensed and governed as commons — available to other co-ops and commoners rather than enclosed as co-op property; this is the criterion that makes "cooperative accumulation" (C3) cooperative-*accumulative* rather than cooperative-*enclosing*, and is the criterion that makes external contributors' work return to the commons rather than being extracted into co-op property.

**C12** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Characteristics·Criteria as Proposed by Michel Bauwens · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Open_Cooperatives]
Criterion 4 (glocal organizational structure — local autonomy plus global coordination) expresses at the open-cooperativism organizational layer the same subsidiarity-and-global-commons logic that `cosmo-local-production` expresses at the production layer; the two criteria are structurally equivalent but operate at different substrate layers (open-coop: organizational form; cosmo-local: production form), establishing a cross-reference between this note and `cosmo-local-production` as the paired governance-layer and production-layer imports of the same subsidiarity-plus-commons discipline.
