# FairCoin Failure — Opposition to the Filtering-Membrane Primitive

This Pass 2 / Phase B opposition note closes a **self-referential coverage gap** created by Pass 1. Pass 1's Spore opposition bridge note (`spore.connection.what-the-commons-is-missing-opposition`) absorbed De Angelis's "filtering membrane" (De Angelis 2017a, 2017b) as part of the commons-boundary vocabulary used to critique absorption-without-operational-surface in Spore/IC/PM canon. But the P2P Foundation wiki **itself carries a published empirical refutation** of that exact concept — Sam Dallyn and Fabian Frenzel's *Antipode* article (2021, 53(3):859–883), `Limits of FairCoin as a Commons-Based Cryptocurrency` — which studies the FairCoin/FairCoop cryptocurrency project (explicitly designed around the filtering-membrane framing) and documents its collapse due to insufficiently clear commons boundaries, convertibility pressure toward a peg with external state-backed fiat currency, dependence on external-market valuation, and the inability of the "filter" to actually filter out market logic.

Dallyn & Frenzel propose four additional postcapitalist commons-boundary design principles as a corrective. This opposition note registers the refutation as opposition evidence against any Spore, IC, or PM canon that imports the filtering-membrane framing — or adjacent currency-design / mutual-credit / commitment-pooling primitives — without concurrently carrying the four design principles Dallyn & Frenzel specify. The disposition is `no change` for all affected canon: the wiki literature does not warrant a new primitive, it warrants a **declination** — a standing opposition edge against filtering-membrane absorption until the receiving canon independently carries the operational surface the FairCoin failure identifies as missing.

This note is the **empirical check** Pass 1 lacked. Pass 1 cited De Angelis but did not surface the FairCoin refutation that is native to the same wiki corpus. That asymmetry — absorbing a concept's theoretical frame while ignoring its in-corpus empirical refutation — is the specific gap this note closes.

## 1. De Angelis's filtering membrane as invoked by FairCoin designers

De Angelis (2017a *Omnia Sunt Communia*; 2017b) proposes the "filtering membrane" metaphor (drawn from cell biology) as a solution to the central problematic of postcapitalist commons: a commons operating *within* capitalism needs some capital inflow to survive, yet must *bound* that inflow so market logic does not displace commons values. The filtering membrane is "a selective filter which is intended to secure capital for certain items that cannot be acquired internally within the commons (such as electricity and heating), but with certain boundaries to prevent the erosion or weakening of the shared (postcapitalist) values of the commons itself" (Dallyn & Frenzel's paraphrase, §Why Did FairCoin Fail).

FairCoop/FairCoin (launched 2014) was designed with this framing explicit. Its intended function, in the words of one FairCoop activist interviewed by Dallyn & Frenzel, was to be "a membrane that interacts both with the capitalist system and with the Fair economy that is postcapitalistic or going towards postcapitalistic ... That doesn't mean that it completely 100% prevents capitalistic value extraction, it just means that it prevents value extraction of capitalistic behaviour so that its more that you get a value benefit into the Fair economy." The filtering membrane was the load-bearing design primitive: FairCoin would let capital flow in from external cryptocurrency exchanges to sustain the commons, while its FairCoop-internal price would protect merchants and users from the volatility of external speculation.

Pass 1's Spore opposition note (`what-the-commons-is-missing-opposition`) invokes this exact De Angelis vocabulary — specifically "membrane" composition language — as part of the commons-adjacent surface Spore canon must not absorb without operational commitment. But Pass 1 did not cite the FairCoin refutation, even though the refutation is native to the same wiki corpus Pass 1 was intaking. That is the coverage gap this note now closes.

## 2. The four failure mechanisms Dallyn & Frenzel identify

The FairCoin project collapsed not on ideological grounds but through four interacting, mechanical failure modes. The collapse was empirical — traceable through the divergence of FairCoop's internal 1.2 Euro / FairCoin consensus price and the external cryptocurrency exchange price (which fell from around the 1.2-Euro range in January 2018 to 0.11 Euros per FairCoin by 19 March 2020 (FairPlayGround Statistics 2020) — a drop of roughly an order of magnitude from the official internal rate). Dallyn & Frenzel name the four mechanisms across §Boundaries in Postcapitalist Commons and §Why Did FairCoin Fail:

**(a) Unclear/insufficiently specified commons boundaries.** "FairCoop itself had no membership and was essentially open. The only recognised boundary was that a couple of FairCoop participants would need to vouch for people before they could become involved in governance and core tasks within the commons. So, in effect FairCoin had no boundaries around external trading and investment, while FairCoop had no membership and minimal commons boundaries in terms of who could participate." Without Ostrom's (1990) first design principle — "individuals or households who have rights to withdraw resources units from the CPR must be clearly defined, as must the boundaries of the CPR itself" — the filter could not filter: there was no bounded *inside* from which an *outside* could be distinguished.

**(b) Convertibility pressure toward external fiat.** FairCoop made commitments to exchange FairCoin for Euros at the internal consensus rate (up to 1000 Euros per month per active participant, subsequently reduced in 2019). But the commons itself had no mechanism to generate sustainable Euro inflow at that rate. "The expectation that FairCoop could continue to exchange FairCoin for Euros at the 1.2 Euros-a-FairCoin rate was clearly unsustainable." The commons's peg to Euros was the vector through which market logic re-entered — not through the "outside" but through an obligation the commons had voluntarily taken on.

**(c) External-market-valuation dependence (the two-price model).** FairCoop's initial strategy was to "hack" cryptocurrency exchanges: a rising external valuation of FairCoin would channel capital into the commons, allowing the internal consensus rate to rise with the external rate. "The functioning of the commons itself became dependent on an increasingly favourable valuation of FairCoin by capital, a problem that was compounded by insufficient commons boundaries." When Bitcoin (and FairCoin's market price tracking Bitcoin) fell in 2018, the commons's viability collapsed with the external rate — the filter did not hold; the external signal re-entered wholesale.

**(d) Arbitrage drainage through the price divergence.** Because FairCoin could be purchased cheaply on external exchanges (e.g., 0.11 Euros per FairCoin in March 2020), but FairCoop committed to exchange FairCoin to Euros at 1.2 Euros-a-FairCoin, any actor could buy FairCoin externally, buy FairCoop-priced goods (particularly high-value items like electric bikes), and have FairCoop drain its Euro reserves converting those FairCoin at the internal rate. "This problem of arbitrage was extenuated by the selling of certain high-end technological products in FairCoin, particularly electric bikes ... These FairCoin could easily have been acquired by consumers from external cryptocurrency exchanges at a fraction of the cost." The price-divergence gap, unbounded by membership verification, became a pump that drained the commons.

These four mechanisms *interacted*: no membership → arbitrage vector open → convertibility commitment → external-market dependence → collapse when external market turned. The filter, as designed, did not filter. The social ties of the commons frayed under the pressure: "look at what happened to us, now we are fighting each other because of the fucking Euros" (FairCoop activist Maro, Madrid, 2019).

## 3. The four alternative design principles Dallyn & Frenzel propose

From the FairCoin failure case, Dallyn & Frenzel deduce four additional commons-boundary design principles for postcapitalist commons that seek to scale (their conclusion, §Why Did FairCoin Fail):

**(i) Resilience to capital divergence and decreasing returns.** "An economic model that is as resilient as possible to a divergent evaluation by capital and decreasing capital returns." The sustainability of the commons must not depend on an *increasingly* favorable market valuation; the commons must absorb unfavorable valuations without collapsing. (Alternative proposals within FairCoop included the Fairo model — a 35%-above-market pricing unit for internal exchange — and the FairCredit mutual-credit model intended to remove FairCoin from external exchanges entirely.)

**(ii) Explicit value framing prioritized over private capital interests.** "A clear value framing in which the values around the maintenance of the commons are placed above private interests in securing capital, and this must be true for new participants as much as existing ones." FairCoop's values (Integral Revolution, P2P Collaboration, hacker ethics) were articulated but not made binding on participation; the commons admitted participants whose primary orientation was speculative upside.

**(iii) Peer-to-peer reputational feedback tied to resource allocation.** "Means to ensure that these commons principles are reflected in practices, we have suggested peer2peer reputational feedback as a potential way of doing this." Access to scarce resources (Euro conversion) should be earned via contribution, assessed by the peer community, rather than guaranteed to any "FairCoop participant" without contribution scaling. This is stricter than Ostrom's membership/sanction principle: it couples resource allocation to *graded* contribution assessment.

**(iv) Transparent accounting at the commons level.** "There must be transparency and clarity around the accounting process in which all participants are aware of how limited capital resources are and potential risks arising from this." FairCoop's reliance on the mystique of founder Enric Duran, combined with opaque accounting, meant unsustainable commitments were made that no single participant (even Duran) fully understood at the portfolio level.

These four principles, taken together, describe what a filtering membrane would actually need to *be* to filter — not as a metaphor but as an operational commitment surface. Any Spore/PM canon invoking filtering-membrane framing without concurrently specifying its positions on principles (i)–(iv) inherits the FairCoin failure surface by omission.

## 4. Why this is a self-referential closure of Pass 1

Pass 1's `spore.connection.what-the-commons-is-missing-opposition` names Joel Dietz's consensus-without-commitment critique, Wood/Brenner's market-dependence critique, and Conaty/Lewis's pricing-for-externalities critique — and invokes "membrane composition language" in its §4 cross-project opposition pattern as part of the Spore-canon surface it is opposing absorption of. But Pass 1 did not read `Limits of FairCoin as a Commons-Based Cryptocurrency`, did not cite Dallyn & Frenzel, and did not surface the four-principle corrective. The result: Pass 1's opposition to "filtering-membrane" absorption is framed as *general discipline* (operational commitment, livelihood specification, market-dependence position) without carrying the *specific* empirical failure surface the wiki itself provides.

That asymmetry — absorbing a concept's theoretical frame while ignoring its in-corpus empirical refutation — is exactly the pattern Pass 1 opposed in others' canon. This note makes the opposition symmetric: the filtering-membrane primitive is not just unworthy of absorption without operational commitment (Pass 1's point); it is a primitive with a published empirical failure case, native to the same corpus, whose failure modes are mechanical and specifiable (this note's point). Any future canon — Spore, IC, or PM — that imports filtering-membrane framing must carry the FairCoin failure surface *explicitly*, not merely the Dietz/Wood/Conaty general-discipline surface.

The `filtering-membrane` concept in the v2 frozen vocabulary (`docs/research/concepts-p2p-wiki.yaml:236–240`, frozen 2026-04-17) is labeled explicitly as *"De Angelis; refuted by FairCoin study"* and marked as an **opposition primitive**. This note is the evidence backing that label.

## 5. Tensions and cautions

1. **Not a refutation of boundary-commoning wholesale.** The FairCoin failure shows that the *filtering-membrane* metaphor, absent operational boundary-specification, does not hold. It does *not* show that boundary-commoning (the broader frozen concept) is untenable. Dallyn & Frenzel themselves propose alternative boundary mechanisms (mutual credit, graduated reputational access, transparent accounting) that remain within a boundary-commoning frame. The opposition is against *filtering-membrane as primitive*, not against commons-boundary design as such.

2. **Not a refutation of alternative currencies.** The paper notes the "postcapitalist peer2peer alternative currency space is an experimental and generative one with continuing different lines of flight" (Economic Space Agency, Fairo, Holo). The opposition is against the *specific* filtering-membrane + two-price + open-membership design pattern that FairCoin instantiated, not against mutual-credit, commitment-pooling, or commons-backed currency as such. PM's commitment-pooling and mutual-credit work can proceed — but must register the FairCoin failure surface as a known-risk lineage.

3. **Convertibility pressure is PM-relevant, not just Spore-relevant.** Mutual-credit systems that offer any form of pegged convertibility to external fiat face the same arbitrage-drainage pattern FairCoop faced. The PM design space must either (a) decline convertibility entirely, (b) gate convertibility behind membership and reputational access, or (c) absorb the FairCoin failure surface explicitly and name its own position. Silent accumulation of convertibility-adjacent language in PM grammar defaults to the FairCoin failure mode by omission.

4. **Opposition-vs-deferral conflation risk.** A no-change disposition for *opposition* reasons reads identically in the database to a no-change disposition for *insufficient-evidence* reasons. The stance-intent HTML comment in frontmatter flags the opposition intent for human readers; the projection script encodes it as an opposes edge per `project_bridge_notes.py:591`.

5. **Single-case generalization risk.** Dallyn & Frenzel themselves note "there are clearly limitations to deducing design principles in terms of postcapitalist commons boundaries from a close investigation of a single case." This note's R-claims are not derived from FairCoin alone — they compose the single-case failure with Ostrom's CPR principle (drawn across many cases), the Wood/Brenner market-dependence framing, and the broader P2P Foundation corpus. The opposition surface is cross-evidential, not single-case.

## 6. Why disposition is no change

Disposition `no change` here is an active opposition directive, not default deferral. The Dallyn & Frenzel refutation warrants stating opposition against filtering-membrane absorption in canon explicitly (per the R-claims below) while declining to add any new primitive, alias, or clarification. Adding a "filtering-membrane-done-right" primitive would inherit the failure mode the refutation names: substituting refined vocabulary for the operational change the critique demands. The script encodes this stance: `disposition=no-change → opposes edge`, per `project_bridge_notes.py:591`. The opposition edges retire only when the receiving canon independently demonstrates carriage of the four Dallyn-Frenzel design principles (resilience-to-divergence, binding value framing, reputational-feedback access, transparent accounting) — per-target, not globally.

## 7. Disposition

**Disposition: no change.** The wiki evidence warrants explicit opposition edges against (a) any Spore canon invoking filtering-membrane framing without operational boundary-specification, (b) PM mutual-credit / commitment-pooling canon that does not name its position on convertibility pressure, and (c) PM protocol surfaces that use membrane-adjacent framing without carrying the FairCoin failure surface.
