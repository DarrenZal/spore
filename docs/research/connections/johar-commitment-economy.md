# Johar — A Human Economy (II): From Wages to a Commitment Economy

**Source:** Indy Johar, "A Human Economy (II) : From Wages to a Commitment Economy," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/a-human-economy-ii-from-wages-to

## What It Is

An essay (1606w) arguing that the wage contract fails under discovery — work whose value is produced through navigation of the unknown rather than execution of the known. When organisations force discovery into wage grammar, they simulate certainty with planning theatre, mistake throughput for progress, and push risk onto individuals through informal overwork and precarious contracting. The essay proposes a replacement: the commitment economy, where the primitive unit shifts from "hours in a role" to "obligation-and-exposure-for-rights" — bounded responsibilities in changing fields, with governed standing, compensation, and participation rights. The structural vocabulary — standing, commitment signatures, contribution mixes, governed renegotiation — directly parallels Spore's commitment primitives.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Commitment as primitive:** Spore's grammar is built on commitment protocols — explicit, scope-bound obligations with provenance, evidence, and review. The essay proposes commitment signatures as the replacement for job descriptions: "explicit obligations to hold something that matters" with declared verification modes. The structural parallel is remarkably close.

2. **Standing and membership:** Spore's holon model includes membership — agents participate in holons with defined roles, rights, and obligations. The essay's concept of standing (institutional membership that provides a security floor and participation rights) maps to Spore's membership model. Standing is "the mechanism that turns 'who can afford to take the risk' into 'who can legitimately participate.'"

3. **Governance of scope change:** Spore's commitment lifecycle includes revision and renegotiation — commitments are not permanent but governed. The essay calls for "governed renegotiation for adaptive scope" as a core commitment-economy primitive, making scope change explicit and auditable rather than handled through informal politics.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Wage Grammar Fails Under Discovery

The essay's diagnostic: the wage contract performs four critical functions (security floor, legible obligation, risk placement on capital, fairness story) that work for predictable throughput but break under discovery. Discovery cannot be specified in advance, supervised conventionally, or reduced to output metrics. When organisations force discovery into wage grammar, the result is planning theatre, moral injury (uncertainty treated as personal failure), and legitimacy fracture.

For Spore, this matters because the grammar operates in discovery conditions — coordination under novelty where the work itself is partially unknown. The essay argues that economic forms must match the epistemic conditions of the work. Spore's commitment grammar is already designed for uncertainty; the essay proposes extending that design to economic participation.

### 2. Commitment Signatures as Replacement Unit

The essay proposes commitment signatures — explicit, bounded responsibilities with declared verification modes — as the replacement for job descriptions. Four types: delivery commitments (produce a capability), discovery commitments (reduce uncertainty through disciplined exploration), stewardship commitments (maintain coherence, care, legitimacy), and risk commitments (anticipate failure modes, preserve resilience).

This directly enriches Spore's commitment vocabulary. Spore has commitment protocols but does not distinguish commitment types by epistemic character. The delivery/discovery/stewardship/risk taxonomy names distinctions that Spore's grammar could carry — each type has different verification modes, temporal horizons, and governance requirements.

### 3. Standing as Participation Infrastructure

Standing is institutional membership inside a vehicle: protected by the risk pool, accountable to its norms, entitled to its rights. It is paired with a security retainer — a stable income floor conditional on maintaining good standing (minimal participation norms, transparency, integrity, responsiveness), not on completing predefined tasks.

For Spore, standing is the mechanism that makes coordination broadly participable under uncertainty. Without it, participation in discovery is restricted to those who can self-insure. This connects to the free-markets essay's argument that material sufficiency is a precondition for agent capability: standing is one structural implementation of that precondition.

## Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §1–2 wage contract and discovery]
The wage contract fails under discovery because its four functions (security floor, legible obligation, risk placement, fairness story) depend on work being specifiable in advance, supervisable in execution, and verifiable by output. Discovery breaks all three assumptions. When organisations force discovery into wage grammar, they produce planning theatre, spurious metrics, moral injury, and legitimacy fracture.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §3 commitment as replacement unit]
The commitment economy replaces hours-in-a-role with obligation-and-exposure-for-rights: bounded responsibilities in changing fields, with governed standing, compensation, and participation rights. The primitive unit is the commitment signature — an explicit obligation with declared verification mode. Four types: delivery (produce capability), discovery (reduce uncertainty), stewardship (maintain coherence), and risk (preserve resilience).

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §4 standing]
Standing is institutional membership that provides a security floor and participation rights without requiring that work be fully specifiable. It is conditional on participation norms (transparency, integrity, responsiveness), not on task completion. Standing is the mechanism that makes discovery participation broadly accessible rather than restricted to those with private buffers.

**C4** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §5–6 commitment signatures and contribution mixes]
Commitment signatures carry four structural properties: every commitment has an explicit verification mode (quantitative, qualitative, peer review, or procedural proof), declared scope and succession pathways, visible obligation-and-exposure terms, and governed renegotiation triggers. Compensation becomes a contribution mix — a layered bundle of rights rather than a single wage — distributing upside without forcing precarity.

Spore's project vision should name commitment economy as a framing concept for economic coordination under discovery: the structural account of why wage grammar fails when work is epistemic and what replaces it. The entry should include: (a) the diagnostic — wage contracts depend on specifiability assumptions that discovery violates, (b) commitment signatures as replacement primitives with four types (delivery, discovery, stewardship, risk), (c) standing as participation infrastructure that makes coordination broadly accessible under uncertainty, (d) governed renegotiation as a core economic mechanism parallel to Spore's commitment lifecycle, and (e) the direct connection to Spore's existing grammar — the commitment economy is the economic instantiation of the coordination pattern that Spore already provides for governance.
*R1 is supported by C1, C2, C3.*

## Open Questions

1. **Is Spore's commitment grammar already a commitment-economy grammar?** The structural overlap between the essay's proposals and Spore's existing primitives is substantial. Standing ≈ holon membership with security floor. Commitment signatures ≈ commitment protocols with verification modes. Governed renegotiation ≈ commitment lifecycle revision. Whether these are the same mechanisms or merely analogous — and whether Spore should explicitly claim the commitment-economy framing — is unresolved.

2. **Does the delivery/discovery/stewardship/risk taxonomy belong in Spore's grammar?** The essay proposes four commitment types differentiated by epistemic character. Spore currently treats commitments as generic. Adding a commitment-type taxonomy could enrich the grammar's expressiveness but also add complexity. Whether this taxonomy is universal or specific to economic coordination is unresolved.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate pattern** — The commitment economy names an economic coordination pattern that directly parallels Spore's governance grammar. The essay's vocabulary (commitment signatures, standing, governed renegotiation, contribution mixes) enriches Spore's existing commitment primitives with economic specificity. The structural overlap is the strongest of any Johar economy essay — this is not analogy but convergent design.

**Promotion status: defer** — Single-author source. Despite the remarkable structural parallel, the essay proposes a normative economic program, not an empirical description of existing practice. Promotion should wait for either: (a) convergence from another source that independently arrives at commitment-based economic primitives, or (b) production evidence from an organisation operating on commitment-signature governance (which would validate the structural claims).
