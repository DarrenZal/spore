# Constructive Hyperstition

A synthesis bridge note on the constructive/extractive distinction in hyperstition theory and how Spore's constitutional commitments offer candidate structural criteria for that distinction.

## The Problem the Gitcoin Article Identifies

The Gitcoin article distinguishes "productive" from "extractive" hyperstitions. Productive examples: Ethereum's "world computer" narrative, the solarpunk aesthetic, ReFi. Extractive examples: meme coins, speculative pump-and-dump schemes. The article argues we must choose carefully which fictions we encode into infrastructure.

But the article identifies this distinction without resolving it structurally. It appeals to judgment ("choose carefully") without specifying what criteria would ground the choice. The question remains: **what makes a hyperstition constructive rather than extractive?**

## Candidate Criteria from Spore's Constitutional Commitments

Spore's constitutional commitments — provenance, forkability, contestability, meaningful local autonomy, authorized boundary crossing, reviewable authority, pluralism with interoperability — offer candidate structural criteria for the constructive/extractive distinction:

**Provenance** — Can the narrative's origins, claims, and evidence chain be traced? Extractive hyperstitions typically obscure provenance (who benefits, what evidence supports the vision, what has been tried before). Constructive ones make their lineage legible.

**Forkability** — Can participants exit and take their contribution with them? Extractive coordination locks participants in (sunk cost, illiquidity, social pressure). Constructive coordination preserves the right to fork — sovereignty is real, not nominal.

**Contestability** — Can the narrative be questioned from within? Extractive hyperstitions require faith; dissent is treated as defection. Constructive ones build in mechanisms for objection, revision, and legitimate challenge.

**Meaningful local autonomy** — Does the shared narrative require total absorption, or can participants engage partially, on their own terms? Extractive hyperstitions demand all-in commitment. Constructive ones allow partial adoption and local variation.

**Authorized boundary crossing** — When the narrative crosses boundaries (between communities, between scales), is that crossing authorized and legible? Extractive hyperstitions spread virally without consent. Constructive ones propagate through declared, governed channels.

**Reviewable authority** — Who decides what the narrative means and how it evolves? Extractive hyperstitions concentrate interpretive authority. Constructive ones distribute it with explicit scope, escalation paths, and revocation conditions.

## Why These Are Candidates, Not Answers

These criteria are offered provisionally. They emerge from Spore's own design commitments, which means they carry Spore's assumptions about what makes coordination legitimate. A different coordination grammar — with different constitutional commitments — might draw the constructive/extractive line differently.

The criteria also do not address all dimensions of the problem. For example:
- **Temporal dynamics** — A hyperstition might start constructive and become extractive as it scales (or vice versa). Static criteria may not capture this.
- **Perspective dependence** — What looks constructive from inside a coordination scope may look extractive from outside it. The criteria assume a view from within the grammar.
- **Power asymmetries** — Even with provenance, forkability, and contestability formally available, material power differences can make them practically inaccessible.

## What This Suggests for Spore

The comparison with hyperstition theory does not suggest changes to Spore's grammar. Instead, it offers a new lens for communicating what the constitutional commitments are *for*: they are structural safeguards that keep coordination constructive as shared narratives scale. This is a framing insight, not a structural recommendation.

The disposition for this synthesis is: **no change** to Spore's core grammar. The constitutional commitments already provide the structural criteria. What the comparison adds is a communication frame — and the recognition that these criteria should be tested against concrete cases where the constructive/extractive line is contested.
