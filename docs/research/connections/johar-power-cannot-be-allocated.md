# Johar — Power Cannot Be Allocated: Designing Organizations for a Complex World

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Power Cannot Be Allocated: Designing Organizations for a Complex World," Dark Matter Labs / Substack, April 2026.
**URL:** https://substack.com/@indyjohar/p-193897671

## Core Argument (Johar)

Traditional organizations treat power as a **resource to be pre-allocated** — roles defined in advance, authority attached to titles, accountability bounded by structure. In complex environments, that assumption breaks. Johar's alternative: **power is enacted capacity** — it is constructed in relation to real situations by actors who can read what's emerging, interpret it, commit, and act.

**Three forms of organizational power:**
- **Allocational power** — structural stability (roles, responsibilities, boundaries). *Stabilizes.*
- **Expressive power** — life articulating meaning, desire, perception, and dissent within the system. *Vitalizes.*
- **Constructed power** — situational agency emerging dynamically in response to what presents itself. *Adapts.*

**Four enabling conditions** (leadership's actual job):
1. **Space** — room for interpretation without over-constraint. *"Without space, action collapses into compliance."*
2. **Mission** — shared directional alignment enabling distributed action without centralized control.
3. **Resources** — material access (time, tools, relationships, institutional support) to act meaningfully.
4. **Knowledge** — distributed comprehension across the organization, not centralized.

**Pre-reaction:** the ability to act *before* situations fully form — sensing emerging dynamics and responding to incomplete signals. Cannot be codified or role-assigned.

**The substitution trap:** "One can assign authority without generating comprehension. One can define responsibility without producing commitment." Structure supports power but cannot substitute for it.

## Synthesis

Johar's framework reframes organizational design from **role-as-carrier-of-power** to **field-as-condition-for-power**. A Spore node that pre-assigns what each participant can see and do based on a fixed role schema is enacting allocational power only — it will fail when emergent situations exceed predefined categories.

The stronger design target: Spore as a **field** where trust graphs encode current relational topology (not a static org chart), knowledge visibility is dynamically reconstructed, and federation events create space for **constructed power** — moments where actors across nodes encounter real situations and build coherence through engagement.

Johar's four enabling conditions become **evaluation criteria for Spore's interface design:**

| Condition | Spore design question |
|---|---|
| Space | Does the interface allow interpretation without forcing premature categorization? |
| Mission | Is there a shared directional layer (intents, goals) visible across the trust graph? |
| Resources | Can actors access the data they need relationally rather than by role? |
| Knowledge | Does the system distribute comprehension — can any node understand what others are seeing? |

### The Substitution Trap

There's a risk of designing Spore as if the right graph topology will *automatically produce* good collective agency. It can't. Spore's job is to create conditions. The rest is still irreducibly up to the actors. This should inform how Spore describes itself and what promises its design makes.

### Designing for Plural Balance

Spore needs to serve all three power forms:
- **Allocational** layer: stable graph structures, persistent relationships, legible roles when appropriate
- **Expressive** layer: affordances for articulating meaning, dissent, and desire
- **Constructed** layer: dynamic visibility, trust graph reconfiguration, encounter events, pre-reactive signal propagation

Over-engineering any one layer produces a recognizable failure mode. A Spore that's all allocational structure is a traditional org chart. A Spore that's all expressive affordance without structure is ambient noise.

### Relationship to Situational Truthing

The situational-truthing bridge asks: *can the interface enact situated knowledge?*
This bridge asks: *can the interface enable situated agency?*

Together they suggest a design target: **Spore as epistemological + agentive infrastructure** — a field in which situated actors can both know and act, without either being collapsed into a view-from-nowhere or a permission-from-above.
