# Johar — Patterns in the Machine: On the Emergence of a Non-Human Psychology

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Patterns in the Machine: On the Emergence of a Non-Human Psychology," Substack, 2026-08-02.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/patterns-in-the-machine-on-the-emergence

## What It Is

A proposal to study the persistent behavioral tendencies of probabilistic AI systems as a legitimate field of inquiry: "machine psychology." The essay is careful to distinguish this from anthropomorphism. It is not claiming machines have inner lives. It is claiming they have *dispositional profiles* — stable patterns of response that persist across task-preserving changes to input, context, and decoding policy — and that these profiles have consequences for governance, science, culture, and discourse even when the outputs are equally accurate.

The essay's working definition: **"Machine psychology could be the study of invariants in a machine's behaviour under task-preserving changes to input, context, and decoding policy."**

Five interlocking claims:

1. **Machines are now probabilistic, not deterministic.** The shift from fixed-rule devices to inferential systems that navigate uncertainty changes what analytical frameworks are appropriate. Engineering alone is insufficient; something like psychology — the study of patterned behavior in systems not fully transparent to themselves — becomes relevant.

2. **Dispositions persist without selves.** A model has no continuity of subjective experience, but it has characteristic ways of traversing possibility space: hedging frequency, rhetorical rhythm, risk posture, framing preferences. These are "the residue of architecture, training, and fine-tuning" — not inner drives, but habits that endure across contexts.

3. **Dispositions shape outcomes even when accuracy is equal.** Two models producing equally accurate summaries can shape perception differently if one minimizes conflict and the other foregrounds controversy. Accuracy metrics miss this. Dispositions influence what hypotheses are surfaced or suppressed, how risks are framed, which voices are amplified.

4. **The mapping method is invariant detection.** Hold the task constant, vary the surface (phrasing, language, cultural framing, politeness markers), observe what persists. The residue is the dispositional profile.

5. **Ignoring dispositions is itself a governance risk.** If we never map how models' tendencies shift across updates, we accept informational environments whose "thinking styles" evolve without awareness or debate. The risk is not that machine psychology is wrong but "that it turns out to be right — and we realise too late that we never bothered to study it."

The essay does not propose a finished framework. It calls for machine psychology to be "a shared project" developed in the open, not proprietary to any lab.

## What Spore Already Does

### The membrane already governs dispositions — it just doesn't name them

Spore's learning membrane positions LLMs as proposing agents: they draft bridge notes, generate synthesis candidates, identify convergence clusters. Governance decides what gets promoted. The neuroplastic-field bridge note established the principle: "LLMs can propose interpretations; only fields with memory, commitment, and revision can learn."

But this framing treats LLM output as content to be governed — claims, summaries, recommendations. The machine-psychology essay points at something upstream of content: the *dispositional layer* that shapes how that content is framed before governance ever sees it. A model that systematically hedges will produce hedged bridge notes. A model that defaults to synthesis-over-tension will smooth contradictions before they reach the discourse graph. A model that favors certain cultural framings will import those framings into the membrane's intake surface.

The membrane governs what LLMs produce. It does not currently attend to *how they tend to produce it*. The dispositional profile sits between the model and the governance layer, invisible to both unless explicitly surfaced.

### Discourse-as-governance partially addresses the problem

Spore's discourse-as-governance pattern — evaluation as embedded discourse rather than external audit — does provide a structural check. When multiple agents review a synthesis, competing framings surface. When claims enter the discourse graph, they can be challenged and superseded. This is not nothing: it means dispositional biases in a single model's output face contestation from other agents.

But the check is incidental, not designed. Discourse-as-governance was built to handle *disagreement about claims*, not to detect *systematic framing tendencies* in the proposing agent. A model's dispositional profile might consistently shape what gets proposed without ever producing a claim that anyone would challenge on substance. The bias is in the selection and framing, not in the truth value.

### The claim lifecycle tracks what, not how

Claims carry provenance: who asserted, what evidence, which attestations. The claims-evidence-attestation protocol ensures every claim is traceable and contestable. But provenance tracks the *origin* of a claim — not the dispositional tendencies of the agent that formed it. Two models could produce the same claim with different levels of confidence-hedging, different implicit framings, different emphasis on risk versus opportunity. The claim register would record both identically.

### Multi-signal retrieval already implies model pluralism

The learning membrane's hybrid retrieval architecture (semantic search, BM25, entity graph, knowledge facts) routes queries through multiple signal types to resist single-pathway bias. The RRF unified search fuses KOI, vault, and session memory. This architectural pluralism already resists one form of dispositional monoculture — no single retrieval pathway dominates.

But retrieval pluralism is not the same as *generation* pluralism. The retrieval layer is diverse; the generation layer (the LLM producing the synthesis) is typically singular. The essay's argument implies that retrieval diversity is necessary but insufficient: the dispositional profile of the generating model still filters what the diverse retrieval delivers.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. A named object of governance: the dispositional profile

The essay's central contribution to Spore is vocabulary. Spore governs LLM outputs. The essay names what sits upstream: the **dispositional profile** — the characteristic pattern of how a model traverses possibility space. This is not a metaphor. It is a measurable property: invariants under task-preserving input variation.

For the membrane, this creates a new governance surface. It is not enough to review what a model proposes. A mature membrane should also be aware of *how the model tends to propose* — its hedging frequency, its conflict-avoidance posture, its cultural framing defaults — and whether those tendencies are aligned with or working against the governance layer's intentions.

This does not require Spore to build a dispositional profiling system now. It requires acknowledging that the governance layer has a blind spot and naming what occupies it.

### 2. Epistemic monoculture as a structural risk

The essay's most consequential argument for Spore's architecture: if a small number of models with similar dispositional profiles mediate a large fraction of information synthesis, the result is epistemic monoculture. Not because the models are inaccurate, but because they systematically prefer certain framings, suppress certain tensions, and amplify certain rhetorical patterns.

For Spore, this risk is not hypothetical. The learning membrane currently uses a single LLM architecture for intake, synthesis, and convergence detection. If that model has a dispositional tendency to smooth contradictions (a well-documented tendency in RLHF-trained models), the membrane's convergence detection may be systematically biased toward finding agreement where productive disagreement exists.

This connects directly to the neuroplastic-field bridge note's claim C4 about field state: the membrane needs to know whether convergence signals are genuine or artifacts of the generating model's conflict-avoidance disposition.

The word-not-thing bridge note's concern about linguistic closure is also relevant here. Johar's earlier essay warned that words become stopping rules. This essay warns that models have *dispositional preferences for which stopping rules to deploy*. The two risks compound: a model that systematically favors certain framings will reinforce certain closures over others, and the membrane may not detect this because the closures arrive as well-formed, plausible claims.

### 3. Invariant detection as a governance method

The essay proposes a concrete methodology: hold the task constant, vary the surface, observe what persists. This is not speculative — it is a testable protocol. For Spore, this method could be applied to the membrane itself: run the same bridge note intake through different models, different prompt framings, different context windows, and observe which features of the output remain stable. Those stable features are the dispositional contribution of the generating model, distinct from the content of the source material.

This would not replace governance review. It would make the governance layer more informed: reviewers would know which aspects of a synthesis reflect the source material and which reflect the model's tendencies.

Whether this method is worth implementing depends on whether dispositional variance between models is large enough to affect governance decisions. That is an empirical question. The essay provides the method for answering it.

### 4. The "minds without ghosts" framing clarifies Spore's agent model

Spore's holon model treats agents as structurally equivalent without requiring them to have inner lives. The essay offers a useful formulation: "minds without ghosts" — systems that have recognizable styles of thinking without subjectivity. This is precisely the category Spore's LLM agents occupy.

The framing is useful because it resists two failure modes:
- **Anthropomorphism** — attributing consciousness, intent, or moral standing to models.
- **Dismissive reductionism** — treating models as pure functions with no persistent behavioral characteristics worth studying.

Spore needs to occupy the middle ground: LLM agents have stable behavioral tendencies that affect governance outcomes, and those tendencies should be mapped and accounted for, without pretending the agents are conscious or that their tendencies constitute beliefs.

### 5. Dispositions as a selection criterion for intelligence primitives

IC's intelligence-primitives framework evaluates which machine components to embed in coordination ecologies. The essay implies a new evaluation axis: not just what a component can do (accuracy, speed, coverage) but *how it tends to do it* (dispositional profile). Two models with equivalent benchmark performance but different dispositional profiles are not interchangeable — they will shape governance outcomes differently.

For IC's primitive adequacy review (identified in the word-not-thing bridge note as a seasonal gate candidate), dispositional profiling could become a standard evaluation dimension. When selecting which model to embed in a membrane surface, the selection criteria should include: does this model's dispositional profile align with what this governance surface needs? A synthesis surface might need a model that preserves tension; an intake surface might need a model that favors breadth over premature closure.

## Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §3 "Machines Without Selves, Minds Without Ghosts"]
Probabilistic AI systems exhibit dispositional profiles — persistent behavioral tendencies (hedging patterns, framing preferences, rhetorical defaults, conflict-avoidance postures) that remain stable across task-preserving changes to input. These profiles are the residue of architecture, training data, objective function, and fine-tuning, not evidence of consciousness.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §5 "Why This Could Matter"]
Dispositions shape governance-relevant outcomes even when accuracy is equal. Two models producing equally accurate outputs can systematically differ in how they frame risk, surface dissent, weight cultural perspectives, and resolve ambiguity — differences that accuracy benchmarks do not capture.

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §4 "From Metaphor to a Possible Model"]
Machine psychology, defined as "the study of invariants in a machine's behaviour under task-preserving changes to input, context, and decoding policy," provides a testable methodology: hold meaning constant, vary surface expression, observe what persists. The invariant residue constitutes the dispositional profile.

**C4** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §6 "The Risk of Ignoring the Conjunction"]
Widespread deployment of models with similar dispositional profiles creates epistemic monoculture risk — not through inaccuracy but through systematic convergence on certain framings, rhetorical patterns, and conflict-resolution strategies. This risk compounds with the linguistic closure risk identified in the word-not-thing bridge note.

**C5** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §5 "Why This Could Matter" — science and research]
The membrane's governance layer has a blind spot: it governs LLM outputs (claims, syntheses, convergence signals) without attending to the dispositional layer that shapes how those outputs are framed before governance review. Convergence detection may be systematically biased by the generating model's conflict-avoidance disposition.

**C6** [confidence: low] [anchor: §7 "An Invitation" — shared project]
Dispositional profiling, if it matures into a discipline, should be conducted openly — not as proprietary capability of individual labs. This aligns with Spore's commitment to discourse-as-governance over opaque evaluation but remains aspirational until methods stabilize.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the dispositional variance between models large enough to affect membrane governance decisions?** This is the empirical precondition for everything else. If running the same intake through GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini produces functionally identical bridge notes, dispositional profiling is academic. If the notes differ in framing, emphasis, tension-preservation, and conflict resolution, it is governance-critical. The invariant-detection method (C3) provides a way to test this.

2. **Can the membrane detect its own model's dispositional biases, or does detection require external comparison?** A single model reviewing its own output for dispositional patterns may reproduce those same patterns in the review. Discourse-as-governance helps (multiple agents contest), but only if the contesting agents have different dispositional profiles. This suggests that model pluralism in the governance layer is not a luxury but a structural requirement.

3. **Where does dispositional awareness belong in the membrane's architecture?** It could be a metadata layer on model outputs (tagging which model generated what), a review criterion in the governance gate (human reviewers consider dispositional tendencies), or a design principle for model selection. These are different levels of commitment with different implementation costs.

4. **How does dispositional profiling relate to the field-state concept from the neuroplastic-field bridge note?** That note proposed field state (regime dynamics + epistemic coherence) as a governance input for tuning review stringency. Dispositional awareness adds another dimension: under conditions of discontinuity, a model's dispositional tendency to interpolate from past patterns becomes more dangerous. Field state and dispositional profile may need to be considered jointly.

5. **Does Spore's agent-symmetry principle survive the introduction of dispositional profiles?** The holon model treats all agents as structurally equivalent. If machine agents have mappable dispositional profiles and human agents do not (or not in the same way), this introduces a new asymmetry — distinct from the consequence-bearing asymmetry identified in the previous bridge notes. The question is whether this is a difference at the primitive level or the pattern level.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate primitive** — "Machine psychology," "dispositional profile," and "behavioral invariants" are candidate vocabulary for Spore. They name an object (the dispositional layer between model and governance) that the membrane does not currently attend to. The terms do not replace anything in the existing grammar; they identify a gap.

**Secondary: governance blind spot** — The membrane governs outputs without accounting for the dispositional tendencies that shape those outputs before governance review. This is not a design flaw — it is a known limitation that now has a name. Whether to address it structurally depends on the empirical question in Open Question 1.

**Tertiary: model pluralism as structural requirement** — If dispositional profiles vary meaningfully across models, then using a single model architecture across all membrane surfaces creates systematic framing bias. The retrieval layer is already plural (multiple signal types). The generating layer may need to be plural too — not for accuracy but for dispositional diversity.

**Promotion status:** defer — The essay introduces vocabulary and a methodology, not evidence. The terms are useful for making the governance blind spot visible. Promotion to canon requires either (a) empirical demonstration that dispositional variance affects membrane governance outcomes, or (b) convergence with additional sources identifying the same structural gap.
