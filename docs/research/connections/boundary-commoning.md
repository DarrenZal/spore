# Boundary Commoning

This bridge note grounds the seed observation from `spore.connection.p2p-wiki-field-scan` — that "boundary commoning" is the cleanest external cousin of Spore's membrane/field composition vocabulary — against the P2P Foundation wiki's primary sources on commons governance and commons-vs-institution boundary conditions. It is the Tier 1b MVP of the wiki intake plan (`sequential-questing-sparrow`): the first bridge note exercising the wiki-RID-in-anchor metadata contract, and the gate on parallel fan-out to `protocol-society.md`, `knowledge-commons-governance.md`, and `commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit.md`.

## 1. What boundary commoning names

Massimo De Angelis (2017) introduces "boundary commoning" as a concept drawn from systems theory and cellular biology to describe coordination at the edges between distinct commons systems. Unlike within-commons governance (which manages a single commons' members, resources, and rules), boundary commoning describes how commons systems connect, compose, and reshape adjacent institutions without merging into a single commons. Benjamin Birkinbine's reading frames boundary commoning as an organizational model for "diverse and distributed commons-based movements" working toward common goals by "interweaving" through boundary commoning activity.

The concept is explicitly about **cross-commons composition**: the commoning that exists at the boundaries, that opens up boundaries, establishes connections, sustains commons ecologies, and can "reshape existing institutions from the ground up through commonalisation and create new ones" (De Angelis 2017, 24).

## 2. What Spore's field/membrane vocabulary already does

Spore's `field` and membrane vocabulary carries two kinds of work that boundary commoning also carries: (a) naming a bounded coordination surface with distinct rules, and (b) naming how those bounded surfaces compose without dissolving into a single larger surface. The P2P wiki provides external language with commons lineage for both — especially for (b), where "membrane composition" is an internal coinage while "boundary commoning" has published prior art traced to De Angelis, Ostrom-adjacent commons governance, and Birkinbine's movement-coordination framing.

Adopting boundary commoning as an alias does not replace Spore's vocabulary — it gives Spore a commons-audience-legible translation of the composition operations Spore already performs, with explicit scope separation from within-field rules.

## 3. The scope distinction Spore should import

The wiki prior art — Ostrom's design principles at `Commons_and_their_Governance` on one hand, and De Angelis's boundary commoning on the other — operates at two different scopes that Spore should keep distinct:

- **Within-commons / within-field scope**: clearly defined boundaries, congruence of appropriation rules with local conditions, collective choice arrangements, accountable monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms, minimal recognition of rights to organize. These are Ostrom's design principles for governing a single commons.
- **Between-commons / cross-field scope**: boundary commoning — establishing connections, sustaining commons ecologies, opening boundaries without merger.

These scopes are not competitors. They are composed: a federation of commons uses within-commons governance per commons AND boundary commoning between commons. Spore should state this explicitly in the field/membrane documentation so the coordination grammar doesn't conflate membership rules with composition rules.

## 4. Operational discipline: commons are not lightweight

The workshop discussion at `Commons_and_their_Governance` records the operational position bluntly: "Governing the commons is complex, but according to Ostrom, it is the only way and non evitable price that has to be paid to guaranty sustainable management of common resources." Monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution, and adaptive rules are load-bearing, not aspirational culture.

This directly rebuts any reading of Spore's collective-agency vocabulary as anti-governance. A coordination grammar that claims commons lineage must carry the operational surface the tradition specifies — or explicitly say it is doing something else.

Polycentric governance (from Ostrom's later work, surfaced in the workshop's conclusion) is the scale-up mechanism for commons governance under complexity: multiple decision centers with limited autonomy, formal interaction, adaptive rule systems. This sits closer to Spore's mycelial-holarchy architecture than does central-coordination or full-fragmentation framings.

## 5. The negative-boundary discipline Spore and IC should both import

Lanham's P2P Commons Boundary Conditions names the commons via negatives as well as positives. A commons is NOT a corporation (which has a defined purpose beyond useful sharing), NOT a co-op (which maximizes intrinsic asset value), NOT a state (which holds policing/enforcement beyond rudimentary social constraints), NOT a commune (which would be inconsistent with individual property rights). These negatives are governance discipline: they prevent false-equivalence drift when commons language gets explained to adjacent-institution audiences (corporate partners, co-op federations, state actors, commune-curious interlocutors).

This negative-boundary discipline is valuable for IC's memory-layers surface as well as Spore's field surface: an explicit list of what memory-layers / knowledge-commons infrastructure is NOT sharpens the governance surface the framing claims to cover.

## 6. Tensions (cautions for any Spore canon edit)

1. **Lanham's anti-institutional stance is stricter than Spore's stance.** Lanham treats "the role of parties, states, churches, co-ops, corporations, shareholding owners, and other collective action bodies" as "intentionally minimized" in commons governance. Spore composes with institutions (and extitutions) as part of multi-actor coordination. Any adoption of boundary-commoning language should state this difference plainly rather than implying full alignment with Lanham's position.
2. **De Angelis's boundary commoning may under-specify authorization operations** that Spore's membrane vocabulary carries (who speaks for the field, how cross-field claims are validated, what fails the authorization check). Adopting boundary commoning as alias is safe only if the authorization operations remain explicit in Spore canon.
3. **Scope drift risk**: conflating Ostrom's design principles (within-commons) with boundary commoning (between-commons) would produce a governance story that is either too strict at the boundary (applying within-commons sanctions across the boundary) or too loose inside (treating within-commons rules as cross-commons composition). Spore's field language needs both, kept distinct.

## 7. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The wiki evidence warrants adding "boundary commoning" as an external cousin / aliased cross-reference in Spore's field/membrane lexicon, importing the within-vs-between scope distinction to sharpen existing composition language, and explicitly naming polycentric governance as the scale-up logic in the mycelial-holarchy architecture. It also warrants IC importing the negative-boundary discipline for memory-layers. None of these clarifications require new canon primitives, which is consistent with the Move 0 moratorium (R-claims listed below do not become canon PRs during the clock).

## 8. Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Boundary_Commoning]
Boundary commoning (De Angelis 2017, via Birkinbine) names the commoning that exists at the boundaries between distinct commons systems — coordination that opens boundaries, establishes connections, sustains commons ecologies, and can reshape existing institutions from the ground up through commonalisation without merging the underlying commons into a single commons.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Boundary_Commoning]
Boundary commoning is positioned in the source tradition as the organizational substrate linking diverse commons-based movements into a hybrid movement of "connected subjectivities" — making it a coordination primitive for commons-of-commons composition, not only for single-commons governance.

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Workshop Report · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Commons_and_their_Governance]
Ostrom's design principles for robust common-pool resources — clearly defined boundaries, congruence of appropriation/provision rules with local conditions, collective choice arrangements, monitors accountable to appropriators, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms, and minimal recognition of rights to organize — are load-bearing operational governance for any commons claim, not aspirational culture.

**C4** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Commons_and_their_Governance]
The workshop's consensus position — "Governing the commons is complex, but according to Ostrom, it is the only way and non evitable price that has to be paid to guaranty sustainable management of common resources" — directly rejects commons-as-lightweight-coordination framings and requires any commons-adjacent claim to carry the monitoring / sanctioning / conflict-resolution surface explicitly.

**C5** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Commons_and_their_Governance]
Polycentric governance (multiple decision centers with limited autonomy, formal interaction, adaptive rule systems across interacting centers) is positioned in the same source as the scale-up mechanism for commons governance under complexity — rejecting both central coordination and full fragmentation as commons scale-up strategies.

**C6** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Positive Boundary Conditions · orn:p2p-wiki.page:P2P_Commons_Boundary_Conditions]
The P2P-commons tradition (Lanham) treats irrevocability of commons assets as a primary boundary condition: assets placed in the commons cannot be removed for personal gain, and the commons is dissolved (with prior-asset reuse maximized) rather than privatized if it can no longer be sustained — a stricter anti-capture stance than generic open-source licensing.

**C7** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Positive Boundary Conditions · orn:p2p-wiki.page:P2P_Commons_Boundary_Conditions]
The same tradition treats reduced hierarchical control and greater individual autonomy as first-order primary conditions — the role of parties, states, churches, co-ops, corporations, shareholding owners, and other collective-action bodies is intentionally minimized in commons governance — a stance stricter than Spore's composition-with-institutions framing.

**C8** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Negative Boundary Conditions · orn:p2p-wiki.page:P2P_Commons_Boundary_Conditions]
The commons is defined negatively as well as positively in the P2P-commons tradition: it is NOT a corporation (which has a defined purpose beyond useful sharing), NOT a co-op (which maximizes intrinsic asset value), NOT a state (which holds enforcement and public property), NOT a commune (which rejects individual property rights). These negatives are governance discipline against false-equivalence drift, not rhetorical boundary-policing.

**C9** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Boundary_Commoning]
Boundary commoning (between-commons) and Ostrom-style design principles (within-commons) operate at structurally different scopes and should be named as such in any governance-grammar import: Ostrom specifies coordination WITHIN a single commons; boundary commoning specifies coordination BETWEEN commons. Applying them to the same surface without the distinction conflates membership rules with composition rules and produces governance that is either too strict at the boundary or too loose inside.

## 9. Review Claims

**R1** [review claim] [target: spore.term.field] [concept: boundary-commoning]
Spore's field/membrane lexicon should formally acknowledge "boundary commoning" as the external cousin-concept for cross-field composition — adding an aliased lexicon entry that cites the De Angelis / Birkinbine source tradition and states the scope distinction explicitly: field/membrane rules govern coordination operations WITHIN a single field; boundary commoning names coordination operations BETWEEN fields without merger. The lexicon entry should preserve Spore's existing authorization-operation vocabulary (who speaks for the field, cross-field claim validation) because boundary commoning under-specifies those operations in the source tradition.
*R1 is supported by C1, C2, C9.*

**R2** [review claim] [target: spore.mycelial-holarchy-architecture] [concept: polycentric-governance]
The mycelial-holarchy architecture should explicitly name polycentric governance (multiple decision centers with limited autonomy, formal interaction, adaptive rule systems across interacting centers) as the scale-up logic for holon coordination under complexity. The architecture text should also import the operational surface Ostrom's design principles specify — clearly defined boundaries, congruence of rules with local conditions, collective choice, accountable monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms — as first-class architectural concerns, not as downstream implementation details. This directly rebuts any reading of the architecture as anti-governance or commons-as-lightweight-coordination.
*R2 is supported by C3, C4, C5.*

**R3** [review claim] [target: ic.memory-layers] [concept: knowledge-commons]
IC's memory-layers framing should import the P2P-commons negative-boundary discipline from Lanham: memory-layers is NOT a corporation (which would have a defined purpose beyond knowledge stewardship), NOT a co-op (which would maximize intrinsic asset value of accumulated memory), NOT a state (which would hold centralized enforcement over contributed memory), NOT a commune (which would reject individual property rights over contributed material). Stating these negatives in IC canon sharpens the governance surface memory-layers must cover, prevents false-equivalence drift when memory-layers is explained to adjacent-institution audiences, and aligns IC's externally-facing framing with the governed-knowledge-commons positioning recommended in the Tier 1a field-scan.
*R3 is supported by C6, C7, C8.*
