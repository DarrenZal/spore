# What the Commons Is Missing — Opposition

This Tier 3 tension note generates **opposition evidence** for the learning-field convergence board. Where Tier 1b and Tier 2 intake produced 59 R-directives — all supporting canon clarifications or extensions — this note records a cross-project critique the P2P Foundation wiki's own sources articulate: commons discourse, including its Spore/IC/PM-adjacent uses, can drift into *consensus without commitment*, *alignment without institutions*, and *market-friendly vocabulary without deciding whether the system reproduces market imperatives*. The wiki's Joel Dietz, the Ellen Meiksins Wood / Robert Brenner reading of market dependence, and the Pat Conaty / Michael Lewis *Resilience Imperative* framing of the social solidarity economy converge on the same diagnosis: elegant commons vocabulary is not load-bearing without operational commitment, sanctions, a viable livelihood model, and a named position on market-dependence. This note registers that tension as a **no-change** disposition against Spore, IC, and PM canon — opposing absorption of commons-adjacent language into any of the three canons where operational commitment, sanctions, and livelihood specification are not already concurrent.

The cluster's central thesis: the wiki literature on what the commons is missing does not recommend a *new* canon primitive for Spore, IC, or PM. It recommends a **declination** — Spore canon MUST NOT allow collective-agency or membrane-composition language to substitute for operational commitment; IC canon MUST NOT allow knowledge-commons or memory-governance language to substitute for sanctioned, resourced stewardship; PM canon MUST NOT allow coordination-substrate or commitment-pooling language to substitute for a stated position on market-dependence and on how participants actually sustain livelihoods inside PM-routed coordination. The disposition is *no change* for all three projects — the clusters do not warrant absorption; they warrant explicit declination.

## 1. Joel Dietz's five-point critique

Joel Dietz's *What the Commons Is Missing* (post-Berlin "Economics and the Commons" 2013) records a first-person account of attending the commons conference and coming away with "an eerie sensation that something was missing." His five points are operational, not aesthetic: (1) natural-ecosystem models of what commons should look like under optimal health are underspecified — "at times it seems we lack models for what an optimal ecosystem is and looks like"; (2) commons discourse substitutes *the effort to agree* for *the ability to debate* — "consensus without commitment is useless. A consensus that arises from a common commitment to build an ecosystem that can accommodate real people is valuable"; (3) the livelihood model is missing — "there seems to be a lack of viable hybrid models. Either you have a profit driven companies (AirBnB) or people that are in some way living off of state funding ... If a commons is to truly be built, we need more institutions that produce value, but are not driven by profit, and that ideally do not fuel themselves primarily via resources extracted via taxes"; (4) reactionary framing without positive vision — "too much is tied implicitly or explicitly to Marx, and there are far too few references to spirituality, aesthetics, positive health, and the great philosophical traditions of the non-Western world ... this often seems to make the movement at times more reactionary, in the sense of simply throwing off existing incompetent overlords, rather than casting a positive vision for the future"; (5) the world needs more do-ers — "can you prevent whole forests from being cut to the ground with mere words? I have the sense that many people are content to merely comment on these trends that are ripping apart the world. The world needs more do-ers."

Points (2) and (3) are the load-bearing ones for the Spore/IC/PM learning field: consensus-without-commitment and missing-livelihood-model. Spore/IC/PM canon, which composes commons-adjacent language throughout, inherits these critiques by proximity and must either state explicit declinations against the failure modes or accept that the critiques apply to the canon as written.

## 2. Ellen Meiksins Wood on market-dependence

The `Market Dependence Theory` page records Wood's distinction (Monthly Review 1999, citing Robert Brenner): "the difference between market opportunities and market imperatives, or market-enablement and market-dependence." Brenner's definition of capitalism — "a system in which 'economic units—unlike those in previous historical epochs—must depend on the market for everything they need'" — positions market-dependence not as an incidental feature but as the *defining* characteristic of capitalism. Wood makes the framing operational: market-dependence and subjection to competition have "explanatory status distinct and apart from, even prior to, the relation between capital and labor."

This is a sharp framing for Spore/IC/PM canon: any coordination grammar that uses market-adjacent language (matching, routing, exchange, pooling) without specifying whether participants are market-*enabled* (can use the market when they want) or market-*dependent* (must use the market regardless of preference) is taking a political position by silence. Wood's framing does not demand any of the three projects adopt a specific position on market-dependence — it demands that any of them invoking market-adjacent language state *which* position they are taking. Silence defaults to reproducing market imperatives, which collapses the distinction from capitalism proper.

## 3. The Five Framing Conditions reading of social solidarity economy

Pat Conaty and Michael Lewis's *Resilience Imperative* (on `Five Framing Conditions for a Commons-Oriented Economy`) extends the critique operationally. Four headline framings in their reading: Reclaiming the Commons, Reinventing Democracy, Constructing a Social Solidarity Economy, and Pricing As If People and the Planet Mattered. On solidarity: they recover it as "a framework for designing and implementing strategies that strengthen the resilience of communities, regions, and societies" — not a value-statement but a *design framework*. On the social solidarity economy: it is "the economic expression of civil society's social consciousness" and "reciprocity should be the central economic principle that shapes the management of markets, trade, and capital." On pricing: "If humanity had to pay for the consequences of a degraded eco-system the bill could ... run to about $47 trillion." The fourth framing — pricing externalities — treats ecological cost as operational measurement that institutions must compute, not rhetorical invocation.

Conaty and Lewis also carry Raj Patel's observation that "when negative externalities are not paid for, the beneficiaries are in effect engaging in theft from those who bear the cost of their behaviour" — establishing that governance vocabularies that claim sustainability lineage without specifying the measurement operations are complicit by omission. For Spore/IC/PM: coordination-grammar claims that invoke ecological or commons lineage without specifying the pricing/measurement/externality-accounting operations are in the same category Patel names — beneficiaries of unpriced externalities, under a softer framing.

## 4. The cross-project opposition pattern

These three wiki sources — Dietz on missing-institutions-and-commitment, Wood/Brenner on market-dependence, Conaty/Lewis on pricing-for-externalities and social-solidarity framing — converge on a single opposition pattern the Spore/IC/PM canons should register as a *no-change* position:

- **Spore opposition**: Spore canon MUST NOT allow collective-agency, field, membrane, or holon language to substitute for operational commitment-sanctions-livelihood specification. Commons-adjacent framings in Spore canon that invoke the vocabulary without carrying the Dietz-critique surface fail the test and MUST NOT be absorbed as clarifications.
- **IC opposition**: IC canon MUST NOT allow knowledge-commons, memory-layers, or intelligence-primitives language to substitute for sanctioned, resourced, livelihood-modeled stewardship. Invocation of commons lineage for IC without the institutional-crystallization surface fails the test and MUST NOT be absorbed as an alignment move.
- **PM opposition**: PM canon MUST NOT allow coordination-substrate, matching, routing, pool, or commitment-pooling language to substitute for a stated position on market-dependence and for a specified livelihood model for PM participants. PM MUST decline to absorb commons-adjacent language without the Wood/Brenner surface.

This is the cluster's distinctive feature relative to all prior Tier 1b/2 work: prior clusters worked inside individual project canons and across adjacent clusters; this cluster names a cross-three-projects opposition that can only be expressed as a coordinated *declination* on all three canons.

## 5. Why disposition is no change

Disposition `no change` here is not default deferral — it is an active opposition directive. The critique warrants stating the declinations explicitly in canon-facing language (per the R-claims below) while declining to add any new primitive, alias, or clarification. The alternative — absorbing the critique as a set of positive clarifications — would inherit the very failure mode the critique names: substituting elegant vocabulary ("Spore now clarifies its commitment surface...") for the operational change the critique demands (actually specifying commitment, sanctions, livelihood, market-dependence position). The script encodes this stance: `disposition=no-change → opposes edge`, per `project_bridge_notes.py:591`.

## 6. Tensions (cautions for any reader of this opposition note)

1. **Opposition-vs-deferral conflation risk**: A no-change disposition for *opposition* reasons reads identically in the database to a no-change disposition for *insufficient-evidence* reasons. This note documents the opposition reason explicitly so downstream readers do not treat the edges as low-signal deferrals. The stance-intent HTML comment in frontmatter flags the intent for human readers; the projection script encodes it as an opposes edge per the line-591 rule.
2. **Reactionary-reading risk**: Declining to absorb commons-adjacent language into Spore/IC/PM canon could be misread as siding with the anti-commons position. It does not. The opposition is specifically against *absorption without the operational surface*, not against commons framing itself. If and when Spore/IC/PM canon carry the operational surface Dietz, Wood/Brenner, and Conaty/Lewis specify (commitment, sanctions, livelihood, market-dependence position, pricing-for-externalities), the disposition can shift.
3. **Cross-three-projects coordination risk**: This note registers opposition edges to three project canons simultaneously. Any canon shift in one project that addresses its opposition claim does not automatically retire the opposition in the other two projects — each canon must independently clear the operational-surface test for its specific R-claim to be retired.
4. **Self-application risk**: This opposition note itself carries a vocabulary surface without the operational commitments it demands of others. Readers should note: the note does not propose canon language; it names declinations. This is structurally different from an R-claim that adds vocabulary to canon — no new canon language is generated by accepting these R-claims.

## 7. Disposition

**Disposition: no change.** The wiki evidence warrants explicit opposition edges against Spore, IC, and PM canon on the consensus-without-commitment, missing-livelihood-model, and unstated-market-dependence-position failure modes. No canon primitives, aliases, or clarifications are added. The script encodes the opposition stance via `disposition=no-change` per `project_bridge_notes.py:591`. This stance may shift when and if each target canon demonstrates operational-surface carriage of the Dietz/Wood-Brenner/Conaty-Lewis critique independently.

## 8. Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:What_the_Commons_Is_Missing]
Joel Dietz's post-Berlin commons critique (Economics and the Commons Conference 2013) records a first-person operational diagnosis — "despite near consensus among interested parties on the importance of the 'commons' and 'commoning,' I had an eerie sensation that something was missing ... I suspect that tangible outcomes would be limited, that something more was needed to bring people together" — establishing that near-consensus in commons discourse is not a legitimizing signal but a warning signal when tangible outcomes and operational commitment surface are absent.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:What_the_Commons_Is_Missing]
Dietz's consensus-without-commitment formulation is explicit: "Consensus without commitment is useless. A consensus that arises from a common commitment to build an ecosystem that can accommodate real people is valuable. If you abandon critical thinking and feedback mechanisms, you get agreement that does not include tangible outcomes" — making the presence of critical-feedback mechanisms and of common-commitment a load-bearing test for any commons-adjacent governance claim, not an optional discipline.

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:What_the_Commons_Is_Missing]
Dietz's missing-livelihood-model critique specifies the operational gap concretely: "sustainable solutions must also include a way for the people who live in the ecosystem to have all the necessary resources needed to live. For most of this includes currency of some kind. Here there seems to be a lack of viable hybrid models. Either you have a profit driven companies (AirBnB) or people that are in some way living off of state funding ... If a commons is to truly be built, we need more institutions that produce value, but are not driven by profit, and that ideally do not fuel themselves primarily via resources extracted via taxes" — establishing that coordination-grammar claims without a specified livelihood/resource-flow model for participants are structurally incomplete under the commons tradition's own self-critique.

**C4** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:What_the_Commons_Is_Missing]
Dietz's reactionary-framing critique — "too much is tied implicitly or explicitly to Marx, and there are far too few references to spirituality, aesthetics, positive health, and the great philosophical traditions of the non-Western world ... this often seems to make the movement at times more reactionary, in the sense of simply throwing off existing incompetent overlords, rather than casting a positive vision for the future" — names a specific failure mode (anti-oppressor framing without positive-vision specification) that applies to coordination grammars that define themselves primarily against extant institutions without specifying what the grammar is positively for.

**C5** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:What_the_Commons_Is_Missing]
Dietz's do-ers-not-commenters critique — "can you prevent whole forests from being cut to the ground with mere words? I have the sense that many people are content to merely comment on these trends that are ripping apart the world. The world needs more do-ers" — establishes the practical-action test as a final gate for commons-adjacent claims: vocabulary and analysis without enacted coordination fails the test, independent of the elegance of the vocabulary or the rigor of the analysis.

**C6** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Context · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Market_Dependence_Theory]
Ellen Meiksins Wood's market-dependence framing (Monthly Review 1999) distinguishes "market opportunities and market imperatives, or market-enablement and market-dependence" as a politically-consequential distinction — establishing that any coordination grammar invoking market-adjacent language (matching, routing, exchange, pooling) must specify which of the two positions it occupies, and that silence on the distinction defaults to reproducing market imperatives by omission.

**C7** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Market_Dependence_Theory]
Robert Brenner's definition of capitalism (via Wood) — "a system in which 'economic units—unlike those in previous historical epochs—must depend on the market for everything they need'" — gives market-dependence "explanatory status distinct and apart from, even prior to, the relation between capital and labor," making market-dependence a primary rather than derivative feature of capitalism; coordination-grammar claims using market-adjacent language without stating a market-dependence position are taking a position by silence that collapses the distinction from capitalism proper.

**C8** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Market_Dependence_Theory]
Wood's explanatory-priority claim — market-dependence and subjection to competition are "prior to the relation between capital and labor" — rebuts coordination-grammar framings that treat market-adjacent language as neutral infrastructure to be composed with any labor-relation, and establishes that the market-dependence vs. market-enablement position must be named at the infrastructure layer, not left for downstream specification.

**C9** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Reclaiming the Commons · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Five_Framing_Conditions_for_a_Commons-Oriented_Economy]
Pat Conaty and Michael Lewis (*Resilience Imperative*, citing Elinor Ostrom) establish that "centralized, distant, and locally unaccountable power cannot accomplish the transition to low-carbon, ecologically sustainable communities ... resilience requires a quality of social capital — trust, collaboration, cooperation, and leadership — rooted in the places where people live" — rebutting coordination-grammar claims that operate primarily at abstract/translocal scale without specifying how locally-rooted trust-and-leadership operations are sustained inside the grammar.

**C10** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Constructing a Social Solidarity Economy · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Five_Framing_Conditions_for_a_Commons-Oriented_Economy]
The social-solidarity-economy framing (Conaty/Lewis, via John Pearce's third-system typology) specifies reciprocity as "the central economic principle that shapes the management of markets, trade, and capital" — making reciprocity a measurable, position-bearing principle rather than a rhetorical stance, and establishing that any coordination grammar invoking commons lineage must specify how reciprocity-measurement and the three-system composition (private / public / third-system) are operationalized, not only named.

**C11** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Pricing As If People and the Planet Mattered · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Five_Framing_Conditions_for_a_Commons-Oriented_Economy]
The pricing-for-externalities framing (Conaty/Lewis, citing Raj Patel) names the operational test: "when negative externalities are not paid for, the beneficiaries are in effect engaging in theft from those who bear the cost of their behaviour ... If humanity had to pay for the consequences of a degraded eco-system the bill could ... run to about $47 trillion" — establishing that coordination-grammar claims invoking ecological or commons lineage without specifying the externality-pricing, measurement, and accounting operations are complicit in the unpriced-theft pattern Patel names, independent of the grammar's stated intent.

**C12** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Pricing As If People and the Planet Mattered · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Five_Framing_Conditions_for_a_Commons-Oriented_Economy]
Conaty/Lewis's leadership-failure framing (citing Thomas Friedman) — "markets don't price externalities when they don't have to. It is a leadership failure" — rebuts coordination-grammar claims that frame externality-accounting as a technical-infrastructure problem solvable by better data-pipelines alone, and establishes that without a stated institutional-leadership commitment to price-in externalities, the grammar reproduces the leadership-failure pattern rather than correcting it.

**C13** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Reinventing Democracy · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Five_Framing_Conditions_for_a_Commons-Oriented_Economy]
The Conaty/Lewis reading of Bowles/Gintis establishes that "the experience of individuals as economic actors is a major determinant of their personal capacities, attitudes, choices, interpersonal relations, and social philosophies" and that narrowed economic-actor choices (consumption + renting out labor) are "antithetical to the development of democratic culture" — rebutting coordination-grammar framings that treat participant-agency as a given input rather than as an output shaped by the grammar's own economic surface; grammars that do not specify how they preserve or extend participant democratic-cultural capacity inherit the narrowing pattern by default.

**C14** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §lead · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Category:Commons_Economics]
The P2P Foundation wiki's Category:Commons Economics corpus-level framing — "Commons Economics doesn't start with the scarcity paradigm, but with maintaining and growing abundance through mutualization and sharing resources, and it looks systematically for win-win strategies" — establishes that the commons-economics tradition the Spore/IC/PM learning field draws on has an explicit *starting* discipline (abundance/mutualization, not scarcity/competition) that coordination grammars invoking commons lineage must either adopt or explicitly decline; silent invocation without the starting discipline replicates the neoclassical-neoliberal assumption the category explicitly rejects.

**C15** [confidence: low] [anchor: §Key Concepts of This Category · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Category:Commons_Economics]
The corpus-breadth evidence at Category:Commons Economics positions commons-based economics as requiring specific discipline — "What pricing is for the market, and planning is for state-driven economies, so is mutual coordination for commons economics" — making mutual coordination a category-defining operation rather than a decorative addition; coordination grammars invoking commons lineage without the mutual-coordination operational surface (beyond the term itself) fail the category's own test for belonging to the commons-economics tradition rather than merely borrowing its vocabulary.
