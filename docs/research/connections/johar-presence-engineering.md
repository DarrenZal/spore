# Johar — Presence Engineering: Designing for an Explosion of Intelligence

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Presence Engineering: Designing for an explosion of intelligence," Substack, Aug 2025.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/presence-engineering-designing-for

## What It Is

A design-paradigm essay proposing "presence engineering" as the successor to process engineering. Where process engineering externalizes intelligence into scripts, presence engineering designs the *conditions* under which agents can exercise situated discernment. The essay does not prescribe an architecture; it names a design orientation and a set of characteristics that implementations would need to satisfy.

The argument has three layers:

1. **A triadic developmental logic.** Intelligence operates through sensing (staying in live relationship with reality), not-knowing (operating with epistemic humility under irreducible uncertainty), and crafting (embodied, improvisational responsiveness). These three are not sequential stages but concurrent modes that presence engineering must sustain simultaneously.

2. **Five core characteristics.** A presence-engineered system moves: (a) from procedural abstraction to contextual coherence, (b) from linear execution to feedback-rich recursivity, (c) from compliance-based coordination to sovereign sense-making, (d) from prescriptive action to craft-enabled responsiveness, (e) from single-axis optimization to inter-dimensional intelligence.

3. **A paradigmatic shift from control to coherence.** The essay's central claim: coherence replaces control as the coordination mechanism. Coherence is "a living, relational alignment achieved through sensing, feedback, and situated discernment." This is not anti-structure; it is "structure in dialogue with reality."

The essay ends with eight open questions about condition engineering, ethical contexts, economies of presence, and the relationship between context engineering and presence engineering. These are genuinely open -- the essay treats them as research agenda, not rhetorical devices.

**Relation to the neuroplastic-field essay:** The neuroplastic-field essay (`spore.connection.johar-neuroplastic-field`) diagnosed the problem -- LLMs are fixed inference, the field is the adaptive unit. Presence engineering names what the field should be *designed for*: conditions that sustain situated intelligence rather than processes that externalize it. The two essays are complementary: one identifies where plasticity lives, the other describes what the arrangement should cultivate.

## What Spore Already Does

### Discourse-as-governance is sovereign sense-making, operationalized

Johar's third characteristic -- "from coordination through compliance to sovereign sense-making" -- describes precisely what discourse-as-governance provides. Spore's discourse graph is the structured medium through which agents interpret, decide, and act with integrity in relation to dynamic systems. The discourse graph does not dictate outcomes; it distributes epistemic authority by giving proposals, arguments, evidence, and decisions equal structural standing regardless of which agent produces them.

The match is structural, not cosmetic:

- **Sovereign sense-making** requires that agents "hold multiple perspectives, respond in context, and take responsibility for one's interpretations." Spore's discourse grammar achieves this through contestability -- disagreement engages specific claims with specific evidence, not through power dynamics or compliance hierarchies.
- **Graduated formality** (personal / project / federation) is Spore's version of contextual coherence -- the discourse medium adapts its structure to the scale and stakes of the decision, rather than imposing a universal protocol.

Where the match is imperfect: Johar emphasizes *emotional* and *somatic* sensing as integral to sovereign sense-making. Spore's discourse graph is entirely propositional -- claims, evidence, attestations. It has no structural affordance for registering affective or embodied signals. This is not necessarily a gap (Spore may be correctly scoped to the propositional layer), but presence engineering would ask whether the propositional layer alone is sufficient for genuine discernment.

### The learning membrane's staged promotion is a form of not-knowing governance

Johar's second triad element -- not-knowing -- describes "the ability to act with clarity and integrity inside ambiguity, without denying complexity or collapsing into paralysis." The learning membrane's four surfaces (personal intake, project semantic memory, governance memory, production proving ground) implement this by refusing to collapse inference into doctrine.

LLM-generated bridge notes, convergence clusters, and synthesis proposals enter at the lowest surface. They are not treated as knowledge; they are treated as structured expectations awaiting governance review. Promotion requires human attestation, evidence linking, and governance decision. The membrane metabolizes uncertainty by giving it a structured place to sit -- draft status, deferred promotion, open questions -- rather than forcing premature resolution.

This is precisely Johar's "coherent not-knowing": acting (the membrane processes, generates, proposes) without collapsing (nothing is promoted to canon without governance decision).

### Commitment pooling is a sensing/feedback field

Johar's first characteristic -- contextual coherence -- requires "attunement to local conditions, responsive structures, and affordances that invite real-time orientation." The commitment pool is such a structure. Needs and offers enter the pool as vectors; routing computes field-level alignment rather than pairwise matching. The pool senses what the ecology needs and what it can supply, and routes accordingly.

The pool's lifecycle (PROPOSED through REDEEMED, with a DISPUTED branch) is a feedback-rich recursive system -- Johar's second characteristic. Evidence attachment, dispute resolution, and redemption are all feedback loops that modulate future commitment behavior.

But the pool currently operates at the commitment level, not at the sensing level. It processes commitments that have already been articulated. Johar's presence engineering asks about the prior layer: what conditions enable agents to sense what commitments are worth making in the first place? Spore's intent publication and activation pattern addresses some of this, but the question of whether the ecology sustains adequate *sensing conditions* is not explicitly asked anywhere in the grammar.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. A name for the design orientation Spore implies but has not declared

Spore's architecture is built around conditions rather than scripts. The coordination grammar does not prescribe what agents should do; it provides the structural conditions under which coordination can emerge. The commitment lifecycle does not dictate outcomes; it creates conditions for accountability. Discourse-as-governance does not enforce decisions; it creates conditions for structured reasoning.

This is presence engineering. Spore has been doing it without naming it.

The value of the name is not branding. It is design-level clarity: when evaluating a proposed feature or pattern, "does this engineer conditions or prescribe behavior?" is a useful discriminator. Features that prescribe behavior are process engineering; features that cultivate conditions for situated response are presence engineering. Spore should be systematically on the conditions side.

Whether "presence engineering" itself enters the Spore lexicon is a separate question. The design orientation it names is already present; the term is a candidate for making it explicit.

### 2. Contextual coherence as a named design criterion

Johar defines contextual coherence as "structure in dialogue with reality" -- intelligence that is situated rather than generic, responsive to time, place, history, and lived complexity. This is a useful criterion for evaluating whether Spore's patterns remain context-sensitive as they scale.

The risk Johar identifies: abstraction kills contextual coherence. Universal protocols, by definition, ignore context. Spore's grammar is abstract (it defines primitives, not implementations), but its patterns are meant to be instantiated contextually (a Victoria pool operates differently from a Cascadia-wide pool). The criterion asks: does each layer of Spore's architecture preserve room for contextual adaptation, or does abstraction accumulate until local intelligence is squeezed out?

This is not a criticism of Spore's current state. It is a standing question that presence engineering makes explicit.

### 3. Craft-responsiveness as a gap indicator

Johar's fourth characteristic -- craft-enabled responsiveness -- describes "embodied epistemology, a method of knowing through doing." This is the dimension where Spore's grammar is thinnest. The coordination grammar is deliberative: agents sense, interpret, claim, attest, intend, commit, coordinate, act, revise. This loop privileges the propositional-deliberative mode of intelligence.

Craft -- knowing through making, adapting in real time, improvisational responsiveness -- operates beneath and alongside deliberation. An agent in the middle of fulfilling a commitment is not pausing to file attestations; they are responding to materials, conditions, and emerging constraints in a way that the deliberative grammar cannot fully capture.

This does not mean Spore should try to formalize craft. Formalizing craft is precisely the process-engineering move that presence engineering rejects. But it does suggest that Spore's patterns should be evaluated for whether they *obstruct* craft -- whether the governance overhead of structured deliberation creates friction that undermines situated responsiveness.

### 4. Condition engineering as the implementation layer question

The essay's open question 4 asks: "What is the role of condition engineering -- the creation of environments, rhythms, affordances, and structures that support sovereign responsiveness?" This maps to an unanswered question in Spore's architecture: what sits between the grammar (abstract coordination primitives) and the running system (an actual deployment with agents, pools, and discourse)?

The grammar defines what coordination looks like structurally. Condition engineering asks what the deployment environment must provide -- rhythms (how often does the membrane cycle?), affordances (what makes it easy for agents to publish intents?), structures (what feedback loops exist outside the formal governance process?) -- for the grammar to produce coherent behavior rather than bureaucratic overhead.

Spore has not named this layer. The instance model (`spore-instance-model`) describes canon, node, agent, and site decomposition, but does not address the environmental conditions under which instances sustain presence. Condition engineering may be where this gap lives.

### 5. The control-to-coherence shift as a cross-project principle

Johar's central claim -- "from control to coherence" -- is implicit in both Spore and IC. Spore's grammar does not control agents; it enables coherent coordination. IC's membrane does not control what enters production; it enables coherent maturation of claims. The commitment pool does not control routing; it enables coherent composition of forces.

Stating this explicitly as a cross-project principle has diagnostic value. When a proposed feature introduces control (a gatekeeper that can unilaterally block, a rule that overrides local judgment, an optimization that narrows what agents can do), the principle flags it. Not every control mechanism is wrong -- the membrane's promotion gates are control mechanisms that serve coherence -- but the default orientation should be toward coherence, with control as the exception that requires justification.

## Open Questions

1. **Should "presence engineering" enter the Spore lexicon, or is the design orientation sufficient without the term?** The orientation (conditions over scripts, coherence over control) is already embedded in the architecture. Adding the term makes it inspectable; not adding it avoids importing vocabulary from an external frame that may carry connotations Spore does not endorse.

2. **Does Spore's propositional grammar need a non-propositional complement?** The discourse graph handles claims, evidence, and attestations. Presence engineering foregrounds emotional sensing, somatic intelligence, and affective attunement. If Spore's scope is correctly limited to propositional coordination, this is not a gap. If coordination *requires* non-propositional signals to remain coherent, then the grammar has a structural blind spot.

3. **Where does condition engineering fit in the instance model?** The instance model decomposes into canon, node, agent, and site. Condition engineering -- rhythms, affordances, environmental structures -- does not map cleanly to any of these. Is it a property of the node (each deployment configures its own conditions)? A property of the site (the interface layer shapes what agents can sense)? Or a separate concern that the instance model should name?

4. **Can coherence be distinguished from consensus without explicit criteria?** Johar's coherence is "living, relational alignment." Spore's discourse-as-governance does not prescribe a decision procedure (consent, consensus, majority). But if coherence is the coordination mechanism, how does the ecology know when it has achieved coherence as opposed to premature consensus or comfortable conformity? What signals indicate genuine coherence vs. its mimicry?

5. **How does the sensing/crafting triad relate to the coordination loop?** Spore's loop is: Sense, Interpret, Claim, Attest, Intend, Commit, Coordinate, Act, Revise. Johar's triad is: Sensing, Not-Knowing, Crafting. The Sense phase maps to Sensing; the Revise phase (via discourse-as-governance) maps loosely to Not-Knowing. Crafting maps to Act, but Act in Spore's loop is a single phase, not a sustained mode. Is the coordination loop too discrete for what presence engineering describes as continuous responsiveness?

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate primitive** — "Presence engineering" names a design orientation that Spore's architecture embodies but has not articulated. The term is a candidate for IC's or Spore's primitive vocabulary. It arrives from outside Spore's lineage and requires evaluation on its own terms before any promotion.

**Secondary: standing design criterion** -- Contextual coherence (C6) and the control-to-coherence shift (C3) are candidate criteria for evaluating proposed patterns and features. They formalize what Spore already does implicitly.

**Tertiary: gap indicator** -- Craft-responsiveness (C4) and condition engineering (C5) point to areas where Spore's architecture is thin. These are not defects requiring immediate action; they are named gaps that production experience should inform before any design response.

**Promotion status:** defer -- this is new vocabulary from a single source. No review claims are minted. The concepts need a second independent source or production evidence of the named gaps before promotion to canon is warranted.
