# Johar — Identity Formation in an Entangled World: From Boundaries to Inter-Becoming

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Identity formation in an Entangled World: From Boundaries to Inter-Becoming," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/identity-formation-in-an-entangled

## What It Is

A short, focused essay (607w) arguing that identity formation must shift from boundary-based to inter-becoming. Boundary identities — nation, firm, individual as legal subject — drive three systemic consequences: legibility bias (bounded entities are tractable, relational systems are invisible), incentive drift (boundaries are defended, policed, monetised), and externalisation logic (what lies outside the boundary is ignored or displaced). Inter-becoming identities are relational, provisional, co-authored over time. The essay proposes four scaffolds: porous belonging protocols, contribution-based incentives, norm-making spaces, and rotational custodianship.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Overlapping membership:** Spore's holonic architecture is a semilattice, not a tree — agents participate in multiple overlapping wholes. This already embodies inter-becoming structurally: identity is defined by participation across holons, not by a single boundary.

2. **Contribution-based governance:** Spore's governance patterns emerge from artifacts, not authorities. Participation is constituted through contribution (claims, evidence, commitments) rather than role assignment. This parallels the essay's "contribution-based incentives."

3. **Revisable commitments:** The commitment lifecycle allows scope change, renegotiation, and attestation decay. Identity within the grammar is not fixed — it evolves through the contributions an agent makes and the commitments they hold.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. The Structural Failure of Boundary Identities

The essay's diagnostic: boundary identities are not morally wrong but structurally pathological. Three consequences follow from organising around bounded entities: relational systems become administratively invisible, boundaries become self-reinforcing through incentive drift, and externalisation becomes the default for anything outside the boundary. This is a structural account, not a moral critique — "not a moral failure but a structural one."

For Spore, this connects to the grammar's semilattice design: if holons had rigid boundaries, they would produce exactly the legibility bias, incentive drift, and externalisation the essay describes. The overlap architecture is already the structural corrective.

### 2. Inter-Becoming as Identity Principle

Inter-becoming identities "refuse to be fixed essences." Four principles: relational anchoring (defined by relation rather than boundary), temporal openness (evolving, not permanent), polyvalent belonging (overlapping affiliations across care, practice, and place), and commons orientation (value in shared generativity, not exclusive possession).

For Spore, this names the identity principle that the grammar's overlapping holon model enacts structurally. The essay adds a developmental dimension: identity is not merely overlapping membership but an ongoing process of relational formation — "not what we are in isolation but what we are in relation to others."

### 3. Scaffolds for Inter-Becoming

Four concrete scaffolds: porous belonging protocols (membership by degrees of contribution, not binary inclusion/exclusion), contribution-based incentives (rights accrue from participation, not possession), norm-making spaces (assemblies, rituals, commons where shared meaning evolves), and rotational custodianship (rights and roles that renew, rotate, or expire to prevent ossification).

These map closely to Spore's existing patterns — holon membership, commitment-based participation, governance through artifacts — but add the explicit design intent of preventing identity ossification.

## Open Questions

1. **Is inter-becoming already the grammar's identity model, or is it in tension with it?** Spore's holon model allows overlapping membership and porous boundaries. But holons still have boundaries — they are not unbounded flows. Whether the essay's more radical "inter-becoming" (identity as continuous relational formation) is what the holon model already provides or something the grammar cannot yet express is unresolved.

2. **How does rotational custodianship relate to Spore's commitment lifecycle?** The essay proposes that roles expire and rotate to prevent ossification. Spore's attestation decay already models the degradation of claims over time. Whether custodianship rotation is an instance of attestation decay applied to roles, or a structurally different mechanism, is worth investigating.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate primitive** — Inter-becoming names an identity principle that Spore's grammar enacts structurally through its semilattice holon model but has not articulated as a named concept. The essay is short (607w) but conceptually sharp — it directly names the shift from boundary to relation as an identity design requirement and proposes concrete scaffolds.

**Promotion status: defer** — Single-author source. The concept is sharp but the essay is brief, and the scaffolds (porous belonging, rotational custodianship) are suggestive rather than fully developed. Promotion should wait for convergence.
