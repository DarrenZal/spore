# Institutions and Extitutions

This Tier 2 cluster note synthesizes the P2P Foundation wiki's treatment of institutions and extitutions — how formal rule-bearing structures and their informal, network-form complements coexist, compose, and fail — across eight primary wiki pages and six corroborating pages, anchored by the `Category:Governance` corpus index. Where the Tier 1b `boundary-commoning` note imported the within-commons/between-commons scope distinction and the Wave 1 `polycentric-governance` cluster imported the multi-decision-center scale-up logic, this cluster addresses a cross-cutting clarification Spore canon needs: what *kind* of social structure is a Spore field, membrane, or holon, in the vocabulary the P2P tradition already uses for institutions, extitutions, and assemblages?

The cluster's central thesis: in the wiki's institutional-theory corpus, **extitutional is not the contrary of institutional** (Schingler/de Filippi, Extitutions.org) — the two are complementary lenses onto the same social phenomena. Spore's multi-actor coordination grammar, which composes with both formal institutions (organizations, contracts, protocols) and extitutional processes (rhizomatic assemblies, network-form coordination, citizen-driven self-empowerment), should state this composition discipline explicitly rather than inheriting either the anti-institutional or pro-institutional valence by default.

## 1. What "institution" names in the corpus

The `Institutions` page gives a working definition (Andrea Farias, via diome.xyz): "An institution is a structure or mechanism of social order governing the behavior of a set of individuals within a given community. Institutions are often seen as central to recurrent social problems and play a critical role in societies by establishing the framework within which social interactions occur." The page specifies three constitutive features — shared purpose, structured interactions (protocols, rules, norms, practices), and constituent actants (human actors, technological systems, physical infrastructure, informational resources) — and distinguishes formal institutions (officially established rules governed by laws and official procedures) from informal institutions (unwritten norms enforced through social customs and cultural traditions).

Arran Gare's extended discussion on the same page locates institutions in the institutionalist-economics tradition (Veblen → Berger/Luckmann → Bourdieu) where "institutions emerge when patterns of activity come to be stabilized, taken to be objective, and then internalized in people's actions." Bourdieu's `field` concept enters here as the macro-dynamic extension of institutional analysis — providing Spore with a direct commons-tradition lineage for the `field` vocabulary, not only an IC-internal coinage.

## 2. What "extitution" names in the corpus

The `Extitution` page (Richard Bartlett, reporting the Madrid 15-M coinage): "If institutions are organizational systems based on an inside-outside framework, extitutions are designed as areas where a multitude of agents can spontaneously assemble." The `Extitutional Theory` page (Schingler and de Filippi, Berkman Klein Center) makes the framing precise: "Extitutional theory posits that the institutional framework is just one specific lens through which we can make sense of social behaviour. Social dynamics that are not part of an institution are not unstructured, just differently structured. Specifically, institutions focus on the static and inert elements of social structures — the aspects that persist over time — whereas extitutions focus on the dynamic and mutating elements of social structures — the aspects that continuously evolve over time."

Extitutions.org's explicit statement is canonical: "Extitutions are not the opposite of institutions, nor can they be understood as a new type of institution. Extitutional scholars suggest that institutional theory and extitutional theory are two distinct perspectives or 'lenses' to the same set of social phenomena or 'assemblage'." The institutional lens focuses on roles and rules; the extitutional lens focuses on individuals and the relationships among them, and specifically on "the ways in which participation and mutual recognition pull people into alignment through local interactions." This is a scope distinction, not a binary opposition.

## 3. The "membrane organizations" reading Spore should note explicitly

Benjamin Life's bioregional-coordination reading (quoted on the `Extitution` page) extends the extitutional framing directly into Spore-adjacent language: "Bioregional coordination requires what we term 'extitutional' organizing — coordination mechanisms that operate alongside and through existing institutions without being captured by their limitations. Extitutions are not anti-institutional but rather post-institutional." Life then names the organizational form: "Extitutions operate through what we term 'membrane organizations' — organizational forms that facilitate connection and coordination without claiming ownership or control. Like biological membranes, these organizations are simultaneously permeable and selective, allowing and facilitating beneficial flows while filtering out harmful elements. They maintain coherence without rigidity, enabling collective action without sacrificing individual autonomy."

This is explicit prior art for Spore's membrane vocabulary, with a post-institutional framing that is compatible with Spore's composition-with-institutions stance. The Massumi reading on `Creating a Membrane between the Commons and the Market` supplies a complementary membrane operation: "A membrane creates a separation, but at the same time allows for movements across. It has a certain porosity." These two membrane framings — the bioregional extitutional membrane (Life) and the commons/market membrane (Massumi) — are both operationally closer to Spore's membrane than the cellular-biology analogy boundary-commoning draws on, and warrant explicit citation in any canon-edit expanding membrane vocabulary.

## 4. Extitutional processes and field ontology

The `Extitutional Processes` and `Extitutional Urbanism` pages (VIC, Madrid 15-M tradition) give the operational surface of extitutions as *processes* rather than as structures: "extitutional surfaces can be understood as able to eventually assemble a multitude of different agents." Processes are "rhizomatic assemblies" that share "non-economic capital … symbolic, relational, knowledge, care, affects, work, health capital" without funding or central coordination. The Madrid eviction-resistance case (PAH, Stop Deshaucios, V de Vivienda) documents the pattern at scale: multiple initiatives, associations, research platforms, and body-action platforms "develop a common objective but not by consensus. Each of them develops their own strategies to function as an assemblage of multiple entities."

The `Extitution` page adds a load-bearing theoretical contrast: "Whereas administrative institutions bring categorical hierarchies to their systems … extitutional space is made up of field ontologies, shifting assumptions about what dimensions of the real deserve attention or operative preeminence adequate (again) to the contingency of the encounter." This explicit identification of "field ontology" as the extitutional-space analog directly engages Spore's `field` lexicon entry — the extitutional tradition uses "field" in a sense closer to Spore's than the bare Bourdieusian sense from the institutional-theory lineage.

## 5. The Spicer reading: extitutions inside institutions

André Spicer's reading (on `Extitutional Theory`) offers a different but complementary framing: "I have argued that institutions are driven by a tension between attempts to create conformity to modern rational myths and attempts to escape or resist these myths in various ways. … I have developed the concept of the extitution. I have argued that this is a kind of formless life which exceeds institutional parameters, but which institutions seek to capture. In many ways we can look at an institution as a huge machine designed to regulate and contain these extitutional elements, whereby the work of institutions is precisely this work of containment."

Spicer's framing is structurally dialectical: institutions and extitutions are not two separate things but the two poles of an ongoing tension, where institutional work is the containment of the extitutional excess. This adds a failure-mode reading to the neutral lens-complementarity framing: coordination grammars that import only the institutional lens will understate the extitutional overflow (soft governance failure); grammars that import only the extitutional lens will understate the containment work institutions perform (hard governance failure).

## 6. Scale dynamics: when extitutional escapes institutional capture

The `Scaling Up of Human Institutions` page (Gary Feinman, critiquing Graeber/Wengrow's *Dawn of Everything*) documents a counter-vailing scale-law for institutions: "human organization and leadership generally shift and differentiate as the density of interpersonal interaction increases" and "when human groupings in sustained close contact exceed certain demographic thresholds, organizational adjustments take place … some kind of supra-household institution emerges if these aggregations are sustained." This is a direct empirical rebuttal to fully anti-institutional framings: sustained cooperation at scale generates institutions whether the participants prefer them or not.

This pairs with the extitutional-processes framing to give Spore a two-sided discipline: (a) extitutional processes exist and operate in a scale-limited, dynamic, mutating register; (b) at scale-over-time, institutional crystallization is structural, not optional. Spore's coordination grammar at bioregional scale cannot assume indefinite extitutional fluidity.

## 7. Institutional self-negation: a governance pattern worth importing

Benjamin Life's `Institutional Self-Negation` page names a governance practice worth pulling forward into Spore and IC canon: bioregional backbone organizations "explicitly recognize their own limitations. They refuse to claim authority over the bioregion. They commit to flowing significant resources directly to network-governed allocation. They implement rotating leadership with clear term limits. They make their decision-making and resource flows transparent." The Life text then makes the stance explicit: "This institutional humility is a spiritual practice — an ongoing recognition that the institution is always servant to the living commons, never its master."

This is operationally the institution-side version of Lanham's negative-boundary discipline on the commons side (per `boundary-commoning` C8). Taken together they give Spore and IC a matched pair of governance patterns: on the commons side, specify what the commons is NOT (corporation / co-op / state / commune); on the institution side, specify what the backbone institution REFUSES to claim (authority / ownership / indefinite tenure / opacity). These are the twin disciplines the institutions-and-extitutions cluster makes available for Spore canon's multi-actor coordination language.

## 8. Unger and Allen: institutional experimentalism and measurement-driven institutional change

Two corroborating sources ground the institutional side with additional operational discipline. Roberto Unger's `Institutional Experimentalism` names "the quickened practice of institutional experimentalism: motivated, directed, and cumulative experimentation with the institutional forms that now define representative democracy, market economy, and free civil society." This framing treats institutions themselves as objects of iterative design, not as givens — compatible with Spore's stance but at stronger-than-rhetorical register.

Douglas Allen's `Institutional Revolution` adds a complementary empirical frame: institutional change in early-modern England was driven by the ability to *measure* performance. "[P]rogress has been often little more than the removal of randomness in outcomes." Allen's insight is that new measurement capacities open new institutional possibilities — a claim that is directly relevant to any coordination grammar that proposes new measurement primitives (per Spore/IC/PM discussions of evidence capture, commitment records, claim registers) as drivers of institutional innovation.

## 9. Assemblage theory as the unifying lens

The `Manuel DeLanda on Assemblage Theory and Social Institutions` and `Assemblage` pages supply the theoretical frame Extitutional Theory explicitly builds on (per the Extitutional Theory header quote: "Extitutional theory builds upon assemblage theory to conceptualise the ordering logic and dynamics of social phenomena"). The `Assemblage` page (Otto Paans): "A better word for the interactions of any entity embedded in a given context would be assemblage: the notion that all components of a given entity are intimately and dynamically related; moreover, that in actualizing their relationships, they bring something genuinely new into the world. All components of the assemblage can be identified, but their functioning cannot be analysed apart from the relationships they have to the other components."

Assemblage theory (DeLanda via Deleuze/Guattari) is therefore the shared theoretical substrate for both the institutional lens (Bourdieu's field extension) and the extitutional lens (Schingler/de Filippi, Madrid 15-M tradition) — the common ground Spore can cite as the theoretical home of both lenses at once. This is load-bearing: Spore's field/membrane/holon vocabulary is closer to assemblage theory than to either pure institutionalism or pure anti-institutionalism, and the `Assemblage` / DeLanda pages are the proper commons-tradition prior art to cite.

## 10. Category breadth: governance as a federation of specialized surfaces

The `Category:Governance` page is thin as a standalone entry but structurally revealing: it points to `Category:Peergovernance`, `Category:P2P Hierarchy Theory`, `Category:P2P State Approaches`, and the `Commons – Governance` cross-reference. As with the `polycentric-governance` cluster's observation on the same category (per that note's C16), the Category:Governance structure is itself a federation of specialized sub-categories — with institutions-and-extitutions material distributed across Encyclopedia, Peergovernance, P2P Theory, P2P Hierarchy Theory, P2P State Approaches, and Relational. The wiki does not treat institutions and extitutions as a single isolated topic; it treats them as a cross-cutting concern that surfaces in every major governance subcategory. Spore canon adopting this framing should carry the same cross-cutting discipline: institutions-vs-extitutions is a lens-selection question that can be asked of any Spore primitive, not a topic for a single canon section.

## 11. Tensions (cautions for any Spore / IC / PM canon edit)

1. **Lens-vs-ontology conflation risk**: Extitutional Theory is explicitly a *lens*, not an ontological claim. Spore canon adopting extitutional language must preserve the lens framing — the field/membrane/holon is not "an extitution rather than an institution"; it is a social phenomenon that can be analyzed through either lens. Conflating this into an ontological claim ("Spore is extitutional, not institutional") inherits anti-institutional valence that the wiki tradition explicitly repudiates.
2. **Scale-denial risk**: Feinman's scale-law (demographic thresholds → supra-household institutions) rebuts sustained extitutional-only framings at bioregional scale. Spore canon cannot assume indefinite extitutional fluidity and must state how institutional crystallization is anticipated, governed, and (per Life's institutional-self-negation pattern) self-limited when it occurs.
3. **Containment-work under-specification**: Spicer's dialectical reading warrants explicit acknowledgment that institutional work includes the containment of extitutional overflow. Spore canon that imports only the "field ontology + spontaneous assembly" extitutional imagery without the institutional-containment counterweight will under-specify how governance handles overflow.
4. **Membrane-vocabulary provenance**: Spore's membrane vocabulary has at least three distinct commons-tradition sources available — cellular biology (via De Angelis boundary-commoning), bioregional extitutional organizing (Life), and commons/market separation (Massumi). Any canon edit clarifying membrane operations should cite the specific source whose operational surface is being claimed, not membrane-in-the-abstract.
5. **Assemblage-theory substrate citation gap**: Extitutional Theory's own foundational citation is assemblage theory (DeLanda/Deleuze). Spore canon importing extitutional language without also citing assemblage theory will appear to import the surface framing without its theoretical substrate, weakening the prior-art claim.
6. **Institutional-self-negation optional-discipline risk**: Life's institutional-self-negation pattern is a strong-form governance discipline (rotating leadership, term limits, transparency, resource flow to network governance). Importing the rhetoric without the operational surface produces cheap-talk institutional humility. IC canon adopting the pattern must specify the four operational commitments, not only the stance.

## 12. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The wiki evidence warrants Spore carrying the institutions-extitutions lens-complementarity discipline explicitly in its lexicon entries for `field`, in any mycelial-holarchy-architecture text that describes composition with external organizations, and in the protocol-society framing that describes governance-via-protocol. It warrants IC importing the institutional-self-negation operational pattern (four commitments: no ownership authority, network-governed allocation, rotating/term-limited leadership, transparency) alongside the boundary-commoning negative-boundary discipline. It warrants PM acknowledging the extitutional-processes frame (rhizomatic assembly, non-economic capital sharing) as prior art for coordination-substrate beyond market-like matching. It also warrants citing assemblage theory (DeLanda) as the shared theoretical substrate for field/membrane/holon vocabulary. None of these clarifications require new canon primitives, consistent with the Move 0 moratorium and with the Tier 1b and Wave 1 cluster dispositions.

An institution (Farias, via diome.xyz) is a structure or mechanism of social order governing behavior within a community, consisting of (a) shared purpose — common goal or values guiding collective action, (b) structured interactions — protocols, rules, norms, practices that govern behavior and relationships, and (c) constituent actants — human actors, technological systems, physical infrastructure, informational resources — with the standard typology distinguishing formal institutions (officially established, laws and procedures) from informal institutions (unwritten norms, social customs, cultural traditions); this definition is the baseline Spore must either work within or explicitly reframe when adopting institution-adjacent vocabulary.

**C8** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Extitutional_Processes]

**C12** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Institutional_Self-Negation]

This pairs symmetrically with Lanham's negative-boundary discipline on the commons side (per boundary-commoning C8) and is importable into IC memory-layers as the institution-side governance discipline.

**C13** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Disccusion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Creating_a_Membrane_between_the_Commons_and_the_Market]

**C14** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Institutional_Experimentalism]

**C15** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Review · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Institutional_Revolution]

**C16** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Typology · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Institutions_of_Open_Source]
Lars Zimmermann's institutions-of-open-source typology specifies concrete institutional instruments — READ ME files, licenses, version-control systems, foundations, standards — as load-bearing institutional infrastructure for open-source coordination, and notes that "insecurities indicate best a lack of working institutions" (on open-source hardware licensing); this supplies an operational test for institutional presence — the presence of widely-adopted concrete instruments, not the absence of hierarchy — that Spore and IC can use to assess whether their coordination grammars carry the institutional surface they claim.

**C17** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Manuel_DeLanda_on_Assemblage_Theory_and_Social_Institutions]
DeLanda's assemblage-theory treatment of social institutions — working through authority, Weber, emergent properties, social movements, state, and market via the Deleuzean assemblage frame — establishes assemblage theory as the unified theoretical substrate for analyzing both institutions and extitutions (per the Extitutional Theory header quote that extitutional theory "builds upon assemblage theory"); Spore's field/membrane/holon vocabulary is closer to assemblage theory than to pure institutionalism or pure anti-institutionalism and should cite the assemblage-theory lineage as its shared substrate when importing either lens.

**C18** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Assemblage]
Otto Paans's assemblage definition — "the notion that all components of a given entity are intimately and dynamically related; moreover, that in actualizing their relationships, they bring something genuinely new into the world. All components of the assemblage can be identified, but their functioning cannot be analysed apart from the relationships they have to the other components" — gives the operational content of the assemblage-theory substrate: identifiable components, inseparable functioning, genuinely novel emergent properties; all three are load-bearing for Spore's holon and field-composition vocabulary.

**C19** [confidence: low] [anchor: §Related Categories · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Category:Governance]
The P2P Foundation wiki's `Category:Governance` is a thin index page that delegates to specialized sub-categories (Peergovernance, P2P Hierarchy Theory, P2P State Approaches) and to the `Commons – Governance` cross-reference — institutions-and-extitutions material is distributed across Encyclopedia, Peergovernance, P2P Theory, P2P Hierarchy Theory, P2P State Approaches, and Relational categories rather than concentrated in a single governance subcategory; this cross-cutting distribution is corpus-breadth evidence that institutions-vs-extitutions is a lens-selection question that can be asked of any governance primitive, not a topic for an isolated canon section.
