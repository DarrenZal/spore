# Solidarity Economy and Mutual Aid as PM's Substrate

This bridge note is Wave 3 of the P2P Foundation wiki Pass 2 intake. It grounds Poietic Match's coordination substrate — the layer below commitment-pooling, CVLE, and any matching protocol — in the solidarity-economy and mutual-aid traditions as the P2P Foundation wiki carries them. The disposition is `clarify existing term`: PM already names `coordination-substrate` and `commitment-pooling`, and Pass 1 already connected to commitment-pooling and mutual-credit; this note does not propose new canon primitives but clarifies what substrate PM is standing on and what genealogy it inherits. The note also deliberately holds — not resolves — the Nelson non-market-socialism critique (DH-PM-1) that accounting itself may leak exchange-value logic even in solidarity-economy framings.

**Decentralization-myth-bundle inheritance.** This note's claims inherit the decentralization-myth-bundle critique (Seeds 1+2+3: administrator-capture, decentralization-theater, digital-labor trap). See the Pass 2 opposition notes `decentralization-theater-opposition`, `digital-labor-peer-production-opposition`, and `peer-governance-wikipedia-opposition` for the standing counter-evidence. Solidarity-economy and mutual-aid framings do not inherently carry power-decentralization mechanisms — they carry reciprocity-as-design-principle and need-orientation. PM adopting solidarity-substrate as ground does not inherit answers to power-over questions; it inherits a substrate and must specify governance separately.

## 1. What the wiki calls solidarity economy

The P2P Foundation wiki's primary `Solidarity_Economy` page frames the solidarity economy as a coordination tradition organized around four principles: **self-management, cooperation, equity in all dimensions, and pluralism**. The page foregrounds the Brazilian case (cooperatives linked through a state secretariat for solidarity economy) and Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) as operative instances. The defining feature across variants is that exchange, production, and distribution are organized to meet concrete needs (use-value) with reciprocity as design principle — not as supplementary ethic layered on a market substrate, but as the substrate itself.

The framing is older than the wiki. The wiki's citations trace solidarity-economy language through Latin American ("economía solidaria") and Québécois ("économie solidaire") organizing traditions that predate digital-coordination framings by decades. What is new in the wiki's intake is the framing of solidarity economy as **substrate for digital coordination** — the claim that platform cooperativism, commitment pooling, mutual-credit networks, and similar infrastructures should run on solidarity-economy principles rather than on market-efficiency principles with solidarity retrofitted as externality mitigation.

## 2. What mutual aid names

`Mutual_Aid_Societies` (28KB on the wiki) records mutual aid as a coordination form that predates both market and state by centuries: Kropotkin's ethology-and-history argument that mutual aid is a pervasive evolutionary and historical pattern among species and societies; 19th-century friendly societies and African-American benefit societies; contemporary lending circles; Freelancers Union; Mission Asset Fund; and (per the companion page `Citizen-Led_Mutual_Aid_Groups`) the COVID-19 wave of 582 UK volunteer groups organized in weeks during 2020. The wiki carries these as a single coordination tradition — not as market alternatives (mutual aid does not compete with markets for price-signal efficiency) and not as state proxies (mutual aid does not attempt monopoly on legitimate force). Mutual-aid infrastructures are **horizontal, voluntary, need-oriented, and scale-limited** by the relational density of their participants.

The wiki's evidence carries three specific features PM should inherit. First, mutual aid operates on **diverse substrate logics** — BAMA on gift-economy and emotional support, Freelancers Union on negotiated group insurance rates, COVID-19 groups on horizontal voluntarism mediated only by WhatsApp. There is no single mutual-aid protocol. Second, the contemporary resurgence of lending circles documents **99% repayment rates** in small-circle commitment-pooling — a data point suggesting commitment-pooling is operationally robust at small scale with high relational density. Third, the wiki carries mutual aid as *operative-and-fragile*: it works, and it does not scale arbitrarily.

## 3. The gift-obligation substrate

The wiki's `Gift_Economy` page (25KB, Pinchot/Mauss lineage) establishes that gift-economy coordination encodes valuation through **ongoing obligation**, not through price. Mauss's original argument is that the gift creates an unbounded relation of reciprocity: the recipient incurs an obligation to reciprocate that does not close with the counter-gift, because each gift re-opens the relation. Pinchot's extension frames scientific communities as gift economies: status in a research field accrues via contribution (published work, mentorship, peer review) rather than via accumulation of possessions, and attribution in the citation graph encodes the obligation to acknowledge and build on prior work.

This is the `gift-obligation` concept in the frozen vocabulary (v2). For PM, the relevance is structural: **commitment-pooling, mutual aid, and solidarity economy all carry gift-economy dynamics at their base, not only at their rhetorical surface**. A commitment pool that treats commitments as closing-transactions (you gave X, I return X, relation closes) is operating as mutual-credit with solidarity rhetoric. A commitment pool that treats commitments as opening an ongoing reciprocity relation (you gave X, I now carry a non-closing obligation to the pool) is operating as gift economy. PM's vocabulary should distinguish these — and recognize that scalable commitment-pooling protocols tend toward the former while the substrate traditions PM draws from operate as the latter.

## 4. Commons-as-verb: the substrate is a practice

Bresnihan's `commons-as-verb` framing (v2 frozen concept) is the substrate-level correction: the commons is not a noun (a set of resources) or an institution (a set of rules) — it is **the ongoing activity of commoning**, the acting and doing in common across human and non-human participants. Care, provisioning, territorial stewardship, and mutual aid are the primary coordinating practices; governance is what you do to preserve and extend the practice, not what constitutes it.

For PM, this reframes `coordination-substrate` itself. Pass 1 treats the substrate as infrastructure (technical, linguistic, normative) that enables coordination. Bresnihan's framing suggests the substrate is the **practice being coordinated through** — tontines don't operate on a substrate layer below the credit relations, the tontine *is* the practice of women gathering weekly to pool and rotate credit. Ollas communes don't use provisioning infrastructure, the ollas *are* the provisioning. PM's commitment-pooling claim needs to distinguish: is PM building infrastructure for solidarity practice (the Pass 1 reading), or is PM attempting to encode solidarity practice as infrastructure (a stronger claim)? The latter risks collapsing practice into protocol — which `commons-as-verb` explicitly rebuts.

## 5. The Federici genealogy: tontines and ollas as PM prehistory

`Women_and_the_Commons` documents Federici's core argument that women-led commons — particularly **tontines** (rotating credit circles common in West Africa and the African diaspora) and **ollas communes** (common cooking pots operative in Chile, Peru, and elsewhere during the 1970s–80s economic crises) — are centuries-long operative prior art for what PM calls commitment-pooling and provisioning. Tontines are mutual-credit pools: participants contribute fixed amounts at fixed intervals and each period's pool rotates to one participant. Ollas are provisioning pools: participants contribute food, labor, and fuel, and the collective cooking pot feeds all contributing households and others in need.

Both predate modern mutual-credit theory by centuries. The wiki evidence carries two claims PM should inherit explicitly: (a) commitment-pooling and provisioning pools are not novel coordination technology — they are the resurrection of suppressed knowledge about how care economies actually coordinate value across time and across households; and (b) these traditions are explicitly **women-led and reproductive-labor-grounded** — their erasure from mainstream mutual-credit theory is itself an instance of reproductive-labor invisibilization (DH-Spore-1 in the Phase A synthesis). PM's vocabulary should acknowledge this genealogy — not as decoration but as the actual source of the coordination pattern PM is naming.

## 6. Decentralization-myth-bundle header

Solidarity economy, mutual aid, and gift-obligation framings do not inherently carry power-decentralization mechanisms. Horizontal voluntarism (COVID-19 mutual aid via WhatsApp) can drift into coordinator-capture; reciprocity networks can drift into in-group closure; gift economies can drift into status hierarchies via accumulated attribution. Any PM primitive that claims solidarity-substrate lineage inherits the decentralization-myth-bundle critique by proximity — administrator-capture, decentralization-theater, digital-labor trap — unless PM explicitly declines each through specified governance.

## 7. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The wiki evidence does not warrant new PM canon primitives. It warrants: (a) clarifying PM's `coordination-substrate` by naming solidarity economy and mutual aid as the substrate traditions PM sits on; (b) clarifying `commitment-pooling` by anchoring the Federici tontine/olla genealogy as operative prior art, not inventing as novel; (c) clarifying `curation-valuation-limitation-exchange` (CVLE) by showing CSA and gift-economy instances that already implement all four functions on solidarity-substrate terms; (d) carrying forward — unresolved — the Nelson non-market-socialism critique as a standing tension PM canon should not silently resolve; and (e) inheriting the Bresnihan `commons-as-verb` discipline as a check against PM protocol-ization of what is fundamentally practice.

## 8. Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Definition · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Solidarity_Economy]
The solidarity economy is organized around four principles — self-management, cooperation, equity in all dimensions, and pluralism — with exchange, production, and distribution oriented to concrete needs (use-value) and reciprocity as design principle, not as ethic layered on a market substrate. Brazilian cooperatives (linked through a state secretariat for solidarity economy) and Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) are the wiki's primary operative examples.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Definition · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Solidarity_Economy]
CSA (Community Supported Agriculture) implements the four CVLE functions on solidarity-substrate terms: curates membership (seasonal shareholders), values contribution through shared risk (shareholders absorb crop failure), limits extraction (fixed seasonal quantities), enables exchange (seasonal shares). CVLE is not a PM-novel specification — it is a reconstruction of coordination patterns already operative in solidarity-economy instances.

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §History · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Mutual_Aid_Societies]
Mutual aid is documented in the wiki as a coordination form predating both market and state: Kropotkin's ethology-and-history lineage, 19th-century friendly societies, African-American benefit societies, contemporary lending circles, Freelancers Union, Mission Asset Fund. Mutual aid is neither market-alternative (does not compete on price-signal efficiency) nor state-proxy (does not claim monopoly on legitimate force) — it is a horizontal, voluntary, need-oriented coordination tradition distinct from both.

**C4** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Contemporary · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Mutual_Aid_Societies]
Contemporary mutual aid operates on diverse substrate logics — BAMA (Bay Area Mutual Aid) on gift-economy and emotional support without mutual-credit accounting; Freelancers Union on negotiated group insurance rates mediated by market infrastructure; lending circles on direct commitment-pooling with documented 99% repayment rates at small scale and high relational density. There is no single mutual-aid protocol.

**C5** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Full Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Citizen-Led_Mutual_Aid_Groups]
The COVID-19 mutual aid wave in the UK organized 582 volunteer groups in weeks during 2020 — a vernacular response to underfunded state health infrastructure, coordinated primarily through horizontal voluntarism on consumer messaging apps (WhatsApp) with no dedicated coordination substrate. This is operative evidence that commitment-pooling-adjacent practice scales in emergency conditions without protocolization, and also evidence that such coordination is fragile — dependent on proximity, trust density, and specific crisis conditions.

**C6** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Gift_Economy]
Gift-economy coordination (Mauss/Pinchot lineage) encodes valuation through ongoing obligation rather than price: the gift creates an unbounded relation of reciprocity that does not close with the counter-gift. Pinchot's extension frames scientific communities as gift economies — status accrues via contribution, and citation attribution encodes ongoing obligation to acknowledge and build on prior work.

**C7** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Gift_Economy]
The gift-economy framing distinguishes coordination forms that treat commitments as closing-transactions (you gave X, I returned X, relation closes — mutual-credit with solidarity rhetoric) from those that treat commitments as opening ongoing reciprocity relations (you gave X, I now carry a non-closing obligation to the pool — gift economy). Both patterns appear in commitment-pooling, solidarity-economy, and mutual-aid instances; they are not interchangeable and operate under different scaling dynamics.

**C8** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Women_and_the_Commons]
Tontines (rotating credit circles documented in West African and African-diaspora communities) and ollas communes (common cooking pots operative in Chile, Peru, and elsewhere during the 1970s–80s economic crises) are Federici-documented women-led reproductive-economic commons. Tontines are mutual-credit pools with fixed periodic contributions and rotating payouts; ollas are provisioning pools with contributions of food, labor, and fuel feeding all participating households and others in need. Both are operative centuries-long prior art for what modern mutual-credit and commitment-pooling theory treats as novel coordination technology.

**C9** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Women_and_the_Commons]
Federici's framing positions tontines, ollas, and related reproductive-economic commons as explicitly women-led and reproductive-labor-grounded; the wiki records the erasure of these traditions from mainstream mutual-credit and cooperative-economics theory as an instance of structural invisibilization of reproductive labor, not as neutral oversight.

**C10** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Full Text · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Mutual_Aid_Societies]
The wiki records mutual-aid infrastructure as operationally robust at small scale with high relational density (lending circles with 99% repayment), and as fragile under scale-expansion or relational-density loss — BAMA, Freelancers Union, and COVID-19 groups each solve different parts of the coordination problem under different substrate assumptions, none generalizing to arbitrary scale. This is evidence that commitment-pooling is a structure (scale-dependent, load-bearing on relational density) rather than a protocol (scale-invariant).

## 9. Review Claims

**R1** [review claim] [target: pm.grammar] [concept: solidarity-substrate]
PM's grammar should name `solidarity-substrate` as the explicit substrate-tradition layer under `coordination-substrate`. Specifically: coordination-substrate is the generic layer (technical, linguistic, normative infrastructure enabling coordination); solidarity-substrate is the specific substrate-tradition PM draws from, grounded in the solidarity-economy principles (self-management, cooperation, equity, pluralism) and mutual-aid coordination form (horizontal, voluntary, need-oriented, scale-limited). This clarification makes PM's substrate commitments explicit rather than inherited by default, and distinguishes PM from market-substrate matchmaking platforms that carry solidarity only as rhetorical overlay.
*R1 is supported by C1, C3, C4.*

**R2** [review claim] [target: pm.connection.commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit] [concept: commitment-pooling]
The commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit connection note should anchor the Federici tontine/olla genealogy as operative centuries-long prior art for the commitment-pooling pattern. PM's commitment-pooling vocabulary is a specialization of coordination patterns already operative in women-led reproductive-economic commons — not a novel coordination technology. This R-claim honors the lineage Darren's work with Will Ruddick sits in: PM is inspired by but broader than commitment pooling, and commitment pooling itself is inspired by (not originating from) mutual-credit scholarship that in turn reconstructs what tontines have done for centuries. Acknowledging this genealogy is precision, not decoration — it prevents PM from claiming technical novelty that the tradition does not support.
*R2 is supported by C8, C9.*

**R3** [review claim] [target: pm.grammar] [concept: curation-valuation-limitation-exchange]
PM's CVLE grammar should be presented as a reconstruction of coordination functions already operative in solidarity-economy instances (CSA as the wiki's paradigmatic case), not as PM-originated specification. CSA implements all four CVLE functions on solidarity-substrate terms: curates membership through seasonal shareholder agreements; values contribution through shared risk (shareholders absorb crop failure); limits extraction through fixed seasonal quantities; enables exchange through seasonal shares. Framing CVLE as reconstruction rather than specification makes PM's grammar operationally auditable against operating solidarity-economy instances, and avoids the implicit novelty-claim that Pass 1 grammar language risks.
*R3 is supported by C1, C2.*

**R4** [review claim] [target: pm.protocol] [concept: market-dependence]
PM canon must carry, as a standing unresolved tension rather than a settled position, the Nelson non-market-socialism critique against solidarity-substrate framings themselves. Nelson's argument (carried in the corroborating `Non-Market_Socialism` wiki page and in Phase A synthesis §4c DH-PM-1) is that any accounting operation — including solidarity-economy bookkeeping, mutual-credit ledgers, gift-attribution graphs, tontine contribution records — may leak exchange-value logic because accounting itself encodes commensurability, which is the substrate of exchange. Solidarity-substrate framings (C1, C6, C8) do not resolve this: CSA accounts for shares, tontines account for contributions, scientific gift economies account for citations. PM adopting solidarity-substrate does not bypass market-dependence by rhetorical commitment to non-market principles — it inherits the tension. This R-claim explicitly holds both positions without resolving: (a) solidarity-substrate as operationally viable substrate (C1–C5, C8), and (b) the Nelson position that accounting-as-such is the deeper problem. Canon stewards decide; do not let PM silently assume solidarity-substrate bypasses the accounting-dependence question flagged in the REA/ValueFlows bridge note (R4 there).
*R4 is supported by C1, C2, C6, C7, C8 (solidarity-substrate as operational) and opposed by the Nelson position carried in corroborating `Non-Market_Socialism` and Phase A DH-PM-1. R4 holds the tension; it does not resolve it.*

**R5** [review claim] [target: pm.protocol] [concept: commons-as-verb]
PM's protocol specification should inherit the Bresnihan `commons-as-verb` discipline as a check against protocol-ization of practice. Commitment-pooling is a coordination practice; PM infrastructure is not the practice itself but scaffolding for the practice. The distinction matters operationally: a PM deployment that treats commitment-pooling as fully specifiable through protocol rules (you can commitment-pool through PM without doing the practice) is attempting to encode solidarity as infrastructure, which the `commons-as-verb` tradition explicitly rebuts. Mutual-aid evidence (C4, C5, C10) — where BAMA, Freelancers Union, COVID-19 groups, and lending circles each solve different parts of the coordination problem on different substrate logics under different scale-constraints — is direct evidence that commitment-pooling is structure (scale-dependent, load-bearing on relational density) not protocol (scale-invariant). PM should state this bound explicitly in protocol canon: commitment-pooling is not a scale-invariant protocol, it is a practice-coupled structure with scale-limit behavior that emerges from relational-density constraints.
*R5 is supported by C4, C5, C10 (mutual-aid fragility / diverse logics) and the `commons-as-verb` framing carried in Bresnihan `More-Than-Human_Commons` (cross-reference to `spore.connection.reproductive-commoning` Wave 2 note).*

**R6** [review claim] [target: pm.connection.commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit] [concept: gift-obligation]
The commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit connection note should distinguish two operative patterns at the commitment layer: closing-transaction commitments (you gave X, I returned X, relation closes — mutual-credit with solidarity rhetoric) and ongoing-obligation commitments (you gave X, the pool now carries a non-closing obligation — gift-economy substrate). Both appear in solidarity-economy, mutual-aid, and commitment-pooling instances; they are not interchangeable. Scalable protocol-based commitment-pooling tends toward closing-transaction commitments; the substrate traditions PM draws from operate as ongoing-obligation commitments. PM's commitment-pooling canon should name this distinction and make explicit which pattern any given PM deployment operates under. The distinction is not a rhetorical preference — it determines downstream dynamics around status accumulation, attribution, and pool longevity.
*R6 is supported by C6, C7.*
