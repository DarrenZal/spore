# Johar — Four Dimensions of Societal Organizing: A Co-Held Architecture of Legitimacy

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Four Dimensions of Societal Organizing: A Co-Held Architecture of Legitimacy," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/four-dimensions-of-societal-organizing

## What It Is

A short essay (768w) proposing four foundational dimensions that must be co-held to compose a viable architecture for societal decision-making. No single organizing principle can carry the full burden of societal coordination — legitimacy can no longer be anchored to one dominant axis. The four dimensions: (1) Democracy — legitimacy through participatory alignment (quadratic voting, citizens' assemblies, liquid democracy); (2) Entrusted Stewardship — legitimacy through mandated authority based on competence and bounded mandate; (3) Scientific Society — legitimacy through experimentation and learning (A/B policy trials, adaptive regulation, feedback institutions); (4) Radical Markets — legitimacy through dynamic allocation and revaluation (Harberger taxation, quadratic funding, data labor rights). These are compositional, not adversarial — a polycentric arrangement where divergent institutional logics mutually counterbalance and amplify.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Multi-projection as polycentric sensing:** Spore's eight projections offer distinct lenses on the same coordination reality. The essay's four dimensions describe four distinct legitimacy logics that must operate simultaneously — a structural parallel to Spore's multi-projection approach.

2. **Governance from evidence and iteration:** Spore's learning membrane — claims are revisable, attestation decays, evidence accumulates — embodies the "Scientific Society" dimension: governance as a continuous learning system that evolves through iterative hypotheses and feedback.

3. **Contestability as democratic infrastructure:** Spore's constitutional commitment to contestability — the right to challenge claims, decisions, and outputs — provides the participatory alignment the "Democracy" dimension requires.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Co-Held Architecture of Legitimacy

The essay's foundational move: legitimacy is plural. No single axis — democratic will, technocratic expertise, delegated authority, or market rationality — suffices alone. The crisis is not absence but imbalance: over-optimising for one dimension while neglecting others. The task is not to rank these logics but to recognise their interdependence and design institutions where "no single instrument dominates, but all are" in composition.

For Spore, this directly addresses the grammar's multi-modal coordination. The essay provides an institutional taxonomy for why Spore's governance must integrate multiple legitimacy sources: participatory contribution (democracy), competence-based stewardship (entrusted authority), evidence-based learning (scientific society), and dynamic value allocation (radical markets).

### 2. Orchestration Over Dominance

The shift from "models of dominance to frameworks of orchestration" names the governance move that Spore's coordination ecology embodies. The essay argues this is not merely a design preference but a requirement for institutions operating under "entangled ecological, social, and economic transitions." Resilient, reflexive, and regenerative governance requires all four dimensions operating in counterbalance.

For Spore, this strengthens the case for the grammar's adaptive design: governance that privileges one coordination mode (e.g., pure voting, pure expertise, pure market) will fail under complexity. The grammar must accommodate multiple modes simultaneously.

## Open Questions

1. **Does Spore's grammar already instantiate all four dimensions?** The essay's four dimensions map onto Spore's existing features: contestability (democracy), evidence-based claims (scientific society), multi-projection (stewardship via distinct lenses). But "radical markets" — dynamic allocation and revaluation as civic infrastructure — has no clear Spore equivalent. Whether this is a gap in the grammar or whether the economy-and-value syntheses (priceability, human-economy-and-commitment) already address this is unresolved.

2. **How does co-held legitimacy differ from governance-under-volatility?** The governance-under-volatility synthesis names adaptive governance as a response to compound volatility. Co-held legitimacy names plural legitimacy sources as a response to institutional complexity. Whether these are complementary (volatility is the condition, co-held legitimacy is the structural response) or partially overlapping (both address why singular governance fails) affects whether they should converge or remain distinct families.
