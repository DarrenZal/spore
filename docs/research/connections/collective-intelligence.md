# Collective Intelligence

This Tier 2 cluster note synthesizes the P2P Foundation wiki's treatment of collective intelligence (CI) as a commons requiring dedicated language, coordination structure, and governance — the cousin-concept cluster sitting closest to Spore's positioning as "collective-agency substrate" in the Tier 1a field scan. The cluster cites 15 fully-read wiki pages plus two category anchors (`Category:Intelligence`, `Category:Commons`). Its central finding: the wiki corpus treats CI not as a property that emerges automatically from networking individual intelligences, but as a *cultivated substrate* — one that can be captured, enclosed, or diminished as readily as it can be augmented, and that therefore requires explicit governance, dedicated language, and a specified distinction between the CI form (pyramidal / holomidal / self-organizing) a given coordination system is realizing.

This matters for Spore three ways: (a) Spore's `relational-agency-and-holons` canon names collective agency as the basic unit but under-specifies the CI form it claims (pyramidal/holomidal/SOCI); (b) the mycelial-holarchy architecture carries stigmergic-coordination assumptions that the Elliott/Heylighen CI literature has already operationalized with explicit scale-breakpoints (small-group social negotiation vs large-group stigmergy); (c) the Finidori "CI is a commons that needs a dedicated language" thesis directly corroborates Spore's existence as a coordination grammar — and supplies a published source-tradition warrant for the grammar-building project itself.

## 1. What CI names in the corpus (and the definitional plurality)

The wiki's canonical entry records CI as "the capacity of a human community to evolve toward higher order complexity thought, problem-solving and integration through collaboration and innovation" (George Pór), with Pierre Lévy's longer form adding "collective cognitive powers … perception, action planning and coordination, reasoning, prediction, memory, imagination and hypothesis generation, inquisitiveness, problem solving and, above all, learning capacity." Jean-François Noubel reframes it operationally as "the capability for a group to organize itself in order to decide upon its own future and control the means to attain it in complex contexts." Thomas Malone pares it to "groups of individuals acting collectively in ways that seem intelligent." Tom Atlee, Norman Lee Johnson, Tadeusz Szuba, and Francis Heylighen each offer distinct angles (living-systems capacity, symbiotic intelligence, parallel/distributed computation, collective problem-solving).

This definitional plurality is not noise — it is the reason Finidori's "dedicated language" claim is load-bearing: CI operates across multiple scales, substrates, and mechanisms, and the lack of shared vocabulary is itself a coordination failure mode for communities trying to mobilize it across projects.

## 2. The Finidori thesis: CI as a commons that needs protection and language

Hélène Finidori's 2014 Spanda Journal article is the corpus's strongest articulation of the CI-as-commons position. The paradox she names: "at the same time as collective intelligence is making itself increasingly palpable and promising as a whole, the possibility of it being actionable locally and effectively enabling us to get ourselves out of a planetary predicament is becoming remote." Technology "boosts the capacity of individuals and communities to become authors of their own stories … [but] can also lock up potential inside black boxes for just a few to benefit from."

The mobilization of CI at wider systemic levels "involves more complex mechanisms and in particular the capacity to achieve more complex synergies (i.e. systemic coherence) across multiple centres in addition to 'simple' local synthesis … within cohesive centres of shared experience." This is the point of direct contact with Spore's coordination grammar: "systemic coherence across multiple centres" is what Spore's mycelial-holarchy architecture already claims to enable, and Finidori's framing names the failure mode if the coherence-across-centres capacity is absent — CI appears promising globally but remains inactionable locally.

Finidori's constructive move: "a pattern language for systemic change regenerative of commons could be the means of expression of operationalized collective intelligence." Spore's frozen P2P-wiki vocabulary (`concepts-p2p-wiki.yaml`) and Spore's broader lexicon (`spore.term.*`) are operationalizing exactly this claim in practice — though without citing Finidori as prior art.

## 3. The holomidal / pyramidal / SOCI typology

The wiki corpus records three distinct CI organizational forms:

- **Pyramidal CI** (Noubel / CIR Institute): centralized decision making in top-down chains of command. Characteristics: centralized power, division of labor, panopticism (controlled partitioned information), chain of command, authority (via divine right, merit, expertise, law, cast, diplomas), scarcity (as catalyst for hierarchy), standards-and-norms (as mechanisms of proprietarization). Pyramidal CI "doesn't need to have an authoritarian political or social model. Authority can hide in ontological structures … the way society sees and builds its social reality through language." Highly efficient and goal-oriented; structurally prone to capture.
- **Holomidal CI** (Noubel): the form emerging from the Internet era — "local and global, decentralized and distributed, agile, polymorphic, based on leadership, individuation, open source, integral wealth and mutualist economy." Contrasts with pyramidal via *holopticism* (distributed visibility-across-the-whole) rather than *panopticism* (center-to-edge visibility).
- **Self-Organizing Collective Intelligence (SOCI)** (Greenhall): CI as an attractor-driven organism with a developmental lifecycle — an aesthetic or yearning attracts attention/energy, attention makes the attractor more real, generative loops resolve challenges, each resolution becomes a "frozen accident" accreted into core architecture. SOCI move through infancy → adolescence → mature (effective but less creative) adulthood. Bitcoin is the exemplar: a global, virtual, motivation/reward-architecture-embedded SOCI that innovates *against* existing social fabric (money).

The typology matters for Spore: claiming "collective intelligence" without specifying which form (pyramidal / holomidal / SOCI) leaves the coordination stance ambiguous, and the wiki corpus explicitly warns against pyramidal capture hiding in "ontological structures … the way society sees and builds its social reality through language" — directly relevant to any grammar-building project.

## 4. Stigmergy as the scale-breakpoint mechanism

The wiki's stigmergy corpus (Elliott, Heylighen, Brastaviceanu, Georgie BC) operationalizes CI at scale via environmental-mediation: coordination where agents "cannot communicate directly with one another, but rather must engage indirectly via a medium." Elliott's PhD research specifies a structural breakpoint: **collaboration in small groups (2–25) relies upon social negotiation; collaboration in large groups (25–n) is dependent upon stigmergy.** Social mediation, while inherent to collaboration, "can provide a barrier to the rapid and seamless integration of contributions that characterises projects such as Wikipedia.org and the Open Source software movement."

Brastaviceanu's Open Climate Collabathon extension adds a design discipline: stigmergic environments for humans require (a) persistent communication tools for read-access to current/past activity, (b) incentive structures biasing choices toward system goals, and (c) "embedded roles that reduce the choice of action" (analogous to speed bumps vs speed limits — the rule embodied in the medium, not enforced by policing).

Heylighen's extension positions stigmergy as the economics-of-information foundation for open-access development's success, and GeorgieBC's formulation states the governance implication bluntly: "A new system of governance or collaboration that does not follow a competitive hierarchical model will need to employ stigmergy in most of its action based systems. It is neither reasonable nor desirable for individual thought and action to be subjugated to group consensus in matters which do not affect the group." Stigmergy in this reading is not merely a coordination pattern — it is the mechanism that *replaces representation of persons with representation of actions*, which is the structural move Spore's lexicon entry for `spore.term.stigmergy` already anchors.

## 5. Distributed cognition: CI has cognitive-science prior art

The wiki's `Distributed Cognition` entry (Hutchins 1980s) extends CI prior art into cognitive science. Distributed cognition proposes that "human knowledge and cognition are not confined to the individual. Instead, it is distributed by placing memories, facts, or knowledge on the objects, individuals, and tools in our environment." The framework specifies three distribution types: across members of a social group, across internal/external (material/environmental) structure, and through time (products of earlier events transforming related later events).

This is the cognitive-science cousin of Spore's `field` vocabulary and IC's `memory-layers` — distributed cognition names the *substrate-dependence* of cognition rather than treating cognition as an individual property, which supplies CI claims with a recognized cognitive-science lineage distinct from the CI-as-governance and CI-as-commons lineages.

## 6. Malone: CI as designed system (the "genome" move)

Thomas Malone, Robert Laubacher, and Chrysanthos Dellarocas (MIT Center for Collective Intelligence) re-frame CI as a design discipline via the biological-analogy move of mapping "genes" — reusable building blocks — across 250+ CI examples enabled by the Web. The intent: "so 'managers can do more than just look at examples and hope for inspiration.'" This positions CI as an engineering discipline with specifiable design patterns, not only an emergent-property framing.

For Spore and PM, the Malone framing is useful precisely because it pairs naturally with Finidori's pattern-language thesis: if CI needs a "dedicated language" (Finidori) and has specifiable "genes" (Malone), then Spore's coordination grammar and PM's pool/routing/matching primitives can cite both the cultivation-discipline and the design-discipline traditions without picking one over the other.

## 7. Heylighen / Last: Global Brain as mechanism for the Global Commons

Cadell Last's 2016 paper extends Francis Heylighen's Global Brain framing into a governance claim: the Global Brain must be positioned not as a self-organizing substitute for governance but as a **mechanism for the Global Commons**, with two conceptual components — an `automated commons` ("networks related to foundational operations of civilization … built utilizing the 'objective knowledge' of automated machinery and designed around principles of universal access, abolition of property, and phasing out of labour") and a `collaborative commons` ("networks of social exchange mediated by 'offers' and 'demands' … facilitating the self-organization of goods/services built on the foundation of social trust and reputation as primary bonding mechanism"). These components sit on Ostrom's commons-governance literature explicitly.

The `collaborative commons` framing is strikingly close to Poietic Match's grammar: "offer/need" mechanisms, reputation-bonding instead of monetary transaction, self-organization of goods/services through distributed matching. The Last–Heylighen framing supplies PM with an explicit commons-anchored prior art for its coordination-substrate positioning — one that is already entangled with Ostrom via Last's citation, and therefore composes with the polycentric-governance R1 and boundary-commoning R2 lineage without additional disambiguation.

## 8. Pór: augmenting CI of commons-based initiatives (Spore's direct cousin)

George Pór's framework (13th IASC Biennial, Hyderabad 2011) makes the strongest single-paper case for CI-as-commons-infrastructure: "Commons are sustained by 'communities working together in self-governing ways in order to protect resources from enclosure or to build new openly-shared resources.' Self-governance needs shared knowledge. The scalability of commons-based production and distribution depend on the capacity of the communities to augment their collective intelligence." His stated motivations map directly onto Spore's project:

- Make visible and augment the CI of commons-based initiatives.
- Increase connectivity in and among commons.
- Evolve "collective sensing and meaning-making organs … to the growth and evolution of the commons."
- Seed a research community around Commons-and-CI.

The Pór paper supplies Spore's positioning in the Tier 1a field scan with a published prior-art source: Spore is not inventing a new category; it is a contemporary instance of the Commons-and-CI research program Pór called for in 2011, operating with vocabulary (`field`, `membrane`, `holon`, `stigmergy`, `intent-pressure`, `linguistic-closure`) that Finidori's pattern-language thesis directly anticipates.

## 9. Category-level breadth: CI is a corpus-scale organizing frame

The `Category:Intelligence` page is not a flat tag — it opens with the injunction "Please compare the characteristics of Holomidal Collective Intelligence with those of Pyramidal Collective Intelligence" and catalogs a deep corpus including Cynefin (Snowden), Dyson's four species/minds framework, Jenkins's three CI models (aggregative/curatorial/deliberative), the Wisdom Lexicon Project, Dunbar's Social Brain Hypothesis, Teilhard de Chardin's "Brain of Brains," Merlin Donald's evolution-of-cognition stages, and the Noosphere tradition. The category tags itself as `Category:P2P_Domains` — placing CI among the foundational domain categories of the P2P corpus, not among specialized sub-topics. This is corpus-breadth evidence that CI is a pervasive P2P organizing frame, not a single-page concept.

The `Category:Commons` cross-anchor confirms the commons-CI entanglement at category level: Bauwens's three-part commons definition ("shared resources … maintained or co-produced by a community or group of stakeholders … managed according to the rules and values of that community") is listed as `Category:P2P_Paradigms`, and the Pór paper's intake into this category (via `Category:Commons` membership on the augmentation-framework page) places CI-as-commons squarely inside the wiki's commons paradigm layer, not merely adjacent to it.

## 10. Tensions (cautions for any Spore / IC / PM canon edit)

1. **Definitional plurality as genuine signal, not merely noise.** Six+ distinct CI definitions in the canonical wiki entry (Lévy, Noubel, Pór, Atlee, Malone, Szuba, Heylighen) each carry different assumptions about substrate, scale, mechanism, and success criterion. Spore citing CI as cousin concept should pick a definitional stance explicitly rather than implying the plurality collapses into a single referent.
2. **Pyramidal-capture risk via language.** Pyramidal CI "doesn't need to have an authoritarian political or social model. Authority can hide in ontological structures … the way society sees and builds its social reality through language." For a grammar-building project like Spore, this is first-order: the coordination grammar itself is a site where pyramidal capture can occur, and explicit governance over vocabulary evolution (cf. the frozen yaml + Move 0 moratorium) is the concrete discipline that addresses it.
3. **SOCI lifecycle risk (frozen-accidents).** Greenhall's SOCI framing states that architectural choices made during infancy become "frozen accidents" shaping future paths. For Spore, any canon adopted during the Tier 2 intake — vocabulary choices, disposition conventions, RID anchoring format — are the frozen accidents of this phase. This is not a reason to avoid adoption; it is a reason to state the choices as temporary/reversible where possible and to carry the SOCI-lifecycle-risk caution in the canon documentation.
4. **Small-vs-large-group scale discontinuity.** Elliott's 2–25 vs 25–n breakpoint (social negotiation vs stigmergy) is empirical. Spore's coordination grammar should state which scale(s) its primitives target — "field" and "membrane" seem to cover both; "linguistic-closure" and "intent-pressure" seem to target the small-group end — rather than implying a scale-free claim that the empirical CI literature does not support.
5. **Commons-vs-engineering-discipline tension.** Finidori (CI-as-commons-needing-language) and Malone (CI-as-engineerable-design-patterns) are both in the corpus. Spore adopting one without the other leaves a gap in external credibility: CI communities reading Spore from the commons tradition will ask "where is the cultivation discipline?"; CI communities reading from the engineering tradition will ask "where are the design patterns?" The Pór framework is the in-corpus bridge.
6. **Global-Brain-mechanistic-determinism risk.** Heylighen's Global Brain framing can slide toward technological determinism if the Last "mechanism for Global Commons" constraint is not carried explicitly. Spore importing Global-Brain vocabulary without the Ostrom/Last commons-mechanism constraint would inherit the determinism risk.
7. **Collaborative-Intelligence alias dead-anchor.** The `Collaborative_Intelligence` redirect on the local mirror (2026-04-15) is a self-referential dead redirect; CQ - Collaborative Intelligence carries the substantive corroborating content. Any Spore language naming "collaborative intelligence" as an alias should cite `CQ - Collaborative Intelligence` explicitly rather than the root slug.

## 11. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The wiki evidence warrants Spore's `relational-agency-and-holons` canon explicitly specifying which CI form (holomidal, not pyramidal; SOCI-shaped in the Greenhall sense) it claims; Spore's `term.stigmergy` lexicon entry explicitly importing Elliott's 2–25 vs 25–n scale breakpoint as the operational criterion for stigmergy applicability; Spore's `term.linguistic-closure` entry importing the pyramidal-capture-via-ontological-structures discipline from Noubel / CIR Institute; IC's `memory-layers` importing the distributed-cognition three-distribution-types framing; and PM's research landscape carrying the Last/Heylighen `collaborative commons` framing as a direct prior-art citation for the offer/need pool-routing architecture. None of these require new canon primitives, consistent with the Move 0 moratorium and the Tier 1b/Tier 2 disposition pattern.

## 12. Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §lead · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Collective_Intelligence]
The canonical wiki definition of collective intelligence (George Pór) — "the capacity of a human community to evolve toward higher order complexity thought, problem-solving and integration through collaboration and innovation" — positions CI as a community-scale capacity for integration, not merely as aggregation of individual intelligences, establishing the cultivated-substrate framing that the rest of the corpus builds on.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Pierre Levy · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Collective_Intelligence]
Pierre Lévy's expansion specifies CI as "the cognitive powers of a group — e.g., perception, action planning and coordination, reasoning, prediction, memory, imagination and hypothesis generation, inquisitiveness, problem solving and, above all, learning capacity … closely related to the group's culture," establishing that CI is culturally-embedded (not content-neutral) and multi-capability (not a single faculty), both of which are first-order claims for any coordination grammar that imports CI lineage.

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Abstract · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Collective_Intelligence_is_a_Commons_That_Needs_a_Dedicated_Language]
Finidori (2014) explicitly frames CI as a commons that needs protection and a dedicated language: "Technology is what boosts the capacity of individuals and communities to become authors of their own stories, and what enables collective intelligence to become aware of itself and to fulfill its long awaited promise. It is also what can lock up potential inside black boxes for just a few to benefit from" — establishing CI-as-commons as a published source tradition with an explicit language-cultivation program, directly cousin to Spore's frozen vocabulary discipline.

**C4** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Excerpt · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Collective_Intelligence_is_a_Commons_That_Needs_a_Dedicated_Language]
Finidori specifies the scale-up mechanism: "The mobilization of collective intelligence at wider systemic levels beyond the boundaries of our habitual communities to solve wicked interconnected problems involves more complex mechanisms and in particular the capacity to achieve more complex synergies (i.e. systemic coherence) across multiple centres in addition to 'simple' local synthesis (i.e. complexity reductions) within cohesive centres of shared experience" — positioning cross-centre coherence as the load-bearing operation, directly corroborating Spore's mycelial-holarchy-architecture claim that holon composition (not only within-holon coordination) is a first-order concern.

**C5** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Characteristics · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Pyramidal_Collective_Intelligence]
Pyramidal CI is characterized by centralized power, division of labor, panopticism (controlled/partitioned information), chain of command, and authority-hidden-in-ontological-structures — explicitly: "An authority-based pyramidal society doesn't need to have an authoritarian political or social model. Authority can hide in ontological structures (see invisible architectures, i.e. the way society sees and builds its social reality through language)" — establishing that pyramidal capture is a first-order risk for any grammar-building project, not only for explicit institutions.

**C6** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Holomidal_Collective_Intelligence]
Holomidal CI (Noubel) is the distinct CI form enabled by the Internet — "local and global, decentralized and distributed, agile, polymorphic, based on leadership, individuation, open source, integral wealth and mutualist economy" — structurally contrasted with pyramidal CI via holopticism (distributed visibility-of-the-whole) rather than panopticism, supplying Spore's coordination grammar with vocabulary for the CI form it implicitly claims but has not named.

**C7** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Characteristics · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Stigmergy]
Elliott's empirical framework specifies the collaboration-scale discontinuity: "Collaboration in small groups (roughly 2-25) relies upon social negotiation to evolve and guide its process and creative output … Collaboration in large groups (roughly 25-n) is dependent upon stigmergy" — establishing stigmergy applicability as a function of group size with a specified breakpoint, which Spore's lexicon entries covering coordination primitives should import rather than claiming scale-free applicability.

**C8** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Stigmergy and the Web · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Stigmergy]
Stigmergy is defined operationally as coordination in which agents "cannot communicate directly with one another, but rather must engage indirectly via a medium" — the medium must support both read access and write access, "everyone has to be able to not only read it but to be able to write into it also" — establishing the structural requirement (read+write symmetric substrate) that distinguishes stigmergic coordination from broadcast, one-way, or bilateral-channel coordination.

**C9** [confidence: high] [anchor: §How a Stigmergy of Actions Replaces Representation of Persons · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Stigmergy]
GeorgieBC's synthesis positions stigmergy as the structural mechanism replacing "representation of persons" (hierarchical committees, representative democracy) with "representation of actions" — "a new system of governance or collaboration that does not follow a competitive hierarchical model will need to employ stigmergy in most of its action based systems … it is neither reasonable nor desirable for individual thought and action to be subjugated to group consensus in matters which do not affect the group" — making stigmergy a governance move, not only a coordination pattern.

**C10** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Global_Brain_as_Mechanism_for_the_Global_Commons]
Cadell Last (2016) specifies two complementary commons structures built on Global Brain mechanisms: the `automated commons` ("networks related to foundational operations of civilization … built utilizing the 'objective knowledge' of automated machinery and designed around principles of universal access, abolition of property, and phasing out of labour") and the `collaborative commons` ("networks of social exchange mediated by 'offers' and 'demands' … facilitating the self-organization of goods/services built on the foundation of social trust and reputation as primary bonding mechanism") — establishing offer/need mechanism, reputation-bonding, and social-trust substrate as published commons-anchored prior art for coordination grammars like Poietic Match.

**C11** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Global_Brain_as_Mechanism_for_the_Global_Commons]
Last explicitly positions the Global Brain under Ostrom's commons-governance lineage: "GB theorists interested in 'guiding the self-organization of the Global Brain towards a Global Commons' should pay particular attention to the commons literature established by Elinor Ostrom (1990, 2009)" — foreclosing the technological-determinism reading of Global Brain framings and requiring any import of Global Brain vocabulary to carry the Ostrom commons-governance constraint explicitly.

**C12** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Abstract · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Framework_for_Augmenting_the_Collective_Intelligence_of_the_Ecosystem_of_Commons-based_Initiatives]
George Pór's 2011 framework makes CI-as-commons-infrastructure the explicit research agenda: "Commons are sustained by 'communities working together in self-governing ways in order to protect resources from enclosure or to build new openly-shared resources.' Self-governance needs shared knowledge. The scalability of commons-based production and distribution depend on the capacity of the communities to augment their collective intelligence" — establishing Commons-and-CI as a named research program with specified motivations (visible CI, cross-commons connectivity, collective sensing/meaning-making organs, Commons-and-CI research community).

**C13** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Self_Organizing_Collective_Intelligences]
Greenhall's SOCI framing specifies a developmental lifecycle: "some sort of attractor — some aesthetic sensibility or yearning — that is able to grab the attention and energy of some group of people … when those people apply their attention and energy to the SOCI, this makes it more real, easier for more people to grasp and to find interesting and valuable … As the SOCI develops, the choices that it makes — the solutions that it crafts — become part of its core architecture. 'Frozen accidents' in its development, these begin to shape and define its future paths" — establishing that early-phase architectural choices in a SOCI are structurally path-dependent, relevant to any Tier 2 vocabulary choice Spore makes during its own infancy phase.

**C14** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Distributed_Cognition]
Distributed cognition (Hutchins) specifies three distribution types: "Cognitive processes may be distributed across the members of a social group … may be distributed in the sense that the operation of the cognitive system involves coordination between internal and external (material or environmental) structure … may be distributed through time in such a way that the products of earlier events can transform the nature of related events" — supplying CI-adjacent cognitive-science prior art that is substrate-dependent (not individual-mind-centric) and that explicitly includes temporal distribution alongside social and material distribution.
