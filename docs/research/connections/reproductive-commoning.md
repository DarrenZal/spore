# Reproductive Commoning

This bridge note carries the single most structurally important finding from Phase A of the P2P Foundation wiki Pass 2 intake: **commoning is first an act of reproduction — care, provisioning, territorial stewardship — not of coordination.** The implication for Spore is non-trivial: field, membrane, holarchy, and federation-protocol should be re-read as *reproductive apparatus*, not as production systems with reproductive add-ons. This reframes Pass 1's entire canon review. The feminist commons literature (Federici, Bhattacharya), the more-than-human commons literature (Bresnihan, Linebaugh), and the circulation-of-the-common literature (Dyer-Witheford) each arrive at the same structural point from different angles: capitalist enclosure depends on the epistemological separation of the social/natural and the productive/reproductive, and commons-based alternatives must invert that separation rather than replicate it in new protocol form.

This note does not propose new Spore primitives. It proposes that existing primitives be re-framed — and, per user decision on DH-Spore-1, that **reproductive-labor invisibilization be explicitly named as a first-order power-capture concern** in `spore.mycorrhizal-federation-protocol`, alongside (not subordinate to) protocol lock-in, gatekeeper roles, and data asymmetry.

## 1. The reproduction-primacy thesis

The wiki evidence across six pages converges on a structural claim that Pass 1 did not carry: **the fundamental unit of commoning is the reproduction of life, and coordination is a derivative practice that emerges from and must serve reproductive work.** Federici's "Communalization of Housework" frames this from the material-feminist tradition: "the reproduction of human beings is the most labor-intensive work on earth" and "cannot [be] mechanize[d]... except at a terrible cost." Bresnihan's More-Than-Human Commons frames it from the relational-ontology tradition: "use-rights in the commons... must be contingent on ongoing participation in the production and care of the commons understood as the entire collective of humans, animals, artifacts, elements that are necessary to maintain life processes." Bhattacharya's Social Reproduction Theory frames it from the classical-Marxist tradition: "the production of goods and services and the production of life are part of one integrated process," and capitalism's "naturalizing into nonexistence" of reproductive labor is the defining analytic move SRT exists to undo.

Pass 1's canon review took coordination as primary and treated care as a downstream culture-layer concern. The wiki evidence says this gets the ordering backwards. Spore's grammar should carry the inverted ordering explicitly, not implicitly.

## 2. Federici's housing-as-spatial-commons is direct Spore-lineage

Federici's "Communalization of Housework" argues that housing is *itself* a spatial commons — not a container where separate-from-commons life happens, but the oikos on which the economy is built. The nineteenth-century materialist-feminist tradition she revives (communal kitchens, cooperative households, workers' control of reproduction) understood the home as "an important 'spatial component of the oppression of women'" that commoning must reclaim as "a center of collective life, one traversed by multiple people and forms of cooperation."

This is Spore's `field` concept, with direct commons-scholarship lineage. A Spore field is a bounded coordination surface that is *also* a site of material reproduction, traversable by multiple subjects, provisioning shared life. Federici gives this an 150-year genealogy and a source-literature anchored in proletarian-neighborhood practice, not protocol design. Framing `field` as reproductive apparatus (where Federici's housing-as-spatial-commons is the prior art) is stronger and more honest than framing it as a novel coordination primitive.

## 3. Tontines and ollas communes as commitment-pooling prehistory

Federici's "Women and the Commons" documents two forms of pre-monetary commoning that sit upstream of anything PM can legitimately claim as novel:

- **Tontines** (West African, Cambodian, Senegalese): "autonomous, self-managed, women-made banking systems that provide cash to individuals or groups that have no access to banks, working purely on a basis of trust" — mutual-credit pools with centuries of operational history, structurally distinct from World Bank microcredit (which "functions on a basis of mutual policing and shame").
- **Ollas communes** (Chilean and Peruvian, 1980s): "common cooking pots" — provisioning pools set up by women who could no longer afford to shop alone during hyperinflation.

These are commitment-pooling (tontines) and provisioning-pooling (ollas) in the operational sense PM uses both terms. They are also *women-led reproductive-economic commoning*. Any PM framing that treats commitment-pooling as a novel technical primitive owes an acknowledgment to the genealogy that has been operating this pattern for far longer than the PM protocol has existed. This note flags that correction for `pm.connection.commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit`.

## 4. Bresnihan: commoning is a verb, and care is the primary coordinating practice

Bresnihan's More-Than-Human Commons (following Linebaugh, Federici, De Angelis) argues that commons is not a resource but a relation — "something that is only ever constituted through acting and doing in common." The relational subject of the commons is "always already caught up in a world that is intimately shared," and "use-rights" follow from "ongoing participation in the production and care of the commons." This is the `commons-as-verb` concept (v2 frozen) and it has direct implication for `spore.relational-agency-and-holons`: the primary coordinating practice in a Spore field is *care* (reproductive labor), not coordination-as-message-passing. Coordination emerges from care; the field holds because participants reproduce it; participation IS reproduction.

Bresnihan explicitly extends this to the human/non-human boundary: the commons "is not only human but also non-human" and the commoning collective includes "the entire collective of humans, animals, artifacts, elements that are necessary to maintain life processes." This is the more-than-human extension of relational agency that Spore's holon-theoretic language is compatible with but under-specifies.

## 5. Dyer-Witheford's A-C-A' circuit reframes federation as commoning mechanism

Dyer-Witheford's Circulation of the Common proposes that the cellular form of a post-capitalist society is the *common* (a good produced to be shared) as distinct from the *commodity* (a good produced for exchange). The basic circuit is `A ─ C ─ A'` — Associations producing Commons which sustain further Associations — and replaces the capitalist M-C-M' circuit (Money-Commodity-Money') as the governing metabolic logic. Crucially: "we are dealing not only with Commons instead of Commodities, but with Associations instead of Money. The implication is that collective organization, not market exchange, governs the distribution of Commons."

This reframes Spore's `mycorrhizal-federation-protocol` and `mycelial-holarchy-architecture`. Federation is *not* a substitute for associational organizing nor a protocol layer that sits atop commons activity. Federation is the coordination mechanism by which associations sustain, expand, and interconnect their commons. Dyer-Witheford also explicitly includes "labour (in its broadest sense, comprising reproductive and productive work)" as a commons category, bringing reproductive labor into the associational-circuit vocabulary at its foundation.

Pass 1's federation framing reads too easily as "protocol governs commons"; the commons-scholarship framing says "associations govern commons, and protocols are substrates embedded in associational practice." The R-claim below calls for this re-framing in canon.

## 6. Reproductive-labor invisibilization is a first-order power-capture mechanism

Bresnihan cites Mies via Federici: "'all the labour that goes into the production of life... is not seen as the conscious interaction of a human being with nature... but rather as an activity of nature, which produces plants and animals unconsciously and has no control over this process.'" He argues that "capitalist enclosure and biopolitical control necessarily involve the de-valorizing and 'invisibilizing' of those myriad, situated relations and practices of (re)production that exist between people and the manifold resources they rely on." The Anthropology of the Commons page reinforces this: the wiki's commons literature treats gender, race, and sexuality as *structurally load-bearing*, not incidental — and Gibson-Graham's critique of "social capital" shows how capitalocentric nomenclature strips reproductive relations of their specificity by folding them into "bonding, binding, and linking" as investment-categories.

The implication for Spore's power-capture doctrine is direct. Pass 1 frames power-capture as protocol lock-in, gatekeeper role accrual, and data asymmetry. These are necessary but not sufficient. **The historically prior and more fundamental capture mechanism is the erasure of reproductive labor from coordination visibility** — the relegation of care, provisioning, and maintenance into "naturalized," invisibilized substrate that coordination-as-protocol appears to float atop. A federation-protocol that does not explicitly render reproductive labor visible as first-order coordination work is, by the wiki's commons literature, structurally vulnerable to the same capture mechanism that destroyed the historical commons.

Per user decision on DH-Spore-1, `spore.mycorrhizal-federation-protocol` should name reproductive-labor invisibilization as a capture mechanism with equal standing to protocol lock-in, gatekeeper roles, and data asymmetry. This is the note's operational contribution to Spore power-capture doctrine. R5 (below) carries it.

## 7. Tensions (cautions for any Spore canon edit)

1. **Reproduction primacy vs coordination primacy** (framing-level). Spore canon has tacitly operated on coordination-primacy: define the coordination surface, then treat care as a cultural value realized on top. The wiki evidence inverts this. The R-claims below propose re-framing, not replacement. But the inversion is load-bearing — if applied honestly, it changes how `field`, `relational-agency-and-holons`, and federation-protocol are motivated in canon. This is the note-level disposition commentary in §8.
2. **Reproductive-labor invisibilization as capture** (power-capture doctrine). Pass 1 under-specified this. DH-Spore-1 was unresolved at the Phase A checkpoint; user has now resolved it as **YES — encode as first-order power-capture concern**. R5 carries it forward. Sibling bridge notes (policentric-governance, federation-power-capture-resolution from Pass 1 Section 5) should also reference this.
3. **Tontines-as-commitment-pooling-genealogy vs PM novelty framing** (PM-side risk). The tontine/ollas prior art is structurally upstream of any novelty claim PM might make about commitment-pooling or mutual-credit. PM canon should acknowledge the genealogy rather than claim technical novelty; R6 flags this for `pm.connection.commitment-pooling-and-mutual-credit`.
4. **More-than-human scope under-specification** (relational-agency risk). Bresnihan's commoning-collective extends "to humans, animals, artifacts, elements." Spore's `relational-agency-and-holons` language is compatible with this but does not operationalize non-human agency. This bridge note raises the under-specification but does not propose a fix; `spore.connection.pluriversal-commoning` (separate bridge note) is the expected vehicle.
5. **Material vs "immaterial" commons false split** (Bresnihan §Social-Commons caution). Bresnihan explicitly rebuts the Hardt-style split between material (ecological, scarce) and immaterial (knowledge, infinitely reproducible) commons: reproductive labor is what binds them together, and the split "excludes the socially productive and reproductive labor of humans involved in caring for the 'natural' resources they rely on." Any Spore framing that treats knowledge-commons as clean-abstract and ecological-commons as messy-material inherits this error. The R-claim on `spore.term.field` below carries the integrated framing.
6. **Federici is prescriptive, Bresnihan is descriptive** (mode mismatch). Federici writes in an explicit political-program register ("we must include in our political agenda... this time it is women who must build the new commons"). Bresnihan and Dyer-Witheford are descriptive-theoretical. Spore canon is grammar (descriptive of coordination operations), not program. R-claims below import the descriptive structural insight, not Federici's specific prescriptive-program content.

## 8. Disposition

**Disposition: clarify existing term.** The six wiki sources do not warrant a new Spore primitive — `reproductive-commoning` (v2 frozen concept) is a *framing* that re-reads existing primitives, not a new primitive alongside them. But the re-framing is **load-bearing**: if applied honestly across the R-claims below, it changes how `spore.term.field`, `spore.relational-agency-and-holons`, `spore.mycorrhizal-federation-protocol`, and `spore.mycelial-holarchy-architecture` are motivated in canon.

**Note-level commentary on reproduction primacy** (per user Phase A review decision — carry explicitly, not just in proto-claims). Phase A's single most important structural insight is that commoning is first reproduction and only derivatively coordination. Spore's canon review from Pass 1 operated on the inverted ordering. If the reproduction-primacy framing is honest, Spore's coordination grammar should be motivated as *grammar for reproductive apparatus*, and care as *the primary coordinating practice that protocol serves*. The R-claims below propose motivating language edits, not new primitives. But the motivating-language layer is where canon is read — so the note-level framing matters for how canon downstream reads even when no primitive changes.

**Power-capture extension** (per user decision on DH-Spore-1). Reproductive-labor visibility is encoded as first-order power-capture concern in R5, targeting `spore.mycorrhizal-federation-protocol`. This is the operational contribution of this note to Spore power-capture doctrine and the concrete DH-Spore-1 resolution commitment.

**Disposition implications for Move 0**: The R-claims below are re-framing edits (motivating language) plus one doctrine extension (R5). None propose new primitives. Consistent with the Pass 1 moratorium lift of 2026-04-16, these are canon-review edits eligible for Move 0 consideration alongside the Spore #1 (polycentric-governance import) and Spore #10 (federation power-capture resolution) priority items from the Pass 1 post-intake synthesis.

## 9. Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Communalization_of_Housework]
Federici frames housing itself as a spatial commons — the oikos on which the economy is built — and calls for the "communalization of housework" as the foundation of new forms of social reproduction. Reclaimed commons are not abstract coordination surfaces but material sites of collective life (communal kitchens, cooperative households, shared reproduction of life), with a 150-year materialist-feminist lineage from mid-nineteenth-century utopian socialist experiments through the 1920s, suppressed by the Red Scare.

**C2** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Communalization_of_Housework]
Federici's structural argument for reproductive commoning: "the reproduction of human beings is the most labor-intensive work on earth, and to a very large extent it is work that is irreducible to mechanization" — cannot be robotized except "at a terrible cost for the people involved." Shared responsibility and cooperative work are therefore not one lifestyle option among many but the necessary substrate of adequate reproduction. Privatization of reproduction under capitalism is read as a historically specific and destructive deviation from the collective-reproduction norm.

**C3** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Women_and_the_Commons]
Tontines (in West African, Cambodian, Senegalese women-led practice) and ollas communes (in 1980s Chilean and Peruvian inflation contexts) are documented by Federici as autonomous, self-managed, trust-based pre-monetary commoning institutions — tontines as commitment-pooling / mutual-credit substrates, ollas as provisioning pools. These are structurally distinct from microcredit-as-mutual-policing and represent a long, continuously operating substrate of women-led reproductive-economic commoning in the Global South.

**C4** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Discussion · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Women_and_the_Commons]
Federici's first lesson from these practices: "the 'commoning' of the material means of reproduction is the primary mechanism by which a collective interest and mutual bonds are created. It is also the first line of resistance to a life of enslavement and the condition for the construction of autonomous spaces undermining from within the hold that capitalism has on our lives." Material reproduction-commoning precedes, enables, and grounds other forms of commoning — not the other way round.

**C5** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:More-Than-Human_Commons]
Bresnihan: "the commons is produced together, reflecting our inter-dependence, the assumption that our world is already shared. Second, and arising from this, the obligation that such inter-dependence demands of us. The commons is not a 'thing' that we have access to because we hold a title deed or authorization, but something that is ours because we produce and care for it, because we common." The commoning relation — production + care — is logically prior to any title, authorization, or membership rule.

**C6** [confidence: high] [anchor: §The commons is not a resource, but a relation · orn:p2p-wiki.page:More-Than-Human_Commons]
Bresnihan (citing Linebaugh): "to speak of the commons as if it were a natural resource is misleading at best and dangerous at worst, the commons is an activity and, if anything, it expresses relationships in society that are inseparable from relations to nature." The noun "commons" is expanded into the continuous verb "commoning" to denote continuous making and re-making through shared practice — the commons is never a static a priori community or an a posteriori society-to-come but something constituted through acting and doing in common.

**C7** [confidence: high] [anchor: §The commons is not a resource, but a relation · orn:p2p-wiki.page:More-Than-Human_Commons]
Bresnihan (citing Federici, Mies, Shiva, De Angelis): capitalist enclosure relies on the epistemological separation of the social and the natural, the productive and the reproductive. "The relegation of 'women's work' (childbirth, child rearing, cleaning, cooking, caring) to the domestic sphere outside of the 'productive' economic sphere represents the 'naturalizing' of this kind of labour." Enclosure is therefore not only land-privatization but the de-valorizing and invisibilizing of reproductive labor — an epistemological as well as material operation.

**C8** [confidence: high] [anchor: §The Concept · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Social_Reproduction_Theory]
Bhattacharya: "the production of goods and services and the production of life are part of one integrated process"; capitalism "acknowledges productive labor for the market as the sole form of legitimate 'work,' while the tremendous amount of familial as well as communitarian work that goes on to sustain and reproduce the worker, or more specifically her labor power, is naturalized into nonexistence." SRT's defining analytic move is to make this hidden reproductive labor visible as constitutive of class, not as an add-on to a deeper economic process.

**C9** [confidence: high] [anchor: §The Concept · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Social_Reproduction_Theory]
SRT treats "questions of oppression (gender, race, sexuality) in distinctly nonfunctionalist ways precisely because oppression is theorized as structurally relational to, and hence shaped by, capitalist production rather than on the margins of analysis or as add-ons to a deeper and more vital economic process." Gender, race, and sexuality are therefore structurally load-bearing in any commons/coordination analysis, not incidental cultural overlays.

**C10** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Description · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Circulation_of_the_Common]
Dyer-Witheford: the cellular form of communism is the common (a good produced for shared use); the basic circuit is `A ─ C ─ A'` — Associations organize Commons into productive ensembles that create more shared resources which in turn provide the basis for the formation of new associations. "Collective organization, not market exchange, governs the distribution of Commons, whether through mutual aid, public planning or gift economies." Protocols / institutional forms are substrates embedded in associational practice, not substitutes for it.

**C11** [confidence: high] [anchor: §Contextual Citation · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Circulation_of_the_Common]
Dyer-Witheford explicitly includes "labour (in its broadest sense, comprising reproductive and productive work)" in the commons taxonomy of land / labour / language — placing reproductive labor inside the A-C-A' circuit at its foundation rather than outside or beneath it. The circulation of the common is defined over productive AND reproductive labor as one integrated substrate.

**C12** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §Citations · orn:p2p-wiki.page:Anthropology_of_the_Commons]
Gibson-Graham (via the Anthropology of the Commons page) warns that "social capital" nomenclature folds reproductive relations into "bonding, binding, and linking" as ingredients of social cohesion and potential monetization — a capitalocentric move that strips these relations of their specificity. Gudeman frames the commons as "the patrimony or legacy of a community... the lasting core, though changeable over time, the base," and rejects market-first framings that treat commons as arrangements serving market ends. Together these positions mark the commons-literature consensus that reproductive and care-based relations are structurally load-bearing and distinct from market-logic overlays.
