# Johar — LLMs Are Not Neuroplastic, But Fields Can Be

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Provocation: LLMs Are Not Neuroplastic — And They Do Not See the Future… but Fields," Substack, 2026-04-04.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/provocation-llms-are-not-neuroplastic

## What It Is

A governance-layer provocation arguing that large language models are fixed inference architectures, not adaptive intelligences. Once trained, they do not self-revise through situated experience. What appears as learning in LLM-based systems is always located outside the model — in the surrounding arrangement of data pipelines, retrieval systems, human actors, interfaces, and institutional processes. The model is a component; the **field** — the distributed socio-technical arrangement — is the real unit of intelligence.

The essay makes five interlocking claims:

1. **LLMs are not neuroplastic.** They generate responses but do not recursively alter the conditions of their own operation.

2. **LLMs do not see the future.** They interpolate over compressed representations of the past. Prediction-appearance is conditional on continuity; it fails under regime shifts, nonlinear shocks, and strategic adaptation.

3. **Plasticity is in the arrangement, not the model.** "The adaptive capacity remains a property of the wider arrangement: of the institutions, infrastructures, and collective processes that can turn inference into revision, and revision into action."

4. **The real unit of intelligence is the field.** "It is the distributed system through which signals are sensed, interpretations are formed, assumptions are tested, commitments are revised, and resources are reallocated over time."

5. **The design challenge is fundamentally organisational.** It "requires new forms of memory, new forms of accountability, new forms of participation, and new forms of decision-making that are continuous rather than episodic."

The essay does not prescribe an architecture. It identifies where the gap is and names what an arrangement capable of learning would need to do.

**This essay arrived at Spore independently from outside.** The relation is not influence — it is convergence. Johar is working from governance and institutional design; Spore is working from coordination grammar and agent infrastructure. They are naming the same underlying structure from different sides.

## What Spore Already Does

### The field is already in Spore's vocabulary

Johar writes: "The real unit of intelligence is not the model. It is the field." Spore does not import this term — it is already present in the grammar, distributed across several contexts:

- **In commitment-pooling:** "A pool is a **shared field** where many vectors can be composed. Needs (pull vectors) and offers (push vectors) from multiple agents enter the pool. Routing and scoring compute **field-level alignment** rather than pairwise matching." (`docs/patterns/commitment-pooling.md:31`)

- **In comparative intake and IC:** The learning field is the governed project surface through which bridge notes, claims, convergence detection, and synthesis notes mature over cycles.

- **In the epistemic/context layer:** The entity graph is not a database of facts but "a relational field in which claims, evidence, attestations, and entities constitute context through connections." (Explored in the previous Johar bridge note.)

The three uses are not three different concepts. They are the same underlying structure instantiated at different scales and purposes — the socio-technical arrangement through which intelligence is distributed. Johar's essay provides the unifying name and the theoretical grounding for all three.

This implies a lexicon gap: Spore uses "field" across three contexts without a canonical definition that relates them. See R1 in the claim register.

### The commitment ecology is Johar's prescription operationalized

Johar identifies what a learning arrangement must do: "register consequences, preserve memory, revise commitments, and alter its own structures in response."

Spore's commitment lifecycle is not a metaphor for this — it is a direct implementation:

- **PROPOSED:** An agent declares a commitment, entering the field's scope.
- **VERIFIED:** Governance confirms alignment with vision and roadmap — the field's normative layer accepts the commitment.
- **ACTIVE:** The commitment is live. Consequences are beginning to accumulate.
- **EVIDENCE_LINKED:** Fulfillment evidence is attached. Consequences are registered as constraints. This is precisely Johar's requirement: "register consequences as constraints on future behaviour."
- **REDEEMED:** The commitment cycle closes — the field has learned through the complete arc.
- **DISPUTED / RESOLVED:** When fulfillment is contested, the discourse layer activates — the field learns from disagreement, not just from success.

The commitment lifecycle is the mechanism by which inference becomes consequential in a Spore-governed field. Johar says LLMs cannot do this; the commitment ecology can.

### The coordination loop is Johar's recursive arrangement

Spore's coordination loop — Vision → Roadmap → Intent → Commitment → Evidence → Learning — is Johar's "recursive environments in which human and machine capacities are coupled into ongoing loops of sensing, interpretation, commitment, and revision," expressed as a concrete governance grammar.

- **Sensing** → signals, intent publication
- **Interpretation** → governance review, discourse
- **Commitment** → commitment lifecycle
- **Revision** → evidence, learning, superseded claims

### The learning membrane's staged promotion is inference infrastructure correctly embedded

LLMs draft bridge notes, generate synthesis proposals, and identify convergence clusters. Governance decides what becomes durable. This is not a workaround — it is the correct embedding of inference infrastructure in an arrangement capable of learning.

Johar: "If coupled to systems that can absorb feedback, contest interpretations, update assumptions, and reorganise behaviour, these models may significantly enrich the conditions under which adaptation becomes possible."

The membrane's four surfaces (personal intake → project semantic memory → governance memory → production proving ground) are exactly such a coupling.

### Memory, accountability, and participation are already present

Johar calls for "new forms of memory, accountability, and participation." These are not absent from Spore:

- **New memory:** Five layers (working, episodic, semantic, governance, production) across Spore + IC.
- **New accountability:** Claims-Evidence-Attestation — every claim carries provenance; every commitment carries evidence requirements.
- **New participation:** Discourse-as-governance — eval is embedded discourse, not external audit.

These were not built in response to Johar. They were built in response to the same underlying problem. The essay's value here is clarifying the problem Spore has been solving.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. A standing design rule

Johar gives Spore and IC a one-line principle that can travel across both repos:

> **"LLMs can propose interpretations; only fields with memory, commitment, and revision can learn."**

The three conditions map exactly to Spore's architecture:
- **Memory** → governance-memory layer + IC's five-layer model
- **Commitment** → commitment ecology
- **Revision** → discourse-as-governance + claim lifecycle

This is not new content. It is a compressed expression of Spore's architecture that can be used as a design criterion when evaluating whether a proposed system genuinely learns or merely infers.

### 2. Named vocabulary: field neuroplasticity / architectural plasticity

Johar names what Spore's architecture is trying to achieve but hasn't articulated as a target: **field neuroplasticity** — the capacity of the system-as-field to reorganize itself through situated experience, even when individual LLM components remain fixed.

A commenter on the essay sharpened this further: **architectural plasticity** — the system's structural capacity to reorganize in response to feedback, as distinct from biological plasticity. This is the more precise term for what the learning membrane provides.

Either term is a design criterion, not a constitutional commitment. Constitutional commitments are normative ("we must..."); architectural plasticity is descriptive of what the arrangement needs to achieve. Candidate for IC's foundations as a named design criterion.

### 3. Continuity/discontinuity and field state

Johar's most important constraint for system designers: LLMs are reliable under continuity (the future remains statistically consistent with the past) and unreliable under discontinuity (regime shifts, nonlinear shocks, cascading failures).

This implies that **field state** — the current regime conditions of the external domain — is a relevant input to membrane governance, particularly for determining how much human review to require before promoting an LLM synthesis output.

Field state probably needs two axes, not one enum, to avoid conflating different kinds of instability:

- **Regime dynamics:** `stable | shifting | discontinuous` — describes the rate and nature of world-change
- **Epistemic coherence:** `settled | contested` — describes whether the field's own interpretations are agreed or in dispute

Collapsing these into one axis mixes world-change with disagreement, which are distinct problems requiring different governance responses.

This is a sketch, not a design commitment. If field state ever tunes convergence thresholds or review stringency, it must itself be explicit, reviewable, and attested — otherwise it becomes a hidden governance variable with major epistemic consequences.

### 4. Structured expectation vs. world-grounded claim

Johar distinguishes between "structured expectation" (what an inference system generates by interpolating over the past) and "grounded prediction" (genuine foresight tracking causal dynamics). His point: LLMs produce the former, not the latter.

For Spore and IC, a better formulation is:

- **Structured expectation:** What an LLM produces — coherent, well-formed, plausible, but anchored in the compressed past, not in world-state.
- **World-grounded claim:** A claim that has been tested against production evidence, attested by multiple agents with independent observation, and subjected to governance review.

Avoiding "grounded prediction" matters: even production-backed claims are conditional, not predictive. The spectrum runs from structured expectation (lowest grounding) to world-grounded claim (highest grounding) without implying that any claim can predict the future.

This sharpens how IC communicates its grounding primitive.

### 5. The organisational-not-technical framing

Johar states explicitly what Spore implies but has not declared: "The design challenge is fundamentally organisational, not technical. It sits at the intersection of institutions, governance, finance, interfaces, and computational systems."

This framing is implicit in every Spore design decision (commitment over algorithm, discourse over optimization, governance over automation). It has not been stated as an explicit commitment. Worth adding to Spore's constitutional artifacts or project vision.

### 6. Human/machine asymmetry — tension deepened

This essay approaches the human/machine relationship from a different angle than "The Word Is Not the Thing." The previous essay argued that humans contribute non-symbolic knowing (embodied, relational, ecological) that machines cannot replicate. This essay argues that machines cannot bear consequences and therefore cannot be adaptive agents — adaptation requires consequence-bearing, and only humans (and planetary life) bear consequences in the full sense.

Two Johar essays are now pointing at the same structural tension in Spore's grammar: Spore treats all agents as structurally equivalent holons at the primitive level, while Johar insists on a deep asymmetry in what different agent types contribute.

The tension is productive and should not be smoothed. A candidate resolution:

> **Spore's structural agent symmetry should be preserved at the primitive level. The asymmetry between human and machine agents should be placed at the membrane and governance pattern level: different agent types contribute different kinds of knowing, and consequence-bearing accountability belongs with human agents or human-accountable governance bodies.**

This is consistent with Spore's holon model — holons are structurally symmetric, but not fungible in practice. It is also consistent with the commitment lifecycle: an accountable holon that has accepted scope responsibility is a commitment-bearing unit, not a named individual — but responsibility must be locatable in an agent capable of bearing consequences.

This remains an open question (see below), not a settled claim.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the lexicon entry for "field" clarify the relation among three uses without collapsing them?** The three uses (shared field, learning field, relational field) are the same concept at different scales and purposes. A lexicon entry should illuminate how they relate without forcing one definition to overwrite the others. What is the right grain of description?

2. **Is architectural plasticity a design criterion or a design principle?** As a criterion, it says: the arrangement should achieve this. As a principle, it says: design decisions should be evaluated against this. The distinction affects where it belongs (foundations vs. design commitments) and how it is enforced.

3. **How does consequence-bearing relate to Spore's holon model?** The essay argues that adaptation requires consequence-bearing, and only humans and planetary life bear consequences in the full sense. Spore's holons are structurally symmetric. Can a collective holon (a governance body, a commons) bear consequences in a morally relevant sense? Or is consequence-bearing ultimately irreducible to individual humans? This is a foundational question for Spore's accountability model.

4. **Should field state be introduced as a governance input now, or only after production evidence?** Field state would tune review stringency and potentially convergence thresholds. That is a high-leverage intervention. The design principle in the learning membrane is that patterns require production evidence before promotion. Does field state need a prototype or experiment before it becomes a governance input?

5. **Do the two Johar essays together constitute sufficient evidence for upgrading human-machine complementarity from an unresolved tension to a candidate design principle?** Two independent essays pointing at the same structural gap is meaningful convergence. But the tension with Spore's primitive-level agent symmetry is deep. Should this be the trigger for a synthesis note, or does more evidence (from other sources or from production) need to accumulate first?

## Disposition

**Primary: clarify existing term** — The essay names and provides theoretical grounding for mechanisms Spore already has but hasn't articulated this clearly. "Field," "inference infrastructure," "arrangement-plasticity," "architectural plasticity" are vocabulary additions that make implicit design rationale explicit.

**Secondary: unresolved tension** — Human/machine complementarity vs. Spore's structural agent symmetry. Two Johar essays now point at this tension. It is productive and should not be smoothed. The candidate resolution (symmetry at primitive level, asymmetry at membrane/governance pattern level) is a review claim, not settled doctrine.

**Tertiary: design criterion** — "LLMs can propose interpretations; only fields with memory, commitment, and revision can learn" is a standing design criterion candidate for both repos. Field state (two axes) and category-adequacy review are candidates for IC's seasonal gate.

**Promotion status:** defer — the intent is clear; convergence and production evidence are not yet sufficient for canon changes.
