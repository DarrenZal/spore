# Johar — The Word Is Not the Thing

**Source:** Indy Johar, "The Word is Not the Thing — And That Is Where the Trouble Begins," Substack, 2026-04-02.
**URL:** https://substack.com/home/post/p-192984143

## What It Is

A philosophical essay arguing that language, while indispensable for coordination, introduces a structural risk: the word becomes a "stopping rule" that terminates inquiry, substitutes recognition for perception, and severs contact with the relational, temporal, embodied reality it claims to describe. Under conditions of accelerating change, this risk becomes critical — inherited abstractions can't see the transformations that invalidate them.

The essay calls for "deconditioning of linguistic closure" — not rejecting language but moving *through* it: treating naming as a threshold that opens inquiry rather than an endpoint that settles it. It identifies multiple modes of knowing beyond the symbolic (embodied, relational, situational, ecological, affective, attentional) and argues that the human contribution to human-machine systems is precisely the capacity to operate beyond symbolic dominance.

Johar is already cited in Spore as a foundational inspiration (`project-vision.md:160`; `relational-agency-and-holons.md:33,83`). This bridge note asks: what does this essay offer that Spore doesn't already have?

## What Spore Already Does

The grammar contains structural mechanisms that respond to the problems Johar names:

1. **Against linguistic closure:** The claim lifecycle (`proposed→supported→challenged→superseded`) prevents claims from becoming permanent. Attestations decay. The discourse graph enables the grammar to reason about and revise itself. These are mechanisms against the word-as-stopping-rule.

2. **Against the invisible map-territory gap:** Intent pressure makes the normative-epistemic gap computable. Sensors inject world-state. The coordination loop cycles through revision. These keep abstractions answerable to reality.

3. **Against single-abstraction compression:** Eight graph projections offer eight lossy views that together recover depth. Dual representation (narrative + graph) refuses single-mode sufficiency.

4. **Language as threshold:** The legibility progression (intent→promise→commitment) treats each naming as a stage that opens further coordination, not a conclusion. The learning membrane metabolizes outside work through stages rather than flattening it.

5. **Pre-linguistic sensing:** The signal primitive captures directional cues before they're formalized into claims. Stigmergy enables coordination through environmental traces without declaration. Inferred intent arises from accumulated patterns, not naming.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Named Failure Modes

Spore guards against failure modes it doesn't name. The essay provides terminology:

- **Linguistic closure** — when a name terminates inquiry. The claim lifecycle and discourse graph prevent this, but the *concept* of what they prevent isn't in the grammar's vocabulary. Naming it makes the design rationale explicit and inspectable.

- **Stopping rule** — the specific mechanism of closure: the word arrives and attention relaxes. The grammar should be aware that its own primitives (holon, membrane, signal) can become stopping rules — categories that feel understood and therefore cease to provoke inquiry.

- **Epistemic substitution** — when representations replace encounter so completely that the substitution becomes invisible. Intent pressure measures the gap but not its *invisibility*. A system can have computable intent pressure and still suffer epistemic substitution if agents treat the computed gap as the whole gap.

### 2. Sufficiency Error

The essay identifies a failure mode distinct from "gap" or "loss": the moment when compression is *mistaken for comprehension*. The word carries "an illusion of comprehension." This is not about the magnitude of what's lost but about the *phenomenology of false completeness*.

Spore's intent pressure measures the gap between normative and epistemic frontiers. But what if the epistemic frontier itself is an abstraction that feels sufficient? Sensor data is itself compressed. The epistemic graph tracks "what counts as knowing" — but the essay warns that any system of accounting for what counts as knowing is itself a compression that can feel complete.

**Candidate concept:** "Sufficiency error" or "closure risk" as a named property — not of claims, but of *the grammar itself*. A reflexive awareness that the grammar's own categories are provisional.

### 3. Perceptual Fidelity

The essay names a quality the grammar's sensor grounding needs but hasn't articulated: the quality of a system's contact with pre-abstraction reality. "Perceptual fidelity" is not the *amount* of sensor data but the *depth of encounter* — whether the sensing preserves relational, temporal, contextual richness or reduces phenomena to quantified proxies.

For Spore, this could sharpen the design requirement for sensor nodes. A water quality reading is a signal; the full ecology of a watershed is what that signal compresses. Perceptual fidelity would be the criterion for evaluating whether a sensor arrangement is adequate — not "do we have data?" but "does our data preserve contact with the relational field?"

**Candidate lexicon entry or design commitment.**

### 4. Human-Machine Complementarity

Spore treats all agents as structurally equivalent holons. The essay argues this obscures something design-relevant: machines and humans contribute *different kinds of knowing*.

- **Machines extend the symbolic layer** — graph composition, routing, lifecycle management, pattern matching, scheduling. They process representations with extraordinary efficiency.
- **Humans anchor the system in what exceeds representation** — embodied perception, relational awareness, situational sensitivity, the capacity to sense what's changing before it can be named.

This doesn't require abandoning agent-type agnosticism at the primitive level. The membrane model already protects sovereign internal knowing. But it suggests that a coordination ecology should be *designed for complementarity*: the system works best when human agents contribute what machines can't generate (grounding depth, felt wrongness, embodied context) and machine agents contribute what humans can't sustain (scale, consistency, compositional reasoning).

**Candidate design principle:** In multi-agent coordination ecologies, human and machine agents are complementary, not fungible. Machine agents extend the symbolic layer; human agents anchor the system to what exceeds it. The membrane marks the boundary between what can be represented and what must be encountered.

### 5. Becomingness as Design Commitment

Spore's network axis captures "overlap, cross-cutting participation, lateral reach" — structurally oriented toward flow and becoming. The event graph tracks temporal unfolding. But the grammar doesn't name "becomingness" as a first-class concern.

The essay argues that the dominant tendency of language is to convert processes into objects, becoming into being, relations into identities. Spore's grammar should explicitly commit to resisting this tendency: the network axis and event graph are not just structural features — they are the grammar's commitment to tracking *what is becoming*, not just *what is*.

**Candidate enrichment for holonic-network-architecture or a constitutional commitment.**

### 6. Context as Relation, Not Information

The essay implies that context is not data to be preserved in layers but a relational field to be participated in. When Johar describes the bird, the "context" is not facts about the bird — it's the entire relational ecology through which the bird is "continuously made and remade."

For Spore, this reinforces the entity graph's role: "Salish Sea Herring" connecting fisheries science, indigenous knowledge, ecological monitoring, and policy documents simultaneously. Context isn't stored in one location; it *is* the network of relations. The network axis is where context lives — not as information but as connective tissue.

This could sharpen how Spore thinks about the knowledge graph / epistemic graph: it's not a database of facts but a *relational field* in which claims, evidence, attestations, and entities constitute context through their connections.

This framing now has a canonical anchor in `spore.term.field`, which relates the relational-field usage here to Spore's shared-field and learning-field language.

## Claim Register

**C1** [confidence: high] [anchor: §What Spore Does Not Already Have — linguistic closure risk]
Johar's "deconditioning of linguistic closure" identifies a structural risk the grammar must guard against in its own terms — "holon," "membrane," "intent pressure" are compressions that can become stopping rules if the culture of use treats them as settled rather than threshold-opening.

**C2** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §What Spore Does Not Already Have — human-machine complementarity]
Human-machine complementarity in Johar's frame grounds Spore's sovereignty and translation model: humans contribute non-symbolic knowing (embodied, relational, ecological) that machines cannot replicate — this is what the membrane must protect, not just symbolic data.

**C3** [confidence: medium] [anchor: §What Spore Does Not Already Have — discourse graph]
Spore's discourse graph and attestation mechanisms partially operationalize Johar's requirement for "treating naming as a threshold that opens inquiry rather than an endpoint that settles it" — but the structural mechanism is only partial; cultural practice around naming is also required.

**C4** [confidence: low] [anchor: §What Spore Does Not Already Have — relational field]
Spore's knowledge graph should be understood as a relational field in which claims, evidence, attestations, and entities constitute context through connections — not a database of facts. This framing may clarify communication of Spore's epistemic model.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the grammar guard against its own terms becoming stopping rules?** "Holon" is a compression. "Membrane" is a compression. The discourse graph allows revision, but does the culture of use treat these names as settled? Is there a structural mechanism, or does this require practices beyond the grammar?

2. **Does the membrane model adequately handle human-machine complementarity, or does it need explicit support?** The membrane protects sovereignty — what a human knows embodiedly stays sovereign until translated into a signal, claim, or attestation. Is that sufficient? Or should there be a property that distinguishes *what kind of translation loss* different agent types introduce?

3. **How does "perceptual fidelity" become operational?** Is it a quality of sensor configurations? A property of the sensing phase? A metric? Or is it irreducibly a judgment that only human agents can make?

4. **Does Spore need a term for what can't be represented?** The essay argues that some knowing is enacted, not stored — present only in the act of encounter. Should the grammar acknowledge a boundary beyond which its own representational machinery cannot reach?

## Disposition

**Primary: clarify existing term** — The essay provides names (linguistic closure, stopping rule, sufficiency error, perceptual fidelity, becomingness) for failure modes and design qualities that Spore's grammar addresses structurally but hasn't named. Adding these terms to the vocabulary would make implicit design rationale explicit.

**Secondary: unresolved tension** — Human-machine complementarity creates productive tension with agent-type agnosticism. The membrane model may accommodate it, but this needs explicit articulation and possibly a design principle.

**Tertiary: candidate pattern** — "Becomingness awareness" as a named orientation for how the network axis, event graph, and signal primitive should be interpreted and used.
