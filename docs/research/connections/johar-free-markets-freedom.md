# Johar — Making Free Markets and The Needs for Deeper Freedoms

**Source:** Indy Johar, "Making Free Markets and The Needs for Deeper Freedoms," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/making-free-markets-and-the-needs

## What It Is

A long essay (4868w) arguing that the "free market" has never existed because markets can only be free if agents themselves are free — and agents are rarely granted the systemic freedoms required to act wisely in complexity. Freedom is reconstructed from absence of interference into systemic capability: eight interlinked capacities (cognitive security, temporal wealth, system awareness, material sufficiency, relational agency, option space, sovereignty, care) that agents need to function as distributed intelligence. Without these, markets compute distortion, not knowledge. The essay bridges from ideological critique (free market as organising myth) through ontological reframing (complexity requires freedom as infrastructure) to a capabilities framework that defines what "free" structurally requires.

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Sovereignty as design commitment:** Spore's constitutional commitments include meaningful local autonomy, forkability, and contestability. These are structural freedoms — the grammar does not assume agents are free; it provides the infrastructure for exercising sovereignty. The essay makes the same argument for markets: freedom is not given but infrastructured.

2. **Agent capability assumptions:** Spore treats agents as entities with "enough coherence to perceive, decide, and act." The essay's eight capabilities elaborate what that coherence requires: cognitive clarity, temporal wealth, material sufficiency, relational agency. Spore names the threshold; the essay fills in the structural preconditions.

3. **Distributed intelligence through coordination:** Spore's coordination ecology assumes that intelligence emerges from distributed agents sensing, proposing, committing, and learning. The essay makes the same argument for markets: "the promise of the free market was a computational advantage born of distribution." Both fail when distribution is nominal — when agents lack the capacity to contribute genuine intelligence.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. Freedom as Infrastructural Capability

The essay's core move: in a complex, emergent world, freedom is not ornamental but infrastructural — the condition that enables adaptive intelligence. Without systemic capabilities (cognitive security, temporal wealth, system awareness, material sufficiency, relational agency, option space, sovereignty, care), agents cannot discover appropriate responses in context. Markets without free agents compute propaganda, desperation, and collapse.

For Spore, this reframes agent sovereignty from a constitutional commitment to a capability requirement. Sovereignty is necessary but insufficient — agents also need the material and cognitive conditions to exercise it. The essay implies that a governance grammar that declares sovereignty without attending to capability conditions is structurally incomplete.

### 2. Eight Systemic Capabilities

The essay proposes eight interlinked capabilities as preconditions for freedom in complexity:

1. **Cognitive security** — perception as the foundation of freedom; without epistemic integrity, markets compute propaganda
2. **Temporal wealth** — time as horizon of appropriateness; scarcity-time collapses agents to immediacy
3. **System awareness** — literacy in entanglement; without it, freedom degenerates into externalization
4. **Material sufficiency** — freedom from existential coercion
5. **Relational agency** — capacity to coordinate, not just transact
6. **Option space** — preservation of futures; extractive systems close option space
7. **Sovereignty** — capacity to contest, redirect, and refuse
8. **Care and tenderness** — operating under partial knowledge with humility

This connects to the human-machine-complementarity synthesis: the question of what agents need to function as distributed intelligence applies to both governance and economic coordination.

### 3. Markets as One Coordination Device Among Many

The essay's closing move: markets are appropriate for commodities and pre-definable goods but inadequate for care, trust, ecological stability. "To absolutize them is to collapse freedom into capture." This reframes market coordination as one modality in a wider ecology — parallel to the priceability essay's argument for plural exchange. For Spore, this supports the existing principle that the grammar serves multiple coordination forms, not only market-based ones.

## Open Questions

1. **Should Spore's agent model carry capability assumptions?** The essay's eight capabilities specify what agents need to function as distributed intelligence. Spore currently assumes agents have "enough coherence to perceive, decide, and act" without specifying preconditions. Whether the grammar should explicitly model capability requirements (material sufficiency, cognitive security, temporal wealth) or leave these to the governance layer is unresolved.

2. **How do systemic freedoms relate to the commitment economy?** The commitment-economy essay (a-human-economy-ii) proposes standing — institutional membership that provides a security floor — as the mechanism that enables participation. Standing is one implementation of the material sufficiency and option space capabilities this essay describes. Whether they converge on the same structural account of what agents need to coordinate under discovery remains to be seen.

## Disposition

**Primary: candidate pattern** — Systemic freedom as capability requirement names a pattern that Spore's constitutional commitments already address partially (sovereignty, forkability, contestability) but have not articulated as a comprehensive capability framework. The essay argues that formal rights without material conditions are structurally incomplete — governance that declares sovereignty without attending to cognitive security, temporal wealth, and material sufficiency is incomplete by design.

**Secondary context:** The essay also addresses market-legibility-and-moral-provenance (what markets can and cannot compute). The market-legibility angle is secondary here — the primary contribution is the capability framework for agent participation in coordination.

**Promotion status: defer** — Single-author source. The capabilities framework draws on Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum but applies them to market-and-complexity framing that is distinctively Johar's. Promotion should wait for convergence from another source or production evidence.
