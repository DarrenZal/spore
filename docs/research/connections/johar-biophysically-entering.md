# Johar — We Are Really Biophysically Entering a Systems World

**Source:** Indy Johar, "We Are Really Biophysically Entering a Systems World," Substack.
**URL:** https://indyjohar.substack.com/p/we-are-really-biophysically-entering

## What It Is

A precise, compressed essay (1516w) that makes a material claim about operating conditions, not a metaphorical one about vocabulary. The argument: we are not merely "noticing systems more clearly" — we are living through a decline in damping capacity that makes interdependence operational at lower perturbation levels. The "object world" (separable sectors, containable shocks, locally absorbable failures) was never ontologically true — it was a pragmatic approximation that held under conditions of sufficient damping and slack. As buffers deplete, coupling thresholds fall, and what was latent interdependence becomes dynamically dominant.

The essay introduces "degenerative volatility" — not more shocks, but less recovery. The interval between shocks becomes shorter than the time required to recover, producing structural fatigue. Buffer capacity (fiscal, ecological, institutional, infrastructural, social) is spent merely to keep functioning. Critically, the essay argues this is not only exogenous: a large part of buffer loss has been endogenous — decades of optimisation (just-in-time supply chains, platform concentration, lean staffing, degraded public maintenance) have systematically thinned buffers. Degenerative volatility arrives as an accelerant pushing systems already tuned close to their limits beyond their safe operating envelope.

When coupling thresholds fall, the explanatory unit shifts from objects to fields. Policy categories and sectoral portfolios fail not because they lack intelligence but because their boundaries no longer coincide with causal structure. The binding constraint becomes an integrated triad: response-time + legitimacy production + commitment capacity. In a coupled regime, delays are cascade multipliers. The essay concludes that the real asset is "the capacity to keep option-space open by preventing cascades or by shaping cascades into adaptive reconfiguration rather than uncontrolled failure."

## What Spore Already Does

1. **Commitment protocols as commitment capacity:** The essay names "commitment capacity" as a binding constraint — the ability to produce credible shared interpretation and bind commitments across actors quickly enough to act before option-space collapses. Spore's commitment protocols are precisely this infrastructure: structured mechanisms for producing binding commitments across distributed actors.

2. **Coordination loop as response-time infrastructure:** The grammar's sense → propose → commit → learn cycle is response-time infrastructure — the mechanism by which the system detects changes and coordinates action before cascades become irreversible.

3. **Legitimacy scaffolding as the third constraint:** The essay names legitimacy production as part of the binding triad. Spore's constitutional layer (sovereignty, contestability, forkability) and the commitment framework provide the legitimacy scaffolding that makes rapid coordination credible rather than merely expedient.

## What the Essay Offers

### 1. The Biophysical Basis of the Systems Condition

The essay's primary contribution is making the systems condition material rather than interpretive. The shift from "object world" to "systems world" is not a change in lens but a change in the physics of propagation. Coupling thresholds fall not because we have become more aware but because damping capacity is depleting. For Spore, this grounds the grammar's design rationale in material conditions: the coordination grammar is necessary because the buffered, separable world in which simpler governance sufficed is biophysically eroding.

### 2. Degenerative Volatility as a Distinct Condition

The essay distinguishes degenerative volatility from ordinary volatility or crisis: variance is persistent, recovery is incomplete, and the system's capacity to restore order declines over time. This is more precise than the governance-under-volatility synthesis's "compound volatility" — it names the specific mechanism (recovery gap) that makes volatility structurally cumulative rather than episodic. For Spore, degenerative volatility may be a more precise name for the condition the grammar must operate under.

### 3. Endogenous Buffer Loss

The essay's most distinctive claim is that buffer loss is substantially endogenous — produced by decades of optimisation that systematically thinned slack. This reframes the challenge: the problem is not only external shocks but the institutional pattern of treating redundancy and maintenance as inefficiency to be eliminated. For Spore, this connects to the post-permanence synthesis: institutions that optimise for permanence-era efficiency (lean, just-in-time, single-point) are the ones that fail under degenerative volatility. The grammar must resist the optimisation pressure that thins its own buffers.

### 4. The Response-Time / Legitimacy / Commitment Triad

The essay names the binding constraint as an integrated triad — not just speed (response-time), not just authority (legitimacy), not just mechanism (commitment capacity), but all three simultaneously. In a coupled regime with cascading interdependencies, you need to sense fast, interpret credibly, and bind commitments before option-space collapses. For Spore, this provides a structural test for the grammar's adequacy: does the coordination loop operate fast enough, does the constitutional layer produce sufficient legitimacy, and do the commitment protocols bind reliably enough to prevent cascades?

## Open Questions

1. **Should Spore name "degenerative volatility" alongside or instead of "governance under volatility"?** The governance-under-volatility synthesis names volatility as a governance condition. This essay's "degenerative volatility" is more precise: it names the specific mechanism (recovery gap + endogenous buffer loss) that makes volatility cumulative. Whether the grammar should adopt the more precise term or keep the broader framing is unresolved.

2. **Does the endogenous-buffer-loss claim create a design requirement for the grammar?** If optimisation systematically thins buffers, the grammar must resist the same pressure. Does this mean the grammar should carry explicit redundancy commitments — maintenance protocols, slack preservation, anti-optimisation constraints? Or is the learning membrane's built-in revisability sufficient to prevent the kind of lock-in that thins buffers?
