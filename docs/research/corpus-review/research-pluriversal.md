Primitives
• Plural Ontologies (“Many Worlds”): Pluriversal authors insist reality comprises coexisting,
qualitatively different worlds, not just multiple views of one universe. Querejazu explains that
“notions of the pluriverse imply multiple ontologies, multiple worlds to be known – not simply
multiple perspectives on one world” 1 . In practice, indigenous cosmologies do not map onto a
single Nature/Culture schema. As de la Cadena observes (of Quechua‐Cuzco), “Cuzco has never been
singular or plural, never one world and therefore never many either, but a composition…in which the
languages and practices of its worlds constantly overlap and exceed each other.” 2 . Likewise, Viveiros
de Castro’s Amerindian perspectivism describes a “society of nature” where all beings (humans,
animals, plants, even minerals and tools) are agents with culture; the only difference is bodily form,
so each species sees itself as “human” and others as “animals” 3 4 . In short, “world” or ontology
is understood as inherently plural, and each community’s cosmovision is sui generis rather than a
layer of a single scheme.
• Relational Being and Kinship: Persons are constituted by relationships rather than atomistic
individuality. Val Plumwood (drawing on ecofeminist thought) argues for an “ecological” or relational
self that is embedded in communities and nature, where care, solidarity and mutual respect are
fundamental 5 6 . In indigenous traditions, kinship extends beyond humans to the more‐than‐
human world. De la Cadena shows Quechua practice of tirakuna (earth‐beings) in which mountains,
rivers, etc. are treated as kin with agency. These earth‐beings “had a presence that blurred the
known distinction between humans and nature… [and] are also an important presence” in people’s
lives 7 . Runakuna (people) routinely make offerings to earth‐beings (e.g. presenting coca kintu) to
maintain reciprocal relationships 8 . Thus social life is a network of person‐to‐person relations
(human and nonhuman), embedded in caring, communicative practices. (Viveiros de Castro likewise
describes sorcerers “occupying” the perspective of other beings, indicating the porous, interactional
nature of subjectivity 9 .)
• Territory (Living Place): Land and places are viewed as animate wholes. For example, ethnographic
studies report that Achuar and other Amazonian groups consider each territory “a living being, and
spirits are the natural owners” (with ancestral elders or nature‐spirits inhabiting it) 10 . Escobar and
others note that indigenous movements refer to territory as source of life and identity (breathing,
sustaining relations) rather than inert property. For instance, de la Cadena calls Cuzco a “socionatural
territory” defined by interwoven relations between people and earth‐beings 7 . In many local legal
traditions the land itself carries personhood and legal force (see Borrows on natural law below).
Violating territory thus means offending living ancestors or cosmic forces. (This contrasts sharply
with Western property models, and exemplifies how “earth being” and territory merge: e.g.
Ausangate mountain is both a territory and a deity.)
• Cosmovision/Cosmology: The tradition foregrounds holistic cosmologies. Cosmology here is not a
static mythology but a living frame: ancestral stories, shamans, and sacred geography constitute
how people see the cosmos. Viveiros de Castro characterizes Amerindian cosmology as having
universal culture: e.g. in Amazonian myth, humans and animals share culture/kinship, and only the

body type distinguishes “perspectives.” He emphasizes that cosmology demands that one can
“assume the perspective of another” (through ritual cannibalism or shamanism) 9 . De la Cadena’s
fieldwork highlights ceremonies where coca offerings to Ausangate (a mountain‐earth-being)
“welcome” that being into human conversation 8 – showing a cosmology in which mountain and
human engage. In short, cosmovision here means an interconnected worldview with no strict
nature/culture split: spirits, ancestors, and landscape features are woven into social and political life.
These cosmologies inform governance and ethics (e.g. stewardship of ancestors, reciprocity with the
nonhuman).
• Reciprocity (Ritual Obligation): Obligatory reciprocity is a core principle. Material exchange is
embedded in ritual and kinship rather than market logic. De la Cadena describes how Quechua
people “offer coca leaves… among themselves and to earth-beings on social occasions, big or small,
everyday or extraordinary.” The purpose of this kintu offering is precisely to foster “good
remembering, and … a good relationship between us and all those involved” 8 . Here reciprocity is
enacted as ongoing ritual: everyone (even non-humans) is expected to give and receive as part of a
good life. This applies to human gatherings too (feasts, kin‐group weddings, etc. have strict
reciprocal norms). Economic life is therefore a cycle of gifts and obligations: failing these (violating
reciprocity) causes social and even spiritual harm. In most authors’ accounts, there is no separate
market or contract system; even labor and resource use are governed by communal sharing and
gratitude.
• Buen Vivir / Sumak Kawsay (Collective Well‐Being): While not emphasized by all authors, several
mention concepts like buen vivir or Ubuntu as ethical-political frames. For example, Latin American
pluriversal thinkers invoke Sumak Kawsay (“good living”) as an alternative development model
emphasizing harmony with nature and community welfare. (Escobar notes that this concept—rooted
in Andean indigenous cosmologies—has been enshrined in constitutions of Ecuador/Bolivia,
embodying relations of reciprocity and respect to Pachamama 11 .) Although detailed discussion is
scant in primary texts, the spirit of buen vivir underlies much discourse: the goal of policy is collective
flourishing (social and environmental), not individual gain. In African contexts, analogous ideas of
Ubuntu (“I am because we are”) surface in broader “Epistemologies of the South” arguments (cf. de
Sousa Santos). In short, ethic frames like buen vivir or Ubuntu serve to situate governance as caring
for the community and earth, not as calculation of profits.
• Ancestral / Indigenous Law: Indigenous legal tradition is fundamental. John Borrows (among
others) emphasizes that native peoples possess distinct sources of law grounded in tradition. He
identifies at least five sources: sacred (creation stories, treaty relationships), natural (the land itself
and relationships with nature), deliberative (council meetings, feasts), customary (kinship and family
practices), and positivistic (codified rules or proclamations) 12 13 . In practice, these sources
interweave: for instance, sacred narratives dictate land‐use norms; deliberations are framed by
ritual; customs are enforced as law. Unlike Western law, authority often derives from ancestors or
spirits (sacred law) or from elders’ consensus. Land, animals, and ancestors figure as participants in
law (natural and sacred sources) rather than “objects.” Thus “law” in these traditions is embedded in
memory, story and duty, not abstracted by state institutions.
• Earth‐beings / Sentient Non‐Humans: The idea that non-human entities are sentient and hold
agency is explicit. As de la Cadena shows, Quechua peoples personify mountains, rivers, lakes, etc.
(tirakuna or earth-beings) which “have some features of being… shared with runakuna” (humans) 7 .

These earth-beings participate in social life: people negotiate with them via ritual offerings, and view
them as kin or holders of power. In other traditions (e.g. Amazonian), animals and plants are also
seen as persons. Authors stress that treating nature as sentient erases a culture/nature divide. The
living world is coproduced by both humans and nonhumans in intertwined governance (e.g., a forest
is governed by trees as much as by people). Such holism—recognizing “earth others”—is a recurring
primitive: the earth itself is an actor in political and legal relations (for instance, Andes mountains are
sometimes treated as trustees of ancestral laws).

Layering of Realms
Pluriversal thought rejects the notion that there is one unitary reality stratified by culture and nature. As
Querejazu (summarizing Escobar and others) notes, a pluriversal stance “puts the ontological question on
the table”: one-world truth is questioned, and the world is understood as multiple ways of being. In other
words, Western dualisms (nature vs. culture, modern vs. traditional) are not universal axioms, so they
cannot be layered neatly across peoples 1 . Indigenous approaches do not assemble into a single
alternative layer under modernity; rather, each people has its own relational cosmology. For example, de la
Cadena emphasizes that Cuzco society is “a composition (perhaps a constant translation) in which the
languages and practices of its worlds constantly overlap and exceed each other” 2 . This means one cannot
linearly stack an “indigenous layer” beneath a “modern layer” – instead, their ontologies intertwine with
local history, land, kinship, and ritual.
Each Indigenous legal/political tradition, accordingly, provides its own mapping of social‐ecological space.
Kinship, territory and ritual are inseparable, so there is no universal schema (as Descola’s four ontologies)
applicable to all. For instance, an Andean worldview might place mountains as ancestors in the same order
as humans, whereas a Cree worldview might see rivers as relatives, and both resist being collapsed into a
single taxonomic structure. The pluriverse framework therefore insists on multiple coexisting ontologies, not
a generalized multi-layer of one reality. Indigenous scholars stress that trying to synthesize these into one
model violates their premise: modernity’s nature/culture split is itself a colonial product, not a neutral layer.

Placement of Key Concepts
Many familiar concepts are absent or reconceived. Authors generally do not invoke Western technical terms
like “trust” or “signals” in the abstract. Social trust, for example, is enacted through mutual obligations and
respect embedded in ritual exchange 8 rather than computed like a game-theoretic factor. Likewise, what
Westerners call “norms” or “protocols” exist as customary rituals (e.g. prayer ceremonies, council feasts)
rather than formal procedures. Borrows’ account of deliberative law – talking circles and consensus
gatherings 14 – exemplifies this: these are communal forms of decision-making, not impersonal
procedures.
By contrast, “care” and related values are emphasized, but as collective and relational practices.
Plumwood’s ecological/relational self highlights caring for others and nature for their own sake 15 .
Indigenous governance often assumes responsibility toward clan members, land, and future generations.
Memory and identity are crucial – not individual memories, but collective and ancestral memory (oral
histories, genealogies, totems). These appear under terms like “the history of the place” or “the law of the
ancestors,” rather than a modern individual identity construct.

Terms like “state” or “authority” exist but are approached cautiously. Many traditions recognize their own
leaders, councils or clan elders as authority figures; these derive legitimacy from lineage and ceremony, not
from an abstract sovereign. Modern nation-state institutions are typically seen as alien, although some
leaders seek recognition (treaties) for their authority. “Power” is thus relational, spiritual, and distributive
rather than a monopoly (power circulates through ritual hierarchies, not through standing armies or
bureaucracies). Similarly, concepts like “commitment,” “intent,” or “evidence” have no exact analogues.
Commitments are made by oaths in a communal context, intent is shown through duty to kin/spirit, and
“evidence” would be the narrative coherence of a tradition.
Notably, “holon-like units” (simultaneous wholes/parts) are absent: entities are generally understood
organically. For example, a mountain might be an ancestor (a whole) that contains people in its “body” (like
a tribal territory); but this overlap is taken literally, not as an abstract whole-part system. “Boundary” exists
as lineage or land boundary (who belongs to a clan or place), but again it is drawn in relational terms (e.g.
the mouth of a river is where its spirit communicates) rather than arbitrary lines. Reciprocity itself is
foundational (see above) and is present wherever these traditions dwell. Continuity/reproduction of
community is assumed through cyclical rituals and the rebirth of ancestors in nature; political order is
always seen as regenerated continuously by the people and the earth.
In summary, when Western concepts appear at all, they are subsumed under indigenous framings. The
tradition speaks of kawsay (life-energy) or riqsiy (knowing, responsibility) instead of “trust” or “norms.” What
might look like a “protocol” is actually kakun (ways of doing), embedded in etiquette and prayer. Leadership
is about ayni (reciprocity) and honor, not formal power. Hence, many listed terms either do not appear by
name or are reframed. For instance, Borrows’ list of Indigenous law sources shows how even “law” is plural
in principle 12 , and does not easily reduce to a single “legal system” concept.

Internal Disagreements
The pluriversal tradition contains lively debates. One tension is universalism vs. pluralism: advocates
frame the pluriverse as a global vision (often inspired by Zapatista slogans “a world where many worlds fit”),
while critics ask whether invoking the pluriverse imposes a new universalism. Escobar treats pluriversality as
a political project of collective design and empowerment, whereas anthropologists like Viveiros de Castro
are more descriptive, viewing multiple ontologies as empirical facts that do not automatically prescribe
solidarity. Linked to this is incommensurability: must each worldview remain isolated (“à côté de” one
another), or is dialogue possible? Authors like de la Cadena answer that worlds are partially connected
through translation: people can talk across differences (as Mariano and the anthropologist did) but without
erasing the differences 2 8 . In practice, some indigenous thinkers emphasize common causes (e.g.
alliances against mining) while others emphasize separateness (keeping to ancestral laws).
Another debate centers on the politics of the pluriverse. Is it a prescriptive vision (Escobar’s “designs for
the pluriverse” of multiple autonomies) or merely a way to understand reality? Some worry that
emphasizing difference undermines collective action; others worry that collective projects (like indigenous
rights movements) slide into old nationalisms. Within indigenous legal theory there is also strategic
tension: some jurists urge using the settler state’s recognition of tribal laws (treaties, constitutions) to
advance autonomy; others stress that true justice can only come from separate indigenous legal orders.
These internal disagreements do not produce a single consensus, but they highlight that “pluriverse”
discourse is itself heterogeneous.

Critiques and Limits
Critics (often from within indigenous contexts or sympathetic scholarship) warn of pitfalls. A common
critique is romanticization or essentialism: treating Indigenous traditions as pure, static, or inherently
virtuous risks oversimplification. Some commentators find that enthusiastically speaking of “other worlds”
can exoticize or commodify indigenous culture 8 (e.g. turning rituals into touristic spectacles or academic
buzzwords). There is also concern about selective appropriation: Western scholars may highlight aspects
of indigenous ontology that fit post-colonial discourse (e.g. earth beings, caregiving) while ignoring other
aspects (e.g. internal gender hierarchies, resource struggles). This can reproduce colonial dynamics of
speaking for Indigenous peoples.
Another limit is the ambiguity of universal claims: if “the pluriverse” becomes a dominant term, it might
inadvertently re-impose a norm (e.g. that all cultures are equally different), rather than simply describing
diversity. Some indigenous voices question whether talking about a single “pluriversal framework” might
gloss over very real conflicts between communities (e.g. two tribes with competing claims), or
misunderstandings within their own traditions.
Finally, there are practical constraints. Not all scholars or activists agree on the primacy of ontology; some
argue economic or political factors are more urgent. And while concepts like Ubuntu or buen vivir have
inspired constitutions, critics note they can be co-opted by states without real power-sharing. Some justice
activists say that focusing on difference should not let governments off the hook for material inequalities.
In sum, while pluriversal and indigenous thought offer rich primitives (plural worlds, relationality,
reciprocity, living territory, ancestral law, etc.), authors note that these ideas are politically complex and
must be handled with care. The tradition itself spans indigenous authors and ally-scholars, and there is no
unchallenged party line – each assertion (e.g. about what is the world) is subject to debate. As Viveiros de
Castro’s paradox says, occupying another’s perspective (as in this synthesis) inevitably involves translation
and transformation; the most we can achieve is partially connected understanding, not perfect
comprehension 2 8 .
