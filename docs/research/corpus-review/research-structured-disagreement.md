# Structured disagreement as validation: a cross-disciplinary synthesis

This synthesis surveys the 1970s-present tradition of validating knowledge-artifacts — papers, plans, corpora, model outputs — through structured disagreement. The tradition is not unified. It is a loose family of practices sharing one premise: that independent or adversarial critique catches errors a single author or process cannot. What follows is labeled prose across six sections, weighted toward primitives, layering, placement, and internal disagreements. Absences and contradictions are flagged as deliberately as findings.

## §1 — Primitives

**Reviewer.** Universally load-bearing but defined very differently. In biomedical peer review the reviewer is an unpaid external expert acting in a gatekeeping capacity (Rennie 1986; Jefferson et al. 2007). In UFMCS red-teaming the reviewer is a trained skeptic embedded on the commander's staff (UFMCS v9 2019, Ch. 4). In ML benchmarking the "reviewer" is a held-out test set plus the community reading the leaderboard (Liang et al. 2022). In debate-based alignment the reviewer is a human or model judge adjudicating between two AI advocates (Irving, Christiano, Amodei 2018). The concept fractures across dimensions of expertise, employment, anonymity, and adversarial posture. No tradition treats "reviewer" as a settled primitive; most inherit the term from academic peer review and retrofit it.

**Independence.** This is the concept most explicitly contested across the literature. The academic tradition defines independence structurally (no co-authorship, no recent collaboration, no shared grant), but Lee, Sugimoto, Zhang and Cronin (2013) document that in small fields the criterion is largely aspirational. Zenko (2015) treats independence as the single most important red-team property — *"outside and objective, while inside and aware"* — and Millennium Challenge 2002 is his case study of what happens when that firewall is breached: Van Riper's OPFOR sank sixteen Blue ships in minutes, and JFCOM simply reconstituted the fleet and scripted his subsequent moves. The purple-teaming literature (NIST SP 800-115; SANS/AttackIQ doctrine) argues the opposite: strict independence sacrifices the feedback loop that actually improves defenses. W3C's Process Document institutionalizes independence as *horizontal review* — mandatory solicitation from Security, Privacy, Accessibility, Internationalization, and TAG groups that have no stake in the submitting working group. The ML tradition uses "independence" loosely: a held-out test set is called independent, but Sainz et al. (2023) and Dodge et al. (2021) document systematic leakage that negates the claim.

**Adversarial stance vs. impartial stance.** These are not the same. Academic peer review canonically aspires to impartiality (Rennie 1998; Godlee et al. 1998): the reviewer is supposed to assess the manuscript on its merits, not to defeat it. Red-teaming explicitly rejects impartiality — Zenko's practitioners are *"fearless skeptics,"* and the CIA Tradecraft Primer (2009, p.17) codifies Devil's Advocacy as *"building the best possible case for an alternative explanation."* ML red-teaming (Perez et al. 2022) and AI debate (Irving et al. 2018) are adversarial by construction. The two stances diverge sharply on what counts as success: the impartial reviewer succeeds by accurate quality assessment; the adversarial reviewer succeeds by finding failure modes that an impartial reviewer would not have sought. The tradition has no settled term for this distinction.

**Blindness.** Single-, double-, triple-, and open-blind arrangements form a canonical axis in academic peer review, with a substantial empirical literature yielding contradictory results. Godlee, Gale and Martyn (1998, *JAMA* 280:237), van Rooyen et al. (1998, *JAMA* 280:234) and Justice et al. (1998, *JAMA* 280:240) all found null-to-small effects of blinding on review quality. Tomkins, Zhang and Heavlin (2017, *PNAS* 114:12708) found strong effects at WSDM: single-blind reviewers were 1.63× more likely to recommend acceptance for famous authors, 1.58× for top institutions. Okike et al. (2016, *JAMA* 316:1315) found a 19-percentage-point acceptance gap in orthopedics. Blindness is load-bearing as a concept but empirically unstable as a control — it appears to matter most where prestige cues are most salient, and to matter least for error detection.

**Red team / blue team / purple team.** These terms originate in military exercises, migrate into cybersecurity (NIST SP 800-115, 2008; MITRE ATT&CK, 2015), and are now ported into AI (EO 14110, Oct 2023; Feffer et al. 2024). The red/blue distinction carries a strong independence assumption; the purple-team doctrine explicitly breaks it. The terminology is becoming semantically loose — Feffer, Sinha, Deng, Lipton and Heidari (2024, arXiv 2401.15897) document that "AI red-teaming" now refers to activities ranging from adversarial prompting to internal audit to crowdsourced jailbreak contests, with no shared definition of artifact, threat model, or success criterion.

**Bakeoff / benchmark / leaderboard.** These are the ML tradition's native primitive and have no direct analogue in peer review. A bakeoff is an adversarial but rule-bound evaluation on a shared test set. Liang et al.'s HELM (2022, arXiv 2211.09110) and Hendrycks et al.'s MMLU (2020, arXiv 2009.03300) are canonical; Chiang et al.'s Chatbot Arena (2024, arXiv 2403.04132) pushes toward live human adversarial evaluation. The critical literature (Raji et al. 2021; Bowman & Dahl 2021) argues bakeoffs inherit peer review's independence language without its structural enforcement: anyone can optimize against a public test set, and Recht et al. (2019) document ~11-14 percentage-point accuracy drops on ImageNetV2 even while model rankings are preserved.

**Gold standard / ground truth.** Load-bearing in ML and clinical evaluation; nearly absent in academic peer review (where the object of review is typically a novel claim without prior ground truth) and in red-teaming (where "ground truth" is the actual adversary's behavior, unknowable in advance). Zenko (2015) emphasizes this: red teams that are evaluated against a gold standard have already lost the plot. In AI safety, the absence of ground truth for superhuman capabilities is exactly what motivates debate (Irving et al. 2018) and Constitutional AI (Bai et al. 2022).

**Inter-rater agreement.** This is the tradition's most damning self-audit. Rothwell and Martyn (2000, *Brain* 123:1964) reported κ = 0.08 (95% CI −0.04 to 0.20) between reviewers at one neuroscience journal — statistically indistinguishable from chance. Bornmann, Mutz and Daniel (2010, *PLoS ONE* 5:e14331) meta-analyzed 48 studies and found mean κ = 0.17. The NeurIPS consistency experiment (Lawrence & Cortes 2014; replicated by Beygelzimer et al. 2021) found that approximately 50% of accepted papers would have been rejected by a second independent program committee — and the 2021 replication at 5× scale found 50.6% disagreement, essentially unchanged. Pier et al. (2018, *PNAS* 115:2952) found ICC ≈ 0 (95% CI 0 to 0.14) on NIH grant review. The tradition treats inter-rater agreement as a quality metric but rarely acts on the findings.

**Rebuttal / appeal.** Load-bearing and structurally under-theorized. Most academic journals permit rebuttals but rarely re-review; IETF's Last Call (RFC 2026) and W3C's Formal Objection process institutionalize appeal more explicitly, with the W3C Council (AB + TAG) adjudicating sustained objections. ML bakeoffs generally have no rebuttal mechanism at all. Iterative review (round 1 → rebuttal → round 2) is standard in conferences and grant review but uncommon in red-teaming, where the assumption is that the artifact changes between rounds.

**Self-review / self-critique.** The contested primitive of the AI era. Bai et al. (2022, arXiv 2212.08073) operationalize it as Constitutional AI: a model critiques its own outputs against a written constitution, then is trained on the revisions. Madaan et al.'s Self-Refine (2023) claimed roughly 20% average gains across seven tasks. Huang et al. (2023, arXiv 2310.01798) showed that *intrinsic* self-correction — without an oracle, without external tools — actually *degrades* GPT-3.5/4 performance on reasoning tasks; prior gains were attributable to oracle labels telling the model when to revise. Panickssery et al. (2024, arXiv 2404.13076) documented that LLM evaluators recognize and favor their own generations, with self-recognition linearly correlated with self-preference. The older tradition has no analogue: the academic peer-review literature treats author self-critique as a precondition for submission, not a review gate.

**Constitutional constraint.** A young primitive, specific to Bai et al. (2022) and the downstream Anthropic constitution publications. The concept — an explicit rule-set the reviewer applies — has looser analogues in CONSORT-style reporting checklists for clinical trials, IETF's BCP 9 principles in RFC 2026 (*"technical excellence; prior implementation and testing; clear documentation; openness and fairness; timeliness"*), and the UFMCS Handbook's structured-technique inventory. The difference is that constitutional constraint names the rule-set as the reviewer's authority, whereas checklists are used by a human reviewer whose authority derives from expertise.

**Review-of-the-reviewer (meta-review).** Weakly institutionalized across traditions. The JAMA Peer Review Congress (quadrennial since 1989; Rennie 1986, *JAMA* 256:2391) is the closest thing to a reviewer-review tradition in academia; its persistent finding is that peer review's evidentiary base for effectiveness remains thin. IETF and W3C have chair and Council review as meta-review layers. Red-teaming has post-exercise review but (per Zenko) is systematically ignored when its findings embarrass senior commanders. ML has community scrutiny of leaderboards but no formal meta-review process. LLM-as-judge (Zheng et al. 2023) is meta-review by construction but suffers the self-preference biases Panickssery documented.

## §2 — Layering

The tradition layers review along six axes, and subdisciplines occupy sharply different positions on each.

**Artifact vs. process vs. reviewer.** Academic peer review is overwhelmingly artifact-focused: the manuscript is the object, reviewer quality is presumed. Cochrane systematic review methodology inverts this, reviewing the *process* by which evidence was generated (Jefferson et al. 2007). Haddon-Cave's Nimrod Review (2009) found the UK MoD safety-case regime had degenerated into *"paperwork and 'tick box' exercise" — "virtually worthless as a safety tool"* — a failure of reviewer and process simultaneously; its corrective was to reorient around Leadership, Independence, People, and Simplicity rather than documentation. AI responsible-scaling frameworks (Anthropic RSP, OpenAI Preparedness, DeepMind FSF) review capability thresholds (artifact) and organizational commitments (process) but delegate reviewer-review to third parties like METR and Apollo.

**Formal institutional vs. informal team-internal.** Peer review, standards-body review, and grant review are formal-institutional. Red-teaming is heterogeneous: UFMCS institutionalizes it at Fort Leavenworth but practice varies by commander; NYPD's red cell is semi-formal (Zenko 2015). ML benchmarking is informal-institutional — leaderboards are owned by research groups or foundations but compliance is voluntary. AI self-critique inside a model is informal by construction. The tradition does not possess a vocabulary for the intermediate case — a small team's structured internal critique — which is exactly the regime in which Spore operates.

**Pre-publication vs. post-publication.** Academic peer review is typically pre-publication and single-pass. Post-publication review, institutionalized through PubPeer (2012–), F1000Research (2013–), and Retraction Watch (2010–), surfaced the Obokata STAP-cell fabrications within days of *Nature* publication in January 2014 (RIKEN finding 1 April 2014; retraction July 2014). PubPeer survived the 2014-2016 *Sarkar v. PubPeer* suit with anonymity intact. The debate over whether post-publication review is complement or replacement remains unresolved: Knoepfler (2015) argues it catalyzed STAP retractions in months rather than years, while *Nature* editorials have consistently framed it as supplementary.

**Single-pass vs. iterative.** Peer review is typically single-pass with a rebuttal round; red-teaming is iterative by design (UFMCS handbook assumes multiple cycles against evolving plans); ML bakeoffs are single-pass per model version but iterative across model generations. AI debate is explicitly iterative within a single evaluation (Khan et al. 2024, Michael et al. 2023). The mapping of iteration to error-catching rate is understudied across all traditions.

**Human vs. AI vs. mixed.** Until 2022 this was not a meaningful axis. After Bai et al.'s Constitutional AI, Perez et al.'s LM red-teaming, and the explosion of LLM-as-judge work (Zheng et al. 2023, arXiv 2306.05685), the axis is the primary locus of disagreement in the field. Zheng et al. reported GPT-4 judge agreement with humans >80% — comparable to inter-human agreement — but documented position, verbosity, and self-enhancement biases.

**Open vs. blind.** Peer review occupies the full spectrum. IETF and W3C are open by design — reviewer identities are attached to every objection. Red-teaming is mixed: UFMCS encourages identified red-team participation within the command; penetration tests are typically blind. ML benchmarking is open. AI debate is open by construction (both advocates are named/identified at least as model versions). The tradition provides no empirical basis for choosing open over blind in small-team settings.

**Subdisciplinary patterns.** Academic peer review is pre-publication, single-pass, blind-leaning, formal, artifact-focused, human. Red-teaming is iterative, often open, semi-formal, process-and-artifact, human (moving mixed). ML bakeoffs are post-training, single-pass-per-version, open, informal-institutional, artifact-focused, mixed. AI self-review is by-construction mixed-or-AI, iterative, constitutional. These combinations are not theorized comparatively in any single source; the comparison itself is a gap.

## §3 — Placement of universal concepts

**Trust** is the tradition's foundation but is rarely named. Rennie (1998) and Smith (2006) both argue peer review is a trust-generating ritual whose epistemic power is weaker than its social power. *"Peer review is based on faith in its effects, rather than on facts"* (Jefferson et al. 2007). Trust is load-bearing; its audit is the tradition's weakest point.

**Care** is absent as a named concept. The literature treats reviewers as adversaries or judges, not caregivers. The closest approach is Zenko's insistence that red teams must be *"inside and aware"* — loyal to the mission if not the plan.

**Identity.** Load-bearing in open review (IETF, W3C, PubPeer-post-Sarkar) and in blinding debates. Panickssery et al. (2024) gives identity a new twist: LLMs recognize their own generations and favor them, which means model-identity becomes a reviewer bias in AI self-review. Identity is variably concealed, revealed, or fabricated across the tradition.

**Memory.** Weakly theorized. ML bakeoffs have a structural memory problem (test-set contamination across training-data snapshots; Dodge et al. 2021, Sainz et al. 2023). Peer review has institutional memory (reviewer databases, prior-submission history) but individual reviewers rarely retain context between manuscripts. Red-teaming (UFMCS) emphasizes after-action memory but Zenko documents its frequent suppression.

**State, Signals.** Absent as named primitives. The closest is the benchmark *score* as a signal, contested throughout Raji et al. (2021), Bowman & Dahl (2021), and Recht et al. (2019). Chatbot Arena's Elo ratings are signals without gold-standard state.

**Agency.** Implicit in adversarial stance but rarely named. The tradition assumes reviewers are agents with preferences; it rarely models them as such. *Noise* (Kahneman, Sibony, Sunstein 2021) is the major exception: it decomposes reviewer variance into level noise, stable pattern noise, and occasion noise, and treats reviewers as noisy sensors with idiosyncratic transfer functions.

**Norms / protocols.** Heavily load-bearing in the standards-body subdiscipline (RFC 2026; RFC 7282; W3C Process Document) and in Registered Reports / pre-registration (Chambers; Nosek 2015, *Science* 348:1422). Much weaker in peer review and red-teaming, where norms are usually tacit.

**Power / authority.** The tradition is peculiarly silent on power. Smith (2006) and Lee et al. (2013) treat it as bias; Zenko (2015) treats it as the reason red teams get ignored (Millennium Challenge, FAA pre-9/11); RFC 7282 explicitly rejects *"kings, presidents, and voting"* — but "rough consensus" creates its own authority structure through chair judgment. The tradition lacks a shared vocabulary for reviewer authority.

**Commitment.** Nearly absent. The closest is the pre-registered analysis plan (Simmons, Nelson, Simonsohn 2011; Registered Reports), which is a commitment by the author to a specific methodology before data are seen. Reviewer commitments are rarely formalized.

**Intent.** Load-bearing only in adversarial subdisciplines (red-teaming, AI debate). Absent in peer review, where reviewer intent is assumed impartial by fiat.

**Evidence.** The tradition's nominal object but surprisingly contested. What counts as evidence that peer review works? Jefferson et al. (2007) concluded there is *"little empirical evidence."* What counts as evidence that a benchmark measures what it claims? Raji et al. (2021) argue the question is almost never asked. The tradition discusses evidence within artifacts but rarely applies it to its own practices.

**Holon-like unit (whole-and-part).** Absent as a named primitive. The closest analogues are Cochrane reviews (which treat each study as a part in a whole-of-literature review) and the Reproducibility Project: Psychology (Open Science Collaboration 2015), which treats each replication attempt as a part of a distributional estimate of the field's reliability. Neither is formalized as holonic.

**Boundary.** Load-bearing in independence and blinding debates. Structurally enforced in standards bodies (IETF Last Call announcement lists; W3C Wide Review minimum 60 days). Looser elsewhere.

**Reciprocity.** Mostly absent. Peer review is unpaid and structurally non-reciprocal — a reviewer does not expect to be reviewed by the same author. Red-teaming is explicitly non-reciprocal. The replication movement introduces a weak form: by replicating, I commit to being potentially replicated.

**Reproduction / continuity.** This is the replication crisis literature's entire subject matter. Begley & Ellis (2012, *Nature* 483:531) found 6 of 53 Amgen preclinical cancer studies replicable (~11%); Open Science Collaboration (2015, *Science* 349:aac4716) replicated 36-39% of 100 psychology studies at p<.05 with effect sizes halved (mean r 0.403 → 0.197); Camerer et al. (2018, *Nature Human Behaviour* 2:637) replicated 13 of 21 *Nature*/*Science* social-science studies at effect sizes ~50% of original. Reproduction is load-bearing and empirically well-studied; it is the area of the tradition with the strongest quantitative base.

## §4 — Internal disagreements

**1. Does peer review empirically improve quality?** Jefferson et al.'s Cochrane review (2007, updated by Bruce et al. 2016) concluded: *"little empirical evidence is available to support the use of editorial peer review as a mechanism to ensure quality of biomedical research."* Of the trials they analyzed, blinding showed no clear effect on quality assessment; checklists showed modest support in two studies; training showed none. Richard Smith (2006, *JRSM* 99:178) goes further: peer review is *"slow, expensive, largely a lottery, poor at detecting errors, almost useless at detecting fraud, anti-innovatory, biased, prone to abuse"* — citing the BMJ trial in which reviewers found on average fewer than two of eight deliberately inserted errors. Drummond Rennie (2016, *Nature* 535:31) offers the measured defense: peer review is imperfect but essential, and *"without it, there would be no way to ensure the trustworthiness of the medical and scientific literature."* Rennie's position is that the solution is more empirical study, not abandonment. What is at stake: whether the entire scholarly-validation edifice rests on evidence or on faith, and whether proposals for lighter or post-publication-centric review (F1000Research, PubPeer) should be treated as reform or replacement.

**2. Single-blind vs. double-blind efficacy.** The canonical BMJ/JAMA trials (Godlee 1998, van Rooyen 1998, Justice 1998) found null-to-small effects of blinding on review quality. Tomkins, Zhang and Heavlin (2017, *PNAS* 114:12708) at WSDM found that single-blind reviewers were 1.63× more likely to recommend acceptance for famous authors. Okike et al. (2016, *JAMA* 316:1315) in orthopedics found a 19-percentage-point acceptance gap. Budden et al. (2008, *TREE* 23:4) reported a 7.9-percentage-point increase in female first-authored papers after *Behavioral Ecology* adopted double-blind — a finding contested by Webb et al. (2008) and Engqvist as reflecting field-wide trends. The resolution is disciplinary: blinding matters less for error detection and more for accept/reject decisions when prestige cues are salient, and field composition determines the size of the effect. What is at stake: whether structural controls can meaningfully suppress reviewer bias, or whether bias is substantive enough to require different remedies (post-publication scrutiny, algorithmic decision aids).

**3. Red-team purity vs. purple-team integration.** Zenko (2015) treats adversarial independence as the single most important red-team property and Millennium Challenge 2002 as the canonical breach: JFCOM scripted Van Riper's OPFOR after it destroyed sixteen Blue ships. The purple-teaming literature (SANS, AttackIQ, MITRE ATT&CK emulation practice) argues that strict separation creates communication gaps and sacrifices the feedback loop that actually improves defenses. Feffer et al. (2024, arXiv 2401.15897) argue the purity question has become moot in AI because AI red-teaming is so ill-defined that the purity/integration distinction cannot be cleanly applied. What is at stake: whether adversarial validation requires structural independence at cost of learning, or whether integrated adversary-defender cycles dominate once the artifact is iterative.

**4. Can AI review AI?** Bai et al. (2022) and the debate-based alignment lineage (Irving, Christiano, Amodei 2018; Michael et al. 2023, arXiv 2311.08702; Khan et al. 2024, arXiv 2402.06782) argue yes, with structural protocols. Khan et al. report that non-expert LLM judges reached 76% accuracy on QuALITY with debate versus 48% naive baseline, and that optimizing debaters for persuasiveness *increased* truthful-answer win-rate. The critics — Huang et al. (2023, arXiv 2310.01798) and Panickssery et al. (2024, arXiv 2404.13076) — argue intrinsic self-correction degrades reasoning and LLM evaluators linearly favor their own generations. Barnes's *"obfuscated arguments"* (2020, AI Alignment Forum) provides a theoretical limit: a dishonest debater can plant a flawed sub-claim in an argument too large for the honest side to rebut within a bounded round budget. The empirical resolution favoring adversarial multi-agent protocols over single-model self-review remains contested at the superhuman-capability frontier that motivates the debate agenda in the first place.

**5. Are bakeoffs rigorous science or leaderboard-gaming?** Raji et al. (2021, NeurIPS) and Bowman & Dahl (2021, NAACL) argue that general benchmarks like ImageNet and GLUE lack construct validity and fail all four criteria (validity, statistical power, disaggregated reporting, bias coverage). Recht et al. (2019) documented an 11-14-percentage-point accuracy drop on ImageNetV2 while rankings were preserved — interpretable either as adaptive-overfitting-limited (defenders) or as distribution-shift-real (critics). Dodge et al. (2021) and Sainz et al. (2023) document extensive contamination of the C4 corpus with evaluation sets including GLUE and LAMA. Builders respond with diversified metrics (HELM), live human evaluation (Chatbot Arena — though subject to style bias and the Llama-4 campaign-voting incident of April 2024), and execution-graded benchmarks (SWE-bench). The debate is unresolved and the tradition has no consensus standard for what a rigorous benchmark even is.

**6. Is post-publication review complement or replacement?** PubPeer, F1000Research, and Retraction Watch argue functional complement at least, potential replacement at most (Knoepfler 2015). *Nature* editorials and the peer-review establishment argue that gatekeeping requires pre-publication filtering. The STAP cell case is ambiguous: pre-publication review at *Nature* missed the fabrications; post-publication review surfaced them within days. What is at stake: whether the publication-as-imprimatur model can survive the shift to continuous online commentary, and whether small-team coordination structures should model validation as a one-shot gate or a continuous audit.

**7. Is reviewer independence achievable or performative in small fields?** Lee, Sugimoto, Zhang, Cronin (2013, *JASIST* 64:2) document that independence criteria are widely violated in small subfields. The Pier et al. finding (2018, *PNAS* 115:2952) of ICC ≈ 0 on NIH grant review, and the NeurIPS 2014/2021 replication showing ~50% disagreement on accept/reject, suggest that even where independence is structurally enforced, the review signal is noisy enough that independence may not be the binding constraint. Fang, Bowen, Casadevall (2016, *eLife* 5:e13323) showed NIH percentile scores between 3 and 20 are essentially uncorrelated with subsequent publications and citations — evidence that reviewer judgments discriminate poorly at the quality levels that gating actually matters.

## §5 — Critiques and limits

Several critiques cross subdisciplinary lines and deserve explicit statement. First, peer review's institutional centrality vastly exceeds its empirical-validation base; Jefferson, Smith, Rennie and Ioannidis all agree on this even where they disagree on remedies. Second, reviewer bias is well-documented along gender, prestige, and disciplinary axes (Budden 2008, Tomkins 2017, Okike 2016); structural fixes remain contested because the effects are context-dependent. Third, red-team and bakeoff cultures typically lack statistical rigor: Zenko (2015) documents post-exercise critique as ad-hoc; benchmark evaluations rarely report confidence intervals or pre-registered analyses (Bowman & Dahl 2021). Fourth, self-review and constitutional approaches raise circularity concerns that remain partially unresolved: Huang et al. (2023) demonstrates intrinsic self-correction failure on reasoning; Panickssery et al. (2024) demonstrates self-preference bias. Fifth, most review arrangements are statistically underpowered — two to three reviewers catching true errors is modest power, and Pier et al.'s ICC ≈ 0 on NIH grants means that across the plausible reviewer-count range, aggregation buys little. Sixth, review labor is unevenly distributed: peer review is unpaid and shouldered by a small fraction of active researchers; red-teaming often falls to junior staff whose findings can be ignored; benchmark maintenance is informal and under-resourced.

A seventh critique is distinctive to Kahneman, Sibony, Sunstein (2021): noise-versus-bias decomposition reveals that variance (noise) and systematic error (bias) contribute equally to mean squared error in expectation, yet the tradition overwhelmingly targets bias. Their decision-hygiene prescriptions — independent judgments aggregated later, structured protocols, mediating assessments, decision observers — are explicitly disagreement-generating protocols, and the *Noise* framework is arguably the most integrated theoretical treatment of structured disagreement the tradition possesses. But it offers no rigorous guidance for small-N (N=2) review settings, which is the regime a two-reviewer bakeoff occupies.

Two additional structural limits deserve emphasis. Meehl's clinical-vs-actuarial tradition (Meehl 1954; Grove et al. 2000) found that mechanical rules outperform clinical judgment by ~10% on average, with clinicians substantially worse when inputs include rich qualitative data. Tetlock's work (2005; 2015) partially rescues human judgment via aggregation, training, and fox-style epistemic humility, but finds that individual experts remain poor forecasters; superforecasters achieve their results through aggregation across many individuals, not individual brilliance. The implication for small-team review is stark: two reviewers, however well-chosen, represent a regime in which neither Meehl's actuarial advantage nor Tetlock's aggregation advantage is operative. Structured-disagreement primitives at N=2 depend on the mechanism of disagreement-generation (adversarial decomposition, checklist coverage, independent rule-sets) rather than on the statistical power of aggregation.

## R-claims

- **R1**: Structured disagreement is a family of validation practices built on the claim that independent or adversarial critique surfaces errors single-pass authorship misses. [target:meta:structured-disagreement] [concept:evidence]
*R1 is supported by Rennie, Jefferson et al., and Zenko.*

- **R2**: Independence is a real but contested control because strict red-team separation catches blind spots while purple-team integration improves learning loops. [target:candidate:independence-criterion] [concept:protocol-society]
*R2 is supported by Zenko, NIST SP 800-115, and the purple-team / Feffer literature.*

- **R3**: Adversarial validation and impartial review are distinct review postures with different success criteria and should not be conflated. [target:candidate:adversarial-review] [concept:signal]
*R3 is supported by the JAMA peer-review literature, the CIA Tradecraft Primer, and the AI debate papers.*

- **R4**: Inter-rater agreement is persistently low across journals, grants, and conferences, making disagreement a structural property of review systems rather than a rare anomaly. [target:candidate:inter-rater-agreement] [concept:evidence]
*R4 is supported by Rothwell & Martyn, Bornmann et al., the NeurIPS consistency experiments, and Pier et al.*

- **R5**: Multi-agent adversarial review can outperform naive self-review, but intrinsic self-correction and LLM judging remain vulnerable to self-preference and reasoning degradation. [target:candidate:self-review-limits] [concept:signal]
*R5 is supported by Bai et al., Khan et al., Huang et al., and Panickssery et al.*

- **R6**: In small-N review settings, protocol design matters more than reviewer count alone because statistical aggregation is too weak to rescue noisy judgments. [target:meta:corpus-foundational-review] [concept:protocol-society]
*R6 is supported by Kahneman/Sibony/Sunstein, Meehl/Grove, Tetlock, and the NeurIPS consistency work.*

**Flagged sources.** UFMCS Red Team Handbook v9.0 is the primary public doctrinal source but full-text access outside APAN is via third-party mirrors whose fidelity to the official document cannot be independently verified. Rennie's widely-quoted *"if peer review was a drug it would never get onto the market"* is attributed through Smith (2006, *JRSM*); the exact original phrasing is likely a paraphrase of Rennie's 1986/1992 work. The Budden et al. (2008) *Behavioral Ecology* double-blind effect is statistically contested by Webb et al. (2008, *TREE* 23:351-353) and Engqvist. Begley & Ellis's 6/53 figure is self-reported without published replication details. Bayer's 25% reproducibility figure (Prinz et al. 2011) is from correspondence without disclosed specific studies. Purple-teaming's origin lacks a single canonical coinage in surveyed sources.
