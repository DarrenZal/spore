# Viable System Model Literature Synthesis for Spore

*Tradition-specific synthesis — Stafford Beer lineage of management cybernetics, 1972–present. Analytical in tone; internal disagreements prioritised over consensus.*

---

## §1 — Primitives

**Recursion (system-in-system).** Beer formalises recursion as the *Recursive System Theorem*: "In a recursive organizational structure, any viable system contains, and is contained in, a viable system" (Beer, *The Heart of Enterprise*, 1979, p. 118 [print]; restated Beer 1984, p. 14 [open-access]). Every System 1 operational unit of a higher-level VSM is itself a complete viable system with its own five subsystems and its own environment. Beer insists this is not metaphor but a topological requirement: each recursion must be an exact homomorphic replica of the next, grounded in Hegel's Axiom of Internal Relations (Beer 1984, p. 14 [open-access]). Espejo reframes recursion operationally as "complexity unfolding" — the analyst breaks environment and organisation into matched sub-sub-environments until residual complexity is manageable (Espejo & Reyes, *Organizational Systems*, Springer 2011, ch. 6 [paywalled]; Pérez Ríos 2012, pp. 84–140 [paywalled]).

**Requisite variety (Ashby's law).** Beer inherits Ashby's formulation verbatim — "only variety can destroy variety" — and elevates it to foundational status: "Ashby's Law stands to management science as Newton's Laws stand to physics" (Beer 1984, p. 7 [open-access]). Variety is the count of distinguishable system states. The Law is operationalised in three places: (i) variety blocks themselves; (ii) channel capacity; (iii) transducers. These yield Beer's First, Second and Third Principles of Organization (*Heart of Enterprise*, 1979, pp. 97, 99, 101 [print]). Every homeostatic loop — management↔operation, operation↔environment — must be engineered so that varieties on both sides equate, "with minimum damage to people and to cost" (First Principle, p. 97).

**System 1 — operation.** The primary activities. Beer: "The first subsystem of any viable system consists of those elements that produce it (they are the system's autopoietic generators, to use Maturana's terminology). These elements are themselves viable systems" (Beer 1984, p. 13 [open-access]; *Brain of the Firm*, 2nd edn. Wiley 1981, ch. 12 [print]). Each S1 unit comprises a local environment, an operation, and an embedded local management, and it faces a *slice* of the total environment directly.

**System 2 — coordination.** Anti-oscillatory damping between S1 units whose local autonomy would otherwise produce conflict over shared resources, schedules or standards. Beer: "If Two is missing, activity in One can turn deadly and self-defeating as units fight for resources and against entropy" (Beer 1984, p. 16 [open-access], quoting *Brain & Strategy* 1983). S2 is identified neurophysiologically with the sympathetic/parasympathetic chain (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, chs. 7, 9 [print]). Walker's practitioner guide glosses S2 as "shared standards, dependency cadences, schedules, incident forums" (Walker, *The VSM Guide for Co-operatives and Federations*, 3rd edn., §5 [open-access, esrad.org.uk]).

**System 3 — control.** The "inside-and-now" command axis: here-and-now management of the totality of S1, resource bargaining, synergy generation, translation of S5/S4 policy into operational instruction. Beer identifies S3 with the brainstem and basal ganglia — the autonomic command centre (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, ch. 12 [print]). The defining S3 pathology is "narrow-tunnel syndrome": S3 dives into S1 problems and never resurfaces (Beer 1984, p. 16 [open-access]).

**System 3\* — audit.** A *sporadic* direct channel from S3 into S1 operations, bypassing the regular command line and bypassing S2. It exists precisely because the resource-bargain channel filters information S3 may need unmediated — quality, finance, compliance. Diagrams in Beer, *Diagnosing the System for Organizations* (Wiley 1985, ch. 2 [institutional]) render S3\* as a triangle probing into S1. Schwaninger & Scheef's empirical test (*Cybernetics & Systems* 47(7), 2016, pp. 544–569 [paywalled]) found the construct **could not be empirically distinguished from S3** in confirmatory factor analysis — an internal identification problem the tradition has not resolved.

**System 4 — intelligence.** The "outside-and-future" function: environmental scanning, R&D, planning, and maintaining the organisation's model of itself-in-environment. S4 is identified with the diencephalon and the thalamic–cortical relay (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, ch. 13 [print]); Beer's Operations Room / management centre is its physical embodiment (Beer 1984, p. 17 [open-access]). "If Four is missing, Three and Five can collapse into each other, leaving the critical Five subsystem a mere functionary" (Beer 1984, p. 16 [open-access]).

**System 5 — policy / identity.** The logical closure of the recursion, the ultimate authority, and the bearer of ethos and identity. Beer: "What could not have been otherwise is the fact of the logical closure of the viable system by 'System Five' … only this determines an identity" (Beer 1984, p. 14 [open-access]; *Heart of Enterprise*, 1979, "Test of Identity" [print]). S5's function is to arbitrate the 3–4 homeostat and to absorb residual variety that the S3–S4 balance has not resolved. Identified with the cerebral cortex (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, ch. 14 [print]).

**Amplifier / attenuator.** A designed *pair* deployed on every homeostatic loop to equate varieties. Amplifiers expand regulator variety; attenuators absorb or filter operational/environmental variety. "If variety were not requisite in a regulatory homeostat, then either the greater variety must be attenuated, or the lesser variety must be amplified — or both" (Beer 1984, p. 7 [open-access]). In the canonical VSM diagram (Beer 1985, ch. 2 [institutional]) attenuators and amplifiers are drawn on every channel; variety engineering *is* the design discipline of VSM.

**Homeostasis.** Following Ashby, a viable system is a set of "interlocking Ashbean homeostats" (Beer 1984, p. 7 [open-access]) — ultrastable equilibria between paired variety generators. Beer codifies the equilibrium as **Three Axioms of Management** (*Heart of Enterprise*, 1979, pp. 217, 298, 298 [print]): horizontal S1 variety equals vertical metasystem variety; S3 variety equals S4 variety; S5 variety equals the residual variety of the S3–S4 homeostat. A **Law of Cohesion** (p. 355) extends this across recursions. **Algedonic signals** — from Greek *algos* (pain) and *hēdos* (pleasure) — are the alarm/reward channel that bypasses the normal hierarchy and escalates directly to S5 when actuality departs from capability beyond a threshold (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, ch. 10 "The Biggest Switch" [print]).

**Autopoiesis link.** Beer explicitly imports Maturana's vocabulary: S1 units are "the system's autopoietic generators" (Beer 1984, p. 13 [open-access]). In his *Preface* to Maturana & Varela, *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (Reidel, 1980, pp. 63–72 [institutional]), Beer goes further: "human societies are biological systems." This is the most contested primitive in the tradition — see §4 below. In Espejo & Reyes' later reading (2011, ch. 2 [paywalled]), autopoiesis is softened into **organisational closure**: organisations are "closed networks of relations having identity," with *interactions* (not persons) as the self-produced components.

## §2 — Layering

VSM's layering is a dual claim: a fixed five-function vertical typology *and* unbounded recursive embedding.

**The five-function stack.** Beer's necessary-and-sufficient claim is blunt: "five necessary and sufficient subsystems interactively involved in any organism or organization that is capable of maintaining its identity independently … within a shared environment" (Beer 1984, p. 12 [open-access]). The functions are stratified by **time-horizon and locus**: S1–S3 handle the "inside-and-now"; S4 handles the "outside-and-then"; S5 closes the loop with identity. The justification is not conceptual alone — Beer derived the stack from a homomorphic mapping of the firm onto the human nervous system ("yo-yo technique," Beer 1965, reprinted Beer 1984 App. 1 [open-access]): S1↔muscles/organs with reflex arcs, S2↔spinal cord and sympathetic chain, S3↔pons-medulla autonomic command, S3\*↔visceral afferents, S4↔diencephalon, S5↔cerebral cortex (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, chs. 6–14 [print]).

**The recursion principle.** Formally: "every viable system contains and is contained in a viable system" (Beer 1979, p. 118 [print]). The topological consequence is that each recursion level's diagram must be an exact homomorphic replica of its neighbours. Espejo & Reyes (2011, ch. 11 [paywalled]) operationalise this as **recursion analysis**: the analyst unfolds complexity into a "recursion/functions table" mapping primary activities to implementation, and regulatory functions to policy/intelligence/cohesion, at each level.

**Pathology as mis-layering.** The tradition's most distinctive move is to treat *mis-layering as diagnosable pathology*. Beer (1984, pp. 14–18 [open-access]) names several; Pérez Ríos (*Design and Diagnosis for Sustainable Organizations*, Springer 2012, ch. 3, pp. 65–139 [paywalled]) systematises them as the **Taxonomy of Organizational Pathologies (TOP)**.

- Missing S2 → destructive oscillation among S1 units fighting for resources.
- Missing S4 → "Three and Five collapse into each other, leaving the critical Five subsystem a mere functionary" (Beer 1984, p. 16).
- S3 absorbing S4 → "narrow-tunnel syndrome": operational overreach starves intelligence.
- S5 collapsing into S3 → "Five people have usually been promoted from Three. They are uncomfortable as demi-gods with no clear duties beyond being wise and pleasant" (Beer 1984, p. 17).
- **Decerebrate cat** → only the autonomic 3-2-1 loop functions: "It responds reactively, from the autonomic command centres at three, and is incapable of planning and foresight (Four) and will and judgment (Five)" (Beer 1984, p. 17).
- Algedonic-signal failure → pain/pleasure alerts fail to escalate, crises propagate silently.
- **Law of Cohesion violation** at takeover → interconnectivity between recursion levels changes the variety budget; leaving the acquired subsidiary's variety intact may produce reverse-takeover dynamics (Beer 1984, pp. 16–17).

Pérez Ríos distinguishes **structural pathologies** (missing/duplicated systems; absent recursion; vertical incoherence between recursion levels) from **functional pathologies** (defective channels; missing attenuators/amplifiers; algedonic blindness; weak 3-4-5 homeostat) (Pérez Ríos 2012, ch. 3 [paywalled]).

## §3 — Placement of universal concepts

**Identity.** Load-bearing; the *defining* concept of S5 and the criterion of viability. "Only this determines an identity" (Beer 1984, p. 14 [open-access]). Espejo extends identity to Stage 1 of VIPLAN — TASCOI (Transformation, Actors, Suppliers, Customers, Owners, Interveners) operationalises identity via stakeholder analysis (Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 11 [paywalled]).

**Boundary.** Load-bearing. Each S1 unit has a boundary with its slice of the total environment; the whole viable system has a boundary determined by what S5 closes. Espejo stresses that boundaries are *observer-dependent* and emerge from identity ("different observers may attribute diverse purposes to the same organization," Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 6 [paywalled]). This is a second-order-cybernetic inflection absent from Beer's earlier formulations.

**Signals.** Load-bearing and technically specified. Two classes: routine *variety signals* across every channel (governed by the Second and Third Principles, *Heart of Enterprise* pp. 99, 101 [print]); and *algedonic signals* — threshold-triggered alarms/rewards that bypass hierarchy and escalate directly to S5 (*Brain of the Firm* 2e, ch. 10 [print]).

**Power / authority.** Load-bearing but named indirectly. S5 is "ultimate authority"; S3 holds the "central command axis"; the metasystem has "enough authority to maintain cohesion, but not so much as to destroy the autonomy of its viable components" (*Heart of Enterprise*, ch. 14 [print]). Critics treat this as the tradition's weakest flank — see §5.

**Agency / autonomy.** Load-bearing; paired dialectically with cohesion. S1 autonomy is maximised subject to the constraints of system coherence (Walker guide §4 [open-access]); Espejo et al. (1999, *Kybernetes* 28(6/7): 661–678 [paywalled]) formalise autonomy as *discretion distribution*, mapped on the recursion-functions table.

**State.** Load-bearing but specific: S3's "inside-and-now" state is the *actuality* of the 3-2-1 autonomic loop, captured in Beer's triple vector **Actuality / Capability / Potentiality** (*Brain of the Firm*, 1972, p. 163 [print]), yielding Productivity, Latency and Performance ratios.

**Memory.** Marginal to under-specified. Beer has no explicit "organisational memory" primitive; S4 houses the *model of the organisation* and the *model of its environment*, which functionally subsumes long-run memory, but memory is not named as such.

**Norms / protocols.** Load-bearing in S2 (coordination standards) and indirectly in S5 (ethos). Beer: "Rules come from System 5: not so much by stating them firmly, as by creating a corporate ethos — an atmosphere" (paraphrased *Brain of the Firm* 2e ch. 14 [print]; also Metaphorum summary). S2 is explicitly the locus of schedules, shared protocols and standards.

**Reproduction / continuity.** Load-bearing and central: "viability" *is* survival over time. Recursion guarantees viability hereditarily — each S1 must itself be viable — which is the tradition's answer to reproduction.

**Holon-like unit (whole-and-part).** Present structurally but *terminologically absent*. Beer's recursion is logically equivalent to Koestler's holon (each viable system is both whole and part), but Beer does not cite Koestler, and Koestler does not cite Beer. The convergence was only named later by the holonic-manufacturing literature (e.g., Magatef et al., "Recursive Holarchies for Viable Systems," 2018 [paywalled]). This is a notable absence: Spore's "holon" primitive has a structural homologue in VSM but no shared vocabulary.

**Intent / purpose.** Marginal-to-contested. Beer's aphorism **"the purpose of a system is what it does" (POSIWID)** explicitly refuses to privilege stated intent; purpose is *imputed by an observer* and is idiosyncratic (Beer 1984, reporting Allende's own attribution of S5 "to the people," p. 14 [open-access]). Espejo extends this into a pluralist reading: "observers may assign different purposes to the same organization" (Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 6 [paywalled]; Pérez Ríos, 2025, *Systems* 13(9): 749 [open-access, MDPI]). Intent is therefore *not* a load-bearing primitive of VSM; it is a diagnostic artefact subject to observer dependence.

**Commitment.** Absent as a named primitive. The tradition has no equivalent construct to Spore's "commitment." Resource decisions are formalised as the **Resource Bargain** between S3 and S1 management (Beer 1985, ch. 2 glossary [institutional]) — a quasi-contractual allocation — but this is not commitment-as-promise with illocutionary force; it is variety-budgeting between layers.

**Evidence.** Marginal; realised obliquely through S3\* audit and through algedonic reporting. The Resource Bargain presupposes evidence of performance (actuality-vs-capability measurement), and S3\* is precisely the channel for unmediated verification. But "evidence" is not lifted to primitive status.

**Trust.** Absent in Beer's original apparatus; imported later. Reyes' later work on "second-order auditing" (*Systems Practice and Action Research* 14(2), 2001, pp. 157–180 [paywalled]) and Espejo, Bula & Zarama's Colombia audit-office papers (*SPAR* 14(2), 2001 [paywalled]) introduce *contextual trust* vs. *responsible trust* as amplifiers of social complexity. Beer's canonical texts do not treat trust as an engineering variable.

**Care.** Absent. No construct in the VSM tradition maps to care-ethics care. Beer's humanism is expressed instead as the First Principle clause "with minimum damage to people and to cost" (*Heart of Enterprise* p. 97 [print]) and as the stated aim of *Designing Freedom* (1974 [open-access]). These are ethical *boundary conditions on design*, not a primitive.

**Reciprocity.** Absent as a named primitive. Horizontal coordination among S1 units is handled by S2 damping (standards, cadences) and vertical exchange by the Resource Bargain; neither is framed as reciprocity.

The pattern is informative: VSM is strong on **structural/regulative primitives** (identity, boundary, signal, variety, authority, autonomy, recursion) and weak-to-silent on **relational/illocutionary primitives** (commitment, trust, care, reciprocity, intent). Memory and evidence are implicit-but-unnamed. This profile is consistent across Beer, Walker, Espejo & Reyes, and Pérez Ríos.

## §4 — Internal disagreements

**Recursion depth in practice.** Beer's theorem licenses infinite regress in both directions — cells up to nation and planet (Beer 1984, p. 14 [open-access]). Practitioners retreat. Walker's guide counsels that levels of recursion exist to "provide a means of looking at complexity in manageable chunks" (Walker §4, vsmg.lrc.org.uk [open-access]) and in practice works at two-to-four levels. Espejo's VIPLAN Method formalises the retreat: **recursion/functions tables** (Espejo, Bowling & Hoverstadt, *Kybernetes* 28(6/7), 1999 [paywalled]; Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 11 [paywalled]) require the analyst to *select criteria for unfolding* (geography, product line, technology) and to stop at levels where "decision making is devolved to the lowest practical level." Pérez Ríos (2012, pp. 84–140 [paywalled]) offers c. 200 diagnostic questions keyed to chosen recursion levels and warns that one of the most common pathologies in practice is *non-definition of the next lower level of recursion*. The disagreement is less doctrinal than practical: Beer's mathematics insists on identical topology at every level; practitioners admit a *weak recursion* where not every level carries every function with equal weight (Pérez Ríos 2025, *Systems* 13(9):749 [open-access], citing Schwaninger).

**S3–S4 balance / the 3-4 homeostat.** Beer treats the 3-4 homeostat as the system's hardest design problem: S3 lives in the "inside-and-now," S4 in the "outside-and-then," and S5 absorbs whatever residual variety their interaction fails to balance (*Heart of Enterprise*, 1979, ch. on the 3-4 homeostat [print]; Third Axiom, p. 298). The canonical pathologies cluster here: **operational overreach** (S3 starves S4 of attention and resource), **intelligence starvation** (S4 runs on "flights of imagination" without S3 feedback; Beer 1984, p. 16 [open-access]). Schwaninger & Scheef's empirical test (2016 [paywalled]) reported that respondents could not cleanly separate S3, S3\* and S4 constructs, suggesting the homeostat is conceptually clear but operationally fused in real organisations. Espejo & Reyes' response is to introduce a **cohesion mechanism** (comprising S1-S2-S3, "doing things well today") and an **adaptation mechanism** (S3-S4-S5, "ensuring existence in the future"), and to speak of their interplay rather than a single homeostat (Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 6 [paywalled]). Earlier Espejo publications used "monitoring-control mechanism" but, they report, changed the term because "control" carried "socially negative connotation" (Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 6 notes [paywalled]) — a lexical change that itself signals unresolved tension over how much directiveness the homeostat implies.

**Descriptive (diagnostic) vs. prescriptive (design) stance.** Beer straddled: *Brain of the Firm* presents VSM as both a model of how viable organisations *are* and a template for how they *should be* structured; *Diagnosing the System for Organizations* (1985 [institutional]) is explicitly diagnostic. Espejo pushes toward a **second-order-cybernetic, methodology-driven** reading: VIPLAN Method + VIPLAN Methodology emphasise the observer's role, stakeholder identity construction, and "meaning creation" — a movement from "functionalist first-order cybernetics" to self-construction (Espejo, 1997, quoted in Open University, "Managing Complexity," §5.7 [open-access]). Espejo insists VSM is a *reference model* not a blueprint, with VIPLAN supplying the learning loop that makes diagnosis provisional (Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 10–11 [paywalled]; Harwood, *JORS* 63(6), 2012, pp. 748–761 [paywalled]). Reyes (second-order auditing, 2001 [paywalled]) pushes further: the analyst is *inside* the system, and design judgments are participant-constituted. Leonard (*Systemic Practice and Action Research* 2009 [paywalled]) holds a more classical practitioner stance: VSM is a diagnostic instrument whose prescriptive force is constrained by cybernetic necessity. Pérez Ríos titles his book *Design AND Diagnosis* and offers both a 200-question diagnostic protocol and software (VSMod®) for design (Pérez Ríos 2012 [paywalled]). Hoverstadt's *Fractal Organization* (Wiley 2008 [print]) is the most frankly prescriptive. Jackson (*JMS* 25(6), 1988 [paywalled]) classifies VSM as *regulative not emancipatory*: the prescriptive reading is what makes it vulnerable to the charge of managerialism (§5).

**Relation to autopoiesis.** Beer's claim is strong: in his Preface to Maturana & Varela (1980, Reidel, pp. 63–72 [institutional]) he asserts "human societies are biological systems" and social institutions can satisfy Maturana–Varela's criteria. Maturana himself restricts autopoiesis to molecular/living unities with topological boundaries in physical space; social groupings are "third-order couplings" but not autopoietic unities (Maturana 1980, in Zeleny (ed.), *Autopoiesis*, pp. 45–80 [print]; Maturana & Varela, *The Tree of Knowledge*, 1987, p. 43ff [print]). **Varela retreats** explicitly: "the idea of autopoiesis is, by definition, restricted to relations of productions … and refers to topological boundaries. These two conditions are clearly unsatisfactory for other systems exhibiting autonomy" (Varela, *Principles of Biological Autonomy*, Elsevier North-Holland 1979, pp. 54–55 [print]) — he substitutes *organisational closure* as the weaker applicable construct. Mingers (*Self-Producing Systems*, Plenum 1995, ch. 8, pp. 119–152 [open-access PDF on Monoskop]) systematically argues Beer commits a category error: humans belong to multiple organisations; organisations do not *produce their human components* in the sense molecules produce a cell; social organisations lack physical-topological boundaries. Whitaker's *Encyclopaedia Autopoietica* (cybsoc.org, 1995–2001 [open-access]) is lexicographically strict. Espejo & Reyes (2011, ch. 2 [paywalled]) resolve the tension by redefining the components as *interactions / communications* rather than persons — a move that aligns VSM with Luhmann's autopoietic reinterpretation and away from Maturana's own position. The tradition has therefore fractured: Beer's strong reading is effectively abandoned by most recent VSM writers, but is not publicly disavowed.

## §5 — Critiques and limits

**Technocracy / managerialism.** Jackson's sustained critique places VSM in the functionalist–unitary cell of the System of Systems Methodologies: it "neglects the individual/psychological dimension," "assumes unitary goals and cannot handle pluralist conflict," and "ignores coercive contexts" (Jackson, *JMS* 25(6), 1988, pp. 557–573 [paywalled]; *Systems Methodology for the Management Sciences*, Plenum 1991, ch. 9 [print]; *Critical Systems Thinking*, Wiley 2019, ch. 11 [print]). **Ulrich's Habermasian critique** (Ulrich, *JASA* 8, 1981, pp. 33–59 [open-access, academia.edu]) is sharper: Cybersyn reduces practical reason to instrumental reason; cybernetic "viability" cannot by itself ground the legitimacy of the goals being served; VSM lacks boundary critique over *whose* interests define the system's purpose. Beer replied in *JASA* 10, 1983, pp. 115–119 [print], defending Cybersyn as designed *against* both capitalist technocracy and Soviet command-planning. The debate is unresolved; Lehto & Paniagua (*Economy and Society*, 2025 [paywalled]) argue "Beer failed to definitely separate" command-and-control from participatory aims. Espinosa (*SRBS* 42(1), 2025, pp. 171–188 [paywalled]) claims Espejo, Walker and recent practitioners have met Jackson's criticisms by coupling VSM with participatory methods; Jackson's 2019 book grants a partial softening but holds that core VSM remains structural-functionalist.

**Low empirical testability.** The evidence base is overwhelmingly case-study. Schwaninger & Scheef's *Cybernetics and Systems* 47(7), 2016, pp. 544–569 [paywalled] is the first large-n empirical test; they concede "we cannot and do not claim to 'prove' the model to be 'right,' because proof is a matter of the formal sciences only," and their factor analysis could not discriminate S3 from S3\*. Smith & Shaw (*EJOR* 274(2), 2018, pp. 403–416 [paywalled]) argue VSM is not even a Problem Structuring Method given its prescriptive model; Harwood (*EJOR* 2019 [paywalled]) contests this.

**Accessibility / training barrier.** The complaint is widespread and internal. Brocklesby, Cummings & Davies (*Long Range Planning* 29(1), 1995, pp. 49–57 [paywalled]) note that "many, not familiar with the model's cybernetic basis and terminology, have found Beer's original ideas difficult to follow." Walker's *Guide* (3rd edn. 2020 [open-access]), Hoverstadt's *Fractal Organization* (2008 [print]) and Schwaninger's *Intelligent Organizations* (Springer 2009 [print]) are all explicit popularisations, implicitly conceding the barrier. Schwaninger (1991) observed "implementing the VSM necessitates rethinking governance and requires considerable effort, which has led to expectations of failure." Jackson (2019, p. ~325 [print]) flags "difficult language, which creates substantial cognitive barriers."

**Overlap / tension with Soft Systems Methodology.** Checkland's early challenge was "Are organisations machines?" (*Futures*, Oct. 1980 [print]), answered: not in the way Beer's organismic analogy requires. Checkland's review of *Diagnosing the System* (*JORS* 37(8), 1986 [paywalled]) argues VSM imposes a structural template in place of inquiry into worldviews. From the VSM side, Espejo incorporates SSM-style stakeholder identification into VIPLAN (Espejo & Reyes 2011, ch. 11 [paywalled]); Espinosa & Walker (*A Complexity Approach to Sustainability*, World Scientific 2017 [print]) integrate VSM diagnosis with soft participatory workshops. Jackson & Flood's Total Systems Intervention (*Creative Problem Solving*, Wiley 1991 [print]) allocates VSM to unitary-complex and SSM to pluralist-complex contexts; the assignment is contested by VSM practitioners who claim the integrated methodologies can handle pluralism.

**Other limits.** Cardoso Castro & Espinosa (*Kybernetes* 2020, p. 288 [paywalled]) note "there are no known specific procedures to apply VSM diagnosis to address potential dynamics except by using analyst expertise" — i.e., VSM relies on tacit practitioner skill. Beer himself acknowledged Cybersyn "was not fast enough" (*Designing Freedom*, 1974 [open-access]), conceding an information-system scale limit. The **autonomy–cohesion dilemma** — "the metasystem must have enough authority to maintain cohesion, but not so much as to destroy the autonomy of its viable components" (*Heart of Enterprise*, ch. 14 [print]) — is acknowledged by Beer as unresolved and is the recurring practical dilemma in every VSM intervention (Espinosa, Harnden & Walker, *EJOR* 187(2), 2008, pp. 636–651 [paywalled]).
