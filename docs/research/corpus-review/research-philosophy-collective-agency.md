# The analytic tradition of collective agency, 1990–2020

This synthesis maps the Anglo-American analytic philosophy of collective agency against Spore's primitives (intent → commitment → evidence + signal + holon). The tradition is dominated by **a family quarrel over reducibility**: can groups that act together be fully accounted for in terms of individual minds, or does sociality introduce a sui generis element (joint commitment, we-mode, we-intention, group agent)? Every other disagreement — over obligation, language, authority, emotional sharing — plays out inside that axis. Trust, care, embodiment, and power are notably peripheral. The tradition's vocabulary maps cleanly onto Spore's **intent** and **commitment**, awkwardly onto **holon**, and barely at all onto **evidence** and **signal** as technical primitives.

---

## §1 — What this tradition treats as atomic / primitive

Candidates for primacy compete rather than compose. What one author treats as bedrock, another reduces.

**Joint commitment (Gilbert).** Gilbert's career-long claim is that sociality rests on a single non-decomposable phenomenon. In her paradigm example, two people walking together each offer "*his will to be part of a pool of wills which is dedicated, as one, to that goal*" ("Walking Together," *Midwest Studies in Philosophy* 15 [1990]: 7 [paywalled]). Joint commitment is "*a commitment of two or more people*" that is "*not a sum of personal commitments*" (*Joint Commitment*, OUP 2013, ch. 2 [print/institutional]). It is *primitive*: formed by each party openly expressing readiness under common knowledge (*On Social Facts*, Routledge 1989, §4.3–4.7 [print]), and rescindable only by concurrence. Its constitutive output is *directed* obligations and entitlements that are "*of a different kind*" from moral ones (Gilbert, "Shared Intention and Personal Intentions," *Philosophical Studies* 144 [2009]: 178 [institutional]).

**Shared intention as interlocking individual intentions (Bratman).** For Bratman, the primitive is ordinary individual planning agency; shared intention is *built*, not posited. His continuity thesis: "*the conceptual, metaphysical, and normative structures central to such modest sociality are … continuous with structures of individual planning agency*" (*Shared Agency*, OUP 2014: 8 [institutional]). The mechanism is two agents each intending "*that we J*," with "*meshing sub-plans*" (*Shared Agency* 2014: 53 [institutional]), under common knowledge and mutual responsiveness. "*Shared Cooperative Activity*" (*Philosophical Review* 101 [1992]: 327–341 [JSTOR, institutional]) fixes the three functional marks: mutual responsiveness, commitment to joint activity, commitment to supporting the others' efforts. Intention is primitive; "shared intention" is multiply realizable.

**We-intention as biologically primitive (Searle).** Searle's 1990 essay is emphatic: "*'we-intentions' … cannot be analyzed in terms of individual intentions*" ("Collective Intentions and Actions," in Cohen/Morgan/Pollack, *Intentions in Communication*, MIT Press 1990: 401 [institutional/print]). "*Collective intentionality is a biologically primitive phenomenon*" (*The Construction of Social Reality*, Free Press 1995: 24 [print]). Each participant carries a genuine we-form intention "*in the mind of each individual who is acting as part of the collective*" ("CIA" 1990: 414 [institutional]) — internalist metaphysics, anti-reductive semantics.

**We-mode (Tuomela).** Tuomela's primitive is a mode of thinking, not a content. "*To think or act in the we-mode is to think or act as a group member in a full sense, thus for a group reason*" (*The Philosophy of Sociality*, OUP 2007: 7 [institutional]). The we-mode is constituted by three conditions: the Collectivity Condition — "*a collective goal is satisfied for a participant if and only if it is satisfied for any other participant*" (*PoS* 2007: 10, Thesis 4 [institutional]); a group reason drawn from the group's *ethos*; and a collective commitment that is "*a central 'glue' in social life*" (*PoS* 2007: 13 [institutional]). "*We-mode concepts are not reducible to I-mode concepts*" (*PoS* 2007, preface [institutional]).

**Group agent as functional unit (Pettit/List).** List and Pettit treat *agenthood itself* as a functional kind: "*Agents are not defined in terms of the material properties they possess, but rather in terms of the functions they perform*" (*Group Agency*, OUP 2011: 19 [institutional; quoted in EJPE review, OA]). Once a collective satisfies the representation/motivation/action triad robustly, it is "*an agent in its own right with a mind of its own*" (*GA* 2011: 76–77 [institutional]). Group agency is not built from a new mental primitive but by *organizing* individual attitudes under an aggregation procedure.

**Individual intention.** Foundational for Bratman and for Ludwig; retained but insufficient for Searle, Gilbert, and Tuomela. Ludwig radicalizes the Bratmanian side: a we-intention is "*an intention of an individual … directed toward an action of his while … contributing to a collective action*" (*From Individual to Plural Agency*, OUP 2016: 201 [institutional; via NDPR]). Individual intentions differ from we-intentions "*not in their mode but in their content*" (OUP blurb / FIPA ch. 13 [institutional]).

**Commitment (distinct from intention?).** Yes, for Gilbert, Tuomela, and Searle; no, for Bratman and Ludwig. Gilbert insists joint commitment is not the sum of personal commitments; Tuomela treats collective commitment as a third, group-socially normative element atop intention and goal; Searle ties commitment to the illocutionary structure of assertion and declaration ("*When I make a statement I not only express a belief, but I also commit myself to its truth*," *Making the Social World*, OUP 2010: 81 [institutional]). Bratman treats what Gilbert calls commitment as *stability of intention under interdependence*; Ludwig reconstructs deontic features via conditional individual intentions plus tacit agreement.

**Trust.** Peripheral across the tradition. Not primitive in any of the six core authors. Gilbert mentions trust chiefly as an *effect* of joint commitment; Bratman's indices barely register it; Searle locates it in the Background as a pre-theoretical "*sense of the other as candidate for cooperative agency*" ("CIA" 1990: 414 [institutional]); Tuomela devotes a chapter to it (*PoS* 2007: ch. 7 [institutional]) but grounds it derivatively in collective commitment. The near-unanimous displacement of trust by commitment is one of the tradition's most characteristic moves — and its best-known external liability (see §5).

**Authority / standing.** Treated implicitly, not as a primitive. Gilbert derives standing from joint commitment (each party acquires "*demand-rights*" to rebuke the other — *Rights and Demands*, OUP 2018, ch. 1 [institutional]). Tuomela builds authority through "operative members" empowered to form we-mode attitudes on the group's behalf (*PoS* 2007: ch. 6, pp. 124–148 [institutional]). List/Pettit treat authorization as enacted organizational structure rather than as a distinct element (*GA* 2011: 33–35 [institutional]).

**Recognition / common knowledge.** Load-bearing, not primitive. Bratman requires common knowledge of interlocking intentions (*Shared Agency* 2014: 103 [institutional]). Gilbert requires openness of expression under common knowledge (*On Social Facts* 1989, ch. 4 [print]). Searle rejects iterated mutual belief as necessary — the we-form "*can exist even in a brain in a vat*" ("CIA" 1990: 406 [institutional]) — a sharp anti-cognitivist move within a cognitivist tradition.

## §2 — Layering structure

The tradition *does* stratify, but the strata are inconsistent across authors and there is no agreed substrate layer.

**Mental-state layer vs. action layer vs. institutional layer.** Searle is most explicit: collective intentionality (biological, pre-linguistic) grounds status functions ("*X counts as Y in context C*," *CSR* 1995: 28 [print]), which are "*linguistically constituted*" and carry deontic powers — "*rights, duties, obligations, requirements, permissions, authorizations, entitlements*" (*MSW* 2010: 9 [institutional]). Three levels: psychological primitive → collectively accepted status → institutional deontology. Language is a *stratifier*, separating basic cooperation from institutional reality.

**Individual mental states vs. collective mental states.** Gilbert, Tuomela, and List/Pettit insist on a distinct collective stratum. Gilbert's plural subject is "*not … a center of consciousness*" (Gilbert, "Joint Commitment: What It Is and Why It Matters," *Phenomenology and Mind* 9 [2015]: 19–20 [open-access]) but is nonetheless an irreducible social item. List/Pettit give the most explicit layering: group attitudes "*supervene*" on member contributions — "*fixing the A-facts also fixes the B-facts*" (*GA* 2011: 65 [institutional]) — yet "*cannot generally be a function of the members' individual judgments*" propositionwise (List & Pettit, "Group Agency and Supervenience," *Southern Journal of Philosophy* 2006 [open-access preprint, Princeton]). This is the canonical "supervenience without reducibility": the collective level is grounded but autonomous.

**Commitment vs. action vs. institutional standing.** Gilbert's commitment layer sits *between* psychology and institution: it is not a decision, not an action, but a normative transformation. Tuomela's ethos layer functions similarly — ethos "*glues the members together … gives the foundation for the unity and identity of the group*" (*PoS* 2007: 5 [institutional]) — a soft layer above attitudes but beneath institutional rule.

**Substrate layer.** Largely *absent*. The tradition does not theorize material, bodily, or infrastructural substrates on which collective agency runs. Searle's "Background" (*CSR* 1995: ch. 6 [print]) is the closest approximation — "*a certain category of neurophysiological causation*" — but it is unthematized and unconnected to embodiment or ecology in the senses care ethics or phenomenology would demand. Bratman's planning-agent substrate is cognitivist: intentions, sub-plans, common knowledge. No author deploys anything comparable to a protocol, medium, or channel as a primitive. This is one of the tradition's sharpest contrasts with engineering or systems-theoretic vocabularies.

## §3 — Placement of universal concepts

**Trust.** Not load-bearing. Derivative where present (Tuomela, *PoS* 2007: ch. 7); absent-by-choice in Bratman and Gilbert. The absence is *notable*: Baier's 1986 critique of contract-centric moral philosophy applies by extension to Gilbert's joint commitment and Bratman's mutual responsiveness, yet no core author in 1990–2020 treats trust as primitive.

**Care.** Absent. No core author thematizes care, dependency, or asymmetric relations. Paradigm examples across the tradition — painting a house, walking together, preparing hollandaise, singing a duet — select for voluntarist, roughly symmetric, adult planning agents. The absence is notable and is the chief target of feminist critique (§5).

**Identity.** Present but subordinate. List/Pettit treat "identifying with group agents" as a downstream normative achievement (*GA* 2011: ch. 9 [institutional]). Tuomela's ethos "*gives the foundation for the unity and identity of the group*" (*PoS* 2007: 5 [institutional]). No author treats identity as a primitive.

**Memory.** Absent as a technical primitive. Continuity of shared intention over time is handled by Bratman through intention stability and interdependence (*Shared Agency* 2014: ch. 4 [institutional]); Gilbert's concurrence condition substitutes for a memory mechanism. No continuity-through-persistence or institutional-memory theorizing.

**State.** Not a primitive as a systems concept. Intentional states are the unit. No author treats state-of-the-collective as a distinct variable.

**Signals.** Absent as a technical primitive. Communication enters only as open expression (Gilbert), mutual belief (Bratman), or symbolization/declaration (Searle, *MSW* 2010, ch. 5 [institutional]). Pettit's "conversable" groups in *Made with Words* (Princeton UP 2008 [institutional]) come closest: persons and groups are "*things that give and take commitments*," but signaling is subsumed under commitment.

**Agency.** Load-bearing everywhere; defined differently. Functionalist for List/Pettit; planning-theoretic for Bratman; intentionalist for Searle; normatively constituted for Gilbert; mode-based for Tuomela; logical-form-based for Ludwig. No consensus definition.

**Norms/protocols.** Load-bearing. Searle's constitutive rules ("*X counts as Y in C*"), Tuomela's r-norms and s-norms (*Importance of Us*, Stanford UP 1995 [print]), Gilbert's obligation-generating joint commitments, and List/Pettit's aggregation procedures all do norm-work. "Protocols" in the engineering sense are absent.

**Power/authority.** Underdeveloped. Tuomela handles it through operative members; List/Pettit through authorization; Gilbert derives standing from joint commitment. Power as asymmetric capacity is almost never thematized — one of the tradition's most criticized lacunae.

**Commitment.** Load-bearing and contested. Primitive for Gilbert and Tuomela; derivative for Bratman, Ludwig; tied to illocution for Searle; mechanism of group agency for List/Pettit.

**Intent.** Foundational. Divides the tradition according to which *kind* of intention is primitive.

**Evidence.** Absent as a technical primitive. The closest analogues: List/Pettit's truth-tracking results (*GA* 2011: ch. 4–5 [institutional]), Searle's "*epistemic objectivity of ontologically subjective facts*" (*CSR* 1995: 7–9 [print]). No author treats evidence as a unit of collective coordination.

**Holon-like-unit (whole-and-part).** Partially present. List/Pettit's group agent is holonic in effect: a supervenient whole with autonomous functional identity, composed of members, embeddable in larger collectives. Tuomela's ethos-bearing group members "*act as if they were intentionally functioning as parts of an organism*" (*Social Ontology*, OUP 2013: 34 [institutional]). Gilbert's plural subject is less holonic — she explicitly disclaims a supra-individual mind. Bratman's "Group Agents Without Group Subjects" (*Shared Agency* 2014: ch. 6 [institutional]) occupies middle ground. Ludwig rejects holonic structure outright: "*we do not need to accept genuine plural … agents into our ontology*" (*FIPA* 2016: 168 [institutional; via NDPR OA]).

**Boundary.** Present only as group membership. No formal theorization of boundaries as semi-permeable, scale-relative, or contested.

**Reciprocity.** Implicit in Bratman's mutual responsiveness and Gilbert's directed obligations, but never named as a primitive.

**Reproduction/continuity.** Absent. The tradition theorizes formation and persistence of shared intention but not reproduction of collectives across time or generations. Searle's "Background" has a reproductive flavor but is not developed.

## §4 — Internal disagreements

### Disagreement 1 — Gilbert vs. Bratman on whether joint commitment is sui generis or constructible

The flashpoint is whether mutual directed obligations are constitutive of shared activity. Gilbert: they are. When Sue walks with Jack and Jack surges ahead, Sue has *standing* to rebuke — and that standing is not reducible to individual expectations plus moral principles ("Walking Together," *MSP* 15 [1990]: 1–14 [paywalled]; *Joint Commitment* 2013, ch. 2 [institutional]). Bratman's counterexample: shared *immoral* activities (the two torturers, *Shared Agency* 2014: 111 [institutional]; the "pillager and lazy plunderer," *Faces of Intention*, CUP 1999: 132n6 [institutional]). Here there could be no obligation to do one's part, yet the activity is genuinely shared. Obligations are therefore "*typical concomitants*" explained by a Scanlonian expectation-principle, not constitutive (Bratman 1999: ch. 7; 2014: ch. 5). Gilbert's reply: such obligations are still present; they are "*of a different kind*" (2009: 178 [institutional]) — non-moral, directed, "contralateral" — so the immorality counterexamples miss the target. T. H. Smith's distinction sharpens the issue: Bratman gives *constitutive* sufficient conditions, Gilbert gives *causal* sufficient conditions (*Journal of Social Ontology* 1 [2015]: 49–57 [open-access]). The two accounts may be targeting different explananda.

A second layer of this disagreement concerns **unilateral withdrawal**. Gilbert's concurrence condition makes joint commitment non-rescindable by one party; Bratman, Ludwig, Tuomela, and Roth all find this too strong. Circularity is a recurring charge: the joint expression of readiness that forms a Gilbertian joint commitment may itself be a shared activity, inviting regress (Tuomela 1992; Tollefsen 2002; Schmid 2014 raises a parallel objection about communication presupposing joint commitment — *Plural Action*, Springer 2009 [institutional; paraphrased via Salice/Uhl OA]).

### Disagreement 2 — Searle vs. List (and vs. Tuomela) on pre-linguistic collective intentionality and its locus

Searle stakes out the pre-linguistic, internalist, biological-primitivist position: collective intentionality is evolutionarily basic and "*has to exist inside individuals' heads*" (*MSW* 2010: 44 [institutional]). Language is required only for the *institutional* stratum — status functions are "*linguistically constituted*" (*MSW* 2010: 62 [institutional]). Tuomela partly agrees that the we-mode predates language — "*the disposition to we-mode thinking and acting arguably is a stable feature based on culture-gene coevolution*" (*PoS* 2007: 9 [institutional]); rudimentary we-mode emerges "*as early as … the second year of life*" (*PoS* 2007: 6 [institutional]) — but denies Searle's brain-in-a-vat possibility because the Collectivity Condition requires genuine (not merely represented) "*standing or falling together*" (*PoS* 2007: 4 [institutional]).

List and Pettit occupy a different horn. They do not locate collective intentionality in a pre-linguistic substrate at all; group agency is a *structural* achievement, requiring aggregation procedures that effectively demand propositional articulation and hence language-like capacities. Pettit's *Made with Words* (2008) ties personhood to discursive capacity: "*Persons are things that give and take commitments*" (Made with Words, Princeton UP 2008, ch. 4 [institutional; via NDPR OA]). Group agency on this view cannot be pre-linguistic; the discursive dilemma presupposes propositions. Searle's primitivism and List/Pettit's constructivism thus cut across each other: Searle has a pre-linguistic primitive but no organizational theory; List/Pettit have an organizational theory but no pre-linguistic primitive.

### Disagreement 3 — Tuomela vs. Bratman on whether shared agency requires ethos / group reasons

Tuomela classifies Bratman's shared intention as *pro-group I-mode*: group-directed in content but not genuinely we-mode because it lacks group reasons drawn from an ethos and fails to satisfy the Collectivity Condition (*PoS* 2007: 3, Thesis 5 [institutional]). For Tuomela, authentic sociality requires that the group's constitutive goals/values/standards "*give motivating reasons for action*" (*PoS* 2007: 16 [institutional]) that override individual reasons — a top-down normative claim. Bratman's continuity thesis explicitly rejects this: no new normative resource is needed beyond individual planning norms plus mutual responsiveness (*Shared Agency* 2014: 8 [institutional]). This is the tradition's clearest disagreement about **authority**: does collective agency require a normatively prior ethos (Tuomela), or does it emerge bottom-up from interlocking plans (Bratman)?

### Disagreement 4 — List/Pettit's non-reducible group agents vs. Ludwig's singularist reduction

Both sides accept supervenience on individuals. They divide on what follows. List and Pettit argue that holistic (setwise, non-propositionwise) supervenience plus functional role-filling yields *real* agents — "*group agents are relatively autonomous entities — agents in their own right with minds of their own*" (*GA* 2011: 76–77 [institutional; via NDPR OA]) — and that the discursive dilemma shows propositionwise reduction fails (*GA* 2011: ch. 3 [institutional]). List extends this to free will: group agents can satisfy libertarian conditions (*Why Free Will Is Real*, Harvard UP 2019: ch. 7 [institutional]). Ludwig denies the inference from aggregation-failure to ontological realism. His logical-form analysis reveals only multiple agents of a single event: "*collective action … requires no group agents per se*" (OUP blurb, *FIPA* 2016 [institutional]). The we-intention is just "*an intention of an individual … contributing to a collective action*" (*FIPA* 2016: 201 [institutional]); mode is reduced to content. Ludwig extends this to institutions: in corporations "*nowhere will we find, and nowhere do we need … to invoke the agency of anything other than the individuals*" (*From Plural to Institutional Agency*, OUP 2017: 271 [institutional; via NDPR OA]).

### Disagreement 5 — Schmid's plural self-awareness against all the cognitivists

Schmid, a sympathetic internal critic, charges the entire mainstream with cognitive over-construction. "*The 'sense of us' is plural pre-reflective self-awareness*" (Schmid, "Plural Self-Awareness," *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences* 13 [2014]: 7 [paywalled; abstract OA]). It plays "*the same role in the constitution of a common mind that singular pre-reflective self-awareness plays in the individual mind*" (ibid. [paywalled]). The charge: Gilbert's joint commitment, Bratman's interlocking intentions, Searle's we-intention, and Tuomela's we-mode are all *reflective* structures that presuppose a more basic plural first-person experience they cannot themselves generate. Schmid's regress: Gilbertian communication of readiness "*presupposes a joint commitment, which, in turn, presupposes a joint commitment and so forth*" (Schmid 2009, paraphrased in Salice/Uhl [OA]).

## §5 — Critiques and limits

**Feminist and care-ethics critique.** Baier's "Trust and Antitrust" (*Ethics* 96 [1986]: 231–260 [JSTOR, paywalled]) predates but powerfully prefigures the objection: contract/commitment-centric moral theory treats relations "*as simply cases of comembership in a kingdom of ends, in the given temporary conditions of one-sided dependence*" (p. 249 [paywalled]), ignoring the asymmetric vulnerability that characterizes trust — "*vulnerability to not yet noticed harm, or to disguised ill will*" (p. 239 [paywalled]). Held extends this: "*where other moral theories demand impartiality above all, the ethics of care understands the moral import of our ties to our families and groups*" (*The Ethics of Care*, OUP 2006: 10 [institutional]). Mackenzie and Stoljar's *Relational Autonomy* (OUP 2000 [institutional]) targets the individualist picture — autonomy as "*substantive independence and self-reliance, social isolation, and hyper-rationality*" — that underwrites Gilbert's equal-willed contracting parties and Bratman's symmetric planners. The charge lands: Gilbert's joint commitment is modeled on mutual quasi-promising; Bratman's paradigms select for roughly equal planning agents whose sub-plans mesh. Care relations, dependency, affect, and embodiment are absent. A *direct* book-length care-ethical critique of Gilbert or Bratman has not been published — a minor gap in the literature.

**Phenomenological critique.** Zahavi accuses the analytic tradition of fixating on joint action while ignoring emotional sharing: "*When surveying recent philosophical work on … we-intentions, it is striking how much effort is spent on analysing the structure of joint action*" and "*much less work has been devoted to … shared affects and emotions*" ("You, Me, and We," *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 22 [2015]: 84 [paywalled]). "*Emotional sharing … is developmentally prior to and logically more basic than joint action*" (ibid., p. 84 [paywalled]). Bratman, Gilbert, Tuomela "*operate with too simplistic a conception of what it means to be a self and misunderstand what it means to be (part of) a we*" (Zahavi, "We in Me or Me in We?," *Journal of Social Ontology* 7 [2021]: 1 [open-access]). Szanto and Moran's *Phenomenology of Sociality* (Routledge 2016 [institutional]) consolidates the case that pre-reflective we-experience is prior to we-intention.

**Interpretivist critique.** Tollefsen occupies a realist-but-deflationary middle ground. "*Propositional attitudes are not internal states of a system but dispositional states of whole systems*" (*Groups as Agents*, Polity 2015: 110 [institutional; via *Philosophical Studies* OA]). Group agency exists where the intentional stance is explanatorily successful; no internalist architecture required. This sidesteps the Gilbert/Bratman/Searle contest over what *inner* structure constitutes sharedness.

**Sociological critique.** Archer's critical realism charges the tradition with elisionism. "*Structure and agency constitute different levels of stratified social reality, each possesses distinctive emergent properties which are real and causally efficacious but irreducible to one another*" (*Realist Social Theory*, CUP 1995: Introduction [institutional; Cambridge Core abstract OA]). Searle's derivation of institutions from collective acceptance strikes realists as a central conflation that "*holds the two to be mutually constitutive and thus precludes any examination of their interplay by eliding them*" (*RST* 1995: 93ff. [institutional]).

**Non-Western critique.** The direct engagement is essentially absent. Metz's Ubuntu ethics (e.g., "Ubuntu as a Moral Theory," *African Human Rights Law Journal* 11 [2011] [open-access]) and Ames's Confucian role ethics (*Confucian Role Ethics*, Hawai'i 2011 [print/institutional]) develop relational, constitutive-of-persons accounts of sociality that would naturally collide with Gilbert/Bratman's voluntarist contractualism — but no published paper confronts the core analytic theorists by name. **This is a genuine gap in the comparative literature.**

**Aggregate indictment.** Taken together, the external critics converge on the charge that the tradition is (i) *over-cognitivist* — intellectualizing what is pre-reflective, affective, embodied; (ii) *contractualist-in-disguise* — modeling sociality on roughly symmetric quasi-promising; (iii) *trust-deflationary* — displacing trust by commitment; (iv) *Eurocentric by selection* — taking adult, voluntary, symmetric planners as paradigm and ignoring kinship, ritual, and constitutive relationality; (v) *inattentive to power, embodiment, and material substrate*. The tradition's internal disagreements are sharp but narrow; external critics note that this very narrowness is made possible by shared assumptions the internal disputes never touch.

## R-claims

- **R1**: The field is organized around whether collective agency reduces to individual planning states or requires an irreducibly collective stratum. [target:candidate:reducibility-debate] [concept:collective-agency]
*R1 is supported by Gilbert, Bratman, Searle, Tuomela, List & Pettit, and Ludwig.*

- **R2**: Joint commitment, shared intention, we-intention, we-mode, and group agency are rival primitives rather than one settled vocabulary. [target:candidate:joint-commitment-primitive] [concept:commitment]
*R2 is supported by Gilbert, Bratman, Searle, Tuomela, and List & Pettit.*

- **R3**: Commitment is far more central than trust in the tradition's core accounts, with trust usually treated as derivative or peripheral. [target:candidate:trust-derivation] [concept:commitment]
*R3 is supported by Gilbert, Tuomela, and Baier's critique of the tradition.*

- **R4**: Holon-like group wholes are only partially supported here because some authors defend group agents or plural subjects while singularists reject them. [target:candidate:holon-primitive] [concept:holon]
*R4 is supported by List & Pettit, Tuomela, Gilbert, and Ludwig.*

- **R5**: The tradition's paradigm cases assume voluntary, roughly symmetric planners and therefore under-theorize care, dependency, embodiment, and power. [target:candidate:care-gap] [concept:care]
*R5 is supported by Held, Baier, and Zahavi.*

- **R6**: External phenomenological and realist critiques argue that the tradition's internal quarrels are narrower than the social reality it claims to explain. [target:meta:corpus-foundational-review] [concept:collective-agency]
*R6 is supported by Zahavi, Archer, and Tollefsen.*
