# Commons governance and commoning: primitives, layers, silences

The commons-governance tradition is internally bifurcated: the **Bloomington School** (Ostrom et al.) treats commons as a *resource-plus-rules* analytic object whose viability depends on empirically discoverable institutional features, while the **commoning school** (Linebaugh, Bollier/Helfrich, De Angelis, Federici, Caffentzis) treats commons as a *social practice and relation* that exists against or beyond capital. The two wings share vocabulary (commons, commoners, boundaries, reciprocity) but disagree on ontology, politics, and what counts as explanation. This synthesis reports the primitives, layers, silences, and disputes as the primary sources present them.

## §1 — Primitives as defined inside the tradition

**Common-pool resource (CPR).** Ostrom's original economic object is defined by two variables inherited from Vincent Ostrom and Charles Tiebout: **subtractability (rivalry) of use** and **difficulty of excluding beneficiaries**. A CPR is a resource system where one appropriator's withdrawal of resource units reduces units available to others, yet exclusion is costly (*Governing the Commons*, Ch. 2, pp. 30–33; Ostrom, Gardner & Walker 1994, *Rules, Games, and Common-Pool Resources*, Ch. 1). Ostrom insists the resource *system* (the fishery, forest, irrigation canal) must be distinguished from resource *units* (the fish, trees, water delivered). This two-level structure — system vs. units — is the first latent holon-like construct in the tradition, though Ostrom never names it as such. She also separates the good's economic nature from the **property regime** governing it: *res communis*, *res publicae*, *res privatae*, *res nullius* are regimes, not goods (Hess & Ostrom 2003, "Artifacts, Facilities, and Content," *Law & Contemporary Problems* 63).

**Boundary (Principle 1).** Table 3.1 of *Governing the Commons* (p. 90) states: "**Clearly defined boundaries**: Individuals or households who have rights to withdraw resource units from the CPR must be clearly defined, as must the boundaries of the CPR itself." Boundary here is *double*: a **social membership boundary** (who is an appropriator) and a **physical/ecological boundary** (what the resource system includes). Cox, Arnold & Villamayor-Tomás (2010, *Ecology & Society* 15(4): 38, §Results) argue on the basis of 91 case studies that this principle should be split into **1A (user boundaries)** and **1B (resource boundaries)** because the empirical support differs. This split is itself now canonical in the literature — an example of a Spore-relevant primitive that the tradition has *refined under empirical pressure*.

**Rule-in-use vs. rule-in-form.** Ostrom distinguishes *rules-in-form* (formal written rules, statutes, charters) from *rules-in-use* (the "working rules… to which participants would refer if asked to explain and justify their actions," *Governing the Commons* Ch. 2; *Understanding Institutional Diversity* 2005, Ch. 1, pp. 18–20). Compliance is always assessed against rules-in-use; a formal rule that is not the working rule has no causal standing in the IAD framework. McGinnis (2011, *Policy Studies Journal* 39(1): 163–177, §II) treats this as the pivotal methodological commitment of the Bloomington School: institutions are observed behavioral regularities with normative backing, not texts.

**Operational / collective-choice / constitutional levels.** Kiser & Ostrom (1982, "The Three Worlds of Action," in *Strategies of Political Inquiry*) introduce three nested rule levels: **operational** rules govern day-to-day appropriation, provision, monitoring, enforcement; **collective-choice** rules govern how operational rules are made, modified, enforced — i.e. who has authority to change what; **constitutional** rules govern who is eligible to participate at the collective-choice level. Ostrom (2005, *UID*, Ch. 2, pp. 58–62, Fig. 2.4) emphasizes that action at each level is itself an *action situation*, so the levels are recursive rather than strictly hierarchical.

**The seven rule types inside an action situation.** *Understanding Institutional Diversity* (2005, Ch. 7, pp. 186–215) decomposes any rule configuration into **boundary/entry rules** (who participates), **position rules** (what roles exist), **choice rules** (which actions are permitted from a position), **aggregation rules** (how individual choices combine into collective outcomes, e.g. majority, consensus), **information rules** (what is visible to whom), **payoff rules** (what costs/rewards attach), **scope rules** (what outcomes/states are permissible). This seven-part decomposition is the richest formal primitive set in the tradition and directly maps to Spore's operational concerns (see §3 placements below).

**Action arena / action situation / participant.** The *action arena* is the site where interaction occurs; it contains an **action situation** (the choice structure) and **participants** occupying positions (Ostrom 2005, pp. 32–68). The arena is bounded by exogenous variables (biophysical attributes, community attributes, rules-in-use). Participants are modeled under a "second-generation" theory of rationality grounded not in strict maximization but in learned reciprocity, trust, and reputation (Ostrom 1998, *APSR* 92(1): 1–22, §III).

**Appropriator, provider, producer.** Ostrom distinguishes the **appropriator** (withdraws resource units), **provider** (arranges provision of the resource system, e.g. dam maintenance), and **producer** (physically constructs or repairs) (*Governing the Commons*, Ch. 2, pp. 30–32). The same person can occupy multiple roles; the distinction matters because sustainability requires rules addressing provision, not just appropriation.

**Monitoring (Principle 4).** Table 3.1: "**Monitors, who actively audit CPR conditions and appropriator behavior, are accountable to the appropriators or are the appropriators.**" Two claims are load-bearing: monitoring is *endogenous* (by or for appropriators), and it covers both resource state and behavior — closer to Spore's "evidence" primitive than "signal."

**Graduated sanctions (Principle 5).** Table 3.1: "**Appropriators who violate operational rules are likely to be assessed graduated sanctions (depending on the seriousness and context of the offense) by other appropriators, by officials accountable to these appropriators, or by both.**" The escalation property and its peer-enforcement are both essential; Cox et al. (2010) find this principle one of the best supported empirically.

**Conflict-resolution mechanism (Principle 6).** "**Appropriators and their officials have rapid access to low-cost local arenas to resolve conflicts among appropriators or between appropriators and officials.**" Low-cost and rapid are the operative qualifiers — expensive, remote courts do not satisfy Principle 6.

**Nested enterprise (Principle 8).** "**For CPRs that are part of larger systems: Appropriation, provision, monitoring, enforcement, conflict resolution, and governance activities are organized in multiple layers of nested enterprises.**" This principle, added only for larger systems, is Ostrom's formal link between the small-group CPR and the polycentric order. It is also the principle under which Spore's "holon" maps most cleanly — but note that Ostrom's nesting is across *activities/functions* (each activity can be organized at its own scale), not across parts-wholes of a single entity.

**Polycentricity.** The term originates with V. Ostrom, Tiebout & Warren (1961, "The Organization of Government in Metropolitan Areas," *APSR* 55(4): 831–842): "many centers of decision-making which are formally independent of each other" that through contracting, competition, and cooperation function "in a coherent manner with consistent and predictable patterns of interacting behavior." Aligica & Tarko (2012, "Polycentricity: From Polanyi to Ostrom, and Beyond," *Governance* 25(2): 237–262) distill three necessary features: (1) **multiplicity of decision centers with independent prerogatives**, (2) an **overarching system of rules** (often constitutional) that constitutes them, (3) **spontaneous order emerging from competition and cooperation**. McGinnis (2011, §IV.4, citing V. Ostrom 1972 in McGinnis 1999b p. 69) reproduces V. Ostrom's criterion that efficiency of polycentric systems depends on correspondence between government units and scales of effects.

**Commoning (as verb).** Peter Linebaugh (*The Magna Carta Manifesto: Liberties and Commons for All*, 2008, UC Press, Ch. 1, esp. p. 45) famously insists: **"there is no commons without commoning."** The commons is not a resource but an *activity of reciprocal use*. Linebaugh recovers the verb from medieval English sources. Bollier & Helfrich pick this up and radicalize it: in *Free, Fair and Alive* (2019, New Society Publishers; Ch. 1 "Commons and Commoning," ~pp. 21–45; Ch. 2 "The OntoShift to the Commons"), the commons is "a social system" and a "living social process," not an inventory of resources. Their **Triad of Commoning** (Part II intro, ~pp. 95–110; chs. 4–6) comprises **Social Life**, **Peer Governance**, and **Provisioning**, each a set of "patterns" in Christopher Alexander's sense rather than design principles in Ostrom's sense.

**Subsidiarity.** In the Bloomington School, subsidiarity is not a stand-alone primitive but a *corollary* of nested enterprise and polycentricity: functions should be handled at the smallest scale at which the externalities are internalized (McGinnis 2011, §IV.4; Marshall 2008, "Nesting, subsidiarity, and community-based environmental governance beyond the local level," *IJC* 2(1)). Bollier & Helfrich (2019, Ch. 9, "State Power and Commoning," section "Commons and Subsidiarity") adopt the term but stretch it: for them subsidiarity is paired with "**subsidiarity + relationality**," a normative commitment that higher-scale units *enable* rather than absorb lower-scale commoning.

## §2 — Layering

The Ostrom-school justifies layering on three grounds. First, **epistemic**: institutional complexity can be decomposed into analytically tractable levels only by separating operational play from the rules that configure it. Second, **empirical**: the 91-case meta-review (Cox et al. 2010) finds that CPRs failing at the constitutional level (lack of Principle 7, minimal rights to organize) fail regardless of operational sophistication. Third, **design-theoretic**: layering supports the principle that rules made *closest to* the operational setting track rules-in-use better than rules imposed from outside.

Layers are formally nested but **porous in practice**. Ostrom (2005, pp. 58–62) stresses that the "same" individual may simultaneously act at all three levels across a single day — a village assembly meeting is a collective-choice action situation whose members were constituted by constitutional rules and whose output is an operational rule. The **Social-Ecological Systems (SES) framework** (Ostrom 2007, *PNAS* 104(39): 15181–87; Ostrom 2009, *Science* 325(5939): 419–422) adds a parallel layering on the ecological side: resource system → resource units; governance system → actors; each decomposed into dozens of second-tier variables. McGinnis (2011, §V) notes that reconciling IAD's rule-layered structure with SES's attribute-layered structure is unfinished; Cole, Epstein & McGinnis (2014, "Toward a New Institutional Analysis of Social-Ecological Systems") is an explicit bridging attempt.

**Layering breaks down** at three seams. (a) The **rule vs. norm seam**: Crawford & Ostrom (1995, "A Grammar of Institutions," *APSR* 89(3): 582–600) distinguish strategies/norms/rules via the ADICO syntax (Attributes, Deontic, aIm, Conditions, Or else), yet norms without "or else" blur into rules-in-use if sanctions emerge informally. (b) The **polycentric scale seam**: Araral (2014, "Ostrom, Hardin and the commons," *Environmental Science & Policy*) argues the framework works cleanly only for small-N, stationary, observable resources; layering becomes fictive at global scale. (c) The **commoning/rule seam**: Bollier & Helfrich reject the implicit rule-centric ontology of IAD; their "patterns" are not rules in Ostrom's sense but dispositional tendencies, and they explicitly refuse the operational/collective-choice/constitutional stratification as importing a statist governance grammar (*Free, Fair and Alive*, Ch. 2 "OntoShift," §"Complexity Science and Commoning").

## §3 — Placement of universal concepts

**Trust.** Load-bearing and explicit in the behavioral branch. Ostrom's 1997 APSR presidential address (1998, §V) places **reciprocity, reputation, and trust** at "the core of an empirically tested, behavioral theory of collective action" and formalizes them as a positive feedback loop. Bollier & Helfrich treat trust as a *pattern* ("Honor Transparency in a Sphere of Trust," Ch. 5).

**Care.** Absent in the Bloomington School as a theoretical primitive. Central in the commoning school: Bollier & Helfrich coin **"care-wealth"** to replace "resources" (2019, Ch. 3 Glossary; Ch. 2); Federici makes **reproductive/care labor** the missing ground of all commons (*Re-enchanting the World*, 2018, PM Press, Part 2, essays "Feminism and the Politics of the Commons in an Era of Primitive Accumulation" and "From Crisis to Commons: Reproductive Work, Affective Labor and Technology").

**Identity.** Not theorized as such in Ostrom. Commoning school centers it: the "commoner" is constituted through the practice of commoning, not pre-given (Linebaugh 2008, Introduction; De Angelis 2017, *Omnia Sunt Communia*, Zed Books, Ch. 1 on the commons-system triad "commonwealth + commoners + commoning"). Bollier & Helfrich's **"Nested-I"** (2019, Ch. 2, §"The Nested-I and Ubuntu Rationality") is an explicit relational theory of identity.

**Memory.** Silent. Neither school thematizes institutional memory as a distinct primitive, though Ostrom acknowledges that long-enduring CPRs embody "knowledge accumulated across generations" (*Governing the Commons*, Ch. 3). The silence is diagnostic: Ostrom's cases are typically chosen for their longevity (Torbel ~1224; Huerta of Valencia ~15th c.) yet memory as an object of institutional design is not primitized.

**State.** Two senses. As **nation-state**, present in both wings with opposite valences. Ostrom treats the state as one node in a polycentric order — potentially complementary to commons, neither necessary nor sufficient (*Governing the Commons* Ch. 1; Ostrom 2010, *AER* 100(3): 641–672, Nobel lecture). Caffentzis and Federici treat state-market as an integrated adversary; Caffentzis & Federici (2014, "Commons against and beyond capitalism," *Community Development Journal* 49 suppl_1: i92–i105) argue commons are co-opted when they substitute for retreating welfare states. As **system state** (dynamics of the CPR), the SES framework explicitly tracks resource-system state variables (Ostrom 2009 *Science*, Table 1) but the IAD framework proper does not carry a formal "state" object; "outcomes" are the closest surrogate.

**Signals.** The tradition has **information rules** (Ostrom 2005, p. 206) governing what is visible to whom, but signaling as a separate communicative primitive is absent. Ostrom 1998 (§V) notes face-to-face communication "consistently enhances cooperation" but does not decompose communication into signal types.

**Agency.** Methodological individualism lightly held. Ostrom 1998 rejects strict rational-actor models in favor of boundedly rational, learning agents; action situations treat participants as decision-makers with beliefs, information, and strategies. Commoning school rejects the individualist baseline outright — agency is relational (Bollier & Helfrich's Ubuntu Rationality: "I am because we are").

**Norms vs. protocols.** Norms are core: Crawford & Ostrom 1995 distinguishes shared normative judgments ("delta parameters" = internalized costs/benefits of conformity) from strategies and rules. "Protocol" is not a term of art in the tradition; the closest equivalents are *rules-in-use* plus *choice rules* + *aggregation rules*.

**Power / authority.** Thinly theorized in Ostrom — a recurrent critique. Agrawal (2003, "Sustainable Governance of Common-Pool Resources," *Annual Review of Anthropology* 32: 243–262) and Cox et al. (2010, §Discussion) observe that "structural attributes" in the 8 principles "do not expose processes like trust, legitimacy, and transparency" or power asymmetries. De Angelis (2017, Ch. 3) and Federici (2018, "Feminism and the Politics of the Commons") treat power as constitutive; Ostrom's framework registers power only indirectly via position rules and aggregation rules.

**Commitment.** Surfaces in *Governing the Commons* Ch. 2 (pp. 42–45) as "**credible commitment**" — the problem of appropriators binding themselves to future compliance. Mechanism: monitoring + graduated sanctions make compliance *self-interestedly credible*. This is closer to Spore's "commitment" than any other primitive in the tradition, but it is *mechanism-dependent credibility*, not a standalone primitive.

**Intent.** Absent as a theorized primitive. Ostrom's behavioral theory uses "preferences," "beliefs," "strategies"; intent per se is not lifted out. Crawford & Ostrom's ADICO includes "**aIm**" (the action the institutional statement is about), but the aIm is a property of the rule, not of the actor's mental state. The silence is notable: commons theory has sophisticated vocabulary for what people *do* and what *rules prescribe* but not for what they *mean to do*.

**Evidence.** Not primitized. **Monitoring** functions adjacent to evidence — auditing CPR conditions and appropriator behavior generates the informational substrate for sanctions and rule-adjustment. Information rules govern flow. But "evidence" as a truth-bearing artifact linking intent/commitment to subsequent judgment is absent.

**Holon-like unit (whole-and-part).** No explicit primitive. The closest latent holon is the **nested enterprise** (Principle 8) — a whole-that-is-part-of-larger-wholes in the polycentric order. The SES framework's resource system / resource unit distinction is a second latent holon. But Koestler's term and the formal whole/part symmetry do not appear in the literature.

**Boundary.** Explicit and double, as discussed in §1: social and ecological. Bollier & Helfrich reinterpret boundary as "**semi-permeable membrane**" (2019, Ch. 5, "Create Semi-permeable Membranes") — a deliberate softening of the Ostromite "clearly defined" formulation to allow the commons to remain porous to neighbors, newcomers, and nature.

**Reciprocity.** Central. Ostrom 1998 makes it one of three behavioral cores; Oakerson (1993, "Reciprocity: A Bottom-Up View of Political Development") treats reciprocity as the generative principle of polity. Bollier & Helfrich: "**Practice Gentle Reciprocity**" (2019, Ch. 4). Federici (2018, p. 95) defines commons as "constituted on the basis of social cooperation, relations of reciprocity, and responsibility for the reproduction of the shared wealth."

**Reproduction / continuity.** Load-bearing in the feminist-autonomist wing, marginal in Ostrom. Federici (*Re-enchanting the World*, 2018, esp. "Feminism and the Politics of the Commons") argues reproductive labor is the *invisible ground* of every commons; Ostrom's long-enduring CPRs survive because reproductive work (childcare, food preparation, teaching rule-in-use) is performed but unmeasured. De Angelis (2017, Ch. 4–5) treats commons as **autopoietic social systems** whose continuity is self-reproduction. In IAD proper, continuity is an *outcome* (CPR robustness) but not a primitive.

## §4 — Internal disagreements

**The 8 principles: necessary, sufficient, or diagnostic?** Ostrom explicitly pushed back on "blueprint" readings. *Understanding Institutional Diversity* (2005, Ch. 9) warns against treating the principles as a design recipe; the 2007 PNAS "A Diagnostic Approach for Going Beyond Panaceas" (104(39): 15181–87) is a sustained argument that no single set of prescriptions fits all social-ecological contexts. In her Nobel lecture (2010, *AER* 100(3): 641–672, §V) she repeats that the principles are **empirical regularities** from successful cases, not sufficient conditions for success. Cox, Arnold & Villamayor-Tomás (2010) find the principles "well supported empirically" across 91 cases but split Principles 1, 2, and 4 into sub-principles, implicitly arguing the original eight were under-specified. Critics (Agrawal 2003; Cleaver 2012) push further: the principles describe *what robust CPRs have* but not *how they came to have it*, a generative vs. structural gap. The best reading is that Ostrom's principles are **diagnostic markers**, not a design blueprint — and that the tradition's frequent use of them as a checklist contradicts her own epistemology.

**Polycentricity vs. decentralization.** Not the same. Aligica & Tarko (2012, §III) argue decentralization = devolution of authority from higher to lower units within a single hierarchy; polycentricity = *multiple independent* decision centers whose relations are horizontal (competition, contracting, cooperation) and whose coordination is emergent under an overarching constitutional rule system. A decentralized system has one apex; a polycentric system has no apex. McGinnis (2011, §IV.4) notes that in practice most real orders are mixed — quasi-polycentric, quasi-hierarchical. The tradition has not resolved whether polycentricity is a *structural type* (Aligica/Tarko), a *normative aspiration* (Bollier/Helfrich), or a *descriptive scale term* (much applied literature uses it loosely as "multi-level").

**Commons-as-resource vs. commons-as-practice.** This is the tradition's deepest rift. The early Ostromian commons is an economic object with specified attributes (subtractable, non-excludable). Linebaugh's commoning (2008) and Bollier & Helfrich's "OntoShift" (2019, Ch. 2) reject the object-ontology: the commons *is* the practice of commoning; **"there is no commons without commoning."** De Angelis (2017, Ch. 1) splits the difference by defining a commons as a **social system** with three elements — commonwealth (the pooled stuff), commoners (the subject), and commoning (the activity) — attempting to hold resource and practice together. The resource wing risks reifying what are living relations; the practice wing risks losing analytic purchase on why some commons persist and others collapse.

**Market/state relationship: complementary or alternative?** Ostrom's Nobel lecture (2010, §I and §VII) is explicit: commons are **one of many institutional arrangements** that can, depending on context, outperform pure market or state provision; the three are complementary. Caffentzis ("A Tale of Two Conferences," 2004/2012, *Borderlands* 11(2): 1–32) labels this the "**neo-Hardinite**" or "pro-capitalist" use of commons, where commons are tools for repairing market failure. Against this, the "**anti-capitalist commons**" position (Caffentzis, Federici, De Angelis) insists commons are the seed of a post-capitalist society and are co-opted when they merely subsidize reproduction-work that the retreating state has abandoned (Caffentzis & Federici 2014, *CDJ* 49 suppl_1: i92–i105). Harvey (2012, *Rebel Cities*, Ch. 3) makes a similar point: commons at one scale can serve enclosure at another.

**Digital/knowledge commons same or different.** Hess & Ostrom (*Understanding Knowledge as a Commons*, 2007, MIT Press, Ch. 1, esp. pp. 4–11) extend the IAD framework to knowledge but note a core disanalogy: knowledge is **non-subtractable** (one person's use does not diminish another's) and often **anti-rival** (more use increases value, as in languages and software). Benkler (*The Wealth of Networks*, 2006, Yale UP, Ch. 3) develops **commons-based peer production** around this anti-rivalry and models it as a third mode alongside firms and markets. The contradiction: if the original CPR concept is defined by subtractability, and knowledge lacks subtractability, then "knowledge commons" is either a category extension that dilutes the CPR concept or a demonstration that "commons" was always about the property regime, not the good. Frischmann, Madison & Strandburg (2014, *Governing Knowledge Commons*, OUP, Ch. 1) explicitly adapt IAD by dropping subtractability and adding "obstacles" that the commons addresses — a significant theoretical concession. The commoning school welcomes the extension (all commons are relational anyway); the economic wing is more cautious.

## §5 — Critiques and limits

**Marxist / autonomist critiques.** Caffentzis ("A Tale of Two Conferences") and Federici & Caffentzis (2014) argue Ostrom-school commons are compatible with — and sometimes functional to — capital accumulation: when the state withdraws welfare, local commons provide cheap social reproduction that lowers the cost of labor power. De Angelis (2017, esp. Ch. 10–11) argues commons must be organized as **boundary-commoning networks** capable of reproducing themselves *against* value-form logic, or they become auxiliaries of capital.

**Feminist critiques.** Federici (*Caliban and the Witch*, 2004; *Re-enchanting the World*, 2018, Part Two, esp. "Feminism and the Politics of the Commons in an Era of Primitive Accumulation," pp. 85–110) argues the enclosure of the commons was simultaneously an enclosure of women's reproductive autonomy, and that any commons theory silent on reproduction reproduces that enclosure. Ostrom's cases tend to be male-dominated (irrigation officers, fishers, pastoralists); the household-level reproductive work that sustains these institutions is uncounted. Federici's position is not that Ostrom is wrong but that her frame is analytically incomplete — "the commons should not be understood as happy islands in a sea of exploitative relations."

**Decolonial critiques.** Agrawal (2003, *Annual Review of Anthropology*) argues Ostrom-school case selection and principle formulation universalize Western property-and-rule categories; Indigenous commons often do not operate through explicit rules-in-form or graduated sanctions but through kinship, cosmology, and obligation that the IAD framework mistranslates as "norms." Forsyth & Johnson (2014, "Elinor Ostrom's Legacy: Governing the Commons and the Rational Choice Controversy," *Development & Change* 45(5): 1093–1110) develop this: Ostrom's commitment to rational-choice individualism, even softened, is a particular social ontology masquerading as method. Bollier & Helfrich's appeal to Ubuntu is partly a decolonial move but is itself accused of appropriation.

**Scalability critiques.** Stern (2011, "Design Principles for Global Commons," *International Journal of the Commons* 5(2): 213–232) systematically compares local CPRs — bounded at 10²–10³ km², tens-to-thousands of appropriators, shared interests — to global commons (climate, oceans, atmosphere) and finds that the six characteristics underwriting the eight principles **do not hold at global scale**. Araral (2014, "Ostrom, Hardin and the commons," *Environmental Science & Policy* 36: 11–23) concludes that Ostrom's critique of Hardin is valid for small-scale commons and Hardin may be right for large, unbounded, non-observable commons. Ostrom's own 2010 "Polycentric Systems for Coping with Collective Action and Global Environmental Change" (*Global Environmental Change* 20(4): 550–557) is her attempt to answer by scaling *polycentricity* rather than the principles — addressing climate via many partially overlapping experiments rather than a global regime. Whether this answer succeeds is contested.

## Closing note on silences as diagnostic data

Three Spore primitives are load-bearing yet receive no first-class treatment in this tradition: **intent** (the mental/propositional stance that precedes commitment), **evidence** (the truth-bearing artifact that couples monitoring to judgment), and **holon-like unit** (whole-and-part as a formal symmetry). The tradition substitutes for intent with *strategies* and *preferences*; for evidence with *monitoring* + *information rules*; for holon with *nested enterprise* + *resource-system/resource-unit*. These substitutions succeed for analyzing stable CPRs but leave the tradition unable to speak clearly about (a) what a commitment *means* independent of its enforcement mechanism, (b) how evidence circulates as an object separate from the act of monitoring, and (c) part-whole recursion outside the specific frame of polycentric governance. Meanwhile, the tradition's strongest primitives — **rules-in-use**, the seven rule types, graduated sanctions, polycentricity, commoning-as-verb — are all ones Spore does not currently name, and the tradition's internal rift between resource-ontology and practice-ontology is itself a structural question Spore's primitive list does not yet decide.
