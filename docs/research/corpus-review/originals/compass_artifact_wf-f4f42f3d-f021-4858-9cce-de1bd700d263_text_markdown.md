# Autopoiesis, enactivism and the 4E turn: a primary-source synthesis

**The enactive-extended tradition has become the most developed post-cognitivist alternative to computational-representational orthodoxy**, but it remains fractured by three unresolved splits: whether life is necessary for mind, whether cognition requires representational content, and whether an agent's boundary is fixed or mutable. Beginning with Maturana and Varela's 1972/1980 theory of autopoiesis, enriched by Thompson's 2007 phenomenological synthesis and Di Paolo's adaptivity amendment, the tradition now spans four roughly compatible programmes — autopoietic enactivism, sensorimotor enactivism, radical enactivism, and extended/predictive cognition — whose partial overlaps conceal deep disagreements about content, representation, and scale. This report traces the primary literature, maps where specific conceptual commitments are grounded (and where they are conspicuously underdeveloped), and identifies the live fault lines. The research shows strong biological and phenomenological foundations, a robust social extension via participatory sense-making, but comparatively thin elaboration of several concepts a reader may expect to find (trust, commitment, evidence, power) and persistent difficulty scaling from basic minds to linguistic and institutional cognition.

## Autopoiesis as ground: Maturana, Varela, and the adaptivity correction

**Autopoiesis was introduced by Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela as a definition of the living: an organisationally closed, self-producing network of component-producing processes that specifies its own boundary**, formalised in *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living* (Reidel 1980; originally *De Máquinas y Seres Vivos*, 1972). The canonical definition appears in the "Autopoiesis" essay at roughly pages 78–84 of the Reidel edition, accompanied by the thesis — stated in the editorial preface and developed through Part II — that **"living systems are cognitive systems, and living as a process is a process of cognition"** (Maturana's Introduction, reiterated throughout). That identity thesis, together with the concepts of *structural coupling* (system and medium co-vary without instructive information transfer) and *operational closure*, furnishes the philosophical skeleton of every subsequent enactive programme. The full Reidel text is on the Internet Archive and Monoskop (open PDF); Springer hosts the book behind a paywall.

The 1987 popularisation *The Tree of Knowledge* (Shambhala; rev. 1992 with a Varela afterword) distils the programme into two aphorisms — **"All doing is knowing, and all knowing is doing"** and "Everything said is said by someone" (Chapter 1) — and introduces *natural drift* as an alternative to adaptationist evolution plus *languaging* as a form of recurrent consensual coordination (Chapters 8–9). The book is available via Internet Archive (borrow-only) and Shambhala (print). What the popular work makes explicit is that Maturana and Varela's biology already contains an ethics: "we have only the world that we can bring forth with others."

**A widely accepted correction to the original framework came from Ezequiel Di Paolo's "Autopoiesis, Adaptivity, Teleology, Agency"** (*Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences* 4(4): 429–452, 2005; open PDF at ezequieldipaolo.net). Di Paolo argued that autopoiesis alone secures only conservation of organisation — a tautologous viability — and cannot ground sense-making, norms, or agency. **Adaptivity**, the capacity of a system to regulate itself *with respect to the boundaries of its own viability*, must be added. Evan Thompson accepted the amendment in *Mind in Life* (Harvard, 2007), stating in his *JCS* reply to commentators that "autopoiesis plus adaptivity is (i) necessary and sufficient for life, and (ii) sufficient for mind" (Thompson 2011, open PDF on evanthompson.me). This reworking is structurally important: it is what licences enactivists to speak of *normativity* at the biological level without invoking either design or external observers.

## Mind in Life and the deep-continuity thesis

Evan Thompson's *Mind in Life: Biology, Phenomenology, and the Sciences of Mind* (Harvard, 2007) is the central synthesis, weaving Maturana-Varela autopoiesis, Husserlian and Merleau-Pontian phenomenology, Hans Jonas's philosophy of the organism, and dynamical systems theory into a single argument for the **"deep continuity of life and mind"** (Ch. 3, "Autonomy and Emergence"; Ch. 5, "Autopoiesis: The Organization of the Living"; Ch. 6, "Life beyond the Gene"). Thompson's position is that cognition is a *kind of life* — specifically, the sense-making that adaptive autopoietic systems perform as they regulate their coupling with a world that matters to them. Concern, *Sorge* in Jonas's register, is woven into the biology via the concept of **precariousness**: the organism's metabolism is never finished, and its activity is normatively evaluable by reference to its own continued viability (Ch. 5, drawing on Jonas's *The Phenomenon of Life*, 1966). The book is in print and widely held in university libraries; no open full text exists, but Thompson's website posts precis and reply papers.

The concept of **dynamic co-emergence** (Ch. 3) replaces linear upward causation with reciprocal constitution between whole and part, and serves as Thompson's answer to the question of how a boundary can be self-specified without either hylomorphism or external observer-relative carving. Koestler's "holon" is not used; the enactive lexicon prefers co-emergence.

## Varieties of enactivism: sensorimotor, radical, autopoietic

Varela, Thompson and Rosch's *The Embodied Mind* (MIT Press, 1991; rev. ed. 2016 with a new foreword by Thompson and Di Paolo) introduced the term *enaction* in Chapter 8 (~pp. 172–180), along with the slogan **"laying down a path in walking"** borrowed from Antonio Machado. Color vision served as the book's showcase for cognition as "bringing forth" rather than recovering a pre-given world. The 2016 foreword (MIT Press, partial preview on Google Books) explicitly narrates how autopoietic and sensorimotor strands diverged after 1991.

**Alva Noë's *Action in Perception*** (MIT Press, 2004) develops the sensorimotor strand: perception is "not something that happens to us, or in us; it is something we do" (Preface), constituted by practical mastery of sensorimotor contingencies (Chs. 1–2, drawing on O'Regan & Noë, "A Sensorimotor Account of Vision and Visual Consciousness," *BBS* 24(5), 2001). *Out of Our Heads* (2009) extends the anti-neurocentric argument to consciousness itself. Critics — notably Ned Block's *Journal of Philosophy* 102(5) review (2005) — have argued that Noë conflates causation with constitution and oscillates between identifying perception with bodily activity and identifying it with expectations, an interpretive instability visible on his pp. 63–66 and 90.

**Radical enactivism**, articulated by Daniel Hutto and Erik Myin in *Radicalizing Enactivism* (MIT Press, 2013) and *Evolving Enactivism* (MIT Press, 2017), poses the **"Hard Problem of Content"**: no extant naturalistic theory (Dretske, Millikan, Fodor) successfully explains how basic cognitive activity could carry representational content with correctness conditions. Their positive thesis: *basic minds are contentless* — dynamic, interactive, world-involving — while content only enters with socio-linguistic scaffolding in what the 2017 book calls a "duplex" picture. *Radicalizing Enactivism* was reviewed critically in NDPR (Matthew Ratcliffe) and in *Mind* by Shapiro; the MIT Press paperbacks are paywalled but excerpts are on Google Books.

**Anthony Chemero's *Radical Embodied Cognitive Science*** (MIT Press, 2009; open PDF on uberty.org) is the fullest defense of a non-representational cognitive science grounded in *dynamical systems modelling* and an updated Gibsonian *ecological psychology*. Chemero argues, against Clark, that action-oriented representations are representations by courtesy only and collapse under scrutiny; the proper alternative is what he calls the "dynamical stance." Rupert's 2016 *Mind* triple review and Hutto's 2015 *Philosophical Quarterly* review both note that Chemero's treatment of "representation-hungry" tasks (language, memory, deliberation) remains programmatic.

These are not one tradition. **Shaun Gallagher's *Enactivist Interventions*** (Oxford, 2017) explicitly taxonomises four strands — autopoietic, sensorimotor, radical, and social — and argues that they differ on what role representations, contents, and biological autonomy play. Gallagher's Chapter 6 also mounts the most developed enactivist critique of predictive processing as smuggling Cartesian internalism back in.

## Participatory sense-making: the social extension

**The most fertile enactive contribution to social cognition is De Jaegher and Di Paolo's "Participatory Sense-Making"** (*Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences* 6(4): 485–507, 2007; open PDF on ezequieldipaolo.net and ucd.ie). They define it as "the interactive coordination of intentional activity affecting individual sense-making, whereby new domains of sense-making may appear that were unavailable to each [individual] in isolation." The central theoretical move is that the **interaction process itself** can acquire operational closure and autonomy — so it is not merely context for cognition but can be *constitutive* of it. De Jaegher, Di Paolo and Gallagher made the three-way explanatory distinction — contextual, enabling, constitutive — precise in "Can social interaction constitute social cognition?" (*TiCS* 14(10): 441–447, 2010).

This concept does real work for **reciprocity**, **coordination of coordinations**, and by extension **commitment** in joint action, although the last is more fully developed in Gallagher's *Enactivist Interventions* (2017, Chs. 7–8) than in De Jaegher–Di Paolo. The requirement that each interactor retain autonomy gives the framework an implicit normative-political valence: De Jaegher explicitly notes that "when you coerce people or damage people to such an extent that they cannot participate anymore, then it's not a social interaction anymore." That remark gestures at power and authority but the enactive literature has not yet produced a systematic analytic treatment; the gap is real.

**Language** — a long-standing weak point — is addressed most fully in Di Paolo, Cuffari and De Jaegher's *Linguistic Bodies: The Continuity between Life and Language* (MIT Press, 2018), which treats languaging as an extension of participatory sense-making through habit formation and socially recognisable utterance-acts. Maturana's *Tree of Knowledge* treatment of languaging (Chs. 9–10) is its precursor; *Linguistic Bodies* takes up the scaling challenge that Clark and others had posed.

## Extended mind and the representational wars

**Clark and Chalmers's "The Extended Mind"** (*Analysis* 58(1): 7–19, 1998; open at consc.net and PhilPapers) pursues a different, more moderate programme — *active externalism* — with the **Parity Principle** (p. 8) stating that if an external process would count as cognitive were it performed in the head, we should recognise it as cognitive. The **Otto/Inga** thought-experiment (pp. 12–14) argues that Otto's notebook realises dispositional beliefs just as Inga's biological memory does, provided reliability, automatic endorsement, and trustworthiness obtain. Clark's *Being There* (MIT Press, 1997) contains the "007 principle" and the concept of *scaffolding*, while *Supersizing the Mind* (Oxford, 2008) defends Otto against critics and formalises the "complementarity" line as an alternative to parity.

The critical response has been sharp. **Frederick Adams and Kenneth Aizawa's "The Bounds of Cognition"** (*Philosophical Psychology* 14(1), 2001; and the 2008 Blackwell book) diagnose what they call the **coupling-constitution fallacy** and propose a *mark of the cognitive* — non-derived, intrinsic content realised by distinctively cognitive processes — that Otto's notebook fails. **Robert Rupert's "Challenges to the Hypothesis of Extended Cognition"** (*Journal of Philosophy* 101(8): 389–428, 2004; open PDF at spot.colorado.edu) introduced the **cognitive bloat** worry: if availability plus endorsement suffices, the cognitive system threatens to absorb smartphones, Wikipedia, and interlocutors indefinitely. Clark replies in "Memento's Revenge" (in Menary ed., *The Extended Mind*, MIT Press, 2010) and in the 2010 reply to Adams & Aizawa (*PCS* 9(4): 605–618).

The debate remains live; Giulia Piredda's 2017 *Frontiers in Psychology* paper argues that the coupling-constitution fallacy critique misidentifies the thesis, since extended-cognition theorists are precisely rejecting individualist boundary-drawing and must "bite the bullet" by denying the fallacy's framing. Notably, **Noë's extension of the argument to consciousness** diverges from Clark and Chalmers's original restriction (they bracketed conscious experience); Karina Vold's 2015 *JCS* paper is the clearest map of the disagreement.

## Predictive processing: continuity or Cartesian relapse?

**The most consequential current debate is whether predictive processing/active inference is enactive or internalist.** Jakob Hohwy's *The Predictive Mind* (Oxford, 2013) and "The Self-Evidencing Brain" (*Noûs* 50(2), 2016) read the **Markov blanket** as a strict epistemic partition — located, Hohwy suggests, at "the dorsal horn of the spinal cord" (2016, p. 276) — implying that prediction-error minimisation is "neurocentrically skull-bound." Andy Clark's *Surfing Uncertainty* (Oxford, 2016) and "How to Knit Your Own Markov Blanket" (Metzinger & Wiese eds., 2017, open on predictive-mind.net) argue the opposite: Markov blankets in biological agents are neither unique nor stationary, and the framework is compatible with — even supportive of — embodied, extended, enactive cognition.

Michael Kirchhoff and colleagues' "Markov Blankets of Life" (*J. R. Soc. Interface* 15, 2018) and Ramstead, Kirchhoff and Friston's "A Tale of Two Densities: Active Inference is Enactive Inference" (*Adaptive Behavior* 28(4), 2020, open on PMC) develop a reconciliation: generative models are enacted through adaptive behaviour rather than encoded in neural states, and the system's dynamics rather than its representations do the inferential work. Julian Kiverstein, Gallagher and others style this "predictive engagement." Critics inside enactivism (Di Paolo in *Linguistic Bodies* 2018; Marco Facchin's 2021 *Review of Philosophy and Psychology* paper "Extended Predictive Minds") argue that Markov blankets either make vehicle-internalism vacuously true or beg the question against extension. **This debate is where enactivism's future is currently being fought.**

## Social autopoiesis: the Luhmann question

**Niklas Luhmann extended autopoiesis to social systems in *Soziale Systeme* (1984; English trans. *Social Systems*, Stanford 1995)**, defining society as an operationally closed network of *communications* that reproduce themselves communication by communication. Maturana consistently rejected this extension. In the widely cited Poerksen interviews (also quoted in secondary sources like Mingers 2002) and in his 1988 paper "Reality: The Search for Objectivity," Maturana argued that social systems *presuppose* human communicators as their components; communications are reproduced *by humans*, not by one another, and so the analogy from biology to sociology breaks. **John Mingers's "Can Social Systems Be Autopoietic? Assessing Luhmann's Social Theory"** (*Sociological Review* 50(2): 278–299, 2002) is the most careful assessment and judges the application either metaphorical or a weaker analogue — "not autopoiesis in the technical sense." Ib Ravn has noted that Luhmann cites Maturana only three times in his 600-page magnum opus, and Maturana never engaged seriously with sociology — so the "Santiago School" and Luhmann's Bielefeld programme developed in parallel rather than in dialogue.

## Where the tradition is thin

Reviewing the primary literature against a working list of conceptual demands reveals uneven coverage. **Agency, adaptivity, identity, sense-making, and participatory reciprocity are densely theorised** — Barandiaran, Di Paolo and Rohde's "Defining Agency" (*Adaptive Behavior* 17(5): 367–386, 2009) gives the canonical triad of individuality, interactional asymmetry, and normativity. **Care and concern** are substantively present via Thompson's Hans-Jonas-inflected Chapter 5 and Varela's "Organism: A Meshwork of Selfless Selves" (1991). **Norms** are handled by grounding them in adaptive viability (Di Paolo 2005; Thompson 2007, Ch. 6) rather than in convention or contract.

By contrast, several concepts remain genuinely underdeveloped:

- **Trust, commitment, and explicit authority/power** have no systematic enactive treatment. De Jaegher's autonomy-in-interaction condition gestures at power but is not a theory of institutional authority. The closest sustained treatment is Gallagher's work on social institutions in *Enactivist Interventions* Chapters 9–10, but it remains programmatic.
- **Evidence** as an epistemic concept is largely absent in enactivism's own vocabulary; predictive processing imports a Bayesian notion that remains contested.
- **Memory** is treated anti-representationally by Hutto in *Evolving Enactivism* (Ch. 8) but the story for episodic, autobiographical, and semantic memory is still being built.
- **Reproduction** is notably *not* required for autopoiesis in Maturana's original formulation (this is a frequently misread point; see *Autopoiesis and Cognition* pp. 96–100, 112).
- **Signals** in the information-theoretic sense are explicitly replaced by *structural coupling*, which is a feature rather than a gap — but one that bears on how the tradition can or cannot absorb Shannon-style communication theory.

## Conclusion: three live fault lines

The enactive-extended tradition is best read not as a unified paradigm but as **a research programme organised around three productive disagreements**. First, on *life-mind continuity*: Thompson and Di Paolo defend strong continuity (autopoiesis plus adaptivity is sufficient for mind); Clark, Chalmers and Rupert largely bracket biological autonomy as irrelevant to what cognitive kinds are. Second, on *content*: Hutto and Myin, and Chemero, defend contentless basic cognition; Clark defends action-oriented representation and predictive generative models; Noë wavers. Third, on *boundaries*: the Markov-blanket debate between Hohwy and Clark/Kirchhoff recapitulates, in formal dress, the original 1998 Adams–Aizawa dispute about coupling and constitution.

The most important upshot for any reader trying to use this literature is that **you cannot cite "enactivism" without specifying which enactivism**, and the same caution applies to "4E cognition." The tradition's social and biological roots are strong; its formal treatment of language, institutions, memory, and authority is still catching up to the ambition of its founding claims. The citable primary sources are largely accessible — Varela/Maturana texts via Internet Archive and cepa.info, Di Paolo's corpus on his personal site, Clark and Chalmers via consc.net, the Hohwy/Clark predictive-processing debate open on predictive-mind.net — while the book-length syntheses (Thompson 2007, Gallagher 2017, Di Paolo et al. 2018) remain behind institutional paywalls or in print form only. That asymmetry shapes how the field is read and cited, and it rewards readers who go to the 2005–2010 journal literature where the conceptual foundations were actually hammered out.
