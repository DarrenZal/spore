Care Ethics and Feminist Economics (1982–present)
Care ethics and feminist economics center on the moral and social importance of care, dependency, and
reproductive labor. Care ethicists insist that care is not an abstract principle but rooted in real relationships:
it is simultaneously a cluster of practices, tasks, and values. For example, Held writes that care is “both a
practice, or cluster of practices, and a value, or cluster of values” that occurs in actual caring relationships
1 . This echoes Nel Noddings’s view that caring is an activity requiring engrossment in the cared-for, not a
fixed rule-based duty 2 3 . In this tradition care is often conceptualized in three overlapping senses: as
(1) concrete labor (especially unpaid or underpaid labor, e.g. homemaking, nursing), (2) a disposition or
attitude (e.g. receptiveness and concern for others’ wellbeing), and (3) a social practice or network of
relationships. Gilligan, for instance, emphasized an “ethic of care” as an orientation to relationships and
responsibilities rather than an abstract rule 4 . Held similarly contrasts care with justice-based ethics by
noting that care ethics “values the ties we have with particular other persons and the actual relationships
that partly constitute our identity” 5 . In short, care as a primitive is foundational and multifaceted: it
spans labor, emotional commitment, and moral attention.
Dependency and vulnerability are likewise central. Care ethicists hold that all humans are to some extent
dependent and vulnerable, and that moral life is built around responding to others’ needs. As the IEP notes,
care ethics “builds on the motivation to care for those who are dependent and vulnerable” 6 . Kittay, a
leading feminist philosopher of disability and care, argues that we are “all embedded in nested
dependencies” – dependency is not abnormal but a human “species-typical” condition 7 8 . Kittay further
emphasizes that recognizing inevitable dependency (e.g. illness, disability) should be welcomed as part of
the human condition: “It is part of our species typicality to be vulnerable to disability, to have periods of
dependency, and to be responsible to care for dependent individuals” 8 . In this tradition vulnerability is
thus seen not as a defect but as an impetus to care: the very vulnerability of humans (to sickness,
childhood, aging, etc.) underscores why caring relations are morally salient. Dependency is closely linked:
Kittay and others frame justice in terms of dependency relations – for example Kittay proposes a “third
principle” for justice, “To each according to [their] need, from each according to [their] capacity for care, and
such support from social institutions as to make available resources…to those providing care” (Kittay 1999)
9 . Care ethicists hold that society has special responsibilities to support those who care for others and
those who are cared-for.
Responsibility in care ethics is distinct from deontological obligation. Rather than duties commanded by
universal rules, responsibility in care theory is grounded in relationships and responsiveness. Tronto,
summarizing the moral elements of care, defines the second phase of care as responsibility: the
disposition “to respond and take care of” the needs one has noticed 10 . This is not the same as an abstract
duty or legal obligation; it is a willingness emerging from attentiveness to another’s needs. Noddings also
draws a distinction: natural caring arises spontaneously from a connection (“wanting to care”), whereas
ethical caring involves the reflective “I must” – a self-imposed imperative to continue caring when it is
difficult 3 . Thus care ethics locates moral responsibility within the caring relation itself, not in external
mandates. Importantly, care ethicists usually limit obligations: Noddings explicitly notes that we cannot care
for everyone, and so an ethic of care “implies a limit on our obligation” 3 . In practice, responsibility in care

theory means attentively taking on needs we have recognized, within the scope of our relationships and
capacities, rather than treating obligation as a universal duty owed to all.
Attention/attentiveness is highlighted as the first moral phase by Tronto. In Tronto’s model, care begins
with “caring about” – that is, recognizing that another has a need. Attentiveness is the “core ‘moral moment’”
of this phase 10 . Without attentiveness (notice and concern for another’s situation), no further caring
action can occur. Selma Sevenhuijsen explains Tronto’s view: “The first phase is caring about – perceiving the
need for care, with attentiveness as its core ‘moral moment’” 10 . In practical terms, care ethics often
emphasizes training people to be more observant and responsive to others’ suffering or needs – a theme
that recurs in much feminist pedagogical thought on care (e.g. Ruddick’s “maternal thinking” also stresses
attention to children’s needs).
Reproduction (social reproduction theory). Feminist economists link care ethics to the theory of social
reproduction – the idea that household and caregiving work reproduces labor power and social life. Folbre
and others argue that care and support for dependents is essential to the economy and class structure.
Folbre notes that large transfers of money, time, and emotional labor “within the family, community, and
the state” for children, the elderly, etc., are central to society’s reproduction 11 12 . Care ethicists and
feminist economists emphasize that social reproduction is not merely natural altruism but is shaped by
institutions and gendered power relations: for example, Folbre describes care as situated “in modes of
social reproduction characterized by a dialectic of cooperation and conflict,” in which powerful groups (men,
the affluent, older generations) seek to claim more benefits while shirking costs 13 . Federici similarly
connects reproductive labor to capitalism’s origins, showing how the unpaid labor of women in households
underpins exploitation of wage workers and how processes of “primitive accumulation” (e.g. witch-hunts,
enclosure, colonialism) violently imposed the sexual division of labor 14 . In sum, care ethics treats
reproduction of the workforce (childcare, eldercare, domestic maintenance) as a labor of care, often invisible
in official accounts but crucial to any economy.
Invisibility of domestic labor. A key theme is that care work – especially unpaid household labor – has
been hidden by mainstream economics. Waring’s If Women Counted (1988) famously showed how national
accounts exclude women’s unpaid labor, rendering it “worthless” in GDP measures (a form of patriarchal
bias) 15 . Folbre echoes this: she points out that in patriarchal economies, care work is often provided by
low-paid or unpaid labor (e.g. domestic workers, family caregivers) while beneficiaries (often male, older, or
wealthy) reap the rewards 13 . The invisibility of care labor means society undervalues it. As Waring
observed, “If you are invisible as a producer in a nation’s economy, you are invisible in the distribution of
benefits” 16 . Thus feminist economics, aligned with care ethics, insists on recognizing and valuing
reproductive and care labor – even though it is traditionally “unseen” in economic calculations.
Relational self. Care ethics fundamentally assumes a relational conception of the self. Instead of the
autonomous, self-sufficient individual of liberal theory, care ethicists see persons as inherently connected.
Held writes that care ethics “values the ties we have with particular other persons and the actual
relationships that partly constitute our identity” 5 . Gilligan’s own analysis illustrated this: in moral
dilemmas women (in her study) responded by envisioning networks of relationships that must be
maintained, rather than abstract rights of individuals 17 . In Held’s words, care ethics begins “with a
conception of persons as relational, connected and interdependent” 18 (paraphrased). Identity and moral
standing are understood as emerging through caring relations: e.g. Noddings describes the caring person
as aiming to “see the best self in the cared-for and work with him to actualize that self” (a mutual shaping of
identities) 19 . The relational self is thus intrinsic to care ethics: it rejects the idea of an atomistic agent,

2. Layering in Care Ethics
Care ethicists generally do not posit a rigid hierarchy of abstract moral theories layered above real life;
rather, they intertwine theory and practice. Still, two layering schemes are often mentioned in care-related
thought:
• Tronto’s Phases of Care (1993, 2013). In Moral Boundaries Tronto argues that care has four
interconnected phases: (1) caring about (attentiveness to needs), (2) taking care of (taking
responsibility to meet needs), (3) caregiving (competent hands-on work), and (4) care-receiving
(responsiveness of the person cared-for) 10 21 . Each phase involves different roles and moral
qualities (attentiveness, responsibility, competence, responsiveness). Tronto later added a fifth phase
– “caring with” – which introduces a collective, democratic dimension (solidarity and mutual trust) 22 .
These phases are not a strict moral calculus but a process model of caring relations. In Tronto’s
framework, care flows through these phases (and back): one must notice needs, assume
responsibility, competently provide care, and ensure the care is actually received. Each phase is
“load-bearing” in Tronto’s model, meaning ethical failures can occur at any stage (e.g. failing to
notice need, failing to provide good care, etc.) 10 . This offers one way of “layering” moral attention,
but note that Tronto frames these as dynamics of actual care practices, not separable abstract laws.
• Federici’s Layered Critique. Silvia Federici (1970s–) layers her critique of capitalism to include
reproductive labor. In Caliban and the Witch (2004) she describes how capitalism emerged through
primitive accumulation that targeted women’s bodies and household labor. Federici stresses three
interlocking “layers”: waged labor (the formal proletariat), unwaged domestic/reproductive labor
(mothering, cooking, cleaning), and historical processes of primitive accumulation (colonialism,
enclosures, witch-hunts) that violently separated producers from means of subsistence. She argues
capitalism depends on extracting value from unwaged care work and using state/coercive power to
enforce this division. As one commentator notes, Federici’s materialist feminism “revealed the
hierarchies… engendered by a system that depends upon the devaluation of human activity and the
exploitation of labour in its unpaid and low-paid dimensions” 14 . In effect, Federici’s “layers” remind
us that care ethics cannot ignore class and colonial histories. Her view contrasts with any notion that
care can simply be resolved by markets or interpersonal virtue alone.
• Rejection of Separate Theory/Practice Layers. A common stance in care ethics is skepticism about
separating high-level moral theory from lived practice. Held explicitly ties moral theory to practice,
arguing care is understood by examining existing caring practices and then normatively improving
them, rather than starting from abstract principles 1 . She writes that care is embedded in
“unsatisfactory contexts of domination,” so ethics should guide and reform practices. Similarly,
Noddings resists top-down principles and grounds ethics in the concrete caring encounter. In sum,
most care theorists avoid a traditional layering (principles → rules → actions) in favor of an ethics
that grows out of and returns to relationships. There is no sharp division where “moral theory” lives
entirely apart from “the reality of care”; instead, theory and practice are interwoven, with theory
continuously informed by actual caring situations 1 .

3. Universal Concepts in the Care Tradition
Certain abstract concepts from broader philosophy are treated differently in care ethics. Below are some of
those concepts and whether they are emphasized (“load-bearing”) in care/feminist economics:
• Trust: Trust is an implicit theme. Care relationships presuppose mutual trust (the cared-for trusts the
caregiver, and vice versa). Kittay explicitly insists that trustworthiness be built into society’s ethic of
care: she notes a disabled person (actor Richard Pryor) “had to learn trust” in order to live well, and
argues that “the trust that [he] had to learn…and the need for trustworthiness that warrants such
trust—ought to be a feature of all our lives” 23 . Noddings likewise highlights warm acceptance and
trust as central to genuine care 19 . So while care ethics does not theorize “trust” as a standalone
principle, trust is recognized as essential to caring relations.
• Care (as a concept): Obviously load-bearing. The term itself is the tradition’s cornerstone. However,
as noted above, its meaning is expanded beyond sympathy: it entails a certain power to meet needs.
Definitions often link care with responsivity and relational attentiveness 1 6 .
• Identity: In care ethics, identity is seen as relationally formed. The relational self is central: Held
argues that personal identity and moral agency are constituted by ties and responsibilities to others
5 . Gilligan’s narratives likewise treat characters’ identities as bound up with networks of caring
relationships 17 . Thus, identity (the self) is redefined through care, rather than taken as a given.
• Memory: Memory figures in the theory chiefly as motivating past experience. Noddings and others
note that memories of having been cared for – or idealized images of caring – inspire people to care
for others. For example, the IEP summary says care ethics “builds on … memories of being cared
for” 6 . The memory of past care is often cited by theorists (e.g. Noddings) as the emotional source
of the caring attitude. So memory is present but only as background motivation, not as an abstract
principle.
• State: The role of the State is acknowledged, especially by Tronto, Held, Folbre and feminist
economists. Tronto in particular extends care to political life: in Caring Democracy she argues that
democratic institutions should allocate caring responsibilities fairly 22 . Folbre and others analyze
how state policy (welfare, pension, healthcare) affects care distribution 11 . Thus the state is
considered important. However, unlike concepts like care or dependency, “state” itself is not deeply
theorized; rather, state institutions are one arena among others. It is load-bearing in so far as policy
must support care, but the tradition does not have a single theoretical concept of “the state” – it uses
existing political analysis.
• Signals / Norms / Protocols: Formal notions like “signal” or “protocol” (as used in coordination
theory) do not appear in care ethics. Instead, ethicists talk of social norms or expectations of
obligation. For instance, Folbre notes that obligations to care are shaped by “enforceable rules and
internalized norms” in the context of patriarchal social reproduction 24 . So while the tradition
emphasizes norms (normative expectations to care, often unevenly distributed by gender and class),
it does not discuss any abstract signaling mechanisms or codified protocols beyond referring to
cultural norms of gender and kinship. In short, norms (especially gendered norms of caring) matter
a lot; technical notions of signaling/protocol are absent.

• Agency: The care tradition reconceives agency rather than ignoring it. Dominant theories of
autonomy are critiqued; care ethics accepts that individuals make choices but always within
networks of need and responsibility. Virginia Held, for example, contrasts the liberal autonomous
agent with the “interdependent” agents of care ethics 5 . Concepts like “individual agency” are thus
loaded differently: agency is seen as relational and embedded, not atomistic. The term agency per se
is used, but usually to highlight how dependent individuals can still exercise some agency (or how
traditional ideas of agency must be broadened).
• Power / Authority: Issues of power are explicitly addressed under other names. Tronto discusses
the powerlessness of caregivers (especially women) and the power imbalance in care relations.
Folbre’s analysis (above) points out that patriarchal power leads men and elites to claim the
benefits of care while women shoulder the burdens 13 . Held also examines how care must account
for power/violence. Thus power is recognized: care ethics foregrounds how power and hierarchy
shape caring – whether gender, racial, or economic hierarchies. “Power” itself as a category is not
one of the primitive building blocks of care theory, but the tradition is very aware of authority and
power disparities in care.
• Commitment, Intent, Evidence, Holon (whole–part): These coordination-related terms are not
part of care ethics vocabulary. Care ethicists do talk about commitment (e.g. a caregiver’s
commitment to another’s welfare) and about honesty/truthfulness (e.g. Noddings on trust), but not
in the abstract Spore-sense. “Intent” in care might be conveyed as caring intention or motivation, but
again not formalized. “Evidence” has no special role. “Holon” (whole–part unit) is not used. Care
ethics tends to use ordinary language about roles (caregiver, cared-for) and relationships, rather
than analytical primitives like “signal” or “holon”.
• Boundary: Boundaries are an explicit concern: the very title Moral Boundaries (Tronto) signals care’s
aim to redraw who and what counts as in our moral community. Tronto argues that strict private–
public and insider–outsider boundaries have excluded care from politics. Care ethics blurs these
boundaries: caregiving in private life is seen as having public significance, and obligations once
considered “private” (family care) have political implications. Thus “boundaries” (especially moral and
social boundaries) are load-bearing in Tronto’s work. Other care scholars likewise emphasize that
care dissolves the rigid boundaries of abstract moral theory.
• Reciprocity: The notion of reciprocity is transformed. In traditional justice ethics, reciprocity means
an equivalent exchange (tit-for-tat). In care ethics, reciprocity is looser. Noddings describes a form of
mutuality: the cared-for, in responding to genuine care, may “grow” and voluntarily cooperate, which
she terms the cared-for’s reciprocity 25 . However, this is not contractual reciprocity but a byproduct
of authentic caring relations. Some care theorists argue that care ethics de-emphasizes strict
reciprocity; help is given based on need, not expectation of direct return. In sum, care ethics
acknowledges that relationships ideally involve mutual respect and responsiveness, but it does not
require or frame them as equal exchanges. (Notably, a critique of care ethics is that it avoids
demanding reciprocity, which some see as problematic.)
• Reproduction / Continuity: Continuity – sustaining society through time – is indeed a concern.
Social reproduction theory, as discussed, shows that care work reproduces the next generation of
workers and citizens. Care ethicists often implicitly assume some version of continuity: they value
sustaining communities and families. However, the abstract term “continuity” is not commonly used;

5

instead, they speak of generational duties, children’s future, etc. Feminist economists like Folbre and
Federici explicitly analyze how current decisions about care affect society’s long-term survival and
equality.
In summary, care ethics foregrounds the interdependence of persons, attention to needs, and the
relational context of morality. Concepts like trust, norms of obligation, and relational identity are
important. By contrast, technical terms from other traditions (signals, holons, protocols) are absent. Where
terms overlap (e.g. agency, authority), care theorists reinterpret them through a relational lens.

4. Internal Disagreements
Within care/feminist-econ there are notable divergences and debates:
• Gender Essentialism vs. Universality: Gilligan’s early work suggested a gender-linked care
orientation (“women’s moral voice of care”) 4 . Some interpreted this as an essential tie between
women and care. By contrast, Joan Tronto (and later Held, Slote, etc.) stress that care capacities are
universal and not inherently feminine. Tronto, for example, explicitly rejects essentialism: her “caring
with” is phrased in universal terms (solidarity among all) 22 . In practice, many care ethicists have
distanced themselves from strict gender essentialism. The IEP notes this debate: while care ethics
originated with women’s experiences, it “is certainly not a mere description or generalization of
women’s attitudes” 26 . The field remains divided: some (like Gilligan herself) emphasize gendered
patterns as heuristic, while others (e.g. Tronto, Engster) promote a gender-neutral conception of
care.
• Care as Moral Theory vs. Political Theory: Some authors, like Held, approached care primarily as a
moral-philosophical theory, critiquing Kant/Rawls and arguing for care-based moral principles 1 .
Others, like Tronto, emphasize care as a political challenge. Tronto’s work explicitly expands care into
public life and democratic institutions. For example, in Caring Democracy she argues “we need to
think about democracy as the allocation of caring responsibilities” 22 . So a tension exists: is care
ethics mainly a shift in moral psychology/virtue ethics (Held, Gilligan) or a blueprint for reordering
politics and economics (Tronto, Folbre)? Many acknowledge both aspects, but individual authors lean
one way.
• Commodification of Care (Market vs. Non-market): Feminist economists debate whether care
should be fully socialized or partially marketized. Held argues that an ethic of care generally
supports limiting markets in essential care services, because “dominant moral approaches lack
resources to deal with” the commodification of care 27 . Many care ethicists lament market
pressures on care (e.g. privatization of eldercare). However, others point to the potential of paid care
jobs to improve caregivers’ status (e.g. public subsidies for child care). Federici’s Marxist critique, for
instance, is skeptical of wage labor in care: she warns that paying for housework in the market can
co-opt feminist movements (she was a leader in the “Wages for Housework” debate). So care ethics is
split between visions of caring as a shared social good versus as an economic service.
• Care vs. Justice Ethics: A classic debate is whether care is complementary to, or in tension with,
justice-based ethics. Many care theorists (Held, Baier) view care as bridging gaps in abstract justice
theories. Held boldly claims “there can be care without justice… care is probably the most deeply
fundamental value… There can be no justice without care” 28 . That implies care is foundational and

justice must be grounded in care. Other philosophers (e.g. Annette Baier) see care as adding needed
nuance to justice without wholly replacing it. Still others argue for compatibility: Gilligan herself later
resisted framing care vs. justice as a zero-sum conflict 4 . Thus within the tradition there is no
single stance: some argue care completes justice, others treat it as a rival emphasis.

5. Critiques and Limits of Care Ethics/Feminist Economics
Critics (both external and some feminists) have pointed out several issues in care ethics/feminist economics:
• Gender Essentialism (re-emphasized): As noted, one risk is re-essentializing women as carers. Early
critiques warned that focusing on “women’s nature” could lock women into caregiving roles. Care
ethicists have tried to counter this by stressing men’s capacity to care and the socially constructed
nature of gender roles. The literature acknowledges that essentialist language can slip in, and warns
against it 29 .
• Race and Intersectionality: Classic care theory (Gilligan, Noddings, early Held) was criticized for
insufficiently addressing race and class. Black feminist scholars like Patricia Hill Collins have
emphasized that experiences of care are shaped by race and history of racial oppression. For
instance, the IEP notes that women of color bear different care burdens and less social support
compared to white women 30 . Without an intersectional lens, care ethics can overlook how
“powerful groups” appropriate the benefits of care 13 . Recent work explicitly incorporates race (e.g.
Alison Jaggar, Claudia Card), but the original canon had gaps. Thus an important critique is that care
ethics must account for racialized and global inequalities in care provision and dependence.
• Lack of Institutional Focus: Some critics argue care ethics has overly emphasized personal
relationships (e.g. mother-child) and neglected large-scale institutions of care (hospitals, welfare
states, workplaces). Although Tronto, Folbre and others have addressed state and market policies,
many care ethicists still concentrate on micro-level. Critics urge more analysis of how bureaucracies,
workplaces and global economies affect care. This is slowly changing as “political care ethics”
emerges, but institutional care remains under-theorized compared to family care.
• Oversight of Class Dynamics: A Marxist- or materialist-feminist critique holds that focusing on
moral relations can obscure the class basis of care exploitation. Federici (as above) and other Marxist
feminists argue capitalist class interests shape care (through unpaid labor, wage labor stratification).
Care ethicists counter that they do attend to economic structures (Folbre, Fraser), but some Marxists
feel more attention is needed on capitalist imperatives.
• Ambiguity / Action Guidance: Critics also point out care ethics often eschews clear principles,
making it hard to apply consistently. Even the IEP notes that care ethics can be “unduly ambiguous”
and lacking concrete decision rules 31 . Care ethicists respond that care must be flexible and
context-sensitive, though this means less algorithmic clarity. Relatedly, some see care ethics as
parochial (focused on close others) and not easily extended to strangers or global issues, though
many have since worked on extending it (Held on global, Tronto on NGOs, etc.).
In sum, care ethics/feminist economics presents a rich approach that foregrounds human interdependence,
but it must grapple with not reifying gender roles, not ignoring other axes of identity, and extending its
