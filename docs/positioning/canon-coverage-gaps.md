# Canon Coverage Gaps

Spore canon is useful partly because it says what it can carry. It is also useful when it says what it does not yet carry. This note records coverage gaps surfaced by the 2026-04-28 canon-navigability load-bearing test, where a fresh agent navigated Spore, bioregional-coordination, and BKC without prior conversation context.

This is positioning prose, not a foundation doc, pattern admission, or corrective ADR. It does not fill the gaps it names. It makes the boundary collaborator-citable so future readers do not mistake silence for coverage or force a premature admission because the taxonomy would look tidier.

The method is the one named in the canon-rebuild retrospective: honest-rigor cluster-counting, parsimony as earning-test outcome, and decline/defer-with-triggers rather than admission-by-aesthetic-completion. A gap can be real and still belong at pattern layer, protocol layer, an instance repo, or outside current canon scope.

## §1 What Spore Canon Does Cover

Spore's active grammar covers the nine primitives named in `spore.project-vision`: Field, Holon, Membrane, Intent, Commitment, Joint-commitment, Evidence, Signal, and Reproduction. The same vision doc records three cross-cutting doctrines, two modes, two properties, and the current pattern roster.

The foundation layer covers invariant doctrine for federation, holonic structure, governance artifacts and graph projections, structural legitimacy, sensor-oracle governance, representation authority, failure modes, actuator logic, actor governance, maintenance economics, the instance model, and external validation. That layer is intentionally not an implementation manual.

The pattern layer covers recurring coordination recipes such as commitment pooling, federated knowledge exchange, federation encounter, and view templates. Patterns are where compositions become usable without making every recurring form a primitive.

The connection layer maps external or peer-instance work into Spore's vocabulary without merging bounded contexts. `spore.connection.bkc-as-instance-family` maps BKC as an instance-family for federation-encounter evidence. `spore.connection.bioregional-coordination-as-peer-instance-family` registers a peer canon-bearing repo at the broader agentic-bioregionalism layer.

The positioning layer names frames that are strategically useful but lighter than foundation doctrine. `spore.positioning.agents-and-canons` is explicit about this: representational categories for agent-readable canon are committed in foundation docs, while authority, evaluation, regeneration, and routine AI-summary use remain deferred.

That coverage is substantial. It is not exhaustive. The following gaps are real because a careful navigator expected canon help at those surfaces and found only adjacent material.

## §2 Confirmed Gaps

### §2.1 Food Systems Coordination

**Description.** Food systems are absent as a dedicated coordination domain across Spore, bioregional-coordination, and BKC. BKC's coordination-stack mentions food systems organizers as workshop participants and food systems knowledge as an offer inside a commitment pool. That is not the same as a food-systems coordination pattern.

**Evidence anchor.** Spore's project vision names the general coordination ecology and the primitive loop, but it does not specialize food production, distribution, seed systems, foodshed governance, or land-access coordination as a canonical surface. BKC's `coordination-stack.md` brings food systems in as an example of participant knowledge inside the workshop/pool flow, not as an authored domain grammar. bioregional-coordination names economics, mapping, governance, ecological monitoring, and stewardship as broader frame aspects; it does not yet admit food systems doctrine.

**Adjacent territory.** This gap is most likely pattern-layer-shaped or instance-repo-shaped. A food-systems pattern would need evidence from actual foodshed coordination: offers and needs, land and labor commitments, seasonality, storage and distribution constraints, food sovereignty protocols, and fulfillment evidence. BKC may be a source of future evidence, but BKC has not yet authored the pattern.

**Canon-edge boundary.** Not foundation-layer-shaped today. Spore already has Field, Commitment, Evidence, maintenance economics, and federation protocol surfaces that can host food-systems work. The gap is domain-specialized composition, not missing grammar primitives.

**Re-opening triggers.**

1. A Spore-aligned instance records repeated food-systems coordination flows that cannot be explained cleanly by commitment pooling plus federated knowledge exchange.
2. A bridge note shows cross-instance convergence across at least two food-systems deployments, not just one BKC workshop example.
3. A food-systems protocol needs canon-citable standing for seasonality, land access, seed sovereignty, foodshed accounting, or perishable-flow commitments in a way existing patterns cannot carry.

### §2.2 Multi-Actor-Class Federation

**Description.** Spore's actor-governance foundation handles actor admission, role assignment, delegation, power asymmetry, joint actors, federation-actor encounters, capture remediation, governance bodies, and authorization boundaries. It does not yet articulate a coordination grammar for heterogeneous actor classes coordinating in one field: civic society, provincial/federal agencies, private actors, and First Nations or Indigenous governance bodies.

**Evidence anchor.** `actor-governance.md` §4.1 admits actors by inheritance, election/selection, or self-declaration with ratification, and §4.7 governs governance-body composition. That is within-field actor governance. `federation-encounter.md` is a pattern for federation-to-federation encounters. The load-bearing test found no Spore doc that makes heterogeneous-actor-class-to-heterogeneous-actor-class coordination a first-class pattern inside a shared field.

**Adjacent territory.** This may belong as a pattern extending federation-encounter, or as a protocol in a bioregional coordination repo once actual mixed-class governance events generate evidence. It is not automatically a new foundation doctrine because F3 already governs actor standing and F7 already governs instance compatibility.

**Canon-edge boundary.** Pattern-layer-shaped. The foundation layer can say that actor roles require standing, coupling-to-consequence, rule-level decomposition, and contest routes. It does not specify how a fisheries ministry, watershed society, land trust, private funder, and Indigenous nation co-author one ratification field without collapsing differences between legal authority, relational authority, funding authority, and land-based authority.

**Re-opening triggers.**

1. At least two mixed-class coordination events produce governance artifacts whose failure or success depends on actor-class differences rather than ordinary actor-role differences.
2. A federation-encounter implementation repeatedly needs sub-rules for heterogeneous actor-class representation, consent, ratification, or veto scope.
3. A bioregional-coordination ADR admits local doctrine for mixed-class governance and needs upstream Spore mapping to avoid duplicating foundation semantics.

### §2.3 Indigenous-Protocol to Coordination-Grammar Mapping

**Description.** bioregional-coordination names Indigenous-protocol structures alongside, and sometimes prior to, Western legal forms. BKC's coordination-stack mentions Indigenous land defenders as workshop participants. Spore's `ADR-0001` holds pluriversal incommensurability as first-class canon. None of these gives a Cascadia hub a method for sequencing nation-to-nation joint-commitments alongside settler-jurisdictional commitments.

**Evidence anchor.** bioregional-coordination `project-vision.md` says bioregional Field constitutional layers often carry Indigenous-protocol structures alongside Western legal forms. Spore `project-vision.md` and `actor-governance.md` acknowledge pluriversal and Indigenous governance surfaces, including relational identity and pluriversal governance-body framings. `spore:ADR-0001-pluriversal-incommensurability` holds the tension rather than resolving it into Spore's grammar.

**Adjacent territory.** This is boundary-of-canon-scope plus future protocol territory. It requires Indigenous-led practice-grounding and cannot be supplied by Spore by inference. A bridge note may map existing practice once there is consent and standing to do so, but a Spore-side positioning doc should not author operational protocol here.

**Canon-edge boundary.** Foundation-layer-shaped only as an acknowledged residue, not as an admission target ready for direct authoring. The relevant foundation documents already name incommensurability, relational identity, actor standing, and governance-body composition. The missing piece is not more abstract grammar; it is legitimate mapping practice between authority systems.

**Re-opening triggers.**

1. Indigenous-led or consented governance artifacts explicitly ask for Spore translation into coordination-grammar terms.
2. A bioregional coordination instance records a repeated sequencing problem between nation-to-nation commitments and settler-jurisdictional commitments that participants want canon to help route.
3. A bridge note can cite practice-grounded sources without extracting or flattening protocol knowledge into Spore-owned doctrine.

## §3 Agent-Discovered Adjacent Gaps

The load-bearing agent also surfaced concerns that are real but not all canon-coverage gaps in the same sense as §2.

### §3.1 Inter-Domain Composition

**Status.** Agent-discovered; not one of the three original confirmed gaps.

The agent asked how one piece of evidence, such as a watershed restoration attestation, flows simultaneously through mapping update, commitment-pool fulfillment, BFF impact reporting, and governance memory. Spore has the primitives for this: Evidence, Signal, Commitment, graph projections, representation authority, and actuator logic. BKC's coordination-stack narrates a partial operational path.

This is pattern/protocol-layer-shaped. A future pattern could name multi-domain evidence routing if repeated implementations show the same composition problem. It is not a foundation gap today.

### §3.2 Repair of Governance Commitments Made on Bad Evidence

**Status.** Agent-discovered; not one of the three original confirmed gaps.

The agent found the doctrine coherent but operationally thin for retroactive repair: F6 recognizes sensor/attestation/evidence-integrity failure, F4 adjudicates representation precedence, and F5 supplies response categories including amend-declared-state and hold-as-tension. What remains hard is the specific repair protocol for three governance commitments made in good faith on bad data.

This is mostly protocol-layer territory. The foundation layer should not prescribe rollback algorithms or compensation formulas. It should preserve contestability, amendment, escalation, and tension-holding, which it already does.

### §3.3 Detection Mechanisms for Failure Recognition

**Status.** Agent-discovered; not one of the three original confirmed gaps.

F6 names failure classes; it does not specify who notices an eight-month sensor drift, when the drift becomes visible, or what monitoring cadence should exist. That is a real operational question. It belongs to sensor deployment protocols, maintainer-economics, audit cadence design, and possibly future patterns for long-horizon ecological monitoring.

The absence is not a missing foundation category. It is a boundary between category-recognition doctrine and deployment-specific detection machinery.

### §3.4 Specific Compensation Protocols

**Status.** Agent-discovered, but not a canon-coverage gap at foundation layer.

F9 explicitly declines to prescribe rates, allocation algorithms, currency choices, payment frequencies, or settlement mechanisms. That is not a silence to fill; it is deliberate foundation-vs-pattern boundary discipline. BKC's Hub Cultivator, TBFF, and BFF surfaces instantiate maintenance-economics but remain BKC-authored operational specificity.

## §4 Re-Opening Trigger Summary

The practical trigger discipline is:

- Food systems stops being a silent gap when a repeated foodshed coordination pattern appears across practice, not just as workshop participant vocabulary.
- Multi-actor-class federation stops being a gap when mixed-class governance events produce artifacts that existing actor-governance and federation-encounter patterns cannot route without loss.
- Indigenous-protocol mapping stops being a gap only when practice-grounded, consented, Indigenous-led or Indigenous-authorized material asks for translation support.
- Inter-domain composition becomes a pattern candidate when multi-domain evidence routing recurs across deployments.
- Bad-evidence repair becomes protocol work when real cases require replay, amendment, compensation, or held-tension procedures beyond F5 categories.
- Failure detection becomes protocol work when sensor or attestation deployments need canon-citable audit cadences.
- Specific compensation mechanisms remain pattern/protocol work unless the foundation-vs-pattern boundary itself breaks.

Re-opening is not auto-admission. It opens an audit cycle. The audit may still decline, scope-condition, park as prose, admit a pattern, or route to an instance repo.

## §6 What This Doc Does Not Do

This doc does not propose new primitives, doctrines, modes, properties, patterns, protocols, ADRs, or repo structure.

It does not treat every silence as a deficiency. A silence is a gap only when a real navigation or coordination task expects canon help and the existing layer cannot provide it without over-claiming.

It does not make Spore responsible for domains where another canon, an instance repo, or a consented practice community should lead.

Its only commitment is honest acknowledgment: current canon is strong enough to say where it does not yet reach.
